# Direct-Response Copywriting - NEWIF, the Nine Questions, and Three Conversion-Killing Mistakes

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[03-Tactics-and-Playbooks/Tactics and Playbooks Index|Tactics and Playbooks]] → [[03-Tactics-and-Playbooks/Content-Creation-and-Distribution/Content Creation and Distribution Map|Content Creation and Distribution]]

## One-line summary
Sales messages convert on two checklists — is the message New, Unique, Exciting, Easy, Predictable, and Huge (NEWIF), and does it answer all nine questions a skeptical prospect is silently asking — and fail for three repeatable, fixable reasons: burying the benefit, jargon, and claims louder than proof.

## Context
Suby built these checklists from testing ad and landing-page copy across $7.8B in generated client sales. They apply to any sales message — Google ad, Facebook ad, video sales letter, landing page, product description, or homepage — not just paid ads specifically (compare [[Hyperdopamine Ads - Pattern Interrupt, Curiosity, and Native Ad Anatomy]], which is about the surrounding creative/format rather than the message logic itself).

## Key insights
- **A compelling offer beats a convincing argument.** Bad marketing tries to win a debate with the prospect; good marketing makes an offer so good the prospect doesn't need convincing — "I don't need to be convinced, the offer is good enough" is the target reaction.
- **Talk about them, not you.** Track the ratio of "we/our/company name" versus "you/your" in a piece of copy — 80% of the message should be about the prospect's desired end state, 20% about the product/system. Prospects don't care about your brand; they care about themselves and the gap between where they are and where they want to be.
- **Consumption precedes conversion.** If the message isn't read/watched, it can't convert — so the first job of any sales message is to sell its own consumption (via headline, intrigue, and readability), not just to sell the underlying product.
- **Write at a fifth-grade reading level.** The instinct to sound expert by using industry jargon backfires — the moment a reader hits one word they don't understand, they disengage rather than decode it. Run copy through the Hemingway app and edit until the flagged sentences and reading-grade score drop; bestselling books and the largest YouTube channels write below a 5th-grade level, some copywriters target 3rd–4th.
- **Proof has to be louder than the claim.** In a market where every competitor is making the same benefit claim, the claim itself has stopped being differentiating — screenshots, reviews, specific results, and testimonials are what actually move skepticism, because the market has learned to distrust unsupported claims.
- **Specificity is what makes a claim believable.** "Lose weight" is vague and easy to doubt; "lose 12 to 19 kilos in 5 weeks" is specific enough to sound engineered rather than hoped-for. Vague claims read as marketing; specific claims read as evidence.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
### NEWIF — what every message must signal

(Suby's mnemonic; six qualities, tested against every ad and offer):
- **New** — feels current, not a rehash of something the prospect has already seen.
- **Unique** — unlike anything else in the category.
- **Exciting** — a genuinely large opportunity, not a minor tweak.
- **Easy** — low perceived effort required to get the benefit.
- **Predictable** — backed by a "parade of proof" that the outcome reliably happens for others, not just a hopeful claim.
- **Huge** — the benefit can't be small; the message is competing with someone's entire newsfeed for attention, and a modest claim loses that fight by default.

Following this formula tends to make messaging polarizing — Suby's view is that's a feature, not a bug: a message bland enough to offend no one is also bland enough to be ignored.

### The nine questions every sales message must answer
1. **Is this of personal interest to me?** — call out the specific target market directly (e.g., "Attention 40-year-old men who have 12kg to lose").
2. **Why should I pay attention to this right now?** — sell the consumption of the message itself; without urgency, the prospect delays, and delay kills the sale.
3. **Is this the exact specific solution I need?** — the offer should feel custom-built for this reader's specific situation, not generic.
4. **What is NEWIF about this?** — apply the formula above directly to the message.
5. **What is the undeniable proof this is real?** — screenshots, reviews, results; show, don't tell.
6. **How does it actually work?** — after the emotional hook, give the rational brain a logistics walkthrough of the steps.
7. **What are other people saying about it?** — written and video testimonials, and a business process for collecting them continuously.
8. **What guarantee backs this working for me specifically?** — remove the "but I'm different" objection with an explicit, stated guarantee.
9. **What do you want me to do next?** — give an explicit, specific next action ("click this link, you'll be taken to a short survey, fill it out so we can customize your solution") rather than assuming the reader knows.

### Three conversion-killing mistakes
1. **The buried benefit** — leading with company history, founder bio, and a feature list instead of the prospect's desired end state. Fix: open with where the reader wants to be, not who you are.
2. **Jargon overload** — writing like an industry insider. Fix: fifth-grade reading level, Hemingway app, verbatim market language pulled from forums/reviews instead of internal vocabulary.
3. **Claims louder than proof** — asserting a benefit without backing it. Fix: attach specific, undeniable proof to every claim; address the anticipated "yeah but that's not me" objection by showing multiple avatars achieving the result.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Before publishing any sales message, run it against the nine questions as a checklist — any unanswered question is a specific, fixable conversion leak.
- Count "we/our" versus "you/your" occurrences in a draft; rewrite until roughly 80% of sentences address the reader directly.
- Paste ad, landing page, or email copy into the Hemingway app; cut and simplify until flagged sentences are minimal and the grade level is at or below 5th grade.
- Build a running "proof folder" — screenshot every five-star review, client email, or result the moment you see it — so a parade of proof is always ready when building an offer or landing page.
- Replace vague claims with the most specific number you can defend (a range, a timeframe, a percentage) rather than a general benefit statement.
- End every sales message with one explicit, concrete next action — never assume the reader infers what to do.

## Notable examples
- "Here's how to lose weight" (vague, low-converting) versus "here's how to lose 12 to 19 kilos in the next 5 weeks" (specific, higher-converting) as the illustration of specificity increasing believability.
- The Facebook retargeting-pixel dynamic cited as evidence that claims alone no longer differentiate: once a prospect clicks one ad in a category, they're immediately flooded with competitor ads making the identical claim, so proof becomes the only remaining differentiator.
