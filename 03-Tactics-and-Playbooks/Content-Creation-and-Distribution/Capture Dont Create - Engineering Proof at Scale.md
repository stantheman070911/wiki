# Capture, Don't Create: Engineering Proof at Scale

## One-line summary
Bake proof-generation into how you already run your business and document what's already happening, rather than manufacturing content from scratch.



## Context

This page organizes **Capture, Don't Create: Engineering Proof at Scale** as a reusable playbook; the sections below preserve its original framework, steps, and examples.
## Tactics you can apply today

**a) Mine your real meetings for real stories.**
Transcribe your virtual meetings, then prompt AI: "Tell me the interesting moments from these meetings." Speak to genuine examples instead of invented ones — this sits further along the proof-to-risk spectrum than fabricated anecdotes.

**b) Engineer proof into your existing business motion (the strongest move).**
Bake proof components into how you already service customers, so documentation adds no extra time. The **"self-licking ice cream cone" loop**: sell → customers buy → products generate marketing → marketing acquires customers → repeat.

- B2C example (hair extensions): insert sweepstakes/lottery tickets into the product → creates a marketing motion → call winners personally → install in your salon → document the whole loop.
- B2B example (marketing agency): bring the customer in, walk through the campaign, show the results, document it — do it with every customer, regardless of size.

**c) If you lack enough customers, manufacture the opportunity.**
Offer free audits or free work to 5-10 people, and document them. "Let me do real work for free and give it away" — few refuse, and you generate proof publicly.

**d) State facts and show what only you can show.**
Make the strongest verifiable fact or artifact the center of the content: results, customer records, product behavior, reviews, or a live demonstration. If the truthful facts are not compelling, improve the product and collect better evidence rather than warping the claim.

## Content production philosophy: "Capture, don't create"
Volume comes from **capturing** activity you already do, not sitting down to record from scratch. Recording every piece from scratch is miserable and caps your output; instead, do what you enjoy in the course of running your business (e.g. live Q&As with customers) and document/capture those moments. The goal is to feed the proof machine continuously by documenting while you deliver — not by adding a separate content-creation workload on top of your real work.

## Bottom line
AI disrupts creators from lowest risk to highest, in that order. To protect yourself, do the things AI finds hardest to replicate: demonstrate expertise and product quality in front of real people — ideally your customers — and document it. If you lack the customer base to do this privately, do it publicly. AI may eventually do everything, but it cannot yet — win with what you have. "The proof is going to be in the pudding — make sure you've got your first spoon in."

## Key terms
- **Proof** — third-party record (exits, revenue, headcount) + real-time demonstration of expertise.
- **Self-licking ice cream cone** — a self-reinforcing loop where selling generates marketing that generates more selling, with proof baked in.
- **Capture, don't create** — sourcing content from activity you already perform, rather than producing it from scratch.

## Application
- Audit your last month of customer interactions: which ones would have made compelling proof content if you'd simply hit record or taken a screenshot?
- If you're short on customers to showcase, design a small free-work offer (5-10 people) specifically to generate your first batch of documented proof.
- Replace at least one item on your content calendar with "capture" instead of "create" this week — document something you were already going to do anyway.
- Ask of every claim: “What could I show so I no longer need to say this?” Then build that evidence into delivery and capture it by default.
