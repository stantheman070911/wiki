# Clear Over Clever - Write Below Third Grade and Meet the Avatar

## One-line summary
Strip jargon and complexity out of your marketing until it reads below a third-grade level and speaks to the customer's actual outcome ("sad face → happy face, and insurance covers it"); if a reader has to decode a word, you've already lost them.

## Principle
Clarity beats cleverness. Marketing succeeds when the prospect absorbs the message instantly, not when it demonstrates the seller's sophistication. Every word that makes someone pause to decode is a word during which they stopped listening. Meet the avatar where they are—their pain and desired outcome—in the simplest language possible, then compress the message into no more than three memorable benefits.

## Scenario
Use this whenever technical, expert, or highly credentialed operators market to non-expert buyers; when ads or sales copy explain the *method* instead of the *result*; and when a smart founder's own sophistication is sabotaging conversion.

## Diagnosis
- Copy leads with technical detail, mechanism, or credentials rather than the outcome.
- Prospects seem confused or need things re-explained on the call.
- You're proud of the nuance in your messaging, but it isn't converting.
- The founder is highly educated and assumes buyers should meet them at their level.
- Ads describe what you do, not the before/after the customer wants.

## Why It Happens
Comprehension is cognitive load. When a reader hits an unfamiliar word, they stop absorbing and start decoding — and by the time they've decoded, the message has moved on, so they disengage. Experts default to precision and jargon because it signals competence to peers, but to a lay buyer it's friction. The buyer only cares about their outcome and their risk; simple outcome-and-risk language ("I make sad face into happy face, and it's covered") converts, while "talkie-talk" does not. An expert's greatest professional strength (technical depth) is therefore their greatest advertising weakness.

## Recommended Action
1. Run copy through a readability tool (e.g. Hemingway) and drive it below a fifth-grade — ideally third-grade — reading level.
2. Lead with the outcome the avatar wants and the risk removed, not the method: "sad face → happy face" plus any trust proof (e.g. "insurance covers it").
3. Delete every word the reader would have to pause on; if it needs decoding, cut or replace it.
4. Strip out the medical/technical/insider vocabulary and re-say it as the customer experiences the problem.
5. Prefer clear over clever in every asset — ads, pages, scripts — and test simplicity against sophistication.
6. Expect that competitors with big egos won't lower themselves to advertise plainly; that's precisely the opening.
7. Apply the **rule of three**: list every plausible benefit, group them by the outcomes they support, and retain the three distinctions the buyer most needs to know, remember, and care about.
8. Give each selected benefit a short, parallel phrase. If a fourth point is essential, reorganize the three categories instead of appending a memory burden.

## Example from the AMA
A Harvard-trained clinic owner offering a highly technical depression procedure (TMS) struggled to explain it in ads. Hormozi told him to "take how you advertise down 100 levels," citing his own habit of running every piece of copy through Hemingway to hit below third grade: "if someone has to pause when you say a word, you've lost them." The fix was to drop the medical language and speak to the avatar — "I tell you sad face, I can make you happy face, and insurance covers it" — noting that the expert's own brilliance is "everyone else's weak point" because rivals refuse to simplify. He tied it to a broader stance: "the more I've been doing this, the more I'm an advocate of clear over clever." A life-coaching creator was likewise told to keep the meat and "just change the wrapper" so content framed around the customer's experience.

Steve Jobs introduced the iPhone as three familiar things in one: a widescreen iPod, a phone, and an internet communications device. The structure made a novel product easier to encode because the audience had only three parallel ideas to retain.

## Caveats / Exceptions
- Clarity is not dumbing down the offer — it's removing decoding cost from the message.
- Simple outcome language still requires real proof and a genuine result behind it.
- Some technical audiences do want precision; match reading level to the actual buyer, not to peers.
- "Clear over clever" applies to marketing and sales copy; internal documentation may need more precision.
