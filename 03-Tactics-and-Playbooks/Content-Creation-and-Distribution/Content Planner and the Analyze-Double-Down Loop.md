# Content Planner and the Analyze-Double-Down Loop

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[03-Tactics-and-Playbooks/Tactics and Playbooks Index|Tactics and Playbooks]] → [[03-Tactics-and-Playbooks/Content-Creation-and-Distribution/Content Creation and Distribution Map|Content Creation and Distribution]]

## One-line summary
Judge content against an objective checklist rather than vague adjectives, plan a weekly mix of low- and high-intensity pieces, and when something works, repurpose and repost it rather than treating that as lazy.



## Context

This page organizes **Content Planner and the Analyze-Double-Down Loop** as a reusable playbook; the sections below preserve its original framework, steps, and examples.
## Good vs. bad content
| Good content | Bad content |
|---|---|
| Audience-first | Selfish |
| Clear and structured | Vague and unstructured |
| Delivers value | No value to viewer |
| Strong hook | Easy to scroll past |
| Takes viewer on a journey | Passive and forgettable |

Principle: "vulnerable" and "relatable" are useless instructions because they aren't actionable — break formats into objective, checkable features instead.

## Worked example: "Silent Film Storytelling" format
Telling your own story through B-roll, sound, and text. Four tangible, repeatable features:
1. **Open with the struggle.** Visual and text hook both highlight pain (breakup, burnout, financial hardship). Struggle is the universal human denominator; it earns retention. Opening with flexing does the opposite.
2. **Use a sound with rising action.** It insinuates a coming payoff and holds attention toward the beat drop / transformation.
3. **Add a timestamp.** A year, month, life period, or age signals a logical progression is coming, sustaining retention.
4. **Perfect the pacing.** Avoid cuts that are too fast/jarring or frames that linger too long. Test: read each frame's line aloud — you should just finish the sentence before the frame changes.

## The Content Planner
A simple three-row planner giving week-by-week clarity:
1. **Idea** — extrapolated from your Creator Vision (see [[Creator Vision Framework - What x Who x Why]]).
2. **Format** — drawn from studying proven formats.
3. **Intensity** — energy/time required to produce it. Low intensity: under ~1 hour. Medium/high intensity: above ~1-2 hours.

**Recommended weekly mix:** 1-2 medium/high-intensity pieces plus ~5 low-intensity pieces. This sustains output and energy without burnout.

## Analyse & double down
Review what works and what doesn't. When something works, double down: repurpose its format and messaging. Repurposing and even reposting your own content is legitimate and powerful, not lazy — it sustains success without starting from scratch. Claimed result: the same silent-film-storytelling format, repurposed and reposted across Instagram and TikTok, repeatedly drew millions of views.

This "double down on what works" instinct matches Ava Jurgens' observation that reshooting a viral video's hook and topic in a different format usually works again (see [[短影音研究引擎、鉤子與格式]]), and Grace's "consistency x experimentation" growth formula (see [[品牌即媒體公司：注意力到信任框架]]).

## Overall principle
Once the formula is understood — Creator Vision → studied formats → planner → doubling down — content becomes controllable and more predictable. The algorithm can't be guessed perfectly, but studying it raises your hit rate (e.g. from one strong video a week to three or four), growing audience and connection at scale.

## Application
- Before judging a draft as "good," run it against the five-row checklist (audience-first, clear/structured, delivers value, strong hook, takes viewer on a journey) instead of relying on gut feeling alone.
- Plan next week's content as a three-row table (idea/format/intensity) and check the mix lands around 1-2 high-intensity plus ~5 low-intensity pieces.
- When a piece of content clearly outperforms, immediately schedule at least one repurposed version in a different format before moving on to a new idea.
