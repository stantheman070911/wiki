# Hyperdopamine Ads - Pattern Interrupt, Curiosity, and Native Ad Anatomy

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[03-Tactics-and-Playbooks/Tactics and Playbooks Index|Tactics and Playbooks]] → [[03-Tactics-and-Playbooks/Content-Creation-and-Distribution/Content Creation and Distribution Map|Content Creation and Distribution]]

## One-line summary
An ad wins the newsfeed by looking like the content people already choose to consume — tabloid/breaking-news style — while combining a pattern-interrupt image, burning intrigue, and a targeted specific benefit into what Suby calls a "hyperdopamine ad."

## Context
This is Suby's creative-construction layer for paid social ads, built by studying Facebook's own most-viewed-content reports (LadBible, Unilad, E! News topped the list — all tabloid/news formats) and reverse-engineering why those formats out-pull conventional "branded" ad creative. It's the format/creative counterpart to [[Direct-Response Copywriting - NEWIF, the Nine Questions, and Three Conversion-Killing Mistakes]], which covers message logic rather than ad construction.

## Key insights
- **Ads are competing with Netflix, not with the shop down the street.** The real competitive set for attention on a feed is the platform's most dopamine-releasing content (celebrity gossip, sports, entertainment) — an ad that doesn't compete on that axis loses before the offer is even read.
- **Do not make your ad look like an ad.** Native ad-blindness is real; people actively avoid anything that visually signals "advertisement." Content styled like the platform's own organic content (a raw iPhone photo, a native social post format) consistently outperforms polished, obviously-branded creative.
- **Obsess over people, not algorithms.** Media-buying skill (targeting, account structure) is real but secondary — an average media buyer with a genuinely great, high-engagement ad will beat an excellent media buyer running mediocre creative; the algorithm rewards engagement regardless of buyer sophistication.
- **Two images beat one.** Pairing a primary image with a secondary image forces the brain to work slightly harder to reconcile them, which is itself a scroll-stopping mechanism — tabloid outlets use this pattern deliberately and consistently.
- **Positive benefit beats pain avoidance ~8 times out of 10.** "Lose 20–50kg with this one weird hack" tends to outpull "don't put on 50kg this winter" even in high-pain markets — worth testing both, but the default should be the positive-benefit framing.
- **"Learn More" is the safe default CTA.** Across split-testing, a low-commitment, non-threatening CTA outperformed harder asks like "Buy Now" or "Inquire Now" — the CTA's job is to lower friction to the next step, not to close the sale itself.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
### The hyperdopamine ad formula

sits at the intersection of two axes — pure clickbait (high intrigue, no real benefit → poor user experience, platform penalizes it) and a pure targeted benefit with no intrigue (accurate but ignorable). The combination of all three of the following is what the source calls a hyperdopamine ad:
1. **Pattern interrupt** — an image or opening frame odd/unexpected enough to stop the scroll (a weird prop, an intense stare, an unusual composition).
2. **Burning intrigue** — a headline or lead-in that creates an open question the reader must click to close ("This client getting hacked feels illegal — watch carefully, then click").
3. **Targeted specific benefit** — the intrigue is pointed at a named audience and outcome, not blind curiosity bait ("You won't believe what Ivanka Trump does" pulls a broad, low-relevance audience; "Supermodels use these 3 simple tricks to look younger" pulls people who specifically want that benefit).

### Anatomy of the ad, in order of what the reader hits first
1. **Scroll-stopping image** — the pattern interrupt.
2. **Eyeball-grabbing headline** — the intrigue, ideally paired with a specific benefit.
3. **Slippery lead-in copy** — the first line under the headline; changing only this line has produced 5x ROAS lifts in the source's testing.
4. **Intriguing link description** — a frequently-neglected secondary intrigue line.
5. **Body copy** — long-form is generally fine (up to ~2,200 characters, the Facebook/Instagram cross-placement limit at time of recording), but only if genuinely compelling; write first without regard to length, then edit down aggressively rather than padding to fill space.
6. **Non-threatening CTA** — "Learn More" as the tested default.

### Native ad styles (creative formats that read as organic content rather than ads)
- **Raw native** — an unpolished smartphone photo; performs well partly because it doesn't visually read as paid content.
- **SMS-style** — a mocked-up text message screenshot.
- **Breaking news** — tabloid/news-headline framing ("Shark Tank Investor drops [X] bombshell").
- **Native highlight** — a native photo with a secondary callout image and an arrow/circle annotation.
- **Native social post** — mimicking the exact visual format of a tabloid/news outlet's own social post.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Before producing an ad, pull the platform's own most-viewed-content report (or observe what consistently goes viral organically in your market) and reverse-engineer its visual and headline patterns.
- Score any draft ad against all three hyperdopamine criteria — pattern interrupt, burning intrigue, targeted benefit — and don't ship it if it's missing one.
- A/B test the lead-in copy line in isolation before touching image or headline; it's cheap to test and has produced outsized ROAS swings on its own.
- Default to "Learn More" as the CTA unless a harder ask has been specifically tested and won for your audience.
- Rotate ad styles rather than running only one — breaking-news and raw-native are the source's two highest-performing defaults, but over-relying on breaking-news style alone causes fatigue.
- Test a positive-benefit headline against a pain-avoidance headline for the same offer before committing to one angle.

## Notable examples
- Facebook's own transparency report ranking LadBible (#1, ~126M content views), Unilad, E! News, People, and TMZ as the platform's most-viewed pages — all tabloid/news formats, used as the evidence base for the native-content thesis.
- "This client getting hacked feels illegal" paired with an image of the narrator holding a USB drive and staring into camera — cited as a working example combining all three hyperdopamine elements.
- A billion-dollar teeth-whitening advertiser (HiSmile) running "She was skeptical. Wait until you see her results" — gossip-magazine phrasing from a large, sophisticated advertiser, offered as evidence the format isn't just a small-business hack.
