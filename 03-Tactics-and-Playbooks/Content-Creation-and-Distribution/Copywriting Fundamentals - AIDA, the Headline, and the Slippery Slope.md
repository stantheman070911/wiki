# Copywriting Fundamentals - AIDA, the Headline, and the Slippery Slope

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[03-Tactics-and-Playbooks/Tactics and Playbooks Index|Tactics and Playbooks]] → [[03-Tactics-and-Playbooks/Content-Creation-and-Distribution/Content Creation and Distribution Map|Content Creation and Distribution]]

## One-line summary
Copywriting is getting what's in your head into someone else's and influencing their behavior — and it runs on a few durable mechanics: AIDA, a headline that does 80% of the work, a curiosity gap that can't be left unresolved, and a "slippery slope" where each sentence's only job is to get the next one read.

## Context
For anyone writing ads, hooks, scripts, or sales pages who wants the load-bearing fundamentals rather than tips. From Sam Parr (founder of The Hustle; My First Million), drawing on the classic ad canon (Ogilvy, Hopkins, Sugarman) that still governs short-form scripts today.

## Key insights
- **Copywriting = influence, not decoration.** It's understanding what people want and finding the words that get them to act. The goal isn't 100% of people — it's getting the *right* person to read the whole thing, a large share of whom then buy.
- **The headline is ~80% of the job.** Ogilvy: five times as many people read the headline as the body; when you've written the headline you've spent 80 cents of your dollar. Most people write the piece then slap on a headline last — backwards. (YouTubers already know this: the hook/packaging outranks the video.)
- **Mind the gap (curiosity gap / open loops).** A great headline opens a tension the reader *must* resolve — "They laughed when I sat down at the piano… but then I started to play." In short form, stack an open loop roughly every ~8 seconds; retention data shows longer, loop-stacked scripts converting better.
- **Long converts — there's no "too long," only "too boring."** 27-minute video sales letters and 2,000-word pages convert precisely because they hold interest; "ugly" often outconverts pretty. The fix for long is *interesting*, not *shorter*.
- **Punch with the first sentence, then ride the slippery slope.** Sugarman: the first sentence's only job is to get the second read, the second's to get the third. The rule of consistency / foot-in-the-door keeps them sliding — a small first "yes" makes the next more likely.
- **Rhythm and simplicity carry attention.** Write at ~7th-grade level (check hemingwayapp.com; NYT is ~7th grade); one point per sentence (prefer a period over a comma); start sentences with "and/but" for rhythm; vary length short-medium-long-short. Buffett's letters average ~17 words/sentence and got *shorter* with mastery. "Write drunk, edit sober"; "cut a third," then cut again ("kill your darlings").

## Framework / model (if applicable)
**AIDA** — the spine of a persuasive message:
1. **Attention** — win it with the headline / first line (the curiosity gap, a credential, or something visually unexpected).
2. **Interest** — deepen with specifics and story.
3. **Desire** — make them want the outcome (often via a mechanism or reason-why).
4. **Action** — the ask.

**Copy work ("copy hour")** — the practice for *getting good*: by hand, transcribe writing regarded as great (classic ads, top short stories, SNL bits, Buffett letters, the Boron Letters) word-for-word, ~1 hour/day for 3-6 months. Like learning an instrument by copying songs, you absorb the rules and texture of the craft, then recombine them. (Doing focused copy work on 5 million-view scripts turned one creator's ceiling of ~150k views into a 2M-view video.)

## Tactics / how to apply
- Spend disproportionate time on the headline/first line; write many, pick the one that opens a gap or lands a punch.
- Read your draft and, wherever there's a comma, try a period; delete a third; then delete again.
- Run copy through Hemingway; aim ~4th-7th grade.
- Study retention graphs: where viewers drop, insert a fresh curiosity gap.
- Do copy hour by *hand* (physical writing aids retention) on the best work in the genre you want to master.

## Notable examples
- Ogilvy's Rolls-Royce headline ("At 60 mph the loudest noise… is the electric clock") — weeks spent on one line; sold many cars.
- Hopkins' "They laughed when I sat down at the piano…" — a curiosity-gap headline selling a music course; later inspiration for Upworthy-style clickbait that took a site 0→200M monthly visits.
- The Hustle's "My friends think I'm smart. I'm not." ad — ~$10M spent, singularly responsible for the exit.
