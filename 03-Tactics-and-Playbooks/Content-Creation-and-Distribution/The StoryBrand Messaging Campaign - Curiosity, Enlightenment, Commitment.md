# The StoryBrand Messaging Campaign - Curiosity, Enlightenment, Commitment

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[03-Tactics-and-Playbooks/Tactics and Playbooks Index|Tactics and Playbooks]] → [[03-Tactics-and-Playbooks/Content-Creation-and-Distribution/Content Creation and Distribution Map|Content Creation and Distribution]]

## One-line summary
Turn a clear brand message into a repeatable campaign that earns curiosity with a concise promise, builds trust through useful education, and requests commitment with an unmistakable offer.

## Context
Clarifying a message does nothing if it remains inside the company. A campaign installs the same few sound bites across the customer journey so people understand, remember, trust, and eventually act on the offer.

## Key insights
- **A controlling idea disciplines the campaign.** Every asset should reinforce one memorable point; novelty in wording is less valuable than consistent recall.
- **Repetition is the strategy.** Customers ignore most commercial messages and need multiple exposures before a message becomes familiar, so exact sound bites should recur across channels.
- **Different assets serve different relationship stages.** Short copy creates curiosity, longer material enables evaluation, and an offer with urgency asks for commitment.
- **The website is the canonical sales pitch.** Wireframing it forces the company to order its claims, evidence, plan, and calls to action before applying them elsewhere.
- **Owned contact channels prevent forgetting.** A valuable lead generator earns permission to use email or text; nurture content keeps the brand mentally available until the customer’s problem becomes active.
- **Transformation stories reduce perceived risk.** Specific testimonials let prospects “go second” by seeing a similar person move from problem to result.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
Build the campaign in seven linked pieces:

1. **BrandScript:** establish the shared customer story and controlling idea.
2. **One-liner:** state customer/problem, product as solution, and result in a repeatable answer to “What do you do?”
3. **Landing page:** sequence header, stakes, value, guide, plan, explanation, lead generator, and secondary information.
4. **Lead generator:** exchange high-value education, a tool, sample, trial, or event for contact permission.
5. **Nurture and sales sequence:** alternate useful problem-solving messages with direct offers.
6. **Transformation proof:** collect case studies using problem, frustration, difference, turning point, and after-state questions.
7. **Referral system:** give satisfied customers a useful asset, simple sharing mechanism, and, where appropriate, a reward.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Put the offer and direct call to action above the fold; repeat the same action at natural decision points down the page.
- Keep the one-liner to problem, solution, and result; edit it until listeners understand without a follow-up explanation.
- Use a nurture rhythm such as three useful emails followed by one sales email; every sales email should name the problem, solution, success, price or offer, and next action.
- Ask five testimonial questions that reconstruct the customer’s before state, frustration, product difference, moment of proof, and after state.
- Tag leads by the product or problem that attracted them so each follow-up campaign stays relevant.
- Review the campaign as one system: curiosity assets should lead to enlightenment assets, and every enlightenment asset should make commitment easy.

## Notable examples
StoryBrand’s “5 Things Your Website Should Include” guide generated tens of thousands of qualified contacts and helped take the business past $2 million. A design firm won a presentation project despite a less attractive website because it clearly named the customer’s fear, offered a useful guide, nurtured the relationship by email, and repeatedly made scheduling easy.
