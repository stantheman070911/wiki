# Declarative to Sell, Procedural to Deliver

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[03-Tactics-and-Playbooks/Tactics and Playbooks Index|Tactics and Playbooks]] → [[03-Tactics-and-Playbooks/Content-Creation-and-Distribution/Content Creation and Distribution Map|Content Creation and Distribution]]

## One-line summary
Use declarative knowledge—what is possible, why it matters, and how the system works—to create demand, then sell procedural knowledge and implementation that help the buyer do it.

## Context
Educational sellers often teach the entire procedure before making an offer. The audience leaves with a long task list and no urgency because the session delivered instructions before establishing the value of the outcome or the need for help.

## Key insights
- **Declarative knowledge explains the map.** It reveals an opportunity, mechanism, consequence, or new model.
- **Procedural knowledge teaches the steps.** It creates value only after the learner leaves and executes.
- **Education can sell without withholding insight.** Give away the conceptual model and evidence; charge for implementation, feedback, tools, and reduced error.
- **Show what before how.** A working result demonstrates possibility and proof more effectively than a long build tutorial.
- **Context-specific repetition is useful.** Audiences rarely generalize a principle across every industry without seeing themselves named or shown.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
Structure sales education in four layers:

1. **Outcome:** what changes and for whom.
2. **Mechanism:** why the result occurs and why the old approach fails.
3. **Evidence:** demonstrations, comparisons, or cases proving the mechanism.
4. **Implementation offer:** the steps, support, tooling, and feedback required to reproduce it.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Remove step-by-step setup from the sales portion and replace it with a demonstration of the finished capability.
- Teach the conceptual categories and let prospects identify which category fits them.
- Use multiple avatar-specific applications of the same mechanism rather than inventing unrelated lessons.
- Give away complete explanations when useful; monetize speed, execution, feedback, and accountability.
- In events, teach declarative material before the offer and reserve procedural depth for the product.

## Notable examples
For an AI education business, Hormozi recommends showing an automation working in different businesses instead of walking through every connection. For live events, he distinguishes explaining why personalized outreach improves results from teaching the entire technical setup; the first creates informed demand, while the second creates homework.
