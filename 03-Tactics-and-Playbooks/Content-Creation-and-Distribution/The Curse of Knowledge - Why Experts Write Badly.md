# The Curse of Knowledge - Why Experts Write Badly

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[03-Tactics-and-Playbooks/Tactics and Playbooks Index|Tactics and Playbooks]]

## One-line summary
Most bad writing isn't malice or stupidity — it's the curse of knowledge: the writer can't imagine what it's like not to already know what they know, so the fixes are to write for a reader who doesn't share your jargon, make abstractions concrete enough to *see*, pair every generalization with an example, and cut needless words.

## Context
Steven Pinker has studied language and cognition for a career and argues the single biggest flaw in writing and communication is the curse of knowledge — a term from economics for the difficulty of subtracting your own knowledge from your mind to imagine someone without it. It's why brilliant people write impenetrably; the same principle governs any content, pitch, or explanation meant to move a non-expert.

## Key insights
- **Bad writing is rarely deliberate.** It's not academics obfuscating to seem smart (Hanlon's razor: don't attribute to malice what stupidity explains — here, a *communication* stupidity, not a general one). The distinguished biologist who loses a TED audience in four seconds isn't arrogant; he just can't feel that not everyone knows what he knows. Jargon, unexplained acronyms, and abstractions are all symptoms of the same curse — also called egocentrism or the absence of a theory of mind.
- **You can't detect the curse in yourself.** By definition, what's obvious to you doesn't feel like it needs explaining — so introspection fails. The only reliable fix is to **show a draft to a real, flesh-and-blood reader** who is intelligent but outside your field, and see what doesn't land. (Even fellow academics one subfield over are often lost.)
- **Make the reader see.** Understanding isn't a string of words; it's often visual, sensory, and concrete. Great prose of earlier eras reads as vivid partly because writers *had* to reach for shared images (before centuries of abstractions and cliché existed). Say "bunny," not "stimulus"; nobody can picture a "paradigm" or a "framework." Use "for example," visual metaphors, and concrete instances.
- **Generalizations and examples are useless apart.** A generalization erases detail, so nine times out of ten you can't tell what it refers to without an example; an example without a generalization leaves the reader asking "and your point is?" Good writing is the dance between the two — examples supply context, generalizations supply compression.
- **Brevity is the soul of wit — and of clarity.** "Omit needless words" (Strunk & White) is an example of itself. Squeezing prose into a hard word limit "improves it as if by magic": every extra word is more cognitive processing for the reader, and constraint forces concrete language over woolly cliché. Read (or mumble) your prose aloud — if you can't say it smoothly, the reader can't sound it out smoothly either.
- **Informalization has raised the cost of "fancy."** Culture has shifted for a century toward spontaneity, intimacy, and authenticity; elevated, crafted prose now reads as pompous. That's a real constraint, but it's not a license for jargon — the target is *clear and concrete*, not *elaborate*.
- **AI prose is sound but generic.** LLM output is well-structured and plain (rarely academic-jargony), which makes it "good" in one sense — possibly because RLHF hammered it into shape, or because a composite of many voices is smoother than any one (like averaged faces looking more attractive). But it's benign, prosaic, and recognizable; it lacks the freshness and voice good writing needs. Clarity is its strength; style is not.

## Application
- Write for a specific non-expert reader; define or delete every acronym, abstraction, and piece of jargon they wouldn't already share.
- Don't trust your own judgment of clarity — give the draft to an intelligent reader outside your field and watch where they stumble.
- Convert abstractions to images: replace "stimulus/paradigm/framework" with the concrete thing; lead with "for example."
- Never leave a generalization without an example, or an example without the point it illustrates.
- Cut to a hard word limit and read aloud; if it doesn't roll, rewrite it shorter and more concrete.
