# Promise, Proof, Path - Engineering the Video Intro

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[03-Tactics-and-Playbooks/Tactics and Playbooks Index|Tactics and Playbooks]] → [[03-Tactics-and-Playbooks/Content-Creation-and-Distribution/Content Creation and Distribution Map|Content Creation and Distribution]]

## One-line summary
For educational content, the intro must land three things — a promise (what you'll help me do), proof (why I should believe you), and a path (how it will go) — because for educators the "why should I listen to you" is the hook, not a literal match to the thumbnail.

## Context
Hormozi derived this by watching the first three minutes of his top 35 YouTube videos to find what carried viewers through when other videos with similar average-view-duration didn't. The intro is where most reach and watch-time are won or lost.

## Key insights
- **Educators and entertainers hook differently.** Entertainers must literally confirm the thumbnail/headline fast — promise "crush a Lamborghini," show the Lamborghini, or viewers bail. Educators' "Lamborghini" is **proof**: the Vegas-lights answer to "why should I listen to *you*."
- **Two jobs: why-stay, then what-happens.** Promise + Proof answer "why should I stay?" (front-load in the first ~30 seconds to ~3 minutes). Path answers "what's going to happen?" and mainly drives watch-time by setting and then meeting expectations.
- **Answer "what's in it for me" and "why believe you" as fast as possible.** The winning intros stated the payoff and the credibility in one breath.
- **Quality is a power law.** Platforms weight quality heavily: spending 10× longer to make one video ~20% better on click-through / average-view-duration can yield ~100× the views, not 10×. Create quantity to *discover* quality, then invest disproportionately in the pieces that work — which is why bigger creators post less often.
- **Align your objective with the platform's.** Every algorithm optimizes for the same three things: people click, watch all the way through, and then watch something else of yours. Build to those and you never fight the algorithm. Hacks (hashtags, loopholes) die the moment the platform closes them, so don't build on them.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
**The three Ps of an intro:**
1. **Promise** — what you're going to help the viewer do (the payoff). *Why-stay.*
2. **Proof** — why they should believe you can (credibility, the "big Vegas lights"). *Why-stay.*
3. **Path** — the structure of what's coming; set expectations, then meet them. *Watch-time.*

Example intro that hits all three: "I sold my company for $46.2 million — a big part was a guarantee that beat all our competitors — and here are the four things we did to make it stronger." Promise (help you build a stronger guarantee), Proof ($46.2M exit), Path (four things).

## Tactics / how to apply
- Open every educational video by stating the promise and your proof in the first breath, before any preamble.
- Lay out the path explicitly ("here are the four things") so the viewer knows the structure and stays to see it completed.
- Stop copying entertainer hooks (literal thumbnail confirmation) if you educate — lead with credibility instead.
- Use volume to find your winning formats, then pour extra time into the few that perform; accept lower frequency for higher quality.
- Check any tactic against the platform's three objectives (click, watch-through, watch-next); if it doesn't serve them, drop it.

## Notable examples
- Hormozi's audit of his top 35 intros → the Promise-Proof-Path pattern (see [[The Framework for Making Frameworks - Distill by Commonality]]).
- MrBeast's rule of thumb: a video made ~20% better on the key metrics returns disproportionately (≈100×), not linearly.
