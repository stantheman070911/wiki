# We Need to Be Reminded, Not Taught - The Repetition Engine

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[03-Tactics-and-Playbooks/Tactics and Playbooks Index|Tactics and Playbooks]]

## One-line summary
You run out of things to say long before your audience is tired of hearing them — so stop hunting for novel ideas and instead re-tell your core ideas in new stories, formats, and contexts, because people value being *reminded* of what they already believe as much as being taught something new.

## Context
Every creator hits the wall of "I feel like I've said everything." Hormozi's reframe: the wall is an illusion created by the fact that you consume your own content thousands of times more than your audience does. The fix isn't new material — it's disciplined repetition of the same core ideas.

## Key insights
- **The news never changes, just the names.** There's always a war, a crash, a scandal — the *story* is ancient, only the names rotate. Content works the same way: the human condition hasn't changed in thousands of years, so "old" ideas re-told are still valuable.
- **Being reminded is as valuable as being taught.** People follow philosophy pages to re-read Epictetus; they follow you to keep your best ideas top of mind. If a reminder changes behavior, you *did* teach — you just did it through repetition. Reruns (Friends) get re-watched precisely because familiarity is the appeal.
- **You get sick of your content long before your audience notices it.** The Henry Ford anecdote: he asked his marketing director when they'd stop running "this ad I'm so sick of," and was told they hadn't started running it yet. You assume everyone sees everything you post; almost no one does. Put out high volume on the assumption that any given person catches maybe one piece a week.
- **New audience never saw the "old" idea.** A concept you've covered before is brand-new to everyone who arrived since — and a reminder to everyone who didn't.

## How to create variety without new ideas
Take one durable core idea (e.g. the value equation) and vary the surface:
- **New stories for the same concept** — tell it through a mother-and-daughter, then a military vet, then a small-business owner; each reaches a different avatar within your market, "just like the news." This is the "do epic stuff, then talk about what you did" engine — your ongoing life supplies fresh names for the same lesson.
- **New formats and mediums** — written, audio, a course module, a whiteboard, applied to services vs. products vs. software. The same idea in a new format is a new asset.
- **New contexts and applications** — apply the one idea to a different industry or problem each time.

The amount of novelty required to make something feel fresh is very small; layered on top of the ongoing "news of your life," you can restate the same concepts indefinitely.

## Application
- Stop treating "I've already said this" as a reason not to post; treat your best ideas as a rotation to be repeated in new wrappers.
- Build a small set of core ideas and deliberately cycle them through new stories, formats, and contexts.
- Assume near-zero overlap between what you've posted and what any single follower has seen; raise volume accordingly.
- Mine your own ongoing work for fresh "names" (new case studies, new events) to re-tell the same lessons.
