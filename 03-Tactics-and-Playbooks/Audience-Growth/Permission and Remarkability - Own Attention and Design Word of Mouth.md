# Permission and Remarkability - Own Attention and Design Word of Mouth

## One-line summary
Build an audience that explicitly wants to hear from you, then design an experience people choose to discuss because sharing it serves their identity or their peers.

## Context
Rented reach disappears when a platform changes its rules, and unsolicited interruption becomes more expensive as attention grows scarce. Permission creates a durable connection; remarkability turns a delivered experience into voluntary distribution.

## Key insights
- **Permission is anticipated, personal, and relevant.** A contact record is not permission unless the recipient expects the message and would notice its absence.
- **The promise defines the asset.** Permission is earned for a specific kind of interaction, not as blanket authorization for unrelated promotion.
- **Ownership reduces platform risk.** Email, subscriptions, and direct community relationships remain reachable without repeatedly paying a gatekeeper.
- **The audience decides what is remarkable.** Internal enthusiasm, effort, or novelty does not matter unless users find the experience worth remarking on.
- **Sharing is self-interested in a healthy way.** People tell others when doing so helps them connect, signal discernment, raise status, solve a peer's problem, or improve a network they use.
- **Word of mouth must follow value.** Publicity stunts can attract attention, but durable propagation begins with a promise that succeeds in use.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
Build the permission-to-propagation loop:

1. **Earn:** Offer enough relevant value for a person to request the next interaction.
2. **Specify:** State the channel, subject, and frequency they are agreeing to.
3. **Keep:** Deliver that promise consistently and make leaving simple.
4. **Delight:** Create a distinctive result or experience worth discussing.
5. **Enable:** Give successful users a clear story, visible artifact, invitation path, or network benefit.
6. **Compound:** Welcome referred peers into the same permission relationship instead of treating the share as a one-time traffic spike.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Write the exact permission contract in one sentence: what subscribers receive, how often, and why it is useful.
- Move important relationships from algorithmic feeds toward an opted-in channel the organization can reliably reach.
- Remove inactive or mismatched recipients rather than inflating an audience count that no longer represents trust.
- Interview enthusiastic customers with two questions: “What would you tell a friend?” and “Why would telling them benefit you or them?”
- Put the answer into the product through invitations, shareable results, collaborative use, visible artifacts, or a member-to-member benefit.
- Measure replies, retention, referrals, and successful invitees—not only list size or impressions.

## Notable examples
Early email service Yoyodyne treated permission as an asset customers granted for expected, relevant communication. Network products such as fax machines and social platforms add a second mechanism: users recruit others because every additional participant can make the product more useful to the existing network.
