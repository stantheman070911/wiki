# Hire Right, Because the Penalties for Hiring Wrong Are Huge

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[03-Tactics-and-Playbooks/Tactics and Playbooks Index|Tactics and Playbooks]]

## One-line summary
Most organizations hire badly by default — semi-random resumes, semi-random interviewers, gut feel — when hiring is actually a high-stakes process that can and should be made scientific: match the person to the role's real requirements, in the order values, then abilities, then skills, because that's also the order from hardest-to-change to easiest-to-change.

## Context
Eighth chapter of Work Principles, the first of the two "people" chapters that follow directly from establishing that the WHO matters more than the WHAT.

## Key insights
- **Match the person to the design, prioritizing values > abilities > skills.** Values are the hardest trait to change in someone (close to fixed), abilities are moderately changeable, and skills are the easiest — coding, a specific software tool, a sales script are all learnable; character is not. Hiring processes that screen heavily on skills and lightly on values are optimizing the wrong end of that list. Look for the fit to feel obvious — "hear the click" — and for people who "sparkle" relative to the role, not just "any ol' one of those" who technically qualifies.
- **People are wired very differently, so match wiring to the role deliberately** (see [[Understand That People Are Wired Very Differently - Baseball Cards and the Team Dimensions Profile]]). Use personality assessments rather than pure impression. Be aware that interviewers instinctively favor candidates who think like they do, so choose interviewers deliberately for the specific trait you actually want screened, not just by convenience. Look for people willing to self-assess objectively, and bet on demonstrated change rather than hoped-for change — people typically don't change as much as either they or their interviewer expects.
- **Treat teams like sports teams: excellence is required in every position**, and underperformers may need to be cut even if they're personally likable.
- **Pay real attention to track records.** Check references independently rather than trusting the candidate's own framing. School performance predicts memory, processing speed, and diligence — but not creativity, common sense, or vision, so don't over-weight it. Genuine experience counts alongside conceptual thinking (cites the "10,000 hours" idea as one input, not a complete theory). Watch specifically for "impractical idealists" — morally earnest people who don't actually understand how people behave in practice, which makes them dangerous in roles requiring realistic judgment about others. Both character and capability are required together; either one alone is either dangerous (capability without character) or useless (character without capability).
- **Hire people you actually want to share your life with**, not just people who fill the next open role. Great questions from a candidate predict fit better than great answers do. Show candidates your organization's real flaws upfront rather than only its strengths, and look specifically for people who are compatible with you *and* will challenge you — the goal is a team that can "play jazz" together, not one that just executes orders.
- **Compensation should provide stability and opportunity, not just security.** Pay for the person, not strictly the job title, and pay "north of fair." Focus on growing the total pie for everyone rather than optimizing the exact slice size in any single negotiation.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
**Values > Abilities > Skills**, screened in that order, because it's also the order of how hard each is to change after hiring. Skills gaps are the cheapest to close post-hire; values gaps are close to unfixable and should be caught before an offer goes out.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Before writing a job spec, separate the required values, abilities, and skills into three explicit lists, and weight the interview process toward values and abilities, treating skills as the most trainable and least disqualifying gap.
- Deliberately vary who conducts interviews based on the specific trait you want assessed that round, instead of defaulting to the same panel for every stage.
- Ask candidates what questions *they* have, and weight the quality of those questions as seriously as their answers to yours.
- Independently verify references rather than relying solely on a candidate-supplied list; ask specifically about demonstrated behavior change over time, not general impressions.
- When negotiating compensation, aim to be generously "north of fair" rather than minimally fair — Dalio notes this compounds: people he was generous with early, when he had little, stayed and later got rich alongside him.

## Notable examples
- Dalio explicitly warns against using personal pull to get someone a job — a values violation of the hiring process itself, regardless of the candidate's actual qualifications.
- Great people, in this framework, are treated as scarce and worth retention-planning for proactively, not just recruiting reactively when a role opens.
