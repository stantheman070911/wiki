# Building a Content Team - Hiring Funnel, Contractors, and Onboarding

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[03-Tactics-and-Playbooks/Tactics and Playbooks Index|Tactics and Playbooks]]

## One-line summary
Build a content team by hiring where you're the bottleneck, running every candidate through a real-work hiring funnel, choosing contractors vs full-timers deliberately, and onboarding on a 30/60/90 plan with consistent 1:1s.

## Context
The operational half of Caleb Ralston's brand workbook: the practical playbooks for turning a solo creator into a team. It complements the vault's hiring *philosophy* entries with concrete process.

## Key insights
- **Your team is your multiplier.** The right hires amplify everything you do; to scale, the team must move at your speed. Hire for culture and drive; train the technical skill (you can teach editing, not adaptability or thinking three steps ahead).
- **Hire specialists who can wear multiple hats early.** Early hires should be deep in one lane but willing to play multiple positions; niche them down later as you grow.
- **Hire where you're the bottleneck.** If everything slows after you film, your first hire is an editor; if growth is the constraint, hire a strategist before more creatives. Prioritize by platform and impact (a YouTube-driven business needs an editor who understands retention and pacing, not just someone who can cut clips).

## Framework / model (if applicable)
**The hiring funnel (no random resumes).**
1. **Define the role clearly** — creating, managing, or analyzing content? What does success look like at 3–6 months? If you can't describe the role in one sentence, it isn't clear enough.
2. **Filter:** resume (optionally a short video application on *why* they want the role) → **screening call** (align pay, workload, remote vs in-person) → **technical interview + skill test on real work** (the "nitty-gritty" questions and an actual task, no hypotheticals).
3. **Decide** against the defined success criteria.

**Full-time vs contractor/agency.** Choose by permanence, control, and load: full-timers for ongoing, core, culture-carrying work you want deep control over; contractors/agencies for specialized, variable, or spiky needs where speed matters more than long-term integration.

**30/60/90 onboarding.** Set explicit expectations by milestone: first 30 days = learn the systems and ship small wins; 60 = own a workflow with less oversight; 90 = operate independently and improve the process — so a new hire's trajectory is visible and correctable.

**Retention via effective 1:1s and experimentation.** Keep A-players engaged with consistent, structured 1:1s (their agenda, blockers, growth — not just status). Protect a **content hackathon**: dedicated time to experiment with new ideas/formats without the pressure of the normal content calendar, so the team keeps innovating.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Identify your current bottleneck and make the next hire there, prioritized by platform impact.
- Never hire off a resume alone; run the funnel and require a real skill test on real work.
- Decide FTE vs contractor by whether the need is core/ongoing (FTE) or specialized/variable (contractor).
- Give every new hire a written 30/60/90 plan with milestone expectations.
- Hold regular structured 1:1s and schedule experimentation sprints (hackathons) to retain and develop the team.

## Notable examples
- The workbook's rule of thumb: content slows after filming → hire an editor first; growth is the goal → hire a strategist before more creatives.
