# Performance Appraisal as a Management System

## One-line summary
A performance appraisal is a high-leverage intervention designed to improve future performance by judging real output and operating behavior, communicating a few actionable messages, and securing commitment to change.

## Context
Reviews fail when they become retrospective paperwork, vague praise, surprise criticism, or salary justification. The manager must make a subjective but evidence-based judgment, then transmit it clearly enough that the employee can act.

## Key insights
- **Improvement is the primary purpose.** Assessment and rewards matter, but the review earns its cost by raising capability or motivation after the conversation.
- **Judge performance, not potential or managerial appearance.** A leader's rating cannot be detached from the output of the organization they manage.
- **Balance output with internal measures.** Current results, future capability, people development, process health, and risk may carry different weights depending on the work.
- **Account for time offsets.** Today's output may reflect a prior year's management, while today's system-building may not generate visible output until later.
- **Use multiple observations, then synthesize.** Gather indicators, objective progress, one-on-one notes, incidents, and future-oriented work; reduce them to a few causal messages the person can absorb.
- **Three delivery disciplines matter: level, listen, and leave yourself out.** Be frank, confirm the intended thought was received, and keep the manager's guilt or need for agreement out of the employee's review.
- **Commitment is operationally sufficient.** The employee need not agree with every diagnosis, but must understand the decision and commit to the agreed action.
- **Stars deserve the most developmental attention.** Small gains from disproportionate contributors often create more organizational output than extensive work on marginal performance.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
**Assess–synthesize–deliver–follow:**

1. **Define expectations:** clarify the outputs, standards, and future capability the role should create.
2. **Collect evidence:** review objective results, internal measures, time offsets, behaviors, prior feedback, and context.
3. **Write the unedited inventory:** list every positive and negative observation before filtering.
4. **Synthesize messages:** group observations by causal pattern and keep only the few most important for future performance.
5. **Write actions:** specify what should continue, change, or develop, with evidence and a way to observe progress.
6. **Share in advance:** let the employee read and process the written appraisal before the discussion.
7. **Deliver with the three Ls:** level, listen until understood, and leave yourself out.
8. **Secure and monitor commitments:** record owners, dates, support, and checkpoints in subsequent one-on-ones.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Separate output measures from inside-the-system measures, and explicitly state the weight and time horizon used for each.
- Test every conclusion against at least two concrete observations; distinguish repeated behavior from a single unusual event.
- Remove messages the employee cannot act on and reduce the final list until it can be remembered without rereading.
- For serious underperformance, move through recognition, denial, blame, ownership, and solution in order; do not prescribe a plan while the person still rejects responsibility.
- For a high performer, use hindsight to identify the next performance frontier rather than treating praise as completion.
- Ask the employee for feedback on your management, but preserve the leader's responsibility to make the assessment rather than outsourcing it to a self-review.
- Make promotion and compensation visibly follow performance, since these choices teach the whole organization what it values.

## Notable examples
Grove once gave a manager a strong rating because current sales and profit were excellent despite warning signs in turnover and team health. A year later output collapsed; the earlier results had been delayed light from prior work. The lesson was to judge the manager's current contribution with both output and internal measures rather than merely record what was easiest to see.
