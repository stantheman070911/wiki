# CFRs - Continuous Conversations, Feedback, and Recognition

## One-line summary
Continuous performance management combines contributor-led conversations, specific multidirectional feedback, and visible recognition so goals remain human, current, and useful for growth.

## Context
Annual reviews compress twelve months of work into a backward-looking judgment and often entangle development, compensation, and ranking. CFRs create a lighter recurring system that catches obstacles early, makes coaching concrete, and strengthens the relationships required for ambitious goals.

## Key insights
- **Conversations replace inspection with coaching.** The contributor should do most of the talking and set the agenda; the manager's job is to learn, remove obstacles, and help improve judgment.
- **Feedback is networked and timely.** Useful input can move upward, downward, or sideways and should be specific enough to change a behavior or decision now.
- **Recognition is an operating signal.** Frequent, precise, peer-visible appreciation shows which actions and values the organization wants repeated.
- **Development and compensation need different conversations.** Goal attainment is one input to appraisal, not a mechanical bonus formula; separation protects honest ambition and reduces sandbagging.
- **Goals provide shared facts.** Public outcomes make coaching less vague because both people can discuss progress, confidence, dependencies, and evidence.
- **Culture must support candor.** Transparency without psychological safety creates compliance theater; leaders must model vulnerability and respond to bad news without reflexive punishment.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
Run the three-part CFR loop:

1. **Conversations:** Cover goal planning and reflection, progress and blockers, two-way coaching, career growth, and occasional lightweight performance summaries.
2. **Feedback:** Exchange concrete observations and requests continuously across the network, including upward feedback to managers.
3. **Recognition:** Reinforce valuable contribution frequently, specifically, visibly, and in connection with shared goals or values.

Short pulse surveys can complement the loop by detecting changes in clarity, energy, support, or morale between conversations.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Hold regular one-on-ones and ask what is working, what is blocked, what support is needed, and what the contributor wants to learn next.
- Ask for upward feedback directly: What am I doing that helps, what impedes you, and what could I change to make you more effective?
- Prepare coaching around observable start, stop, and continue behaviors rather than personality judgments.
- Separate the goal-setting discussion, development discussion, and compensation decision even if all inform the broader performance process.
- Make recognition peer-to-peer as well as managerial, and connect it to a concrete action rather than generic praise.
- Use anonymous pulses as a listening mechanism, then respond visibly so requesting feedback builds rather than drains trust.

## Notable examples
- Adobe replaced annual rankings with Check-in: clear expectations, regular feedback, and career-development conversations owned by leaders and supported by HR.
- Zume used one-on-ones to discuss personal growth and capacity, not merely task status, making its goal system a vehicle for manager development.
- Lumeris learned that OKRs failed as paperwork until leaders built trust, trained every manager, and practiced "brutal transparency without judgment."
