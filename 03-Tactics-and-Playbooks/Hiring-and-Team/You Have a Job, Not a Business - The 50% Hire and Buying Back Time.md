# You Have a Job, Not a Business - The 50% Hire and Buying Back Time

## One-line summary
Until you hire a team you don't own a business, you own a job — and the thing keeping most founders trapped is measuring every candidate against their own efficiency instead of hiring people who are "only" 50% as good and buying back their time.

## Context
The most common complaint among owners is "I can't find good people." Because they hold every hire to their own standard, they never delegate, so they work 80–120 hours a week for near-minimum effective wage — a job wearing a business's clothes. The unlock is a deliberate lowering of the hiring bar plus a plan for what to do with the reclaimed hours.

## Key insights
- **A job vs. a business is defined by whether it runs without you.** Doing all the selling *and* all the delivery yourself doesn't scale; it just raises your own hour count until it caps.
- **Your comparison stick is the trap.** Judging candidates against your own peak efficiency guarantees no one ever qualifies. It is okay to hire someone who does the role 50% as well as you.
- **Ten people at 50% beat one person at 100%.** Capacity and leverage come from many "good enough" operators, not one irreplaceable expert (yourself).
- **The point of delegation is bought-back time reinvested in growth.** The value isn't the task off your plate — it's the hours redeployed onto the next constraint (building the sales team, the process, the systems).

## Framework / model (if applicable)
**The buy-back loop:**
1. Identify the role eating your hours (for Suby: account management / client "farming").
2. Hire someone who can hit ~50% of your standard on it, accepting they won't go as deep as you.
3. Reclaim the hours that hire frees (Suby: ~100 hours/month from his first account manager).
4. Reinvest those hours into a growth lever, not more of the same work — hiring salespeople, building a unified sales process.
5. Repeat: each cycle funds the next hire and compounds capacity (one AM → two → ten).

## Tactics / how to apply
- Write down the tasks only you currently do; pick the biggest time-sink that a 50%-as-good hire could still move meaningfully, and hire for that first.
- Pre-commit where the reclaimed hours go *before* you hire, so bought-back time doesn't silently refill with low-value work.
- Resist the "no one can do this as well as me" story — it is the exact belief that keeps you scratching it out "with the turkeys."
- Stop splitting yourself across "sell by day, deliver by night"; that pattern (Suby ran it to 70 clients) is the ceiling, not the model.

## Notable examples
- Suby selling all day and doing client work every evening until he had 70 clients — unsustainable. His first account manager didn't need his depth, bought back ~100 hours/month, and those hours went into building a sales team and process — turning a one-man agency into a scalable business.
