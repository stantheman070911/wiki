# Remember That the WHO Is More Important Than the WHAT

## One-line summary
People systematically over-invest in figuring out *what* should be done while under-investing in *who* should decide and do it — but organizations don't make decisions, specific people with specific, knowable qualities do, so getting the WHO right is the higher-leverage move and should come first.

## Context
Seventh chapter of Work Principles, bridging the culture/decision-making chapters (1–6) into the people chapters (8–9) that follow: this is the pivot point where the book shifts from "how should we decide" to "who should we put in charge of deciding."

## Key insights
- **The single most important decision in any organization is choosing your Responsible Parties (RPs).** The highest-leverage RPs are the ones overseeing goals, outcomes, and entire "machines" at the highest levels — get those choices right and much of the rest follows.
- **The ultimate Responsible Party is whoever actually bears the consequences of the outcome.** Make sure everyone — including owners, who answer to investors or clients — has someone they report to; a true, final absence of accountability anywhere in the chain is a structural risk.
- **"Remember the force behind the thing."** Organizations don't make decisions — specific people with specific qualities, strengths, and blind spots do. Change the person, and the outcome changes, even if the process and title stay exactly the same. This means org charts and process diagrams are incomplete without an honest account of who specifically occupies each box and what they're actually like.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
**WHO before WHAT.** When diagnosing why something isn't working, or planning how to fix it, resist the instinct to jump straight to redesigning the process. First ask who is (or should be) responsible, and whether that person has the specific qualities the role requires — a good process run by the wrong person underperforms a mediocre process run by the right one.

## Tactics / how to apply
- When a plan or transition proposal is presented, explicitly check whether it names who will be responsible and what qualities that person needs — a plan that's silent on WHO is incomplete, no matter how detailed the WHAT is.
- Before redesigning a broken process, ask whether the actual root cause is the process or the person running it — these require different fixes.
- Make sure every role in your org chart, including the most senior ones, has a real answer to "who is this person accountable to, and for what."

## Notable examples
- An executive once presented Dalio an impressively systematized succession/transition plan with no answer for *who* would replace him or what specific qualities that successor would need — Dalio's standing illustration of the WHO/WHAT confusion: a beautifully specified WHAT with no credible WHO behind it.
