# Culture as Reinforcement Rules - Codify, Transfer, and Train Behavior

## One-line summary
Culture is the set of spoken and unspoken “if this, then that” rules governing which behaviors an organization rewards, ignores, or punishes.

## Context
Founders often try to replicate culture through slogans or proximity. That fails when a new office or leader must reproduce hundreds of tacit decisions without the founder present.

## Key insights
- **Culture is observable reinforcement, not atmosphere.** People learn what the organization values by watching what happens after behavior.
- **Values are compressed rule sets.** A useful value summarizes many concrete rules; it does not replace defining them.
- **Unwritten culture transfers slowly.** “Soak it up” means observing enough reinforcement events to infer the rules.
- **A culture carrier reduces replication risk.** Move a proven champion only when the original team can sustain the rules without that person.
- **Leaders need rehearsal and fast feedback.** Leadership conversations can be role-played just as sales conversations are.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
1. List the recurring situations where culture appears: meetings, customer failures, deadlines, disagreement, handoffs, hiring, and mistakes.
2. For each situation, write the conditional rule: if a person does X, then the organization does Y.
3. Mark whether Y is a reward, neutral response, correction, or removal.
4. Group related rules into a small set of values.
5. Select leaders who already embody the rules and can explain them.
6. Observe live decisions, give immediate feedback, and role-play difficult conversations before geographic expansion.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Replace adjectives such as “professional” or “accountable” with checkable behaviors.
- Audit what managers actually tolerate; tolerated behavior is an active cultural rule.
- Add examples and counterexamples beneath each stated value.
- Promote a candidate into supervised leadership at the original location before sending them elsewhere.
- Create short feedback cycles around leader decisions, not merely lagging financial results.
- Confirm the home team can reinforce itself before removing its strongest culture carrier.

## Notable examples
In the AMA, a real-estate brokerage opening a second market is advised to codify meeting and interaction rules, install its new leader under “training wheels” at the original office, and role-play leadership conversations before moving that leader to the new location.
