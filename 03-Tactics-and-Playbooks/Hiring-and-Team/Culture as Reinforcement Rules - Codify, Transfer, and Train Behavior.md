# Culture as Reinforcement Rules - Codify, Transfer, and Train Behavior

## One-line summary
Culture is the set of spoken and unspoken “if this, then that” rules governing which behaviors an organization rewards, ignores, or punishes.

## Context
Founders often try to replicate culture through slogans or proximity. That fails when a new office or leader must reproduce hundreds of tacit decisions without the founder present.

## Key insights
- **Culture is observable reinforcement, not atmosphere.** People learn what the organization values by watching what happens after behavior.
- **Values are compressed rule sets.** A useful value summarizes many concrete rules; it does not replace defining them.
- **Unwritten culture transfers slowly.** “Soak it up” means observing enough reinforcement events to infer the rules.
- **A culture carrier reduces replication risk.** Move a proven champion only when the original team can sustain the rules without that person.
- **Leaders need rehearsal and fast feedback.** Leadership conversations can be role-played just as sales conversations are.
- **The game precedes the desired behavior.** Culture cannot be reverse-engineered as a manipulative wrapper around tasks the owner wants completed; the purpose, ethic, rules, and way to win must be real enough that the work follows from them.
- **The leader must visibly play by the same rules.** A value that exempts the owner teaches the organization that power, not the stated principle, is the actual reinforcement rule.
- **People cannot be forced into commitment.** The practical job is to create an environment in which doing excellent work has meaning, clear standards, achievable victories, and an honest relationship to the organization’s purpose.
- **Hiring is the first culture lesson.** The recruiting presentation, individual discussion, selection decision, first-day story, facility tour, role agreement, manual, and training should communicate one coherent game before informal habits define it instead.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
1. State the game first: its purpose, ethical foundation, non-negotiable strategy, and concrete ways to win without ending the game.
2. List the recurring situations where culture appears: meetings, customer failures, deadlines, disagreement, handoffs, hiring, and mistakes.
3. For each situation, write the conditional rule: if a person does X, then the organization does Y.
4. Mark whether Y is a reward, neutral response, correction, or removal.
5. Group related rules into a small set of values.
6. Select leaders who already embody the rules, can explain them, and accept being governed by them.
7. Build the rules into recruiting, onboarding, manuals, meetings, metrics, and recognition rather than relying on speeches.
8. Observe live decisions, give immediate feedback, and role-play difficult conversations before geographic expansion.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Replace adjectives such as “professional” or “accountable” with checkable behaviors.
- Audit what managers actually tolerate; tolerated behavior is an active cultural rule.
- Add examples and counterexamples beneath each stated value.
- Promote a candidate into supervised leadership at the original location before sending them elsewhere.
- Create short feedback cycles around leader decisions, not merely lagging financial results.
- Confirm the home team can reinforce itself before removing its strongest culture carrier.
- Schedule regular reminders of the game and visible intermediate victories; a culture is not self-sustaining merely because it was once explained.
- Change tactics when the game becomes stale, but preserve the ethical strategy that made commitment meaningful in the first place.

## Notable examples
In the AMA, a real-estate brokerage opening a second market is advised to codify meeting and interaction rules, install its new leader under “training wheels” at the original office, and role-play leadership conversations before moving that leader to the new location.
