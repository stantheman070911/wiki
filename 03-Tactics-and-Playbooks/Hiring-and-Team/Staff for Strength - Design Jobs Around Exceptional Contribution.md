# Staff for Strength - Design Jobs Around Exceptional Contribution

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[03-Tactics-and-Playbooks/Tactics and Playbooks Index|Tactics and Playbooks]]

## One-line summary
Build roles and teams to turn one relevant strength into exceptional performance; do not select for the absence of weaknesses or demand contradictory temperaments from a supposedly well-rounded person.

## Context
Organizations exist to combine specific strengths while making weaknesses irrelevant. Hiring for the “least objectionable” candidate produces mediocrity, while redesigning every role around a personality creates favoritism and organizational instability.

## Key insights
- **Ask “Good at what?”** Strength is task-specific. The relevant question is whether one exceptional capacity can materially change the result of this job.
- **Strong people carry strong weaknesses.** Peaks imply valleys; tolerating irrelevant flaws is often the price of exceptional contribution. Integrity is the exception because its absence corrupts every strength placed under it.
- **Repeated failure may indict the job, not the people.** If two or three previously effective people fail in succession, assume the role is structurally impossible and redesign it.
- **Jobs should be objective, large, and demanding.** Objective design protects fairness and diversity; sufficient scope lets a real strength reveal itself and continue producing as circumstances change.
- **Evaluate demonstrated performance, not vague potential.** Start with expected contributions and evidence of what the person has done well, then ask what they can probably do well next and what learning would release more of that strength.
- **Staff opportunities with the best people.** Keeping an “indispensable” performer trapped in an old role usually protects a weak superior, conceals a design problem, or wastes the performer’s growth.
- **Manage upward through strength.** Present the right substance in the form a superior can use—written or spoken, early or fully developed—rather than trying to reform their working style.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
**Strength-based staffing review:**

1. **Validate the job:** Has the role defeated multiple capable people or combined incompatible demands? If so, redesign before recruiting.
2. **Define the contribution:** State the few results that make the role matter.
3. **Find the decisive strength:** Identify the one capacity that would create excellence on those results.
4. **Check the weakness:** Is it irrelevant, guardrailable, or disqualifying because it limits the strength or compromises integrity?
5. **Make the job demanding:** Give enough scope for performance to be observable and consequential.
6. **Review evidence:** What has the person done well, what can they likely do well next, and what must they learn?
7. **Correct placement errors:** Remove sustained nonperformance from critical roles, while treating the failed appointment as a placement decision that may not define the person’s value elsewhere.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Replace deficit-heavy appraisal forms with contribution records and four questions: What did this person do well? What can they therefore do well? What must they learn to use the strength fully? Would I trust my child to work for this person—and why?
- When a role produces serial failure, stop searching for a unicorn and split, narrow, or reconfigure the responsibilities.
- Before rejecting a candidate for a weakness, state exactly how that weakness prevents the required result; if it does not, treat it as noise.
- Put proven performers on new, high-opportunity work; use external hires to expand established work when possible rather than combining a new task with an untested insider context.
- Learn whether your boss is primarily a reader or listener and deliver the same recommendation in the form that activates their strength.
- Review your own work for repeated patterns of ease and results, then structure responsibilities around those patterns instead of imitating someone else’s style.

## Notable examples
- Lincoln’s weakless but mediocre generals lost repeatedly; Grant’s relevant strength was winning campaigns, so Lincoln accepted his visible flaws.
- General Marshall selected and developed World War II commanders by asking what each could do, moved failures out of the wrong command, and then searched for a role in which their strength fit.
- Drucker’s rule for impossible jobs is deliberately structural: after two or three proven performers fail, redesign the job rather than blame the next person.
