# Prototype Before You Pivot - Life Design Interviews and Experiences

## One-line summary
Before committing to a new life direction, build a low-cost experience that answers one important question with direct evidence.

## Context
Thinking about an unfamiliar future cannot reveal how its work, people, or rhythm will actually feel. A life-design prototype converts assumptions into encounters, creates momentum, and makes failure cheap enough to be useful.

## Key insights
- **A prototype asks a question; it does not validate a finished plan.** The goal is learning, not proving that an attractive idea is correct.
- **Low resolution preserves flexibility.** Small tests isolate uncertainty without the financial and identity costs of a full pivot.
- **Experience produces different data from description.** Shadowing, doing a short project, or joining a real setting exposes physical, social, and emotional signals that research misses.
- **Conversations are story-seeking, not job-seeking.** A Life Design Interview asks how someone came to live a possible future and what that life is actually like.
- **Prototypes create relationships and serendipity.** People encountered while learning can co-create opportunities that did not exist in the original plan.
- **Several prototypes beat one elaborate test.** Parallel evidence prevents a single convenient experience from becoming a verdict.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
Use the **Question–Prototype–Evidence** loop:

1. Name a consequential uncertainty in one sentence.
2. Identify the smallest conversation or experience that could expose it.
3. Run the prototype quickly and without requiring it to “succeed.”
4. Record what surprised you, what changed, and what new question appeared.
5. Iterate, combine, or abandon the direction based on evidence.

A conversation prototype gathers a person's story; an experience prototype may involve shadowing, a class, a volunteer shift, a short contract, or a temporary project.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Ask for twenty minutes to hear someone's story about work you are exploring; do not disguise a request for employment as research.
- Prepare questions about daily activities, entry path, trade-offs, energy, and surprises rather than generic career advice.
- For an experience, isolate one assumption: customer contact, schedule, core craft, environment, or team dynamics.
- Set a small time and money budget before the test so sunk costs cannot redefine the question.
- Debrief immediately using engagement, energy, and coherency—not whether the prototype produced an offer.

## Notable examples
Elise opened a deli because she loved imagining and designing one, then discovered she disliked operating it. A few shifts in someone else's deli could have separated enthusiasm for creation from the daily reality of the business. Clara's sequence of a mediation class, part-time justice work, and mission-driven roles shows the opposite: each prototype narrowed the next question without demanding an all-at-once leap.
