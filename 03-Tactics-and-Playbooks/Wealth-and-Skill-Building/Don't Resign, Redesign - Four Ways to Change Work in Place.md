# Don't Resign, Redesign - Four Ways to Change Work in Place

## One-line summary
Before absorbing the risk of a new employer, test whether the existing job can be reframed, remodeled, relocated, or reinvented around a better fit.

## Context
An established workplace contains knowledge, relationships, credibility, and hidden opportunities that disappear when someone quits. A structured redesign tests those assets first while still preserving the option to leave an unsafe or irreparable situation.

## Key insights
- **There are bad fits, not universally bad jobs.** Work quality depends on the match among role, person, context, and current life needs.
- **Internal moves carry asymmetric advantages.** The organization knows your performance; you know its culture and informal network.
- **Redesign begins after honest performance.** A weak record cannot be solved by transferring the problem to another team.
- **A reframe changes the reason for working.** When circumstances cannot change immediately, a new “why” can restore integrity and reduce daily reconsideration.
- **A remodel changes task composition.** Cosmetic changes add more of an energizing activity; structural changes redistribute responsibilities across roles.
- **Relocation and reinvention differ by distance.** Relocation uses transferable capabilities; reinvention requires significant retraining and a new professional story.
- **Iteration can reveal that the original role fit better.** Returning with better self-knowledge is a successful prototype, not a failed move.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
Choose the lightest viable redesign:

1. **Reframe and reenlist:** accept the new context, identify a credible why, and recommit for a defined period.
2. **Remodel:** increase tasks using strengths and reduce or reassign draining work while improving an organizational outcome.
3. **Relocate:** move laterally into an accessible internal role using existing credibility and transferable skills.
4. **Reinvent:** retrain for a substantially different internal career, gaining the expertise, network, pivot, or credential required.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Exclude toxic, unsafe, discriminatory, abusive, or illegal workplaces; plan an exit rather than a redesign.
- Write a 250-word story of each plausible redesign and test it with trusted people.
- Begin a cosmetic remodel with activities already happening informally and demonstrate demand before requesting formal time.
- Pitch structural changes in terms of the manager's outcomes, with a limited trial and measurable result.
- Use internal interviews to learn daily work and requirements before asking for a role.
- Treat graduate school as a purchase of specific expertise, network, identity pivot, or badge—not a generic escape.

## Notable examples
Sarah loved technical leadership but was drained by budgets and schedules. She partnered with Production Engineering, framed the shift around faster, better code and fewer handoff problems, and won a three-month trial that permanently reassigned administrative work while preserving her senior role.
