# The Entrepreneur Apprenticeship - Commercial Awareness and 90-Day Side-Hustle Tests

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[03-Tactics-and-Playbooks/Tactics and Playbooks Index|Tactics and Playbooks]]

## One-line summary
Before betting everything on a startup, learn beside an experienced entrepreneur and run bounded ninety-day side hustles that build commercial judgment, self-knowledge, resource access, and evidence of execution.

## Context
“That would not work for me” often means the person lacks exposure to how customers are won, value is delivered, and resources are assembled. An apprenticeship and finite experiments close that gap with less risk than an immediate full-time launch.

## Key insights
- **Commercial awareness is learned by proximity.** Watching real sales, service, hiring, cash constraints, and trade-offs builds pattern recognition that abstract education rarely supplies.
- **Self-awareness must be operational.** The useful questions are which work creates energy, where results are consistently strong, and which weaknesses need a partner or system.
- **Resource access is know-how plus relationships.** Knowing whom to call for capital, talent, advertising, technology, or advice reduces the apparent risk of future ventures.
- **Side hustles should have an end date.** A ninety-day open-and-shut campaign prevents a small experiment from becoming an indefinite, underpaid identity.
- **The early return is evidence, not income.** Stories of finding a problem, making an offer, handling rejection, and delivering a result create the confidence required for larger bets.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
Use a two-stage preparation cycle:

1. **Apprenticeship (roughly 6–24 months):** work closely with an experienced entrepreneur until you can explain how the business acquires customers, fulfills promises, makes money, and accesses resources—and where you personally add value.
2. **Ninety-day side-hustle test:** choose one problem, one customer, one offer, and one sales motion; define the closing date in advance; run the campaign; document the result and learning; then deliberately close, repeat, or graduate it.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Choose apprenticeship roles with direct exposure to revenue and customers, not only a famous employer.
- Ask to observe sales reviews, customer failures, hiring decisions, and financial trade-offs.
- Design each side hustle around a single paid outcome that can be completed within ninety days.
- Keep tools manual and inexpensive so the test teaches the workflow instead of hiding it behind automation.
- End with a written debrief: customer evidence, economics, personal strengths, missing resources, and the next hypothesis.
- Run several bounded tests before interpreting one failure as a verdict on entrepreneurship.

## Notable examples
Priestley describes early event promotions and short sales-training projects as preparation rather than forever businesses. The projects created commercial repetitions and ended before they could become comfortable traps.
