# Positioning Yourself - Find a Horse to Ride

## One-line summary
Apply positioning to your own career: define and own a specialist position in others' minds, and accept that success comes more from what others can do for you — so find a "horse to ride" rather than trying to do everything yourself.

## Context
The book's most personal chapter turns the doctrine inward. The same rules that position a product position a person: own a clear position, don't chase the everybody trap, and leverage outside forces instead of grinding alone.

## Key insights
- **Be a specialist, not a jack-of-all-trades.** The "everybody trap" fails for people too — try to be all things to all people and you end up nothing. The job market belongs to those who define and position themselves as specialists.
- **Success is mostly what others do for you.** "It may be difficult for the ego to accept, but success in life is based more on what others can do for you than on what you can do for yourself." Reframe: ask what your company can do for *you*.
- **Yourself is the worst horse to ride.** Betting solely on your own effort ("an animal that is mean, difficult, and unpredictable") rarely wins; the jockey who wins usually has the best horse.
- **Positioning restricts creativity — and should.** For a person, this means your presentation (how you dress, speak, and show up) must *match* your position; creative self-expression that undermines the position is worthless.
- **Use an outsider for objectivity.** The outsider supplies "ignorance" — objectivity about how you're perceived from the outside, which insiders can't see.
- **A skill has no fixed market price.** Compensation depends on the economic value the buyer expects it to create, how rare the skill appears in that context, and what alternatives are available—not merely how difficult the skill is.
- **Changing context can outperform adding competence.** The same writer, analyst, designer, or salesperson may earn multiples more in an industry where the skill is scarce and applied to higher-value outcomes.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
**Six horses to ride** (external forces to attach your career to):
1. **Your company** — is it going anywhere? A rising company lifts you.
2. **Your boss** — ask the same of your boss; a boss going places takes you along.
3. **A friend** — cultivate *business* friends, not just personal ones; trade goodwill and useful items (e.g. send people press mentions they'd have missed).
4. **An idea** — "nothing can stop an idea whose time has come"; a real idea contains conflict, not motherhood-and-apple-pie.
5. **Faith** — faith in others and their ideas; get outside yourself to find your fortune.
6. **Yourself** — the last and hardest horse; ride the others first.

**The six positioning questions** (to position a business or a self):
1. **What position do you own?** (Ask the market, not your ego.)
2. **What position do you want to own?** (Long-term; must be preemptable — narrow like Nyquil's "nighttime," not the everybody trap.)
3. **Whom must you outgun?** (Avoid head-to-head with a leader; reposition a competitor if needed.)
4. **Do you have enough money?** (It takes money to build and hold a position; concentrate resources — overspend in one market rather than underspend in many.)
5. **Can you stick it out?** (Positioning is cumulative; rarely change the basic strategy, only the tactics.)
6. **Do you match your position?** (Do your actions and presentation embody it?)

**Skill-context screen:** compare markets on (1) value at stake, (2) scarcity of your skill, (3) buyer ability to pay, (4) credibility of your proof, and (5) adjacency—the amount of domain learning required before your skill transfers safely. Repositioning works best when the underlying ability stays strong but its economic leverage increases.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Write down the position you currently own in colleagues' minds, then the specialist position you want to own long-term.
- Pick your horse(s) deliberately — align to a rising company, boss, friend, or idea rather than pure solo effort.
- Narrow your expertise to one ownable specialty; kill the jack-of-all-trades impulse.
- Make your visible signals (dress, message, output) match the position; drop anything that contradicts it.
- Commit for the long term — improve the tactics, but almost never change the basic position.
- List three adjacent industries where your strongest skill is rarer or affects a larger economic outcome; interview practitioners before assuming the skill transfers unchanged.
- Reframe your portfolio around the value created in the target context, not the hours or technical difficulty involved.

## Notable examples
- Nyquil's "nighttime cold remedy" as the model of a narrow, ownable position applied to career specialization.
- The concentrate-your-fire rule: become #1 scotch in New York first, then roll out nationally.
