# Teach to Learn - Public Obligation and the Feynman Loop

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[03-Tactics-and-Playbooks/Tactics and Playbooks Index|Tactics and Playbooks]]

## One-line summary
Turn learning into a public teaching obligation: the need to explain simply, publish consistently, and respond to feedback converts passive knowledge into usable skill.

## Context
Reading can create familiarity without understanding. Teaching raises the standard: an idea has to be reduced to its essence, expressed clearly enough for someone else to use, and exposed to feedback that reveals gaps.

## Key insights
- **An obligation is stronger than an interest.** A public cadence creates something to lose—attention, trust, or reputation—so loss aversion supports consistency when motivation fades.
- **Simplification is both a learning method and a test.** Excess jargon and length often conceal weak understanding. If an idea cannot be explained in plain language, return to the material.
- **Publishing turns practice into a feedback loop.** Audience questions, confusion, and restatements show which parts transferred and which remained unclear.
- **The medium trains a second skill.** Writing develops precision, video develops articulation, and live teaching develops audience awareness; teaching produces knowledge and delivery skill together.
- **Consistency compounds into reputation and audience.** A daily idea may begin as a private learning practice, but a reliable body of useful explanations becomes a public asset.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
Use the **Learn–Simplify–Share–Review loop**:

1. **Learn:** investigate the topic from several angles until you can distinguish the core mechanism from examples and terminology.
2. **Simplify:** explain it as if to an intelligent child, using fewer and more concrete words.
3. **Share:** publish or teach in a setting where real people can respond.
4. **Review:** ask whether the audience understood well enough to explain or apply it; if not, identify the gap and restart the loop.

The obligation should specify a cadence, medium, and consequence. “I want to write more” is an interest; “I will publish one useful explanation every weekday” is an operating rule.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Choose one domain and commit to teaching one small idea at a fixed cadence for 30 days.
- Draft the explanation without source language, then compare it with the source to find distortion or missing logic.
- Remove every term that requires a second explanation unless it is essential to the model.
- End each lesson with a concrete test, decision, or action so understanding can be observed.
- Track recurring audience questions; they are a syllabus for what to research and clarify next.
- Raise the stakes with a public series, study group, workshop, or accountability partner rather than relying on private intention.

## Notable examples
Bartlett committed to learning and publishing one idea each day. The constraint of a short social post forced compression, while audience response refined both his thinking and delivery; the practice eventually produced a large audience around the learning habit itself.
