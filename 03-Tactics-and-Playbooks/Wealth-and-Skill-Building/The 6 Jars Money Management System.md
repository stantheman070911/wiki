# The 6 Jars Money Management System

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[03-Tactics-and-Playbooks/Tactics and Playbooks Index|Tactics and Playbooks]]

## One-line summary
Split every dollar of after-tax income across six purpose-labeled accounts (Financial Freedom 10%, Long-Term Savings 10%, Education 10%, Play 10%, Give 10%, Necessities 50%) the moment it arrives, so saving/investing and guilt-free spending both happen automatically instead of competing for the same undifferentiated pool.

## Context
This is the concrete allocation system Eker teaches inside Wealth File #14 ("Rich people manage their money well"). The mindset behind it — that the habit of managing money matters more than the amount available to manage — is covered separately in [[Manage Your Money Well - The Habit Matters More Than the Amount]]; this entry is the actionable mechanics.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
**Six jars/accounts, allocated as a fixed percentage of every dollar received (after tax):**
1. **Financial Freedom Account (FFA) — 10%.** Only for investments or passive-income-producing assets. Rule: this money is *never spent, only invested* — even in retirement, you spend the income the fund produces (the "eggs"), never the principal (the "golden goose").
2. **Long-Term Savings for Spending — 10%.** For large planned future purchases (a car, a home down payment, a big trip) — distinct from the FFA, which is never meant to be liquidated.
3. **Education Account — 10%.** For books, courses, coaching, seminars — funding continuous learning as a standing budget line rather than an occasional expense.
4. **Play Account — 10%.** Must be spent in full every month on something that makes you "feel rich" — a deliberate offset to the saving/investing discipline so it doesn't feel purely restrictive, and a way to practice the "receiving" muscle.
5. **Give Account — 10%.** Charitable or gift-giving allocation.
6. **Necessities Account — 50%.** Rent/mortgage, food, bills, and other essential living costs.

**Two supplementary habits:**
- Keep a physical "Financial Freedom jar" at home in addition to the bank account, and deposit into it daily (any amount — even loose change) to keep daily attention on the goal.
- Start immediately regardless of income level — the amount managed matters far less than establishing the habit and the percentages.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Open a dedicated Financial Freedom Account separate from checking/spending accounts, specifically so the "never spend, only invest" rule is structurally enforced rather than relying on willpower.
- If income is too small or irregular to open six formal accounts, use envelopes or a simple spreadsheet with the same percentage splits — the structure matters more than the banking mechanics.
- If you can't yet fund 10% into every category, start at whatever fraction is available (even splitting $1) and hold the *proportions* constant rather than skipping categories — the habit of full allocation across all six is the point, not the absolute amount.
- Treat the Play jar's "must be spent every month" rule as load-bearing, not optional — its purpose is specifically to prevent the saving discipline from triggering a later spending relapse.

## Notable examples
- A seminar participant near bankruptcy started with $1/month total (10¢ into FFA) and committed to doubling the monthly amount; by month twelve she was allocating $2,048/month and, within two years, was able to route a $10,000 bonus entirely into savings without needing any of it for expenses.
- A participant facing a $400 impulse coat purchase caught herself mid-decision with "you'd be much better off putting that toward your FFA account" — evidence the system's value is as much behavioral (a reflex, once installed) as arithmetic.
