# Skill Stacking and the Education-ROI Playbook

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[03-Tactics-and-Playbooks/Tactics and Playbooks Index|Tactics and Playbooks]]

## One-line summary
You are the highest-returning asset you can invest in; buy skills with time and money to pay down "ignorance debt," because education's value equals the gap between where you are and where you want to be.

## Context
Hormozi's core allocation rule for the early years: put savings into yourself, not markets. Skills compound and stack, can't be confiscated, and are the only true way to buy the future at a discount.

## Key insights
- **Invest in the "SME 500," not the S&P 500.** A 10% market return can't compete with tripling your earning capacity. Spend savings on skills until you literally can't spend it fast enough because you're earning too much.
- **Ignorance debt.** The most expensive thing you pay for is the information you don't know but should. The "$950k" illustration: if you make $50k and could make $1M, you pay reality $950k/year for not knowing how — so that education is worth ~$950k.
- **Buy the future at a discount.** Paying four people to compress five years of mistakes into one lets you live years ahead while still young. Hormozi spent $10k on a gym-owner mastermind *before owning a gym* to skip others' mistakes.
- **Skills stack disproportionately.** Sales + finance → raise capital; code + marketing → build and sell software. Knowing one skill may yield a tenth of knowing two. The unique thing is the *stack*, which becomes a moat no one can copy without living your life.
- **You are the permanent hold.** You can't sell yourself and you can't lose your education — which is why entrepreneurs go from zero to a billion and back; the skills survive.
- **Passive income comes last.** Learn to make money awake before making it while you sleep; you don't yet have enough capital for passive income to matter.
- **Build career capital in dependency order.** Knowledge becomes skill through application; useful skill attracts a network, the network expands access to resources, and repeated delivery creates reputation. Optimizing early for money or title can skip the two foundations that survive layoffs, technological change, and business failure.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
**Declarative vs. procedural knowledge.** Declarative = knowing *about* something exists (unknown unknowns; the big breakthroughs from high-level rooms). Procedural = knowing *how to* do it (predictable, from doing). Know which you're buying, and remember you only learn "how" by doing — 100 cold calls teach more than 100 videos.

**Document → Demonstrate → Duplicate** (how Hormozi learned national ads from a $750/hr expert in 8 sessions): have the expert document the process as if they were you, demonstrate it click-by-click while you build a checklist, then duplicate it yourself in front of them until you no longer need correction.

**Rule of 100 (daily):** reach out to 100 people, make a post you spent 100 minutes on, spend $100+ on ads, and spend 100+ minutes making/improving ads — consistently, while getting better.

**The five-bucket sequence:** knowledge → applied knowledge (skills) → network → resources → reputation. When comparing early-career opportunities, score them first by how much durable knowledge and skill they add; a higher salary can be lower total compensation if it stops those buckets from filling.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Set an education/investment budget; each month route the excess (after brutal frugality) into skills, rooms, and relationships.
- When you pay for help via favors, pay in full — do the whole job as if they were a paying client, and collect IOUs you can later cash for skills (barter is the most tax-efficient trade).
- Buy access to rooms with people making 10–100× more for declarative knowledge (unknown unknowns); use "how-to" purchases for specific skills.
- Be the vendor's #1 success story: do everything they say to the letter first, then keep what works and discard the rest ("take in only that which makes you stronger").
- Remember 20 hours gets you competent at almost anything; people just delay the first 20 hours by years — set a 20-hour timer before declaring something hard.

## Notable examples
- Hormozi wrote a $350,000 check for a single dinner with a person worth $7B and "got exactly what I wanted."
- He learned Facebook ads existed (declarative) in 2013 and how to run them (procedural) in one weekend, riding that skill for years.
