# The Algorithm - Musk's Five-Step Process Method

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[03-Tactics-and-Playbooks/Tactics and Playbooks Index|Tactics and Playbooks]] → [[03-Tactics-and-Playbooks/Operations-and-Productivity/Operations and Productivity Map|Operations and Productivity]]

## One-line summary
Improve any product or process in a strict order — question the requirements, delete, simplify, accelerate, automate — because doing them out of order (especially automating first) means you optimize and automate things that should never have existed.

## Context
Musk made every team at his companies "rigorously implement" this five-step method and repeated it "to an annoying degree." Its power is entirely in the *order*: he confesses to running it backward many times, automating and accelerating parts he later deleted.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
**The Algorithm (order matters):**

1. **Make your requirements less dumb.** Start here or you'll get "the perfect answer to the wrong question." Assume your requirements are dumb — *especially* those from smart people, because you question them less. Every requirement must come from a **named person**, not a department, and that person owns it — otherwise you're obeying a spec an intern set off-hand two years ago or someone who's since left.
2. **Try very hard to delete the part or process.** People forget to try removing something entirely. Rule of thumb: **"If you're not adding deleted things back in 10% of the time, you're not deleting enough."** Deleting too little is the more common, more expensive error. Corollary: **"The best part is no part; the best process is no process."**
3. **Simplify or optimize** — but *only now*. The big mistake is optimizing something that shouldn't exist; delete first, then optimize what survives.
4. **Accelerate cycle time.** Speed up the process — but only after steps 1–3, or you're accelerating waste.
5. **Automate** — last. Automating early bakes in the not-yet-deleted, not-yet-simplified process (Musk had to tear out over-early automation at Tesla).

**The failure mode (backward order):** the fiberglass mats on Tesla's battery pack were automated (a $2M robot), accelerated, and optimized (glue, drying speed) while choking the whole line — until someone asked "what's the mat *for*?" (claimed fire safety), a test showed no difference with or without, and the part was simply deleted, removing the entire robotic step.

## Key insights
- **Question before you optimize.** The costliest work is perfecting something that should not exist.
- **Requirements need an owner.** Ownership by a named person is what makes a requirement challengeable and often reveals no one still believes in it.
- **Deletion is the highest-leverage step, and it's under-done.** Aim to over-delete slightly (add back ~10%).
- **Order discipline beats cleverness.** Running the steps in sequence prevents heroic effort spent in the wrong place.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Before improving any process, list its requirements and attach a *name* to each; challenge every one, then cut the ownerless and disputed ones.
- Aggressively delete parts/steps; if you're never having to add ~10% back, you're not cutting hard enough.
- Only then simplify the survivors, then speed up cycle time, then automate.
- Audit anything already automated: was it deleted/simplified first? If not, tear out and re-run the order.

## Notable examples
- The Tesla battery-pack fiberglass mats — automated and optimized for years before being deleted outright.
- "The best part is no part" as Method #26 of the 69 Core Musk Methods.
