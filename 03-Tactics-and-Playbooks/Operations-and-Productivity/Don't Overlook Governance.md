# Don't Overlook Governance

## One-line summary
All the culture, people, and machine-design principles in the rest of the book are worthless without a governance system that constrains power itself — no one, however capable or principled, should be more powerful than the system or effectively irreplaceable, and Dalio admits this was his own biggest, latest-discovered gap.

## Context
Sixteenth and final chapter of Work Principles. Dalio explicitly frames this as a personal blind spot: Bridgewater ran for decades without formal board governance because Dalio himself had always functioned as the informal check on the organization — a structure that only became visibly inadequate once he began stepping back from the CEO role.

## Key insights
- **All organizations must have real checks and balances.** Even inside a genuine idea meritocracy, merit can't be the *only* factor in ultimate authority — owners' vested rights matter too, and have to be reconciled with the meritocratic decision process rather than ignored by it.
- **No one should be more powerful than the system itself, and no one should be irreplaceable.** Dalio relays the historical caution of Julius Caesar's overthrow of the Roman Senate (told to him by China's Wang Qishan) as the canonical failure mode: a system that depends on one person's continued goodwill rather than on structural constraints is not actually a stable system. Beware fiefdoms — pockets of unaccountable local power inside an otherwise well-governed organization.
- **In an idea meritocracy, a single CEO is worse than a great group of leaders.** Bridgewater's own post-2016 structure — co-CEOs and a co-CIO model — is designed specifically to spread key-man risk and combine complementary skills, rather than concentrating both authority and irreplaceability in one person.
- **No governance system substitutes for a genuinely great partnership among capable, principled people.** Governance is a backstop against failure, not a replacement for the actual quality of the people and relationships it's meant to protect.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
**Bridgewater's post-2016 governance structure**: 1–3 chairmen plus 7–15 board members overseeing 1–3 co-CEOs, organized as three concentric circles of authority, trust, and information access — a concrete illustration of distributing power deliberately rather than concentrating it, once the organization recognized it had been relying on one person's informal oversight.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Ask explicitly: if the single most trusted person in this organization left tomorrow, what actually constrains bad decisions? If the honest answer is "nothing, we trusted them," that's an unaddressed governance gap, not a compliment to that person.
- When a founder or long-tenured leader starts stepping back, treat governance design as an urgent, explicit project — not something that will sort itself out informally the way it may have under their direct oversight.
- Watch for "fiefdoms" — departments or individuals who've accumulated authority that isn't actually accountable to the broader system — and address them before a crisis forces the issue.
- Prefer distributing key authority across a small group with complementary skills over concentrating it in a single successor, especially in an idea-meritocratic culture.

## Notable examples
- **The 2016 CEO reshuffle.** Greg Jensen was overloaded trying to serve as both co-CEO and co-CIO simultaneously; the failure led to a painful temporary demotion and Dalio stepping back in as co-CEO for roughly a year. On advice from Jim Collins — "put capable CEOs in place and have a capable governance system to replace them if they're not capable" — Dalio realized Bridgewater had never built formal governance, because he himself had always been the informal check on the organization. This is the direct origin of this chapter and of Bridgewater's current board structure.
