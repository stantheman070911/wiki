# Diagnose Problems to Get at Their Root Causes

## One-line summary
Diagnosis means connecting a bad outcome to the specific person or design responsible and determining whether it's a one-off or a recurring pattern — skipping straight from noticing a problem to proposing a solution, without this step, is the most common failure in management.

## Context
Twelfth chapter of Work Principles, continuing the Client Service Department case study: once a problem has been perceived (Ch. 11), this chapter is about establishing what actually caused it before touching the fix.

## Key insights
- **Ask three questions in order: (1) was the outcome good or bad? (2) who's responsible? (3) is the Responsible Party incapable, is the design bad, or both?** Build a "mental map" of how the machine *should* have worked using a chain of yes/no questions, then pinpoint exactly where reality diverged from that map. Ask specifically "who should have done what differently?" and identify which of the [[The 5-Step Process - Goals, Problems, Diagnosis, Design, and Doing (Dalio)|5 Steps]] actually failed. Avoid Monday-morning quarterbacking — judge decisions against what was knowable *at the time*, not with hindsight.
- **Root causes are described in adjectives (traits), not verbs (specific actions).** "He missed the deadline" is a proximate cause; "he's disorganized under time pressure" is closer to a root cause — and root causes, once correctly named, predict future behavior in similar situations.
- **Five common reasons managers fail to diagnose well**: they're too distant from the actual work; they can't perceive bad quality when they see it; they've normalized to badness (Frog in Boiling Water again); their ego prevents admitting the failure; or they fear the consequences of naming it honestly.
- **Maintain an emerging synthesis by diagnosing continuously**, not as a one-off event triggered only by a crisis.
- **A diagnosis should produce a change in outcome, not just an explanation.** Dalio invokes Einstein's definition of insanity — doing the same things and expecting different results — as the test of whether a diagnosis actually did its job.
- **Diagnosis builds relationships, not just plans.** The process of diagnosing something together, honestly, is itself how mutual understanding between people gets built — it's not separable from the relationship-building covered in [[Cultivate Meaningful Work and Meaningful Relationships]].

## Framework / model (if applicable)
**The drill-down technique**, for reaching an 80/20-useful understanding of a struggling team or department in about four hours: **Step 1** — list the specific problems, naming names, not categories. **Step 2** — identify root causes by repeatedly asking "why?" until you hit an adjective-level trait or design flaw. **Step 3** — create a plan. **Step 4** — execute and track it transparently, so the diagnosis is falsifiable rather than a one-time verdict.

## Tactics / how to apply
- When something goes wrong, force yourself through the three-question sequence (good/bad outcome → who's responsible → capability or design issue) before allowing yourself to propose a fix.
- Practice the repeated "why?" chain until the answer becomes an adjective describing a trait or a design flaw, not another verb describing an action — that's the signal you've actually reached a root cause.
- Set a hard time-box (roughly four hours) for a drill-down on a struggling team, covering all four steps, rather than letting diagnosis expand indefinitely or get skipped under time pressure.
- When judging a past decision, explicitly reconstruct what was knowable at the time before assigning fault — resist hindsight bias.

## Notable examples
- **A "why?" chain about a programmer named Harry's bad code** is Dalio's worked example of drilling from a surface-level complaint ("the code was buggy") down to a root-cause trait (e.g., Harry works fast and instinctively deprioritizes edge-case testing under deadline pressure) that actually predicts and prevents the next incident, rather than just explaining the last one.
- **"The Ray gap."** When Dalio stepped back as CEO in 2011 and his successors struggled, Bridgewater diagnosed the shortfall the same way it would diagnose any machine problem — cataloguing the specific attributes ("shaping" ability, in this case) that Dalio brought which his successors didn't, rather than settling for a vague sense that "something feels off." The lesson: when a team underperforms after a personnel change, map the gap in specific attributes, not general dissatisfaction.
