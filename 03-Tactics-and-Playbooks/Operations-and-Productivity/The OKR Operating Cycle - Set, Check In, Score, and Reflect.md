# The OKR Operating Cycle - Set, Check In, Score, and Reflect

## One-line summary
An OKR creates value only as a live operating loop: set it before the cycle, inspect confidence and evidence throughout, then score, explain, and reflect before choosing the next commitment.

## Context
Goals that appear only during quarterly or annual reporting become ceremonial paperwork. A recurring cadence turns them into current information for reallocating resources, resolving dependencies, learning from misses, and dropping goals whose assumptions no longer hold.

## Key insights
- **Tracking is not surveillance.** The purpose is early learning and course correction, not catching people after failure is inevitable.
- **A goal is not written in stone.** Continue, update, start, or stop are all legitimate decisions when evidence or priorities change.
- **Confidence is a leading signal.** Progress percentages describe the past; a red, yellow, or green confidence forecast shows where help or escalation is needed now.
- **A score needs context.** Objective grading, the contributor's self-assessment, and a short reflection together say more than the number alone.
- **Separate goal grades from performance ratings.** Directly tying pay or appraisal to goal attainment invites sandbagging and discourages worthwhile stretch.
- **Reflection closes the loop.** Ask what enabled the outcome, what obstructed it, what should change, and whether the objective still matters before rolling it forward.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
Run the cycle in four stages:

1. **Set:** Before the period, leaders publish company priorities; teams and contributors negotiate their supporting outcomes and dependencies.
2. **Check in:** Weekly one-on-ones and monthly team reviews update evidence, confidence, blockers, ownership, and resource needs.
3. **Score:** At period end, grade each key result objectively and derive the objective score without disguising misses.
4. **Reflect:** Add a subjective self-assessment, capture lessons, celebrate completions, and decide whether to close, revise, transfer, or carry the goal.

Annual strategic direction and shorter operational cycles can run in parallel when the organization needs both horizons.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Designate an OKR shepherd to maintain the calendar, coach quality, and prevent stale updates.
- In each check-in, ask: What is working, what is not, what is blocked, and what must be changed or escalated?
- Spend review time on at-risk goals rather than narrating every green; invite other teams to help recover the most consequential reds.
- Require evidence that is credible and easily discoverable, such as a published metric, document, system report, or shipped change.
- For a missed commitment, run a non-punitive postmortem on planning, resourcing, priority conflicts, and execution.
- End the cycle by explicitly recognizing what was accomplished before resetting the board.

## Notable examples
- Lumeris replaced end-of-quarter box checking with monthly reviews where leaders "sold their reds"; the group selected the most important at-risk goals and colleagues volunteered help across departmental lines.
- Remind used visible confidence updates to detect goals drifting off track before the period ended.
- Google combines objective scoring with self-assessment because identical scores can represent very different ambition, effort, and learning.
