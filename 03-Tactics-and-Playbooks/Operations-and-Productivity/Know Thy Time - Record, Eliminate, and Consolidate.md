# Know Thy Time - Record, Eliminate, and Consolidate

## One-line summary
Manage time from evidence rather than memory: record where it actually goes, remove recurring waste, and reserve the remaining capacity in uninterrupted blocks large enough for consequential work.

## Context
Time is the limiting resource of knowledge work: it cannot be stored, replaced, or increased, and fragmented minutes do not combine into the attention needed for decisions, writing, or meaningful conversations. Planning tasks before diagnosing time therefore produces schedules that look plausible but cannot be executed.

## Key insights
- **Memory is an unreliable time ledger.** People remember what they believe they should have done, so perceived allocation and actual allocation routinely diverge.
- **Important work has a minimum time quantum.** Six scattered hours may fail to produce what one uninterrupted ninety-minute or half-day block can accomplish because each interruption forces the work to restart.
- **People and personnel decisions require unhurried time.** Knowledge workers need context, expectations, and feedback; rushing these exchanges creates friction and shallow judgment rather than efficiency.
- **Recurring crises usually reveal system defects.** Predictable emergencies are not acts of nature. They point to bad process design, overstaffing, fragmented responsibility, excessive meetings, or information delivered to the wrong person or in the wrong form.
- **Calendar control is continuous maintenance.** New demands refill every space that pruning creates. Effective time management is a repeated audit, not a permanent optimization.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
**Record → eliminate → consolidate:**

1. **Record:** Track actual time continuously for a representative period; do not reconstruct it later from memory.
2. **Eliminate:** For every activity ask whether it needs to be done, whether someone else could do it, and whether it wastes other people’s time.
3. **Diagnose recurring waste:** Look specifically for repeated crises, unnecessary staffing and coordination, meeting overload, and broken information flow.
4. **Estimate discretionary capacity:** Calculate the time genuinely under your control after unavoidable obligations.
5. **Consolidate first:** Put that capacity into large recurring blocks for priority work, then arrange minor work around those blocks.
6. **Re-audit:** Use missed deadlines and encroached focus blocks as signals that the system needs another round of pruning.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Keep a real-time log for two to four weeks in 15- or 30-minute increments, then group the entries by contribution rather than by calendar label.
- Ask three deletion questions: “What would happen if this stopped?”, “Who else could own it?”, and “Which of my habits wastes someone else’s time?”
- Treat meetings consuming roughly a quarter or more of working time as a structural warning. Identify responsibilities or information that are split across too many roles.
- Reserve recurring 60–90 minute blocks before accepting routine appointments. Protect them from every interruption that can safely wait until the block ends.
- Batch operating meetings and routine reviews into defined windows so important work receives continuous time rather than the scraps between appointments.
- Maintain deadline lists for urgent and unpleasant work; repeated slippage is evidence that actual control of time has deteriorated.

## Notable examples
- One chairman believed his time was evenly divided among senior staff, customers, and community work; repeated logs showed that he mostly dispatched routine customer orders that already flowed without him.
- A bank president used ninety-minute, single-topic sessions with no calls because he found that important issues needed that long to become clear, while his attention deteriorated beyond it.
- A hospital’s recurring search for beds was caused by a simple information defect: admissions received a 5 a.m. count but not the discharge notices already sent elsewhere.
