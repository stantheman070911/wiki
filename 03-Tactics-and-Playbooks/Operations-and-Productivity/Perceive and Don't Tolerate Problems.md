# Perceive and Don't Tolerate Problems

## One-line summary
Problems are fuel for improvement — "coal thrown into a locomotive" — but only if they actually get noticed; every organization naturally drifts toward complacency and normalizes its own decay, so perceiving problems has to be actively engineered rather than left to happen on its own.

## Context
Eleventh chapter of Work Principles, and the first of the four-chapter run (perceive → diagnose → design → execute) built on the Client Service Department quality-control case study introduced in [[Manage as Someone Operating a Machine to Achieve a Goal]].

## Key insights
- **"If you're not worried, you need to worry — and if you're worried, you don't need to worry."** Genuine concern is itself a form of vigilance; its absence is the actual danger sign, not its presence.
- **Design or oversee a machine specifically for perceiving good vs. not-good-enough** — or do that perceiving yourself if nothing else exists yet. Assign dedicated problem-perceivers with an independent reporting line so their incentives aren't captured by the people they're evaluating. Beware "Frog in the Boiling Water Syndrome" — people gradually normalize conditions that would have looked clearly unacceptable if they'd arrived suddenly. Beware groupthink specifically: the absence of complaints is not evidence of the absence of problems. "Taste the soup" — actively sample outcomes yourself, the way a chef tastes the dish rather than trusting the recipe was followed. Maximize the number of eyes actually looking for problems, and "pop the cork" — actively solicit feedback rather than passively waiting for it to surface.
- **Be specific about problems, not generalizations.** Avoid the anonymous "we" or "they" that lets a real, attributable problem hide inside vague collective language.
- **Don't be afraid to fix hard things — not fixing them is harder in the long run.** Distinguish problems that already have a planned solution in motion from ones that don't, so effort goes where it's actually needed.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
**Frog in the Boiling Water Syndrome** as the chapter's central diagnostic lens: the danger isn't a single dramatic failure, it's the slow, undetected normalization of declining standards. The countermeasure is structural (independent perceivers, active sampling, solicited feedback), not just "try to notice more."

## Tactics / how to apply
- Assign at least one person (or role) whose job is specifically to notice quality problems, with a reporting line independent of the people whose work they're evaluating.
- Periodically and deliberately sample actual outputs yourself ("taste the soup") instead of relying on reports about outputs.
- When you notice a standard has quietly slipped, ask how long it's actually been slipping — it's often much longer than it feels, which is the Frog-in-Boiling-Water pattern in action.
- Actively solicit problems rather than waiting for them to be reported — silence from a team is not the same as a team with nothing wrong.
- Replace anonymous "we/they" language in problem reports with specific names and specific instances before treating the report as actionable.

## Notable examples
- The Client Service Department's quality slippage (memos going out with sloppy errors) after Dalio stepped back from direct oversight is this chapter's running example of Frog-in-Boiling-Water normalization — nobody had dramatically lowered the bar, it drifted down gradually while attention was elsewhere.
