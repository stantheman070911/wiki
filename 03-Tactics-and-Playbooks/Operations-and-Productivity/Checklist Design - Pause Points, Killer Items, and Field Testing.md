# Checklist Design - Pause Points, Killer Items, and Field Testing

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[03-Tactics-and-Playbooks/Tactics and Playbooks Index|Tactics and Playbooks]] → [[03-Tactics-and-Playbooks/Operations-and-Productivity/Operations and Productivity Map|Operations and Productivity]]

## One-line summary
A reliable checklist is a short, field-tested cognitive aid placed at a clear pause point, containing only the critical steps that capable people are most likely to miss under pressure.

## Context
Experts rarely fail because they know nothing; they fail when complexity, interruption, and urgency cause known essentials to slip. A checklist should protect those essentials without turning skilled work into a script or distracting the operator from the real situation.

## Key insights
- **Choose the pause point before choosing the items.** A checklist needs a natural moment when work can briefly stop—before commitment, after a transition, or before handoff—or it will be ignored when pressure rises.
- **Protect against consequential omission, not every possible error.** Include the few "killer items" whose absence can cause serious failure and that people can plausibly overlook despite training.
- **Keep judgment outside the list.** The list recalls, verifies, and prompts communication; it does not replace the craft required to interpret a novel situation.
- **Make ownership explicit.** One person initiates the check and has authority to hold the process until the required confirmations are complete.
- **Use the right execution mode.** A DO-CONFIRM list lets experienced operators perform from memory and then verify; a READ-DO list guides each action in sequence when order or unfamiliarity matters.
- **Brevity is a safety property.** Long lists compete with the work, invite box-ticking, and make the truly critical items harder to see.
- **A checklist is a versioned operating tool.** Its wording, order, length, and timing must be tested in realistic conditions and revised as the work changes.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
**The checklist design loop:**

1. **Define the failure:** identify a recurring, preventable breakdown in an otherwise competent process.
2. **Select the pause point:** name the exact trigger and moment when the check will run.
3. **Choose the mode:** use DO-CONFIRM for familiar expert work or READ-DO for a prescribed sequence.
4. **Select killer items:** keep only high-consequence, commonly missed actions or confirmations.
5. **Write operationally:** use familiar language, one action per line, and explicit verbal confirmation where team alignment matters.
6. **Assign an owner and stop authority:** specify who calls the check and what happens when an item fails.
7. **Time and field-test:** run it under realistic workload, observe friction and ambiguity, then cut or rewrite.
8. **Date and revise:** treat every release as provisional evidence, not permanent doctrine.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Start from incident reviews and near misses; list what a competent person knew but failed to do, then look for repeated patterns.
- Limit one pause point to roughly five to nine essential items and about a minute of attention when the environment is time-critical.
- Ask of every line: "Would a trained person usually do this without a prompt?" If yes, remove it unless omission is both plausible and dangerous.
- Put the trigger directly on the list—for example, "before publishing," "before funds move," or "before the client handoff."
- Test with the people who will actually use it, in the place and sequence where they work; desk review cannot reveal social awkwardness or timing conflicts.
- Measure both compliance and outcome. If people complete the list but the target failure persists, redesign the check rather than adding ceremonial boxes.

## Notable examples
The Boeing Model 299 prototype was judged "too much airplane" after an experienced pilot crashed because the elevator and rudder controls remained locked. A short preflight checklist enabled ordinary crews to fly the aircraft safely for 1.8 million miles. In the WHO surgical checklist trials, early drafts were too long and vague; successive one-case tests cut non-killer items, clarified verbal checks, and fixed three pause points before anesthesia, before incision, and before leaving the operating room.
