# Meetings as Managerial Production

## One-line summary
Meetings are the production medium for managerial work: recurring forums batch information and coaching, while tightly owned ad hoc meetings produce a specific decision or action.

## Context
Managers exchange information, teach, decide, and align people through interaction. The waste is not meetings as a category but unclear output, excessive attendance, missing preparation, and using an expensive synchronous forum when a cheaper medium would work.

## Key insights
- **Choose the meeting type by output.** Process-oriented meetings regularly exchange information and teach; mission-oriented meetings exist to solve one problem and usually produce a decision.
- **Regularity creates efficiency.** Predictable one-on-ones, staff meetings, and operating reviews batch recurring transactions and reduce random interruption.
- **The one-on-one belongs to the subordinate.** Its leverage comes from mutual teaching, surfacing nagging or potential problems, and building shared context for delegation.
- **Staff meetings develop peer coordination.** They are for issues affecting more than two members, constructive disagreement, and decisions among people with an established working history.
- **Operation reviews connect organizational layers.** Formal presentations let junior operators teach senior leaders about detail while senior leaders transfer context, standards, and feedback.
- **An ad hoc meeting needs one accountable chair.** The chair defines the intended output, decides whether a meeting is warranted, secures the right participants, enforces time, and closes with decisions and owners.
- **Minutes complete the production cycle.** A decision without rapid written confirmation of what, who, and when is unfinished work.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
**Four meeting products:**

1. **One-on-one:** subordinate-led agenda; indicators, changes, people issues, future plans, and preoccupying concerns; supervisor learns, coaches, and asks one more question.
2. **Staff meeting:** supervisor plus direct reports; common issues, peer debate, open-session items, and team decisions; supervisor facilitates rather than lectures.
3. **Operation review:** prepared presentations across levels or functions; reviewers ask and teach, presenters make work visible, audience actively tests the material.
4. **Mission-oriented meeting:** one time-sensitive problem; no more than roughly six to eight decision participants; clear decider, consultation roles, and immediate action record.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Before inviting anyone, write the meeting's intended output in one sentence and ask whether asynchronous work, a call, or a one-on-one can produce it more cheaply.
- Schedule recurring forums on a rolling basis and use a hold file to batch non-urgent items.
- Have the subordinate prepare the one-on-one outline; spend enough time that difficult issues can emerge, and take parallel notes on commitments.
- In staff meetings, stop two-person side issues and schedule them separately; reserve an open session without surrendering the controlled agenda.
- Send mission-meeting agendas with purpose, roles, evidence, and the exact decision to be made.
- End by stating the decision, dissent or unresolved risk, action owner, deadline, and communication audience; circulate minutes while memory is fresh.
- Track the share of time spent in ad hoc meetings. A high share signals missing process, weak ownership, or unresolved recurring problems.

## Notable examples
Grove treats a ninety-minute one-on-one every two weeks as leverage over roughly eighty hours of a subordinate's work. Intel's operation reviews made several organizational levels teach one another. For mission meetings, he notes that ten managers meeting for two hours consume resources comparable to a material purchase, yet organizations often authorize the meeting with less discipline than the purchase.
