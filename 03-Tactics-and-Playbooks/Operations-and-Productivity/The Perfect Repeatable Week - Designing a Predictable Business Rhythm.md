# The Perfect Repeatable Week - Designing a Predictable Business Rhythm

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[03-Tactics-and-Playbooks/Tactics and Playbooks Index|Tactics and Playbooks]] → [[03-Tactics-and-Playbooks/Operations-and-Productivity/Operations and Productivity Map|Operations and Productivity]]

## One-line summary
Translate the annual target into a profitable weekly unit, assign the activities and owners that produce it, and repeat that operating week until evidence justifies a change.

## Context
Small businesses often improvise every week and mistake novelty for progress. A repeatable week creates a stable production unit for revenue, delivery, learning, and accountability.

## Key insights
- **Annual goals need a weekly denominator.** A million-dollar year can be designed as roughly forty $25,000 weeks, allowing for holidays and interruptions.
- **The whole week must reconcile.** Marketing volume, lead follow-up, sales calls, delivery capacity, meetings, costs, and expected profit must fit one another.
- **Roles precede calendars.** Start with the future-state organization chart, then specify what each role does hour by hour in the target week.
- **Repetition builds both skill and confidence.** A weekly introduction workshop becomes more persuasive through live delivery and customer questions; automating it before enough repetitions freezes an immature version.
- **Founders own the cadence.** Monday alignment and Friday reflection are leadership work, not administrative meetings to delegate away.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
Design the week backward:

1. Set annual revenue, gross margin, and profit targets.
2. Divide them into a conservative number of operating weeks.
3. Specify weekly sales and the leads, appointments, and presentations required.
4. Map delivery capacity and every role needed to fulfill the promise.
5. Place recurring activities and meetings on the calendar.
6. Run the week, compare actuals with the model, and refine at the quarterly reset.

Bookend each week with a Monday **three-to-six priorities** meeting and a Friday **done/not-done** debrief.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Model the target week in financial terms before adding headcount.
- Give each person three to six high-value outcomes, not a long activity inventory.
- Encourage constructive “locking antlers” on Monday: challenge whether the chosen work is really the highest leverage.
- Use one live introduction workshop as a recurring demand-generation fixture and deliver it at least fifty times before automating it.
- Meet quarterly to improve the offer, sales materials, role clarity, and next ninety days—not to replace a working cadence with random initiatives.
- Keep a visible weekly LAPS dashboard and treat volatility as an operating problem.

## Notable examples
Priestley compares the model with a touring band: the show, pack-down, travel, setup, and rehearsal form a machine that repeats. The creative work is not eliminated; it is embedded in a reliable operating cycle.
