# Do What You Set Out to Do

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[03-Tactics-and-Playbooks/Tactics and Playbooks Index|Tactics and Playbooks]] → [[03-Tactics-and-Playbooks/Operations-and-Productivity/Operations and Productivity Map|Operations and Productivity]]

## One-line summary
Execution — the fifth and final step of Dalio's process — is where most real value actually gets created, through mundane, often unglamorous persistence rather than brilliant ideas alone, which is why it gets its own chapter instead of being treated as an afterthought to planning.

## Context
Fourteenth chapter of Work Principles, closing out the perceive → diagnose → design → execute sequence: everything in the three preceding chapters is wasted if this step doesn't happen.

## Key insights
- **Work for goals you're genuinely excited about.** Don't act before thinking, but recognize planning time is negligible relative to execution time, so don't let planning become a way to avoid starting. Look for creative shortcuts rather than assuming the only lever is working harder.
- **Everyone has too much to do — there are only three real fixes:** prioritize and say no to the rest, delegate to the right people, or improve your own productivity. Don't get frustrated by the gap between ambition and time available; Dalio cites Churchill's line that "success consists of going from failure to failure without loss of enthusiasm" as the operating attitude to hold.
- **Use checklists, but don't confuse a checklist with full personal responsibility** — a checklist catches known failure modes, it doesn't substitute for someone actually owning the outcome.
- **Allow real time for rest and renovation** — sustained execution requires deliberately protected recovery, not just willpower.
- **"Ring the bell."** Explicitly celebrate when goals are actually achieved, rather than immediately moving to the next thing without marking the completion — the celebration itself reinforces that execution, not just planning, is what's valued.
- **Turn the promise into a live operating loop.** Define sufficient evidence before starting, revisit confidence and blockers during the work, and score and reflect at the end. This makes execution observable without confusing goal tracking with a performance rating.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
This chapter doesn't introduce a new framework so much as it closes the loop on the [[The 5-Step Process - Goals, Problems, Diagnosis, Design, and Doing (Dalio)|5-Step Process]]: goals, problems, diagnosis, and design are all necessary but not sufficient — Step 5 is where the other four either pay off or don't.

## Tactics / how to apply
- When you notice you're over capacity, explicitly categorize the overflow into "cut it," "delegate it," or "get more efficient at it" rather than just working longer hours.
- Use checklists for known, recurring failure points, but keep a named owner accountable for the overall outcome regardless of whether every checklist item was ticked.
- Build in deliberate recovery time as part of the execution plan itself, not as something you'll get to once the goal is hit.
- When a goal is actually completed, mark it explicitly (a real "ring the bell" moment) before moving to the next priority.
- Label each goal as a must-deliver commitment or a stretch aspiration, then review at-risk goals early enough to change resources, scope, ownership, or timing.
- At the end of the cycle, combine the objective score with a short self-assessment: what enabled the result, what obstructed it, and what should change next time?

## Notable examples
- Dalio frames Churchill's "success consists of going from failure to failure without loss of enthusiasm" as the emotional posture execution actually requires — not because failure is desirable, but because sustained execution guarantees you'll hit a long string of setbacks before the goal, and the people who keep going are the ones who treat that as normal rather than as a signal to stop.
- At Lumeris, leaders spent review time "selling their reds" instead of narrating successes: the group identified the most important at-risk goals and colleagues volunteered to help recover them.
