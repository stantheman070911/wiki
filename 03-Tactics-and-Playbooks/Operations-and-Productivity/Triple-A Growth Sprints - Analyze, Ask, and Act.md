# Triple-A Growth Sprints - Analyze, Ask, and Act

## One-line summary
A Triple-A sprint creates a monthly growth rhythm by diagnosing macro outputs, choosing the highest-leverage input, and shipping one or two prioritized changes instead of accumulating disconnected optimization ideas.

## Context
SaaS teams can measure almost anything and still fail to improve the business. A short recurring process keeps the team focused on consequential outputs, the few economic levers that drive them, and experiments small enough to finish and learn from.

## Key insights
- **Outputs locate the problem; inputs create the change.** Signups, upgrades, average revenue per user, churn, recurring revenue, and annual recurring revenue show where performance hurts, but the team's actions are the adjustable inputs.
- **Growth has three multiplicative levers.** Increase customers, increase revenue per user, or reduce churn; mature businesses often underinvest in the latter two because acquisition is easier to celebrate.
- **One bottleneck deserves the sprint.** Spreading activity across every metric prevents enough force from reaching the current constraint.
- **Prioritization makes assumptions discussable.** Impact, confidence, and ease create a common language for comparing unlike projects without pretending the scores are facts.
- **Small wins buy permission for larger changes.** Quick, credible results build trust with product, engineering, and leadership before experiments touch complex systems such as pricing.
- **Process beats isolated tactics.** A monthly loop compounds learning; an unowned list of ideas does not.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
Run a monthly Triple-A sprint:

1. **Analyze:** Compare core outputs over time and identify the weakest or most leveraged growth variable.
2. **Ask:** Define the desired destination, select the lever—churn, average revenue per user, or customer count—and brainstorm inputs that could move it.
3. **Act:** Score inputs by impact, confidence, and ease; implement the top one or two; measure the corresponding output; and feed the result into the next cycle.

Use the value foundation as a diagnostic shortcut: weak signups may signal poor understanding or communication, while weak upgrades and retention often signal failed delivery.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Reserve one or two hours on the first workday of each month to review the prior period with the same output definitions.
- Buy or sign up for your own product regularly to expose broken forms, stale messages, and onboarding friction hidden from internal users.
- Write each proposed input as a causal hypothesis with a target output and a measurement method.
- Keep an input log, score each idea on impact, confidence, and ease, and record the assumptions behind the score.
- Begin with a moderate-to-high-impact change that can ship quickly, then take larger swings after the team has a reliable learning cadence.
- Do not optimize a structurally weak product; fix the underlying value and experience before layering tactics on top.

## Notable examples
- A proposed in-app "Upgrade Now" button can be scored by its expected effect on signup-to-paid conversion, confidence from observed upgrade confusion, and implementation ease.
- HubSpot used quick experiments to build cross-functional trust during its freemium transition, then progressed toward more complex growth opportunities and pricing tests.
- A company can more than double lifetime value through combined improvements to churn, average revenue per user, and customer count even when no single lever grows dramatically.
