# Using AI in Your Business - Train Like an Employee and the Department Playbook

## One-line summary
You don't have to become an AI business — you have to use AI in your business — by training agents the way you'd train a new hire, overlaying your business acumen on the tech, and automating one task at a time across every department.

## Context
The companion operations piece to the AI strategy: how Hormozi's ~$250M-revenue portfolio actually uses AI to make the business better, cheaper, faster, and less risky, with concrete department examples. The framing: AI is the internet 25 years on — a tool you use, not an identity you adopt.

## Key insights
- **Don't become an AI business; use AI as a tool.** Like the internet, you don't advertise that you use it — customers only care that things are faster, cheaper, better, and lower-risk. Don't market "we use AI"; market outcomes.
- **Cloud-to-dirt knowledge is the alpha.** The owner who understands both high-level strategy *and* the granular (how one API connects to another) sees what's possible; a hired "tech nerd" defaults to commoditized automations. The real gains come from business acumen overlaid on technical acumen.
- **Train AI like a new employee.** A bad first output isn't proof it can't work — you'd train a new hire, not fire them. Humans learn by reinforcement; computers pattern-match even better. Most people train humans badly (they never define what "good" looks like), so translating your process into observable behaviors is a superpower for AI training.
- **Apples-to-apples, not John Henry.** Don't pit a half-built AI function against a process optimized over 10–20 years and conclude it fails. Give the AI the same SOP rigor and iteration you gave the human system.
- **AI accelerates a process; it doesn't remove the need for one.** An AI setter dropped into a business with no real sales motion still fails — worse if your process was already bad. It makes what you do easier and faster, not optional.
- **Human psychology won't change.** The rules of persuasion still apply, which is why **proof is the bottleneck of AI content** — endless AI output makes proof the differentiator (B2B especially: a proof-less AI avatar is "just GPT words"; consumer is less sensitive because a beautiful avatar visually implies proof).
- **Agent availability should expand the offer, not only cut cost.** Ask what research, analysis, preparation, and personalized guidance every customer could receive if capable assistants were abundant; this reveals new value that an efficiency-only audit misses.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
**Replace "magic" with pattern recognition.** Whenever you catch yourself saying "I just know / gut / intuition / taste," reframe it as pattern recognition (stimuli + reinforcement generalized into a pattern) and write down the *observable behaviors* that produce the outcome — then an agent can be trained on them. Copy example: instead of "write this email," give "12 rules you can't break + 16 of my writing samples, write only to these" → ~5× better; repeat the loop 100 times (100 minutes vs ~1.5 years with a person).

**The one-task automation drill.** List what you do at the most granular level (not "I run ads" but: build campaigns, set budgets, write copy, make creative, test headlines, analyze results). Take the first task, put it into AI — "help me automate this, what steps would you take?" — do the first step, and when stuck, **screenshot the screen and ask "what do I do now?"**, repeating. Everyone has an AI tutor at their fingertips.

**Department use cases (make it better/cheaper/faster/risk-free):**
- *Marketing:* idea generation; scripting/packaging (headlines, thumbnails, topics); auto-A/B thumbnail tests that build a winning-pattern skill; trend research (formats, verbal *and* visual hooks) cross-referenced with your brand corpus → a Venn of trending × on-brand → idea → headline → script.
- *The self-licking ice cream cone:* content auto-overlaid with a CTA → auto-loaded into an ad campaign that launches daily with yesterday's content; a community "wins" tab → six high-converting templates → auto-run as ads.
- *Sales:* enrich leads, instant personalized voice notes / DMs pulling business-specific detail, AI-to-AI dynamic scheduling.
- *Support / risk:* PayPal cut ~$700M in fraud losses via pattern recognition; JPMorgan's COIN saved ~350k lawyer hours; Klarna replaced ~700 CS agents (~$40M); Hormozi's book launch ran 5 agents over ~120k tickets, resolving ~90% with no human.
- *Legal:* one general counsel running agents (first response, cease-and-desist, etc.) instead of ~100 paralegals.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Pick a fixed weekly time block and automate one portion of your day soup-to-nuts before moving on.
- Define "good" as explicit rules + real examples; feed them and iterate 100 loops rather than accepting the first output.
- Overlay your own business knowledge on the tech instead of delegating the vision to a technician.
- Automate *your own* workflows first, freeing time to find higher-value work.
- Keep (and require) real proof behind any AI-generated content, especially B2B.

## Notable examples
- Hormozi's book-launch support: 5 agents, ~120,000 tickets, ~90% auto-resolved.
- His AI SDR "matching" the human team; AI setters and an AI product trained over years inside the business.

## AMA refinements (Ask Me Anything)
Later Q&A sharpened three points for operators:
- **AI-first requires data-first.** "The first thing you have to do to be an AI-first company is be a data-first company, because AI works with data." Get complete, architected data across the business *before* layering AI on top; using ChatGPT for emails is not being AI-first.
- **Sequence operating leverage: offshore, then AI.** For a supply-constrained service (a CFO-advisory firm that had stopped taking new sales), the path was to add offshore talent to give the existing team 2–3× leverage, build the data layer, then add the AI layer for further leverage — and only then resume selling. This is the "once-in-a-generation move," analogous to the cloud shift 25 years ago; the friction ("the pieces don't all fit yet") is exactly what you get paid to solve.
- **Start with the problem, not the tool.** Don't set out to "learn to code" or "use AI"; start with a customer and a problem, and you'll learn tools in a contextually relevant way (Musk's wrench-and-engine analogy). Modern builders run swarms of 20–50 agents and live in project management and code review rather than hands-on-keyboard.
- **Don't start a course business to escape a hard service business.** Every business is hard; the course "will suck too, you just don't know it yet." If you're supply-constrained, fix delivery — keep the courses/books as marketing assets to deploy when you need more demand.
