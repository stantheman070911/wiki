# Don't Outsource the Thinking - Where AI Belongs and Where It Doesn't

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[03-Tactics-and-Playbooks/Tactics and Playbooks Index|Tactics and Playbooks]]

## One-line summary
AI is a tool to use *inside* your business, not a brain to hand your judgment to — delegating your hardest thinking makes you weaker, an AI will agree with almost anything so it can't own a decision, and the only test that matters for any AI spend is whether it makes you more money.

## Context
This is the cautionary counterweight to the bullish adoption case (see [[05-Intelligence-and-Research/Winning with AI - Workflow Thinking, BYOA, and Risk as the Last Human Job|Winning with AI]]). Hormozi is a self-described AI advocate, but he sees founders "using AI to do a lot of dumb things really fast" — automating non-constraints, building companies the frontier models will swallow, and outsourcing the one asset they can't afford to weaken: their own thinking.

## Key insights
- **The only scoreboard is "are you making more money?"** Token-maxing, elaborate automations, and "AI-first" positioning are all vanity unless revenue moves. Ask the blunt question of every AI initiative before and after.
- **Don't automate a non-constraint.** A business spent $350k building an AI system to replace 11 virtual assistants costing ~$11k/month — three-plus years of cost to automate a process that *wasn't the thing limiting growth*. They were demand-constrained; they needed customers, not a cheaper data-cleaning step. Point AI at the binding constraint, not at whatever is convenient to automate.
- **Use AI *in* your business; don't necessarily start an AI business.** Three common misfires: starting AI businesses that the models will commoditise immediately; *advertising* "we're an AI company" when the customer only cares about the outcome/solution to their problem (and many customers are wary of AI, so it can be a negative); and getting excited about the tech and assuming the customer shares that excitement. Sell the outcome; use AI to deliver it better, faster, cheaper.
- **AI will agree to anything — so it can't hold judgment.** Ask the same real question of three frontier models and you get three different, confident answers "all over the place." That inconsistency is exactly why the decision stays yours. AI gives recommendations; a human still has to *own* the decision.
- **Outsourcing your hardest thinking makes you dumber.** Your thinking is the sharpest asset you have; delegating the hardest cognitive work atrophies it "so weak so fast." Keep the hard problems; let AI take load off the easy, well-specified ones.
- **Clarity of thought is the durable edge.** In a world where everyone can defer to a model, knowing which questions to ask, thinking critically, and owning the call is what stays scarce.

## Application
- Gate every AI project behind one question: *does this make more money?* If you can't draw the line to revenue, it's a hobby, not a strategy.
- Before automating anything, identify the business's actual constraint (demand vs. supply) and only spend AI effort there — automating a non-constraint just buys cost.
- Advertise the customer's outcome, not your tech stack. "AI-powered" is not a benefit; the solved problem is.
- Stress-test AI decisions by asking multiple models the same question; where they diverge, that's your judgment call to make, not theirs.
- Deliberately keep your hardest thinking work yourself; use AI as leverage on the well-defined, not as a replacement for reasoning.
