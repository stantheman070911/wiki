# The Customer-Base Test Kitchen - Distributed R&D Before Rollout

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[03-Tactics-and-Playbooks/Tactics and Playbooks Index|Tactics and Playbooks]] → [[03-Tactics-and-Playbooks/Operations-and-Productivity/Operations and Productivity Map|Operations and Productivity]]

## One-line summary
Use a representative subset of customers as a recurring test kitchen so the whole customer base receives validated improvements without bearing the cost of every experiment.

## Context
An education or service company can remain operationally credible even after the founder stops running the original business when it has a disciplined mechanism for testing current problems with current customers.

## Key insights
- **Distribution can improve expertise.** A broad customer base provides more environments and faster learning than one founder-operated location.
- **Test the largest shared constraint.** R&D should solve the most common expensive problem, not the founder's most interesting idea.
- **Negative results still create value.** A failed test removes a tempting but unproductive path for everyone else.
- **Representative markets matter.** A narrow or exceptional pilot can create a false positive that does not survive rollout.
- **Rollout follows evidence.** The full base gets only the practices that worked under controlled observation.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
The monthly test-kitchen cycle:

1. Gather recurring problems from the customer base.
2. Rank them by prevalence and economic impact.
3. Recruit 10–20 representative customers for the top problem.
4. Allocate a fixed experiment budget and direct expert attention.
5. Run the intervention, capture baseline and outcome data, and document context.
6. Publish both successful and failed findings.
7. Roll successful changes across the full distribution base.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Maintain a structured problem backlog from support, calls, churn, and performance data.
- Define success and failure before starting each test.
- Choose pilots across different sizes and markets rather than only enthusiastic top performers.
- Give pilot customers extra access in exchange for clean participation and data.
- Turn findings into updated playbooks, training, and product changes.
- Preserve failed-test notes so the same idea is not repeatedly rediscovered.

## Notable examples
At Gym Launch, Hormozi reports selecting roughly 10–20 representative gyms and assigning a monthly R&D budget to the biggest shared problem. About half the tests failed, but even those results saved the wider customer base from running the same losing experiments; winners were then rolled out system-wide.
