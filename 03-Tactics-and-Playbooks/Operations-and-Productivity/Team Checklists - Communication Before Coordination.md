# Team Checklists - Communication Before Coordination

## One-line summary
In complex work, a checklist should create brief, mandatory conversations that turn specialists into a team before uncertainty demands coordinated judgment.

## Context
Teams often assemble from capable people who have different information, priorities, and authority. The predictable failure is not lack of expertise but silence: no shared plan, no invitation to raise concerns, and no reliable handoff when conditions change.

## Key insights
- **Coordination starts with social permission.** Exchanging names and roles makes people more likely to speak, notice one another's responsibilities, and interrupt a dangerous course of action.
- **Briefing is a risk-discovery mechanism.** Each discipline sees different failure modes; surfacing expected duration, constraints, concerns, and contingencies exposes gaps no individual can see alone.
- **Communication checks complement task checks.** Task checks catch known omissions; team checks help the group detect and respond to the unexpected.
- **Authority must follow information.** The person closest to a missed condition needs explicit permission to stop the process, even when rank points the other way.
- **Handoffs require read-back, not passive transmission.** The sender states what happened and what remains uncertain; the receiver confirms the intended plan and ownership.
- **The ritual should be short but real.** A rushed recital without attention preserves hierarchy and creates compliance theater instead of shared situational awareness.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
**Brief–pause–debrief:**

1. **Brief before commitment:** introduce names and roles; confirm the goal; state the plan, known risks, resource constraints, and triggers for escalation.
2. **Pause at critical transitions:** verify the few non-negotiable conditions and ask each relevant function for new concerns.
3. **Debrief before handoff:** confirm what was completed, account for outputs and exceptions, name unresolved risks, and assign the next owner.

Each checkpoint has a named caller and the authority to delay progress until the team has resolved or consciously accepted a flagged risk.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Begin a temporary or cross-functional team's first critical task with a thirty-second round of name, role, and responsibility.
- Ask domain-specific prompts rather than "Any questions?": "What is most likely to surprise us?" "What resource could become limiting?" "What would make you stop?"
- Rotate the checklist caller or assign it to the role best positioned to observe process integrity, not automatically to the highest-status person.
- At handoff, require the next owner to restate the plan, immediate watch items, and escalation threshold.
- Track whether junior or peripheral members actually raise concerns; silence from them is evidence that the social design is not working.
- After a near miss, examine which needed conversation failed to occur and add the smallest prompt at the relevant pause point.

## Notable examples
Construction firms use both task schedules and "submittal" schedules that force trades to confer when an unexpected issue arises. In operating rooms, the WHO checklist asks team members to introduce themselves and then has surgeons, anesthetists, and nurses each state their critical concerns. Staff reported better teamwork, and improvement in teamwork scores correlated with fewer complications. Before US Airways Flight 1549 took off, an unfamiliar captain and first officer briefed together; that routine helped them divide flying and emergency-checklist work without argument when both engines failed.
