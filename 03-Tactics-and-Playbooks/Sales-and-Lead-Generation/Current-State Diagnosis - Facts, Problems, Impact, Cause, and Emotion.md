# Current-State Diagnosis - Facts, Problems, Impact, Cause, and Emotion

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[03-Tactics-and-Playbooks/Tactics and Playbooks Index|Tactics and Playbooks]] → [[03-Tactics-and-Playbooks/Sales-and-Lead-Generation/Sales and Lead Generation Map|Sales and Lead Generation]]

## One-line summary
Diagnose a buyer's current state through five layers—facts, problems, impact, root cause, and emotion—so the proposed change addresses the real business problem rather than a surface symptom.

## Context
Two customers can report the same technical issue while suffering very different business consequences. Treating the symptom as the problem produces generic proposals, weak urgency, and feature comparisons; mapping the whole current state reveals what is actually worth changing.

## Key insights
- **Facts define the operating environment.** Relevant processes, volumes, roles, systems, constraints, and performance levels make the situation specific.
- **Problems are undesirable outcomes, not product gaps.** “No dashboard” is a condition; missed decisions, wasted labor, or lost revenue are the problems it may create.
- **Impact determines severity.** A problem matters only through what it costs, delays, prevents, exposes, or makes harder.
- **Technical and business problems are different.** A disconnected database is technical; the extra labor, slow response, and lost customers it causes are business problems that drive change.
- **Root cause prevents symptom selling.** A credible seller explains why the problem exists and does not prescribe until that cause is understood.
- **Emotion affects readiness.** Frustration, fear, insecurity, loss of control, extra work, and past resentment shape whether a rationally attractive change feels safe.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
Map the current state in five layers:

1. **Facts:** What literally happens now?
2. **Problems:** Which outcomes are unacceptable?
3. **Impact:** What does each problem cost or endanger?
4. **Root cause:** Why does it keep happening?
5. **Emotional state:** How do the people involved experience it and the prospect of change?

Trace both directions to test the chain: facts and causes should plausibly produce the problems, and the problems should explain the measured impact.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Scope fact-finding to the parts of the business the offering can affect; exhaustive but irrelevant discovery wastes trust.
- Ask for the exact sequence of a process, including handoffs, manual work, approval points, exceptions, and measurements.
- Reject vague descriptions such as “too slow” or “not enough”; ask “How slow?”, “Compared with what?”, and “What does that cause?”
- Follow a technical issue into its business consequences and quantify them wherever possible.
- Ask why the problem exists more than once, but validate the inferred cause rather than treating the seller's theory as fact.
- Notice emotional and political exposure: who loses control, face, status, time, or safety if the change proceeds?
- Summarize the five layers aloud and invite correction before defining the future state.

## Notable examples
A theater's disconnected ticketing and CRM systems are not valuable merely because integration is technically cleaner. The business consequences—manual data entry, unrecognized first-time buyers, weak follow-up, fewer repeat visits, and lost revenue—make the issue worth solving.
