# Engineering a Grand Slam Offer - Problems, Solutions, Trim and Stack

## One-line summary
The repeatable procedure for constructing an offer: name the dream outcome, brainstorm every problem between the buyer and it, convert each into a delivery vehicle, then trim the low-value/high-cost pieces and stack the rest into one named package.

## Context
This is the construction workflow behind [[The Value Equation and the Grand Slam Offer]] — the *how-to* to that entry's *why/what*. Use it whenever you are building or overhauling a core offer. The core mental shift is from **convergent thinking** (school math: many knowns, one right answer) to **divergent thinking** (business: many unknowns, many answers, one far better than the rest) — the offer is a divergent problem, so generate abundantly before you cut.

## Framework / model
### The four construction steps
1. **Identify the dream outcome.** Sell the destination, not the vehicle — nobody wants a membership, they want the result. State the best plausible outcome, ideally with a compressed timeframe ("lose 20 lbs in 6 weeks").
2. **List every problem.** Walk the buyer's journey in obsessive detail — for each thing they must *do*, list what stops them from doing it and from *continuing*. Sort against the four value drivers: it won't be worth it (dream outcome), it won't work for me / I can't stick to it (likelihood), it's too hard/confusing (effort & sacrifice), it takes too long / I'm too busy (time). Every unsolved problem is a lost sale, so more problems = more to solve.
3. **Turn problems into solutions.** Mechanically reverse each problem into "how to [solve it]" language — this is your checklist of what the offer must deliver.
4. **Create the delivery vehicles.** For each solution, brainstorm *how* you could deliver it, using the delivery cube below. Generate freely, even options you'd never actually run — the goal is to jog past your default.

### The delivery cube (two axes to vary every solution)
- **Level of personalization / ratio:** one-to-one · small group · one-to-many.
- **Level of effort you absorb (DIY → DFY):** do-it-yourself · done-with-you · done-for-you.
- Also vary **medium** (in-person, phone, Zoom, DM, email, voice memo, recorded, tool/software) and **speed/access**.
Same solution, many delivery costs and perceived values — that variety is what you trim and stack from.

### Trim and stack
- **Sales-to-fulfillment continuum:** easy-to-sell things are usually hard to fulfill, and vice versa. The goal is the sweet spot: sells well *and* is deliverable. Mantra: **create flow → monetize flow → then add friction.** Overdeliver first, generate cash and feedback, *then* engineer efficiency.
- **Trim:** remove high-cost / low-perceived-value vehicles; keep high-perceived-value / low-cost ones.
- **Stack:** bundle the survivors into a single named package presented as components, so the buyer thinks "I get *all* this for only that?"

## Tactics / how to apply
- Run the "brick exercise" mindset before building: force divergent output (how many uses for a brick in 120 seconds?) so you generate many vehicles per problem, not the obvious one.
- Aim for 30–60+ listed problems; treat volume as the asset, not a burden — each is a value-add you get to deliver or a bonus you can later weaponize.
- For a *first* offer, deliberately overdeliver — even do things that don't scale — to make sales and gather feedback; systematize the cost down afterward without changing the promise.
- Reserve the most distinct, standalone-valuable, short-but-high-value pieces (checklists, tools, swipe files) to break out as named bonuses rather than burying them in the core.
- Only after the offer converts reliably do you tighten fulfillment or add friction (raise price, offer less for the same price).

## Notable examples
- Hormozi's own progression: flying out to do full 21-day gym turnarounds ("done-for-you," ~8/month, unscalable) → teaching owners to run the same playbook ("done-with-you," 4,000+ gyms) — the promise ("fill your gym in 30 days") stayed identical; only the *how* moved along the continuum to become scalable.
- A weight-loss offer's problem list starts at "buying healthy food is hard/expensive/time-consuming/unsustainable" and cascades through cooking, eating, and exercising — each reversed into a "how to…" solution before choosing delivery.
