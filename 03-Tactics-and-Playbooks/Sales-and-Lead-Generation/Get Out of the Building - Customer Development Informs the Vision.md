# Get Out of the Building - Customer Development Informs the Vision

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[03-Tactics-and-Playbooks/Tactics and Playbooks Index|Tactics and Playbooks]]

## One-line summary
Customer development means leaving the building to test your assumptions against hundreds or thousands of real people — but its purpose is to *inform* your vision, not replace it: it's not a giant focus group, and you cannot build a startup from a spreadsheet of feature requests.

## Context
Steve Blank built the customer-development half of the Lean Startup (see [[01-Business-Strategy/Business-Models-and-Customers/Startups Search, Companies Execute - The Lean Startup Stack|Startups Search, Companies Execute]]). The most-misunderstood part, in his own words, is *why* he built it: not to tally up what customers ask for, but to validate a vision he already had. Startups that turn customer development into a survey — adding up feature requests, or "talking to five people" — get it exactly wrong.

## Key insights
- **It informs the vision; it doesn't create it.** If you don't have a vision of the problem, the customer, and why you're building this, don't get out of the building yet. Customer development pressure-tests conviction — "this time, let me make sure I'm not hallucinating" — rather than sourcing the idea.
- **You can't run a startup from a spreadsheet.** The whole method exists because assumptions live in your head as implicit beliefs; you have to spit them out, write them down, and go check them in reality. A spreadsheet or a focus group can't do that for you.
- **Deconstruct your background assumptions.** Ask the chain: I'm building this because I like it → why? → my friends like it → why? → it solves a problem → *what* problem? Surface each hidden assumption, then test whether it scales.
- **Five people isn't validation.** "I talked to five people" is not customer development. Find out whether *hundreds or thousands* want this — a bounding box around the uncertainty, not an anecdote.
- **Being the smartest in the room is a handicap here.** No founder is smarter than the collective intelligence of their potential customers; the very smart are tempted to pre-compute everything and skip talking to anyone. Assuming "maybe I don't know something" is what gets you out the door.
- **The evidence loop is experiential.** You can read the whole method and still make every mistake — the lessons only stick once you're melting down with real customers behaving unexpectedly. Reading it makes you receptive; doing it teaches you.

## Application
- Before any customer conversations, write your vision down: the problem, who has it, why you believe your solution matters.
- List every implicit assumption behind the product as a testable hypothesis (customers, pain, willingness to pay, channel), using the "why? → why?" deconstruction.
- Get out of the building and test each assumption against a scale that could actually validate it — dozens to thousands, not five — and let the findings sharpen (or pivot) the vision, not dictate a feature list.
- Treat disconfirming evidence as the point: you're looking to catch yourself hallucinating early, cheaply.
