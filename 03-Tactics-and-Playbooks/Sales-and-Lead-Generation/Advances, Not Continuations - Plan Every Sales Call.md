# Advances, Not Continuations - Plan Every Sales Call

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[03-Tactics-and-Playbooks/Tactics and Playbooks Index|Tactics and Playbooks]] → [[03-Tactics-and-Playbooks/Sales-and-Lead-Generation/Sales and Lead Generation Map|Sales and Lead Generation]]

## One-line summary
Define success for each complex-sales call as a realistic buyer-backed action that advances the decision, then plan the questions, fallback outcome, and review needed to earn it.

## Context
Most calls in a long sales cycle produce neither an order nor a clear rejection. Without a stricter outcome definition, pleasant meetings, requests to “keep in touch,” and seller-only tasks can look like progress while the opportunity remains stationary.

## Key insights
- **An Advance changes the decision state.** The buyer agrees to an action that moves the purchase forward, such as involving a stakeholder, sharing criteria, testing, or reviewing a draft.
- **A Continuation preserves contact without progress.** Compliments, vague interest, another unstructured meeting, or a seller sending a proposal alone are not successful outcomes.
- **Buyer action is stronger evidence than seller action.** Mutual commitment demonstrates that the opportunity is real and helps prevent proposal-writing as unpaid speculation.
- **Call planning is the execution layer of account strategy.** Elegant account plans fail when individual calls have no explicit objective or question plan.
- **Plan what to ask, not what to tell.** The questions create understanding and need development; a presentation plan encourages premature advocacy.
- **Skill requires feedback loops.** Recording, classifying, and reviewing actual calls reveals telling habits and missed questions that memory hides.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
Before each call:

1. Brainstorm several possible Advances.
2. Choose the highest realistic Advance as the primary objective.
3. Choose a smaller but still meaningful fallback Advance.
4. Plan problem, implication, and need-payoff areas; add only essential situation questions.
5. Pair each intended Benefit with the Explicit Need that must first be developed.

Afterward, review whether the Advance occurred, what worked, what was missed, and the pattern of questions versus features, advantages, and benefits.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Reject objectives such as “build rapport,” “learn more,” or “show the product” unless they include a buyer action that changes the decision process.
- Make a proposal request conditional on buyer participation—for example, agreement on selection criteria or review of a draft.
- Brainstorm more Advances than you expect to use; flexibility helps when the primary objective proves too ambitious.
- Put the intended Advance at the top of the call plan so every question serves the desired movement.
- With permission, record selected calls and count Situation, Problem, Implication, and Need-payoff questions as well as feature and benefit statements.
- Use a plan–do–review coaching cycle with a peer or mentor; work on one behavior at a time and test it in real calls.
- Recheck periodically because even experts drift back toward telling, excessive situation questions, and early solutions.

## Notable examples
Rackham describes discovering his own regression after months on the speaking circuit: a filmed sales call contained only seven questions, six of them low-value Situation Questions. Reviewing the recording exposed a telling habit his self-image had concealed.
