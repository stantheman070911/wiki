# The Problem Identification Chart - Problems, Impacts, and Root Causes

## One-line summary
Before contacting buyers, map every problem the offering can solve to its possible business impacts and root causes so prospecting, qualification, discovery, and demos stay problem-centric.

## Context
Unprepared sellers default to generic questions and product claims. A Problem Identification Chart (PIC) turns product knowledge into a diagnostic map: it anticipates where value may exist without pretending the seller already knows a particular buyer's situation.

## Key insights
- **Prepare hypotheses, not conclusions.** The chart tells the seller where to investigate; the buyer's evidence determines what is true.
- **Start from customer problems.** Features belong only after the problems they can resolve are defined.
- **Impact converts relevance into priority.** The same problem may be trivial for one account and existential for another.
- **Root-cause knowledge builds credibility.** A seller who can explain why recurring problems arise is more useful than one who merely recognizes symptoms.
- **One map can drive the whole revenue motion.** The PIC informs the ideal customer profile, outreach message, discovery route, qualification, demo selection, and objection handling.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
Build three linked columns:

1. **Problems:** measurable undesirable business outcomes the offering can help change.
2. **Impacts:** possible financial, operational, strategic, or human consequences of each problem.
3. **Root causes:** the finite set of processes, systems, structures, capabilities, or behaviors that produce each problem.

For prospecting, add account evidence and rank targets: A = many problems/high impact, B = fewer problems/high impact, C = many problems/low impact, D = few problems/low impact.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Complete the sentence, “The reason this offering exists is to…” without naming a feature.
- Interview delivery, support, product, and experienced sellers to collect recurring customer problems and causal patterns.
- Rewrite feature-shaped items (“no automation”) as buyer outcomes (“manual reconciliation delays month-end close”).
- For each problem, list multiple plausible impacts, then identify observable signals that an account may be experiencing them.
- Use the chart to select a problem hypothesis for outreach, not to claim certainty before speaking with the buyer.
- During discovery, mark hypotheses confirmed, disproved, or still unknown and add new evidence-backed patterns to the chart.
- Build the demo map only from confirmed problems, ordered by impact; omit everything else.

## Notable examples
For sales consulting, a symptom such as inconsistent execution can connect to missed quota, unreliable forecasts, and excessive acquisition cost, with possible causes in process, coaching, structure, skills, or management. Those links give the seller precise areas to investigate without assuming which cause applies.
