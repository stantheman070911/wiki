# The Launch Sequence - Build in Public, Daily Cadence, Post-Launch Loop

## One-line summary
Most launches flop because the creator treats a launch as a single post; a launch that both grows *and* monetizes without sounding salesy is a four-part cyclical process — content strategy → build-in-public → a multi-touch daily launch cadence → a post-launch positioning loop — that keeps the offer omnipresent from many angles.

## Context
For creators selling a digital product, course, or event who post "it's live," expect their whole audience to buy, and are disappointed. This is a repeatable launch operating procedure that generated ~$10M across launches and sold out a 300-person in-person event.

## Key insights
- **A launch is a dynamic process, not a singular event.** 20,000 viewers ≠ 20,000 buyers; the goal is to grow and monetize *simultaneously*, which requires a full-cycle system, not one launch post.
- **Build-in-public is free selling pressure-off.** Because there's no ask, you must maximize it: the *rule of 7* (people need ~7 exposures before buying) means the product must be in front of the audience repeatedly, showing effort and iteration.
- **Feedback requests do double duty:** asking about features, price, cover, launch date makes the audience feel ownership *and* generates DMs of interest you can screenshot as **pre-launch social proof** — solving the "no testimonials yet" problem. Fifteen "I want this" DMs read as overwhelming demand.
- **Keep the offer omnipresent but vary the angle** so it never feels salesy — announce, then react to the demand, then react to the launch. Each new touch re-peaks landing-page traffic before it wanes.
- **Urgency must be rooted in truth** — early-bird discount, limited spots/inventory, limited codes/time — never fabricated.
- **Match conviction to price.** Low-ticket sells on imagery/story views with light conviction; high-ticket needs passive documentation (results, DMs, spots-remaining) sustained beyond the launch moment.
- **Always shout out buyers** — it makes others want into the experience.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
**The four-step launch cycle:**
1. **Content strategy / growth** — your Creator Vision (what/who/why); if productizing a skill, run all four content missions on it: *attract, nurture, position, convert*.
2. **Build in public (pre-launch)** — ≥1×/week post one of: day-in-the-life, money invested, research, progress, or mistakes; ask for feedback; capture interest DMs.
3. **The launch sequence (a repeatable day, run ~4 days):**
   - 9:00 — feed carousel announcing the launch + an urgency play
   - +10 min — story announcing the launch, same urgency
   - 11:00 — email #1 (from your lead magnets), same urgency, as page traffic wanes
   - 12:00 — story #2: DMs / demand highlight (softer angle)
   - 15:00 — story #3: your reaction to the launch (offer still omnipresent, new angle)
   - 21:00 — reel announcing the launch, same urgency (reels get more reach than carousels)
   Each touch re-peaks traffic; the softer stories dip, buying room for the next day's urgency.
4. **Post-launch loop** — collect reviews, publicly document selling out, organize story highlights as positioning for the *next* launch, then return to regular posting. The cycle repeats.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Before launch week, pre-write ~4 days of the cadence above and stage your urgency plays (spots/inventory counts you can truthfully decrement: "170 spots left").
- During build-in-public, screenshot every "I want this" DM into a demand folder for later story posts.
- Segment as you go: story-warm audience sees it first, then feed, then email — same urgency across all three.
- After a sellout, post it — many creators sell out silently and waste the positioning.

- Jun Yuh's launch plan across products from his first digital product to the multi-million "Creator College," ~$10M total; used to sell out a 300-ticket Creator Life event in NYC.
