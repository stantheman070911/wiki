# The Next Yes - Decision Criteria, Buying Process, and Mutual Effort

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[03-Tactics-and-Playbooks/Tactics and Playbooks Index|Tactics and Playbooks]] → [[03-Tactics-and-Playbooks/Sales-and-Lead-Generation/Sales and Lead Generation Map|Sales and Lead Generation]]

## One-line summary
Move a deal through a staircase of explicit buyer commitments by aligning decision criteria with desired outcomes, mapping every buying step, and requiring mutual effort toward the next yes.

## Context
A seller's activity does not prove a deal is advancing. Predictability comes from observable buyer commitments, a complete map of the decision process, and evidence that both sides are investing more as the purchase approaches.

## Key insights
- **A sale is many small sales.** Meetings, data access, stakeholder introductions, trials, approvals, and documents are all commitments that make the final decision possible.
- **Decision criteria must serve the future state.** Buyers can overvalue familiar or attractive features that do not produce the outcome they said matters.
- **The buying process is distinct from the criteria.** Criteria explain how options will be judged; the process identifies who acts, in what sequence, under which rules, and on what timeline.
- **Mutual effort is evidence of momentum.** If only the seller works harder while the buyer contributes nothing, the deal is moving upward in effort but not forward in commitment.
- **The next yes must be concrete.** “Follow up later” is not a next step; a named person taking a dated action is.
- **Return on sales effort matters.** A trusted adviser can say no to requests that consume resources without reciprocal information or progress.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
For every active opportunity, maintain three linked maps:

1. **Decision criteria:** what the buyer says will determine the best solution, checked against the desired outcome.
2. **Buying process:** all stakeholders, approvals, legal or technical reviews, procurement steps, and dependencies.
3. **Next yes:** the immediate buyer action that advances the process, why it matters, who owns it, and when it will happen.

Pipeline health is the accumulation of verified yeses, not the seller's confidence or volume of activity.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Ask, “How will you decide which solution is best?” and challenge any criterion that conflicts with the stated outcome.
- Ask the buyer to walk through the purchase from evaluation to signature and implementation; identify every person who can block or enable it.
- End each interaction with one explicit next commitment, owner, and date, then record it in the CRM.
- Use “I'm confused—you said…” to surface a discrepancy between the buyer's goals and current actions without forcing a defensive debate.
- When a deal stalls or goes dark, restate the agreed problem, future state, and cost of delay; ask what changed rather than “checking in.”
- Test whether buyer effort rises alongside seller effort. If information, access, or action stays one-sided, return to discovery or disqualify.
- In pipeline reviews, demand evidence for the gap, motivation, criteria, process, next yes, and close date; replace stories with specific data.

## Notable examples
Keenan's ski-school platform won enthusiasm from school leaders, then repeatedly encountered IT and finally HR. The unrecognized HR approval—and its labor-law risk—turned an attractive future state into a negative gap. Missing one required yes made the deal structurally impossible, regardless of product appeal.
