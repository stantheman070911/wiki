# Do Things That Don't Scale - Recruit, Delight, and Learn by Hand

## One-line summary
Early growth is usually hand-cranked: founders manually recruit users, deliver an unusually good experience, and use that close contact to discover what scalable product should eventually replace the manual work.

## Context
Founders often reject small, laborious actions because they cannot imagine a large company doing them. That applies the constraints of the mature company to the larval one, precisely when smallness is an advantage.

## Key insights
- **Startups take off because founders push them.** A good product rarely produces automatic distribution from day one.
- **Tiny numbers can compound.** Ten additional users on a base of 100 looks insignificant, but sustained 10% weekly growth becomes roughly 14,000 users after one year.
- **Manual work is research.** Founder-led sales, onboarding, support, and even doing part of the user's job reveal objections, vocabulary, and missing product behavior.
- **Over-service is safe early.** The likely failure is not having too many delighted users; it is never finding enough users who care.
- **Automate after the pattern is known.** Premature automation freezes guesses into software. Repeated manual delivery exposes which steps are stable and worth encoding.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
The unscalable-growth sequence is: **recruit one user → install or onboard immediately → personally produce the desired outcome → record friction and delight → repeat with similar users → standardize only the repeated pattern**.

## Tactics / how to apply
1. Build a list of 20 plausible early users and contact them individually.
2. Replace “I'll send a link” with live setup while interest is present.
3. Deliver the first outcome with the user, even if it requires concierge work behind the scenes.
4. Track weekly growth and the specific action that produced each new active user.
5. Automate a step only after several users need essentially the same thing.

## Notable examples
Stripe's founders performed “Collison installations” by taking a willing user's laptop and integrating Stripe immediately. Airbnb's founders traveled to New York, recruited hosts door to door, and photographed listings themselves.
