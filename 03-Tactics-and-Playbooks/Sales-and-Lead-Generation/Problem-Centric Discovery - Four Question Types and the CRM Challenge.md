# Problem-Centric Discovery - Four Question Types and the CRM Challenge

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[03-Tactics-and-Playbooks/Tactics and Playbooks Index|Tactics and Playbooks]] → [[03-Tactics-and-Playbooks/Sales-and-Lead-Generation/Sales and Lead Generation Map|Sales and Lead Generation]]

## One-line summary
Run discovery as an evidence-seeking conversation that uses probing, process, provoking, and validating questions to diagnose and qualify the buyer before any prescription or demo.

## Context
Budget, authority, need, and timing reveal what is convenient for the seller, but they do not establish whether a worthwhile problem exists. Problem-centric discovery qualifies through the buyer's situation, willingness to change, and willingness to collaborate.

## Key insights
- **The close happens at the beginning.** A later closing technique cannot repair a shallow or incorrect diagnosis.
- **Ask for information, not for the sake of asking questions.** There is no universal script because each answer should determine the next useful question.
- **Listen more than you talk.** The buyer must supply the facts, meanings, and evidence; the seller supplies structure, curiosity, and relevant expertise.
- **Specificity creates usable evidence.** “We need to grow faster” becomes actionable only when current growth, target growth, deadline, and consequences are known.
- **Discovery qualifies through four buyer commitments.** The prospect has a problem the seller can fix, agrees it exists, wants to fix it, and will work with the seller on the journey.
- **Discovery continues as stakeholders change.** Never assume two buyers in the same company share the same current state, future state, or motivation.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
Use four kinds of questions fluidly:

1. **Probing:** obtain facts, definitions, amounts, and context.
2. **Process:** reconstruct how work actually moves step by step.
3. **Provoking:** help the buyer examine implications, causes, and possibilities not yet considered.
4. **Validating:** paraphrase the interpretation and ask whether it is accurate.

Document current state, problems, impact, root cause, future state, desired outcomes, and intrinsic motivation. The **CRM Challenge** passes when a colleague can identify the opportunity from those notes alone, without account names or other identifying data.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Separate discovery and demo into different sessions; reserve preparation time between them.
- Begin with broad command prompts such as “Tell me…,” “Explain…,” and “Walk me through…,” then pursue the operational detail.
- Stop at every vague answer and request a quantity, comparison, time frame, or concrete example.
- Follow technical problems into business impact, then ask what causes them and how people feel about them.
- Use tone deliberately, especially when a root cause touches the buyer's own decisions, status, or team.
- Validate before changing topics: summarize what you heard and give the buyer a chance to correct it.
- Record evidence in the CRM immediately and revisit discovery whenever a new stakeholder enters.

## Notable examples
When one sales director invited a peer into a proposal, Keenan assumed the two leaders shared the same situation. The second director's problems and desired future were different, the prepared solution did not resonate, and the lost credibility stalled the entire deal—showing why stakeholder-specific rediscovery matters.
