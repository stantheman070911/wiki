# Gap Prospecting - Intrigue, Offer, Ask, and Cadence

## One-line summary
Earn the first yes with a problem-centric message that creates legitimate intrigue, offers more value than the requested attention costs, and develops through a varied multichannel cadence.

## Context
Prospects ignore outreach because attention is scarce and most messages repeat a self-centered pattern. Effective prospecting interrupts that pattern with relevant evidence about a plausible problem and a small, worthwhile invitation to learn more.

## Key insights
- **Prospecting is the first sale.** Reading, replying, or accepting a meeting is a commitment that must be earned, not an administrative step before selling begins.
- **The message matters more than the medium.** Phone, email, social, video, or mail works when it fits the buyer and carries relevant value.
- **Intrigue breaks expected patterns.** Surprise, mystery, or a knowledge gap can create attention, but only when connected to a real business issue rather than a gimmick.
- **The offer must exceed the ask.** The prospect evaluates whether the insight or opportunity is worth the time, effort, and risk requested.
- **Lead with a problem hypothesis, not a product introduction.** The buyer does not owe the seller time to explain who the company is.
- **Persistence needs new value.** Repeating the same request is harassment; varying the problem, impact, evidence, offer, and channel builds a legitimate cadence.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
Construct every outreach around four elements:

1. **Intrigue:** a surprising, credible observation, mystery, or knowledge gap.
2. **Problem relevance:** a plausible issue and its business impact for this buyer.
3. **Offer:** useful insight, diagnosis, evidence, or a path to improvement.
4. **Ask:** the smallest clear commitment appropriate to the value offered.

Use the test `Offer - Ask = Value`; if the likely buyer perceives a negative result, reduce the ask or improve the offer.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Use the [[The Problem Identification Chart - Problems, Impacts, and Root Causes|Problem Identification Chart]] to identify high-impact target accounts and a defensible problem hypothesis.
- Open with the buyer's world and a relevant consequence; remove company history, awards, and generic feature claims.
- Create a knowledge gap with evidence the buyer can verify, not clickbait or manufactured fear.
- State exactly what the buyer will gain from the interaction and request a modest, specific next step.
- Score drafts for relevance, subject line, intrigue, offer strength, ask cost, and brevity before sending.
- Use multiple buyer-appropriate channels across roughly eight to twelve touches, changing the insight or impact each time.
- Do not silently say no for the buyer; persist with value until the buyer declines, while respecting explicit boundaries.

## Notable examples
An expense-software email asking for fifteen minutes to introduce a product offered no reason to respond. Reframing it around the possibility that poor expense processes were losing the buyer at least $10,000 created a concrete knowledge gap and made the same time request potentially worthwhile.
