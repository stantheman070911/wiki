# The StoryBrand Conversion Path - Problem, Plan, and Calls to Action

## One-line summary
Move a prospect from recognition to action by naming all three levels of their problem, establishing trust through empathy and competence, reducing risk with a short plan, and asking clearly for the next commitment.

## Context
A prospect can understand an offer and still hesitate because the problem feels weak, the provider feels untrustworthy, adoption feels complicated, or the requested action is vague. This path addresses those conversion barriers in sequence.

## Key insights
- **External problems attract; internal problems motivate.** The visible issue explains what the product fixes, but the frustration, doubt, embarrassment, or anxiety often explains why the customer buys now.
- **A philosophical layer adds meaning.** A concise “ought” or “shouldn’t” statement can place the purchase inside a larger idea of fairness, identity, or justice without manufacturing a cause.
- **Trust needs empathy and competence.** “We understand” is incomplete without evidence that the guide can deliver; competence is incomplete if the customer does not feel seen.
- **Plans remove two different barriers.** A process plan clarifies the path; an agreement plan reduces fears through promises, standards, guarantees, or shared values.
- **Directness signals belief.** A weak call to action transfers interpretive work to the customer. A clear yes-or-no request communicates confidence that the solution is appropriate.
- **Not-ready is not the same as no.** A transitional call to action preserves and deepens the relationship until the problem becomes urgent enough for a purchase.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
1. **Name the villain:** identify one real root source of the conflict.
2. **Map three problems:** state the external obstacle, the internal feeling it causes, and the philosophical reason it should be resolved.
3. **Become the guide:** express empathy, then show competence using brief testimonials, numbers, awards, press, or relevant client logos.
4. **Lay stepping stones:** give three to six process steps, or a compact agreement that counters the customer’s main fears.
5. **Offer two actions:** repeat one direct call to buy or schedule; pair it with one useful, lower-risk transitional action.
6. **Clarify the stakes:** briefly show the credible cost of delay and the specific success that follows action.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Ask customers what the external problem makes them feel; use their language rather than inventing emotional copy in a conference room.
- Convert the buying process into three visible phases even if delivery contains dozens of internal steps.
- List every common buying fear, then turn the strongest few into an agreement plan or guarantee.
- Replace “learn more” and “if interested, let me know” with a concrete action such as “Schedule a call” or “Place your order.”
- Build a transitional asset—guide, webinar, sample, trial, or assessment—that solves a small problem and earns contact permission.
- Use moderate, truthful loss framing; pair every warning with a credible path back to stability.

## Notable examples
CarMax sells cars while messaging against the internal fear of haggling and being deceived; its no-haggle and quality agreements reduce that fear. A mattress retailer increased sales by stating a three-step process—choose, receive delivery, and optionally remove the old mattress—immediately before asking for the order.
