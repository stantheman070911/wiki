# Commercial Teaching - Insight That Leads to Unique Strengths

## One-line summary
Teach customers an economically important truth they have overlooked, but design the insight so acting on it naturally favors a capability you can deliver better than competitors.

## Context
Customers often perceive less difference among competing products, brands, and service levels than suppliers do. Insight can differentiate the sales experience, but teaching that does not lead back to a unique supplier strength becomes free consulting that a competitor may monetize.

## Key insights
- **Customers value learning over repeated discovery.** In complex categories, they need help determining what they should care about, not another supplier asking them to explain familiar problems.
- **Rapport is not a reframe.** “You understand me” builds connection; “I had not thought about it that way” signals that the conversation created new value.
- **The insight must be commercial.** It should benefit the customer and make the supplier’s distinctive capability more valuable at the same time.
- **Action requires an economic case.** Quantify the cost of continuing the current behavior or the return from pursuing the newly revealed opportunity before calculating ROI on a product.
- **Scale comes from need-based segmentation.** A small library of strong insights mapped to shared behaviors or needs is more repeatable than asking every rep to invent custom consulting.
- **Insight is an organizational capability.** Marketing and sales operations generate, validate, package, and refresh teaching content; reps deliver and adapt it.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
Commercial Teaching follows four rules:

1. **Lead to unique strengths:** the desirable new behavior should favor something you truly outperform on.
2. **Challenge assumptions:** reframe how the customer understands their business, not just how they view your product.
3. **Catalyze action:** demonstrate the cost of inaction or value of pursuing the reframe.
4. **Scale across customers:** apply the insight to a needs-based segment using repeatable messages and diagnostic questions.

The design question is: **What is costing our customers more than they realize that we are uniquely able to help them fix?**

## Tactics / how to apply
- Answer “Why should customers buy from us instead of anyone else?” without using generic claims such as innovative, customer-focused, or solutions-oriented.
- Identify a valuable capability customers currently underappreciate; ask what mistaken belief prevents them from valuing it.
- Build evidence that shows the overlooked economic consequence of that belief.
- Validate that the insight creates a credible path to action and that your offering can actually deliver the promised change.
- Segment by common need or behavior and equip reps with two or three diagnostic questions to choose the right insight.
- Continuously refresh the message library as customer conditions and competition change.

## Notable examples
Grainger shifted customers from tracking what MRO products they bought to examining how they bought them. It showed that unplanned purchases—about 40 percent of MRO spend—carried large hidden process and inventory costs, then connected the solution to Grainger’s distinctive breadth, availability, and delivery scale.
