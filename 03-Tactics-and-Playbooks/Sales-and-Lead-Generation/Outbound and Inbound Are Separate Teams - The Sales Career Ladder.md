# Outbound and Inbound Are Separate Teams - The Sales Career Ladder

## One-line summary
Giving the same reps both outbound (self-generated, cold) and inbound (company-provided, warm) leads at once kills hustle on the harder, more valuable skill — outbound and inbound should be separate roles, with inbound treated as an earned promotion with its own comp structure.

## Principle
Outbound selling — prospecting, cold outreach, door-knocking — is a harder skill than closing inbound leads a company hands you, and it teaches resilience, objection handling, and self-sufficiency that inbound-only reps never develop. When a rep is given both lead types simultaneously, they rationally gravitate to the easier inbound leads and let outbound effort collapse, because the marginal reward for prospecting drops the moment an easier path to commission exists. Treating inbound access as a reward that must be earned through outbound performance preserves the incentive to prospect and creates a legible career ladder.

## Scenario
Use this when a sales team blends self-generated and company-provided leads on the same reps and notices outbound activity (calls, doors, cold outreach) declining, or when designing a sales org from scratch and deciding how to route lead types.

## Diagnosis
- Reps who once hustled on outbound activity taper off once inbound leads start flowing to them.
- Outbound metrics (dials, doors knocked, cold appointments set) decline as inbound lead volume rises, even without a formal policy change.
- There's no explicit rule about who gets inbound leads or how that access is earned.
- Compensation is structured the same way regardless of lead source, even though inbound leads convert at a much higher rate with far less effort.
- New reps are given a mix of both lead types from day one rather than starting on outbound.

## Why It Happens
People allocate effort toward the path of least resistance to the same reward. If a closed deal pays the same commission whether it came from a cold door-knock or a warm inbound call the company generated, a rational rep will always prefer inbound — so any inbound access without a performance gate quietly cannibalizes outbound effort. This isn't a character problem; it's an incentive design problem: the system, not the individual, made prospecting less attractive the moment an easier path to the same paycheck appeared.

## Recommended Action
1. Separate outbound and inbound into distinct roles or distinct pools of reps rather than blending lead types on the same person by default.
2. Treat inbound-lead access as a promotion earned through sustained outbound performance (a quota, tenure, or ranking threshold), not a default benefit.
3. Structure compensation so outbound effort is still worth pursuing even after a rep gains inbound access — e.g., a higher commission rate or bonus specifically for self-generated deals.
4. Use the outbound role as the training ground and filter: it teaches resilience and selects for reps who can handle rejection, before they're trusted with higher-value inbound leads.
5. Make the ladder explicit and visible (outbound rep → senior outbound → inbound-eligible → inbound closer) so reps see prospecting as the path up, not a task to escape.

## Example from the AMA
A door-to-door roofing sales organization saw its outbound door-knocking hustle collapse once reps also started receiving inbound leads — the easier, higher-converting inbound work crowded out the harder outbound prospecting on the same reps. Hormozi's fix was structural: split outbound and inbound into separate functions, and make inbound-lead access something reps earn through outbound performance rather than something handed out alongside it, preserving the incentive to keep prospecting.

## Caveats / Exceptions
- Very small teams may not have the headcount to fully separate roles; in that case, gate inbound access by a clear, measured threshold rather than splitting people, to preserve the incentive effect.
- This applies to comparable-effort-reward lead types; if inbound leads convert so rarely that they aren't actually easier, the crowding-out effect may not occur.
- Some organizations deliberately keep top performers on a blend once they're proven, treating the blended role itself as the top rung of the ladder — the key is that it's earned, not default.
