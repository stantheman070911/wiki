# Selling Upmarket - Contextual Adjacency and Land-and-Expand

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[03-Tactics-and-Playbooks/Tactics and Playbooks Index|Tactics and Playbooks]] → [[03-Tactics-and-Playbooks/Sales-and-Lead-Generation/Sales and Lead Generation Map|Sales and Lead Generation]]

## One-line summary
Win high-value clients by making the value legible, arriving with trusted contextual signals, starting with a low-friction proof project, documenting the result, and expanding only after trust is earned.

## Context
The same service can be worth radically different amounts to different customers. Upmarket buyers have larger and more complex problems, but they also evaluate context, trust, coordination, and downside more carefully than a mass-market buyer.

## Key insights
- **A narrow affluent customer can transform economics.** Specialization raises relevance, proof density, and deal size simultaneously.
- **The pitch must respect time.** Ask permission, then use Name–Same–Fame–Pain–Aim–Game to establish relevance in under a minute.
- **Context speaks before the pitch.** Mutual contacts, respected institutions, locations, brands, books, advisers, and shared suppliers act as credibility markers.
- **Context is audience-specific.** A signal that implies performance and seriousness in one circle can look flashy or untrustworthy in another.
- **Big engagements are earned through small evidence.** Education, research, or a short sprint creates proof and working chemistry without demanding an immediate large commitment.
- **Written reviews enable decisions.** Wealthy or enterprise buyers often decide with assistants, advisers, spouses, or executives; a visual document lets the result travel through that network.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
Use **Land and Expand**:

1. **Land:** offer valuable research, education, or a tightly bounded sprint with minimal friction.
2. **Review:** present what was discovered, what changed, and what should happen next in a concise visual document.
3. **Expand:** ask how the buyer prefers to structure the next engagement, while retaining a prepared fallback proposal.
4. **Review again:** document the paid result and the larger opportunities now visible.
5. **Double down or part well:** name the capacity constraint and ask whether to expand materially or conclude; either answer protects focus.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Define the high-value segment by problem scale and ability to benefit, not wealth alone.
- Research mutual contacts, institutions, interests, and suppliers before meeting.
- Choose a setting and materials consistent with the outcome and buyer culture.
- Make the first proof project genuinely useful and tightly scoped; do not disguise a long sales pitch as free work.
- Produce a shareable one-page review after every meaningful phase.
- Ask satisfied clients which larger problems or relevant peers should now be considered.

## Notable examples
A property-sourcing adviser repositioned exclusively for senior leaders at major accounting firms, replaced generic testimonials with segment-specific proof, and required a multi-year acquisition journey. Relevance and commitment increased together.
