# Distribution Is Destiny - The Sales Channel Power Law

## One-line summary
"If you've invented something new but haven't invented an effective way to sell it, you have a bad business, no matter how good the product" — distribution follows its own power law, so the right move is to find the one channel that fits your price point and nail it, not spread thin across several.

## Context
Engineers and founders systematically underrate sales and distribution because good sales is designed to be invisible (every distribution job has a euphemistic title — "account executive," "business development," "investment banker," "politician" — precisely so nobody feels sold to). Thiel argues this blind spot is one of the most common, correctable causes of startup failure: poor sales, not a bad product, is the most common reason a business gets zero distribution channels working.

## Key insights
- **The two metrics that bound every distribution decision.** Customer Lifetime Value (CLV) — total net profit earned, on average, over a customer relationship — must exceed Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC) — what it costs, on average, to acquire that customer. The higher the price point, the more it makes sense to spend acquiring a customer, which is what determines which distribution method even makes sense for a given product.
- **The distribution continuum runs from complex sales to viral.** *Complex sales* (7-figure+ deals): every detail needs personal, executive-level attention, deals might happen once a year or two, and the best version has no separate "salespeople" at all — Palantir's CEO Alex Karp spends 25 days a month meeting clients himself; SpaceX's Elon Musk personally worked NASA's billion-dollar contracts. Growth in this regime is 50–100% year-over-year, built by expanding gradually from reference customers, not overnight 10x jumps. *Personal sales* ($10K–$100K deals): a modestly-sized sales team, starting with the users who have the most acute pain, expanding relationship by relationship (Box started with the Stanford Sleep Clinic before Stanford-wide adoption). *Distribution doldrums*: a dead zone (~$1,000 price point) where advertising is too broad/inefficient and personal sales is too expensive per unit — this is why many useful tools never reach small and medium businesses; the bottleneck is distribution, not product quality or business owner backwardness. *Marketing and advertising*: works for low-priced, mass-appeal products with no viral mechanism (Warby Parker's TV ads, because at ~$100/pair CLV doesn't justify personal sales but is high enough to justify "the biggest megaphone you can find"). *Viral marketing*: the core product function itself invites more users (PayPal's referral bonus, Facebook's sharing) — if each new user brings more than one additional user, growth compounds exponentially, and the ideal viral loop is as short-cycle as possible.
- **One channel beats several half-working ones—but finding it requires a broad search.** The "kitchen sink" approach — a few salespeople, some ads, a bolted-on viral feature — is the default instinct and it doesn't work. *Traction* complements this power-law claim with a discovery method: consider all nineteen channel families, cheaply test the most plausible few, and only then concentrate on the one that can move the current goal.
- **Channel fit is phase-specific and decays.** A channel that wins the first customers may not support the next order of magnitude. Audiences saturate, click-through rates fall, economics change, and new platforms emerge; concentration should therefore be periodically renewed through testing rather than confused with permanent attachment to one tactic.
- **Distribution follows a power law of its own, so pick the highest-value segment first, not the largest.** PayPal skipped the large, low-velocity market of emigrants wiring money home and targeted a small, high-velocity niche instead: eBay's ~20,000 "PowerSellers," who transacted constantly and had no good existing payment tool. Dominating that segment first made PayPal impossible to dislodge on eBay or beyond — whoever locks the most valuable segment of a market with viral potential becomes the last mover in the whole market.
- **You're also always selling your company, not just your product.** To employees ("clamor to join" doesn't happen without deliberate recruiting), to investors ("banging down our door" doesn't happen without pitching), and to media (nerds who mistrust the press and try to ignore it are ignoring a channel that shapes what employees and investors find when they research you). Everybody sells — "if you don't see any salespeople, you're the salesperson."

## Framework / model (if applicable)
### Placing a product on the continuum

plot your price point against the CLV/CAC math to identify which of the five regimes (complex / personal / doldrums / marketing-advertising / viral) actually fits, rather than defaulting to whichever channel is culturally comfortable (engineers default to "build it and let it go viral," which only works for genuinely viral-mechanic products). If the honest placement lands in the "doldrums," that's a signal to either raise the price to justify personal sales or find a viral hook — not to force advertising or personal sales to work anyway.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Calculate CAC and CLV explicitly for your actual price point before choosing a distribution strategy — let the math pick the regime, not preference.
- If your instinct is "the product should sell itself," treat that as a warning sign: no real product sells itself; someone always believes it does either out of self-delusion or because they're quietly doing the selling.
- Pick the single highest-velocity, most underserved segment within your market to dominate first, rather than the largest segment — velocity and lack of existing solutions matter more than raw size.
- Once you find one working channel, resource it fully before diversifying into a second — the power law says one working channel usually beats three partially-working ones.
- When the working channel stops moving the needle, repeat a structured channel search instead of incrementally optimizing a saturated motion.

## Notable examples
- ZocDoc's product *is* a kind of distribution: each doctor sales rep signs adds value to consumers, which then makes the network more attractive to the next doctor — a self-reinforcing sales motion.
- PayPal's early referral program cost $20/customer but produced 7% daily growth (user base nearly doubling every 10 days) — a case where a paid-acquisition cost looked expensive in isolation but was justified by the CLV math and viral compounding together.
- Better Place's confusing purchase process (Israeli EV startup, $800M raised, ~1,000 cars sold before 2013 bankruptcy) — even excellent underlying technology fails without a legible way to buy it, tying to [[The Seven Questions Every Business Must Answer]]'s distribution question.
