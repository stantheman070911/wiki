# State the Facts - Substantiated Claims and Measurement as Intervention

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[03-Tactics-and-Playbooks/Tactics and Playbooks Index|Tactics and Playbooks]]

## One-line summary
The most compelling marketing claim is a tracked one: measure your results (the tracking itself improves them), then state them plainly as "X% of customers achieve [outcome] in [time] under [conditions]" — and use as few conditions as possible, because every qualifier you add drains the claim's power.

## Context
Hormozi's rule "state the facts and tell the truth" is why Gym Launch stayed the category leader — it simply had better, provable numbers than anyone else. The insight that makes it actionable: most businesses can't make compelling claims because they don't track results, often *because* they suspect the results are bad. Tracking is the unlock, and it improves outcomes before you've changed anything else.

## Key insights
- **If you don't track, you don't care.** Businesses avoid tracking partly to avoid confronting weak results — but the facts are the raw material of every believable claim, so the avoidance is self-defeating.
- **Measurement is itself an intervention.** Simply tracking a number — weight, close rate, ad performance — improves it, a scientifically observed effect, before you deliberately optimize anything. Start by measuring; improvement partly follows attention alone.
- **Claims beat adjectives.** A tracked stat converts an unsubstantiated claim ("we get great results") into a substantiated one ("the average gym adds \$100k+/yr in profit; the bottom 20% roughly break even"). Substantiated claims let you take emotion out of the sale: if the numbers make sense for the buyer, they buy; if not, they don't.
- **Honesty includes the downside.** Stating the bottom-20% result too ("basically broke even") *increases* credibility rather than weakening the pitch.

## The four variables of a claim
State a result along exactly four dimensions:
1. **Who / how many** — the percentage, median, or average of people (e.g. "the average customer…").
2. **Achieve what outcome** — the concrete result.
3. **In what time / after how many attempts** — the horizon.
4. **Under what conditions** — the constraints required.

**Fewer conditions = more compelling.** A claim buried in qualifiers ("gyms that spend \$10k/mo, have 10 employees, run two acquisition channels…") loses the listener before you finish. Hormozi's stated preference: a lower-result claim with *fewer* conditions beats a higher-result claim weighed down by many.

## Application
1. **Instrument the delivery.** Collect data at fixed checkpoints (day 1 / 30 / 60 / 90 / completion) so you have real numbers to cite.
2. **Improve until the data is compelling** — measurement-as-intervention starts this the moment you begin tracking.
3. **Present along the four variables**, stripping conditions to the minimum the claim can honestly carry; include the downside band to build trust.
4. Let the substantiated numbers do the persuading, so the sale becomes a rational "does this make sense for you?" rather than an emotional push.
