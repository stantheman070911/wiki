# Volume and Feedback - Sales as the Training Ground

## One-line summary
Skill = reps × improvement between reps; do enormous volume in a high-feedback environment (ideally high-volume sales), analyze your best and worst, and habituate away fear.

## Context
Hormozi observes that nearly every millionaire and billionaire he knows started in a high-volume, in-person sales environment (door-knocking, cold calling, memberships, insurance, solar) for years, unknown, having thousands of conversations with strangers. That volume is where transferable skills are forged.

## Key insights
- **Skill develops through volume and feedback loops:** number of repetitions × amount of improvement between them. Raw effort alone isn't enough — it's raw effort with an improvement loop.
- **Volume without analysis is wasted.** Posting 100 videos or making 100 calls teaches little unless you review the top 10 and bottom 10, extract what the winners share, and add one improvement to every future rep — building a checklist that becomes skill.
- **"Great — do it again."** Repetition drills the nuance until the action is automatic, freeing attention for the next skill. It's not learning to do it once; it's doing it so many times you don't think about it.
- **Sales skills generalize.** They transfer to dating, negotiating a car/house, and content — real-life skills cross domains (unlike school subjects that feel abstract).
- **Habituation, not affirmation, kills fear.** Exposure repeated enough makes the scary thing ordinary (the spider-room example). Public-speaking fear vanished for Hormozi because he'd shouted through fitness sessions on a box for years.
- **Consistency is the differentiator.** Bad salespeople have a couple good days; good ones get hot streaks; great ones never get cold — because "hot" is just a set of learnable behaviors (pauses, tonality, question order), not magic.
- **Insufficient volume masquerades as volatility.** If daily spend is below daily CAC, one sale every few days feels like feast-or-famine even when the economics are stable; if you reach out to 15 candidates, "no talent exists" may just mean the sample is tiny. Raise the sample size before rewriting the strategy.
- **Inexperience ≠ stupidity.** Early mistakes (like biting a banana with the peel on) are just missing reps; don't judge yourself for them — walk the minefield until people call you "so good," which really means you know what *not* to say.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Put yourself in the highest-volume feedback environment you can (in-person sales is ideal) and stay years, not months.
- After each batch of reps, compare top 10% vs. bottom 10%, isolate one repeatable factor, and apply it to every subsequent rep.
- Engineer your own energy/state for high-tempo work (Hormozi bounced on a small trampoline between sales appointments; positive tonality and light "giggles" raised show-up and close rates).
- Review "game footage" — your calls, content, ads — as your primary learning source; the biggest gains came from data, not advice.
- In paid acquisition, once unit economics work, push spend toward at least daily CAC and launch enough creative to learn; in recruiting or outreach, add volume before concluding the channel is broken.

## Notable examples
- Hormozi ran cheap Facebook ads in 2013 that generated 15–20 in-person consults/day plus phone lead-setting — a flood of reps that turned him from a novice ("she'll come back with her card") into a known closer.
- His self-review process today: look at the last 2,000 ads, study the top 50 vs. bottom 50, do more of what the winners share — repeated across ads, calls, content, and meetings.
- In the AMA, a fitness offer at roughly $3,500 CAC and $1,300/day spend was expected to feel lumpy; Hormozi wanted spend closer to daily CAC and creative volume closer to 100 ads/week.
- A luxury-home builder looking for one salesperson was told most business owners have an advertising problem for talent: use LinkedIn, outreach, and relocation, then add two zeros to the effort before deciding it cannot work.
