# Taking Control - Constructive Tension Across the Sale

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[03-Tactics-and-Playbooks/Tactics and Playbooks Index|Tactics and Playbooks]] → [[03-Tactics-and-Playbooks/Sales-and-Lead-Generation/Sales and Lead Generation Map|Sales and Lead Generation]]

## One-line summary
Take respectful control from qualification through negotiation by holding firm on value, making clear requests, and delaying premature closure long enough to uncover better paths forward.

## Context
Reps often confuse customer-centricity with acquiescence and assertiveness with aggression. That creates discounts, stalled deals, free consulting, and late-stage losses because the seller avoids difficult conversations until negotiation—or avoids them entirely.

## Key insights
- **Control spans the entire sale.** It begins with testing whether an opportunity is genuine, continues through challenging ideas and guiding the purchase process, and includes but is not limited to negotiation.
- **Assertiveness is not aggression.** An assertive rep pursues a clear end while respecting and adapting to the customer; an aggressive rep attacks or ignores the other party.
- **Clarity of direction beats quick closure.** Challengers tolerate ambiguity, silence, objections, and unresolved tension when premature agreement would destroy value.
- **Confidence comes from created value.** A rep can hold firm when they understand the economic problem revealed, implementation expertise offered, and resources invested.
- **Teach customers how to buy.** Complex-solution customers may not know the right stakeholders or sequence; leading and simplifying is more useful than asking them to invent the process.
- **Every concession should purchase something.** Price is one negotiable among scope, packaging, service, timing, risk, access, term, and commitment.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
Use the four-step **controlled negotiation road map**:

1. **Acknowledge and defer:** recognize the request, promise to address it, and gain permission to first understand the full need.
2. **Deepen and broaden:** uncover the outcome behind the demand and place all valued elements of the offer on the table.
3. **Explore and compare:** evaluate alternative trades by supplier cost and customer value.
4. **Concede according to plan:** trade low-cost, high-value elements before price; use meaningful then progressively smaller concessions.

Prepare power positions, needed information, likely objections, desired outcomes, and a concession matrix before the conversation.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Early in an RFP, request access to the stakeholders required to validate value. If access is refused, treat the opportunity as possible price verification and be willing to exit.
- Exchange costly analysis, references, pilots, or implementation expertise for evidence of reciprocal commitment.
- Replace vague next steps with a powerful request that names the action, owner, timing, and business consequence.
- When asked for a discount, ask what business outcome the reduction is meant to achieve before discussing a number.
- Rank the offer’s elements with the customer; remove or redesign low-value, high-cost elements before cutting price.
- Plan several moves ahead and never improvise the concession sequence under pressure.

## Notable examples
A paint supplier’s rep refused to concede a price increase unsupported by the economics. He documented productivity improvements, verified them with plant leadership, and secured a joint conversation with purchasing. The increase was accepted because the rep stood on demonstrated value without attacking the buyer.
