# Sales Systems as Intellectual Property - Process Above the Player

## One-line summary
A world-class sales team is built on a process no individual is allowed to override; when the system — script, training cadence, and standards — reliably turns a raw, high-work-ethic hire into a closer, that process is your company's real IP, and the leader's job is to enforce it, not to collect mercenaries.

## Principle
Sales volatility is usually a process-and-culture problem, not a talent problem. When the process goes above the player — no one is above the script — outcomes become consistent and the skill required of any single rep drops. The system that converts an unskilled but energetic person into a reliable closer in a couple of weeks is a trade secret; it's what makes the company valuable and hard to copy.

## Scenario
Use this for any high-volume inbound or outbound sales team with inconsistent close rates, "cowboy" reps who freelance off-script, and a founder who suspects the problem is hiring when it's actually the system and the leader.

## Diagnosis
- Close rates swing wildly month to month with the same lead quality.
- Scripts exist but are treated as "suggestions" or guidelines.
- Reps go off-script to close and there's no consequence, so everyone improvises.
- The team is a set of mercenaries — every man for himself, no shared culture.
- Staff churn roughly equals hiring, so headcount (and revenue) stays stuck.

## Why It Happens
When script adherence is optional, you get variability by design — one rep says something that works, another copies it, and consistency evaporates. That's a culture problem, and culture is a leader problem: if reps go off-script without consequence, the leader is reinforcing the wrong behavior and can't get the right people in and wrong people out. Mercenary teams also over-promise to close, creating downstream obligations you can't cash. And the constraint on scaling is often talent throughput — churn equal to hiring — which is a demand-gen-for-talent and process problem, not a "where do I find people" problem.

## Recommended Action
1. Put the process above the player: everyone reads the same script; if they won't say it word-for-word, they can't take the leads.
2. Run daily training and enforce memorization — reps must "breathe the script" before they touch calls.
3. Treat sales as a checklist: define the full set of activities (training cadence, scripting, daily/weekly management, career goals) and do all of them; a low, variable close rate means items are missing.
4. Recognize and protect the IP: the "box" that turns raw work ethic into a closer in ~14 days is your trade secret and the source of the company's value — the better it is, the less skill each hire needs.
5. Build a talent demand-gen funnel that sets the frame: send the script before the interview, require a 60-second video of them saying it, then a group call with a quick drill (coachable + prepared advance; the rest are out).
6. If the process still isn't adhered to, the leader is the problem — be willing to gut and rebuild the team to install the culture.
7. Structure the weekly training cadence as a rotation rather than one repeated topic: cover intro, discovery, offer presentation, and objections/looping as fixed weekly slots, plus one rotating slot for whatever hot topic is currently costing the team the most deals.
8. When describing desired rep behavior, use concrete, observable, checkable language, never abstract adjectives. "More energy" is unusable feedback because no one can verify it happened; "raise your voice by X, smile while talking, stand up during the pitch" can be trained, drilled, and audited.
9. Memorize by progressive deletion: read the script aloud, black out one more word each pass, and continue until the rep can deliver from a fully blacked-out page.
10. During role-play, interrupt at the moment of error, model the correction, and repeat the corrected line several times before continuing. End-of-call feedback arrives too late to reinforce the exact behavior.

## Example from the AMA
A real-estate wholesaler ($450k/month) had power months collapsing back to half, with the same lead quality. Hormozi diagnosed a pure sales-ops/culture problem: "if you want a consistent world-class sales team, the process has to always go above the player — no one is above the process." He argued 95%+ of sales teams don't understand sales culture and just hire, see who closes, and fire the rest, breeding mercenaries and volatility. His fix: enforce script adherence ("if they're not saying the script word for word, you cannot be on this team"), run daily training, and treat it as a checklist. He framed the conversion of a raw, work-ethic-only hire into a closer in 14 days as "the value your company has… the trade secret," and laid out the screening funnel (script before interview → 60-second video → group drill) that sets the frame: "either get in or get out, but we win."

A later Cash Cow business breakdown detailed the actual weekly training structure Hormozi used at Gym Launch, nicknamed the "Boiler Room": a five-part rotation covering intro, discovery, offer, objections-and-looping as standing weekly sessions, plus a fifth rotating slot dedicated to whatever specific objection or skill gap was currently costing the team the most money. The same breakdown emphasized giving feedback in concrete, observable terms rather than abstract adjectives like "bring more energy" — since a rep can't practice or be graded against a vague instruction, but can practice and be graded against a specific, checkable behavior.

AMA 3 makes the feedback loop more explicit: a world-class team role-plays every day, each rep reviews real game tape weekly, and managers correct a miss immediately rather than collecting comments for the end. The goal is not six charismatic stars; it is a process that reliably produces six consistent eights.

## Caveats / Exceptions
- "How, not who": the goal is a system so strong that less-skilled hires still produce consistent outcomes — recruiting high-caliber closers is not the fix for a missing process.
- Enforcing the frame will filter out people; winning isn't for everyone, and that's the point.
- Off-script improvisation that "closes" often sells things the company can't deliver — consistency protects fulfillment, not just conversion.
- Tactics come after the root: fix the process and leader first; if that fails, restructure the team.
