# The Bowling Alley Framework - Straight-Line Onboarding and Two Bumpers

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[03-Tactics-and-Playbooks/Tactics and Playbooks Index|Tactics and Playbooks]] → [[03-Tactics-and-Playbooks/Sales-and-Lead-Generation/Sales and Lead Generation Map|Sales and Lead Generation]]

## One-line summary
Onboarding should create the shortest path to the user's desired outcome, then use in-product and conversational bumpers only when they keep the user on that path.

## Context
New users commonly abandon products because onboarding asks them to configure advanced options, inspect features, or make too many decisions before receiving value. The Bowling Alley framework replaces a generic tour with one outcome-specific route and targeted recovery mechanisms.

## Key insights
- **The destination is a life outcome, not the end of a sales cycle.** The product earns conversion by helping the user reach the result they came for.
- **The straight line is intentionally incomplete.** First-run onboarding includes only the actions required for the initial valuable outcome; advanced configuration can wait.
- **Product bumpers guide action inside the application.** Welcome states, focused tours, progress bars, short checklists, tooltips, and empty states are useful only when they advance the outcome.
- **Conversational bumpers restore momentum outside the application.** Email, notifications, video, sales, or customer success should respond to what the user has and has not done.
- **Signals beat schedules.** A power user, an inactive signup, and a new customer should not receive the same day-three message.
- **Bumpers are training wheels.** The long-term goal is habitual, unprompted use; permanent rescue messaging often signals that the product path remains weak.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
Build the alley in three parts:

1. **Straight line:** Map every action from acquisition intent to the first meaningful outcome. Label actions green (required now), yellow (valuable later), or red (remove), then keep only the green path.
2. **Product bumper:** Add the smallest in-app aid needed at a known failure point; do not tour unrelated features.
3. **Conversational bumper:** Trigger a message or human touch from a meaningful signal such as signup, quick win, desired outcome, inactivity, trial expiry, upgrade, or cancellation reason.

Conversational tracks should advance in order: reach a quick win, reach the desired outcome, then convert a good-fit user.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Sign up for the product as a new user and capture every click, field, decision, integration, and wait between intent and value.
- Remove red steps entirely and delay yellow steps until after the quick win; challenge even conventional requirements such as email activation.
- Keep initial tours and checklists to roughly three to five required actions and use focus mode to reduce competing choices.
- Pre-fill some progress where honest and useful, and make each completed action visibly successful.
- Trigger usage guidance only when the corresponding step is incomplete; stop messages immediately when the user advances or upgrades.
- Send the sales touch after experienced value, framing it as help to deepen success rather than as an interruption before value.
- Use post-trial and cancellation responses to route users into the right recovery: extension, support, nurture, roadmap follow-up, or a clean exit.

## Notable examples
- Canva connects intent-specific search pages to matching templates, cutting time-to-value for jobs such as making a poster.
- Buffer's empty state directs a new user to connect a social account because nothing useful can happen before that step.
- Crazy Egg should send installation help only when the tracking script is missing; users who already installed it need the next outcome, not redundant instructions.
- Autopilot uses post-trial reasons to trigger different responses, including an extension for evaluators and a customer-success call when the product felt too complex.
