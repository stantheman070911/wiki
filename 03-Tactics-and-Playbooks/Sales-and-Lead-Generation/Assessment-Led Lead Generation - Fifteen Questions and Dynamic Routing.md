# Assessment-Led Lead Generation - Fifteen Questions and Dynamic Routing

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[03-Tactics-and-Playbooks/Tactics and Playbooks Index|Tactics and Playbooks]] → [[03-Tactics-and-Playbooks/Sales-and-Lead-Generation/Sales and Lead Generation Map|Sales and Lead Generation]]

## One-line summary
A useful assessment earns a lead by diagnosing a meaningful problem, benchmarking ten best practices, collecting five qualification signals, and routing each respondent to a next step matched to fit and readiness.

## Context
Generic lead forms collect little more than contact details and force sales teams to rediscover the customer's situation manually. An assessment gives the respondent immediate value while producing structured information for segmentation and follow-up.

## Key insights
- **The landing page sells the diagnosis.** A frustration or readiness hook, a three-part value proposition, credible authorship, and a low-friction call to action explain why the assessment is worth completing.
- **Best-practice questions create the score.** Ten questions test observable behaviors or conditions that strongly predict the desired result.
- **Qualification questions create commercial context.** Current situation, ninety-day outcome, obstacle, preferred solution, and an open response reveal fit, urgency, budget proxy, and hidden constraints.
- **Results must be dynamic.** A score alone is thin value; respondents need an interpretation, three tailored insights, and a next action appropriate to their situation.
- **Routing protects both parties.** High-fit leads can receive an individual meeting, medium-fit leads a group experience, and poor-fit respondents useful content without consuming sales time.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
Build four layers:

1. **Landing page:** hook → value proposition → credibility → call to action.
2. **Identity:** name and email required; phone optional unless operationally necessary.
3. **Fifteen questions:** ten scored best practices plus five qualification questions.
4. **Dynamic result:** overall reveal → three insights → segment-specific next step → contact and resource links.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Choose one narrow outcome and write ten practices a well-prepared customer should already follow.
- Avoid questions that merely flatter the seller's product; the score should remain useful even when the respondent never buys.
- Use answer options that map cleanly to meaningful customer segments.
- Treat preferred solution as a budget proxy, not a binding promise.
- Keep the open-ended final question; motivated prospects often reveal deadlines, prior exposure, or purchasing constraints there.
- Measure landing-page start rate, completion rate, segment distribution, appointment conversion, and downstream sales—not lead count alone.

## Notable examples
Priestley's model uses a sleep assessment to show the structure: test the environment, routine, and related best practices, then route someone seeking education differently from someone seeking fully managed help.
