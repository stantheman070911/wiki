# Tailoring for Consensus - Stakeholder Outcomes and Widespread Support

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[03-Tactics-and-Playbooks/Tactics and Playbooks Index|Tactics and Playbooks]] → [[03-Tactics-and-Playbooks/Sales-and-Lead-Generation/Sales and Lead Generation Map|Sales and Lead Generation]]

## One-line summary
Build consensus by translating one commercial insight into the distinct outcomes, metrics, and concerns of every stakeholder whose support the decision maker needs.

## Context
Large solution purchases increasingly require broad internal support because no senior buyer wants to carry the risk alone. Reaching the executive directly is therefore less powerful than enabling credible stakeholders across the organization to advocate for the change.

## Key insights
- **Decision makers buy from organizations; end users buy from people.** Senior buyers value widespread support and ease of purchase, while influencers reward professional reps who teach useful perspectives.
- **Stakeholders are customers in their own right.** Each one needs to see how the proposal advances the outcomes for which they are accountable.
- **The information flow reverses.** Instead of extracting intelligence from users to sharpen an executive pitch, give users insight they can carry upward to build advocacy.
- **Tailoring is economic, not cosmetic.** Changing job titles or industry examples is insufficient; the message must connect to role-specific value drivers and success metrics.
- **Role outcomes are finite and reusable.** What CIOs, manufacturing heads, or marketing leaders care about is predictable enough to map centrally and scale.
- **Consensus should be visible.** Documenting each stakeholder’s desired outcome, objection, response, and measurement turns vague goodwill into an auditable support plan.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
Tailor through four nested layers:

1. **Industry:** trends, regulation, competitive forces, and structural economics.
2. **Company:** current strategy, performance, position, and announced priorities.
3. **Role:** functional outcomes, decision criteria, metrics, and recurring concerns.
4. **Individual:** personal goals, motivations, preferences, and credibility risks.

For each stakeholder, capture **outcome → metric → objection → relevant capability → proof**.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Map all people affected by the solution before seeking the final decision; identify both formal authority and informal advocacy.
- Build functional-bias cards for recurring roles: what they monitor, fear, measure, and can change.
- Rephrase the same insight in each stakeholder’s language without altering its core commercial logic.
- Ask stakeholders to validate the outcome, measurement, and objection recorded for them; where appropriate, obtain explicit sign-off.
- Present the completed consensus map to the decision maker as evidence that implementation risk and internal alignment have been addressed.
- Do not use stakeholders merely as sources of intelligence; leave each interaction with insight they can use independently.

## Notable examples
Solae equipped reps with role cards and message-to-role maps so a technical soy-ingredient solution could be discussed differently with manufacturing, marketing, procurement, and executives. Its value-planning sheet documented the project goal, each stakeholder’s outcome, objections, and the capability that addressed them—sometimes with stakeholder sign-off.
