# Referrals - Engineering Word of Mouth Through Goodwill

## One-line summary
Referrals are the only lead source that scales exponentially instead of linearly, and you unlock them by manufacturing goodwill (value delivered minus price paid) and then treating the ask as an offer — because most businesses get few referrals for exactly two reasons: their product isn't as good as they think, and they never ask.

## Context
Every Core Four channel gives roughly linear returns — do more, get more. Word of mouth compounds: one customer brings two, two bring four. The gating equation is **referrals in vs. churn out**: if referral rate exceeds churn rate, the business grows with no other advertising; most small businesses stall because the two roughly cancel. This is the highest-leverage, lowest-cost, highest-quality lead source (referred customers buy more, pay upfront, and cost nothing).

## Framework / model
### Part 1 — Give more value (six goodwill levers, mapped to the value equation)
Goodwill = value − price. Raise it not by cutting price (temporary, loses money) but by delivering more:
1. **Call-outs — sell better customers.** Find what your lowest-churn customers have in common and target only those; better-fit buyers extract more value and refer more.
2. **Dream outcome — set better expectations.** *Lower* the promises slightly (you set expectations, so you can beat them); overdelivery against a modest promise creates referrals.
3. **Likelihood — get more people better results.** Find what your *best* customers did, then force every new customer to do it; make those actions the conditions of your guarantee.
4. **Time delay — make faster wins.** Deliver progress in smaller, more frequent increments; the first 48 hours set the lasting impression, so force early wins and always tell them the next touchpoint.
5. **Effort & sacrifice — keep making the product better.** Run a monthly loop: surface the most common problem → fix → test on a small group → roll out → next problem.
6. **Call to action — tell them what to buy next.** Satisfy the desire to buy again (more, or new) or they buy it elsewhere; more to buy = more to refer.

### Part 2 — Ask (treat the ask as an offer)
Three components: how you give the incentive, what you incentivize with, how you ask. Seven working combos:
- **One-sided** (pay your CAC to referrer *or* friend) · **two-sided** (split CAC to both — the Dropbox/PayPal model).
- **Ask at the moment of purchase** ("people who do this with someone get 3× the results — who else could do it with you?") — a live 3-way intro, not a name.
- **Referral as negotiation chip** (grant a discount in exchange for 3 intros — you ethically change the terms, not just the price).
- **Referral events** (bring-a-friend, time-boxed 1–4 weeks) · **ongoing programs** (always mention the benefit of doing it with others).

## Tactics / how to apply
- Diagnose honestly first: if customers aren't referring, the product usually isn't remarkable yet — ask "why are my customers too embarrassed to tell everyone?"
- Run the **one-question thought experiment** with your team: "You've lost every customer but one and are banned from all advertising forever — how would you treat that customer so they send all their friends?" Then do that.
- Ask *at the point of sale*, on the contract/checkout, for names and a live introduction — not weeks later.
- Pay referrers in your actual CAC (cash or credit); two-sided rewards spread the incentive and keep referral quality high because referrers stay invested in their referral's success.
- Make the success actions your guarantee conditions, so getting results and generating proof are the same motion.

## Notable examples
- **Dropbox** gave storage to referrer and friend and grew ~39× in 15 months; **PayPal** gave $10 to each side and reached a million users in ~2 years, 100M in ~6.
- Hormozi ran ~2 weeks with paid ads accidentally switched off and still did ~$500K/week from word of mouth alone; at a 700-person event, nearly the whole room raised hands to "who heard about us from another owner?"
- A salesman broke event-ticket records doing "the same thing as everyone else" except he asked every buyer who else they'd bring and requested the intro — half his sales were referrals.
- Service businesses routinely added 25–30% more signups by asking for the referral *the moment* someone bought — often covering the entire ad spend.
