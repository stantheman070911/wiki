# The Attention Barbell - Brand as the AI-Era Moat

## One-line summary
As AI commoditizes information and intermediates buying decisions, brand becomes the only defensible asset — and it is best built with a barbell of extreme technology on one end and 1950s human touch on the other, never the middle.

## Context
When every buyer asks an AI agent "who should I hire / go with?", discovery stops running on ads and starts running on reputation. This entry captures GaryVee's read of that shift and the two-ended strategy he prescribes for surviving it.

## Key insights
- **Brand is the only defensible thing in an AI world.** Technology (reach, information, even the ad) is being commoditized; the residual moat is the trust attached to a name that an AI — and a human — will recommend.
- **Information is now a commodity.** People can get any "how-to" from an AI for free, so raw information no longer sells. What people pay for is *connection to the person* delivering it.
- **The barbell, not the middle.** The world is splitting into two extremes: extreme technology (AI, bots, automation) and extreme old-fashioned humanity (1950s-style personal contact). Winners operate at *both* ends; the un-differentiated middle loses.
- **The human end is transaction-free.** Reach out to every past client just to say hello — genuinely, with no ask. The value ("karma") compounds into business as a residual effect, not as a pitched transaction.
- **Adaptability over grievance.** Rather than mourn that 20 years of work got commoditized, go on offense with the same new tools that commoditized it ("I'm not going to cry… I'm just going to go on the offense of the same thing").
- **Software features need an experience moat around them.** A narrow top-tier reputation reinforced by selective access, a valuable peer community, and memorable human experiences is harder to reproduce than code alone.
- **Conversational discovery may strengthen the barbell.** If AI systems increasingly mediate recommendations, machine-readable proof and extreme human accountability become complementary rather than opposing advantages; the advertising details remain a forecast until verified.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
**Jab, jab, jab, right hook (value-first sequencing).** Content is boxing. Jabs/feints = free information given with the hope it helps. The right hook = the ask/sale. If every post is a right hook, the audience learns to duck; the jabs create the karma and energy that make the eventual right hook land. "Doing good things leads to good results."

**The two ends of the barbell, made concrete:**
- *Extreme tech end:* high-volume, interest-graph content and presence everywhere an AI or feed might surface you (so you're the answer when someone asks a bot).
- *Extreme human end:* personal, transaction-free outreach to existing relationships; written connection on platforms like Substack where a good writer turns commoditized information into lead-gen through jabbing.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Audit your content's jab-to-right-hook ratio; if most posts ask for the sale, front-load far more free value.
- Build brand deliberately as AI-era insurance: be consistently visible and credible enough that both people and AI models name you.
- Run the "just say hello" campaign: contact past clients with zero transactional intent; let re-engagement be a byproduct.
- Do homework on Substack (or equivalent written platforms); if you can write, use tips/observations from your domain as lead-gen jabs.
- Make it an action year: mental preparation without reps is like training your mind for a marathon you never physically train for.

## Notable examples
- GaryVee reframes his own commoditization as opportunity, spinning up new accounts and formats rather than defending old advantage.
- The "chiropractor" scenario: when a prospect asks an AI "I need a chiropractor now, who should I go with?", only brand decides the answer.
