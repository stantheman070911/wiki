# Commercialized Sovereignty - Jurisdictions Competing as Service Providers

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[05-Intelligence-and-Research/Intelligence and Research Index|Intelligence and Research]]

## One-line summary
When people, capital, and work become more portable, jurisdictions face stronger pressure to compete on the price and quality of security, infrastructure, law, and residency.

## Context
This is a strategic forecast from 1997, not a settled law. The durable mechanism is exit: a customer gains bargaining power when switching jurisdictions becomes credible, although states retain legal, fiscal, surveillance, and physical powers the original forecast often underestimated.

## Key insights
- **Exit complements voice.** Political influence asks an incumbent to improve; jurisdictional choice creates an outside option and makes poor service more costly to the provider.
- **Mobility is uneven.** Portable professionals, digital firms, and liquid capital can compare jurisdictions more easily than families, local operators, regulated professions, or people dependent on public benefits.
- **Jurisdictions already bundle services.** Taxes and compliance buy different combinations of legal reliability, infrastructure, health, education, safety, market access, and geopolitical protection.
- **Smaller jurisdictions can position narrowly.** A city or small state may compete by serving a defined segment exceptionally well rather than reproducing every function of a large nation.
- **Competition has failure modes.** Regulatory arbitrage can attract harmful activity; low prices can conceal weak capacity; mobile taxpayers may free-ride on systems built by less-mobile residents.
- **Forecasts must price state adaptation.** Governments can coordinate reporting, regulate intermediaries, tax citizens across borders, restrict movement, or improve their own offering.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
Evaluate a jurisdiction with a **sovereignty service scorecard**:

1. Rule-of-law reliability and contract enforcement.
2. Total tax and compliance cost.
3. Physical and digital security.
4. Infrastructure and access to markets and talent.
5. Residency stability and freedom of movement.
6. Policy volatility and fiscal liabilities.
7. Switching cost, including family, language, community, and exit taxes.

The relevant comparison is risk-adjusted lifetime value, not the headline tax rate.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Separate residence, customers, corporate domicile, banking, and data infrastructure; each has different legal constraints and concentration risks.
- Obtain current legal and tax advice before any cross-border action; rules change and often apply through citizenship, control, or permanent-establishment tests.
- Preserve more than one legitimate operational option without creating structures that lack real substance.
- Compare institutional reliability during stress, not merely efficiency in normal times.
- For policymakers, treat talent and business retention as a product problem: learn why valuable contributors leave and improve the service bundle.

## Notable examples
The book uses medieval march regions and Andorra to illustrate how competing authorities limited extraction, then predicts digital work would broaden jurisdictional shopping. Later developments in remote work, digital services, residency programs, and international tax coordination support parts of the mechanism while also showing that territorial states remain adaptive and powerful.
