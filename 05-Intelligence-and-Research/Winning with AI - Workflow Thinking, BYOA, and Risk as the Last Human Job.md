# Winning with AI - Workflow Thinking, BYOA, and Risk as the Last Human Job

## One-line summary
AI is the worst it will ever be, so learning it is priorities one through ten — and the winners will stop thinking in roles and start thinking in workflows, deliver whole departments as trained agents, and understand that risk is the last thing a human gets paid for.

## Context
Hormozi frames AI as the biggest coming shift for Main Street, not just tech, and offers a strategic map: how to think about it, how the org and the economy change, and which bets hold when intelligence and labor approach zero cost.

## Key insights
- **AI will never be worse than right now.** Assuming any improvement, learning it should dominate your priorities. The best time to start an AI-first business is now, while incumbents are busy running (and merely *saying* "AI-first"); AI-native firms can hit millions in revenue per employee.
- **Adaptability survives (business Darwinism).** In game theory the most flexible system survives — not the strongest or smartest. You risk more by *not* adopting than by fearing edge cases (getting hacked once isn't a reason to quit the internet). Non-adoption is complacency: a short-term learning cost people avoid because they think short-term.
- **~20 hours to proficiency; the first hour is delayed for decades.** Spend a weekend tearing the wrapper off and actually building something rather than reading fear-bait articles.
- **Role-based → workflow-based thinking.** For every hire, list the 4–10 things they do with hands and eyes, then ask whether each could be a *workflow* instead of *headcount*. Old: "hire an editor." New: "what five things does an editor do?" — each becomes a workflow. A business is a manufacturing belt (raw inputs + special sauce → more valuable output); org charts organize *humans and communication*, not inputs and outputs.
- **Automate your own job.** If you don't, someone else controls the automation. (A founder friend spun up a division whose mission is to put his larger business out of business.)
- **BYOA / BYOS is the future.** "Bring your own agent(s)/software." Deliver a whole department's output via trained agents and become very valuable — "I am your entire marketing department" (cf. Anthropic's one-person marketing team). Capture it as a contractor, an equity-holding embed, or higher cash. Erase "titleism."
- **Price sensitivity lags.** People used to paying $2,000/month (with labor cost baked in) keep paying it while your cost drops to $50 — huge margin, and enormous operational leverage (one person → millions in revenue, since coordination between humans is a top scaling cost).

## Framework / model (if applicable)
**Humans + superior tools beat humans + inferior tools** — every age (stone→bronze→iron). As long as it's humans-with-tools vs humans-with-tools, you're still competing against humans, so be confident. The day you try to beat the machine itself, you lose (chess, Go, autopilot).

**Risk is the last human job.** As intelligence and labor trend toward zero cost, the last thing a human gets paid for is *taking risk* — no one can take it away from you. Money persists; the value of labor doesn't.

**The AI barbell (a Bezos-style bet):**
- *High-risk/high-reward end:* go AI-first/native across everything, willing to have hard conversations (raise the bar; keep those who clear it, automate roles away) — because a startup that skips that conversation will out-run you.
- *Won't-change end:* bet on what stays. Humans keep bodies (health, fitness, consumables, food, supplements) and gain leisure (entertainment booms — you can make an AI film for near-zero cost, all margin). "Whatever porn adopts first trickles down." Plus a *hell-in-a-handbasket* hedge (if it all collapses, nothing matters) → be all-weather: hope for the best, prepare for the worst.

**The phase shift** (Brian Johnson's metaphor): you've trained your whole life to swim in harder and harder water; the coming change boils the water to gas — however good a swimmer you are, the physics of the environment change.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Block a weekend to actually build one agent workflow end-to-end; treat the 20-hour proficiency curve as the entry fee.
- Re-express every role as its component tasks and convert tasks to workflows; start by automating one task of your own job.
- Package a department-level output as trained agents and sell it (agency, equity, or salary premium).
- Position for the 18-month window and the price-sensitivity lag: charge legacy prices while operating at AI cost.
- Diversify along the barbell: aggressive AI-first bets plus durable body/leisure/entertainment bets.

## Notable examples
- Anthropic's reportedly one-person marketing department as the BYOA archetype.
- Fed data cited in-video: near-zero net private-sector job creation — attributed partly to automation.
