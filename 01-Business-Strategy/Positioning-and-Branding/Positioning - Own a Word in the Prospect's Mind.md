# Positioning - Own a Word in the Prospect's Mind

## One-line summary
Positioning is not what you do to a product — it's what you do to the mind of the prospect: in an overcommunicated society you win by owning one simple, defensible word or idea, working with what's already in the mind rather than trying to change it.

## Context
Ries and Trout's founding thesis (coined 1972). Kotler frames it as the step that precedes the four Ps — after Research, Segmentation, and Targeting, you must decide your **Position**, which then informs product, price, place, and promotion. This is the doctrine behind every "niche down" and "own a category" instinct in the vault.

## Key insights
- **Positioning is done to the mind, not the product.** You start with a product, but you position it in the prospect's mind; changes to name, price, and package are cosmetic moves in service of securing a mental position.
- **We live in an overcommunicated society.** Too many products, companies, and messages; advertising is "a very light fog," not a sledgehammer. The mind screens out most input and accepts only what matches prior knowledge.
- **You can't change a made-up mind.** "Don't confuse me with the facts, my mind's made up." Mind-changing is the road to disaster; work *with* existing perceptions. (News works because people will sit still for what they don't yet know.)
- **The oversimplified message wins.** The mind is a saturated sponge with only 24 hours a day; "in communication, less is more." Jettison ambiguity and sharpen one idea to cut in — it's a *selection* project (pick the material most likely to get through), not a transmission project.
- **Perception is reality; think outside-in.** "The only reality that counts is what's already in the prospect's mind." Focus on the receiver's perception, not the product's reality — the customer is right, the communicator is wrong.
- **Prospects don't buy, they choose.** Your merit matters less than your *position among the choices*.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
**Get into the mind — be first, or go against.** The easy way in is to be *first* (the first person to fly the Atlantic — Lindbergh — is remembered; the second is not). If you can't be first, position *against* whoever is (Avis "We're No. 2, we try harder"; 7Up "the uncola" against the colas' share of mind).

**The mental ladders.** To cope with overload, people rank each category on a mental ladder of rungs (rent-a-car: Hertz, Avis, National). The mind holds only about seven brands per category; before you position anything, know which rung you're on and relate to it. You generally can't dislodge the top rung head-on (no one beats IBM in computers by being a better IBM) — "you can't get there from here."

**Own one word.** The strongest positions are a single attribute owned in the mind: Volvo = *safety*, Häagen-Dazs = *premium*, Little Caesars = *two-for-one*. Once established, a position is like valuable real estate — hard to take, and hard to get back if you abandon it (Little Caesars dropped "pizza, pizza" and sales fell apart).

## Tactics / how to apply
- Ask what position you *already* own in the prospect's mind (from the market, not the marketing manager) before deciding anything.
- Reduce your message to one word or idea you can plausibly own; if a category leader owns it, don't fight for it — go around.
- Avoid the "everybody trap": trying to appeal to everyone (Rheingold's every-ethnic-group beer) appeals to no one. Narrow the focus.
- Enter against the leader by naming the leader's game and offering the alternative (uncola, No. 2).
- Treat perception as the battlefield: change the mind's *connections*, don't try to change its mind.

## Notable examples
- Volvo (safety), Häagen-Dazs (premium), L'eggs (supermarket panty hose), Little Caesars (two-for-one) — positions built into product, price, place, and promotion respectively.
- Avis "We try harder" and 7Up "the uncola" — the classic against-the-leader positions.
- Rheingold's ethnic-appeal beer — the everybody-trap failure.
