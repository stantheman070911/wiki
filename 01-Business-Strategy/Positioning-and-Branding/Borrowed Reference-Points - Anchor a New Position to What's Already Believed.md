# Borrowed Reference-Points - Anchor a New Position to What's Already Believed

## One-line summary
When you have no existing mental picture to build on, don't invent one from scratch — borrow credibility by anchoring to an already-strong reference point (an analogy) or a third-party authority (a list, ranking, or outside expert).

## Context
Ries and Trout's place-marketing cases (Belgium, Jamaica, the Stowe ski resort) solve a problem product positioning usually doesn't face: the prospect has *no* existing mental picture of the offering at all, so there's nothing to reposition against. The fix is to attach the unknown to something the prospect already believes, rather than trying to plant a brand-new image in an already-saturated mind.

## Key insights
- **An unknown entity can't out-compete an established mental picture — so borrow one.** Belgium had no tourist image at all ("Waterloo was a suburb of Paris" to most Americans); rather than build one from zero, the campaign anchored to a picture the prospect already held: Amsterdam. ("In beautiful Belgium, there are five Amsterdams" — built on Michelin's rating of five three-star Belgian cities versus Amsterdam's one.)
- **The strongest borrowed analogy relates to a destination already at the top of the prospect's ladder.** Jamaica positioned against Hawaii ("the Hawaii of the Caribbean") rather than against its direct Caribbean competitors (Bahamas, Puerto Rico, Bermuda) — Hawaii already held the exact mental picture (big green volcanic mountains meeting the sea) Jamaica needed, and the analogy borrowed it wholesale instead of building it from photographs.
- **Third-party authority substitutes for a track record you don't have.** Stowe didn't need to build "top ski resort" perception alone — a travel writer's published "world's top 10 ski resorts" list did it, backed by the credibility of an outside, disinterested expert. The claim ("only one of the world's top 10 is in the East") worked because it came from someone other than the seller.
- **Borrowing works best in three ways at once**: it relates the unknown to something already in the mind (traction), it comes from a credible independent source (the Michelin Guide, the travel writer) rather than self-promotion, and it converts a vague quality ("beautiful") into a countable, listable claim ("five three-star cities," "one of the top 10").
- **Locals often can't see the asset outsiders would pay to see.** Many Belgians undervalued their own country as a destination (an airport sign apologized for the rain); the position had to come from studying the *prospect's* mind, not the insider's.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
**Borrow, don't build, when there's no existing picture:**
1. Identify what mental picture the prospect *already* holds that's adjacent to your true qualities (a competing but well-known place, product, or category).
2. Find or create a countable, listable fact that licenses the comparison (Michelin's star count, a ranked "top 10" list) rather than an adjective ("beautiful," "best").
3. Source the claim from an independent third party wherever possible — the credibility transfers in a way self-description can't.
4. Commit to the anchor long enough for it to stick; borrowed-reference campaigns die fast when new management swaps the strategy before it takes hold (Belgium's program was abandoned mid-flight for exactly this reason).

## Tactics / how to apply
- Before inventing a new image for something unknown, ask what *already-known* thing your offering most resembles in the prospect's mind — and consider naming that comparison directly instead of describing your own attributes.
- Look for (or commission) a countable, external, listable fact — a ranking, a certification, an independent review, a number — that makes the analogy concrete rather than a vague adjective.
- Prefer a third party's words over your own claim whenever the credibility gap is the actual obstacle.
- Protect the anchor once it's chosen: a borrowed-reference strategy needs sustained commitment to pay off, and is especially vulnerable to being dropped by new decision-makers who didn't originate it.

## Notable examples
- Belgium tourism: "In beautiful Belgium, there are five Amsterdams" (borrowed from Amsterdam's mental picture, licensed by Michelin's city-star ratings).
- Jamaica tourism: "The Hawaii of the Caribbean" (borrowed Hawaii's mental picture instead of competing directly with the Bahamas, Puerto Rico, or Bermuda on their own terms).
- Stowe, Vermont: "One of the world's top 10 ski resorts" (borrowed from a named outside travel writer's published ranking, alongside Aspen, St. Moritz, and Kitzbühel).
