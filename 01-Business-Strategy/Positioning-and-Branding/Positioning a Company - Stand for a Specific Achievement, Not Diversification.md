# Positioning a Company - Stand for a Specific Achievement, Not Diversification

## One-line summary
A corporate position must rest on one specific, major achievement the company is known for — not on vague claims like "our people" or "diversification," which say nothing distinct in the prospect's mind.

## Context
Positioning a *company* (as opposed to a single product) has extra pitfalls: everyone inside the company wants credit for the whole portfolio, so corporate messaging drifts toward generic claims that feel accurate internally but land as noise externally. Ries and Trout use Xerox's push beyond copiers into office systems as the case study.

## Key insights
- **"Our people" is a non-position.** Nearly every company believes it has good people, so claiming it distinguishes nothing. The prospect already assumes the market leader has the best people — you don't need to say it, and saying it if you're not the leader creates an inconsistency that doesn't resolve in your favor.
- **Diversification is not a position — it's the opposite of one.** "Positioning and diversification are poles apart." Strong corporate positions are built on major, singular achievements, not broad product lines: GE = the world's largest electrical manufacturer, not a diversified maker of industrial/transportation/appliance products; GM = the world's largest car builder; IBM = the world's largest computer maker. Even the stock market chronically undervalues conglomerates (ITT, Gulf & Western) for the same reason — the market can't find the position either.
- **A broad self-description is a warning sign.** A real company (Bell & Howell) once called itself "a developer and supplier of information systems for work, education, and entertainment" — so broad it was almost meaningless. If your positioning statement could describe half the Fortune 500, it isn't a position.
- **A company can hold a strong position without realizing its fragility.** Xerox owned "copier" so completely that no competitor name (Sharp, Ricoh, Canon, even IBM/Kodak) sprang to mind — yet the company kept trying to escape that position into computers/office systems and kept failing (Xerox Data Systems folded after six years).

## Framework / model (if applicable)
**The "third-leg" strategy — extend along an adjacent slot, not into a leader's owned category.** When a strong position needs to grow, map the landscape as a small number of "legs" already owned by different players (in Xerox's case: AT&T = communication leg, IBM = input/processing leg, Xerox = output leg via copiers). Then:
1. Don't try to cross into a leg someone else owns — cross-leg moves consistently fail both ways (Xerox never won at computers; IBM never won at copiers).
2. Extend *forward* along your own leg into the next adjacent technology, not sideways into someone else's. Xerox's real opportunity was output-side technology (laser printing/scanning — "lasography"), not office computing.
3. A new word that connects to your existing position (lasography ← xerography) is more ownable than a leap into unrelated territory.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Write your corporate positioning claim in one sentence. If it's "our people" or "diversified provider of X," redo it — find the one specific achievement the market already credits you with.
- Before expanding into a new category, map who already owns which adjacent "leg." Don't attack a leg someone else owns head-on; extend forward along your own.
- Pick an expansion name/concept that audibly connects to your existing position (so it inherits credibility) rather than a generic "innovation" rebrand.
- Treat any positioning statement broad enough to apply to a different company in a different industry as a failed draft.

## Notable examples
- GE ("world's largest electrical manufacturer," not "diversified industrial maker"); GM; IBM.
- Bell & Howell's meaninglessly broad self-description.
- Xerox: owns "copiers" outright, but Xerox Data Systems (from the Scientific Data Systems acquisition) folded after six years trying to cross into IBM's leg; the durable opportunity was the adjacent "third leg" (laser/output technology), not computers.
- Ford's "better ideas" corporate theme as a middling example — not a true position, since it doesn't answer why Ford doesn't out-execute GM if the ideas are really better.
