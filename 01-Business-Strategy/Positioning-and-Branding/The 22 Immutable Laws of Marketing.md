# The 22 Immutable Laws of Marketing

## One-line summary
Marketing is a battle of perceptions fought inside the prospect's mind, not a battle of products in the marketplace — and 22 recurring, often-violated laws govern who wins that battle, from being first into the mind to knowing when line extension, hype, or unlimited resources will quietly sink a brand.

## Context
Ries and Trout's 1993 follow-up to *Positioning: The Battle for Your Mind* (see [[Positioning - Own a Word in the Prospect's Mind]]), written after watching companies like GM, IBM, and Sears repeatedly waste billions on well-funded, well-executed marketing programs that violated basic, predictable rules. Where *Positioning* laid out the founding thesis, this book distills 25 years of consulting observations into 22 discrete, named laws — a checklist to audit any marketing plan against before launch.

## Key insights
- **Marketing is perception, not product.** There is no objective reality, no "best product" — only perceptions in the prospect's mind, and the perception is the reality (Law of Perception). Honda is a car brand in the US and a motorcycle brand in Japan, on identical products, because perceptions differ by market.
- **You cannot change a made-up mind.** Once a mind is set, changing it is the single most wasteful activity in marketing — Wang spent millions trying to be seen as a computer company after owning "word processor," and failed for 25 years.
- **The mind is selective and uses ladders.** Prospects rank brands on a mental ladder per category (Hertz, Avis, National in rent-a-cars) and reject information inconsistent with that ladder — Chrysler's head-to-head comparison ads against Honda barely moved buyers, because Chrysler wasn't even on their ladder.
- **Owning one simple word is the most powerful move in marketing** — Volvo owns *safety*, Federal Express owned *overnight*, Domino's owns *home delivery* — and no two companies can own the same word (the law of exclusivity): every safety-themed automotive campaign against Volvo has failed.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
The 22 laws, grouped by theme:

**Getting into the mind first**
1. **Leadership** — It's better to be first than it is to be better (Lindbergh vs. the forgotten second Atlantic solo flyer, Bert Hinkler).
2. **The Category** — If you can't be first in a category, create a new category you can be first in (Michelob as the first *high-priced domestic* beer, rather than chasing Heineken as an import).
3. **The Mind** — Better to be first in the mind than first in the marketplace (IBM beat first-to-market Remington Rand/UNIVAC by winning the mind first).
4. **Perception** — Marketing is a battle of perceptions, not products; there are no facts, only perceptions.
5. **Focus** — The most powerful concept in marketing is owning a single word in the prospect's mind (Crest = cavities, BMW = driving).
6. **Exclusivity** — Two companies cannot own the same word; trying to out-spend a competitor for their word (Energizer vs. Duracell's "long-lasting") fails.
7. **The Ladder** — Your strategy depends on which rung of the mental ladder you occupy (Avis's "We're No. 2, we try harder" acknowledged its rung and worked; "Avis is going to be No. 1" ignored it and failed).
8. **Duality** — In the long run, every market becomes a two-horse race (Coke/Pepsi, Democrats/Republicans).
9. **The Opposite** — If you're a strong No. 2, position against the leader's strength rather than imitating it (7Up as "the Uncola").
10. **Division** — Categories split and multiply over time (computers → mainframe, mini, workstation, PC — each spawning its own leader); don't assume a category stays singular forever.

**Managing perception, focus, and change**
11. **Perspective** — Marketing effects take time to play out and can even run opposite the immediate effect (short-term price cuts can permanently teach customers to wait for sales).
12. **Line Extension** — There is an almost irresistible temptation to extend an established brand's equity onto new products, and it almost always dilutes the brand (a "Harley-Davidson car" would undermine, not extend, the Harley brand — see [[The Line-Extension Trap - Why One Name Can't Own Two Things]]).
13. **Sacrifice** — You must give something up to get somewhere: sacrifice the product line (Federal Express dropped everything but overnight small-package delivery to own "overnight"), the target market, or constant change.
14. **Attributes** — Owning an attribute means the opposite attribute is now available to a competitor (Crest owns "cavity prevention," so Colgate can credibly own "good taste").
15. **Candor** — An admission of a negative, honestly stated, is disarming and can build tremendous credibility for the positive claim that follows ("Avis is only No. 2. We try harder.").
16. **Singularity** — In any given situation, only one bold move produces substantial results — not a combination of half-measures.
17. **Unpredictability** — You cannot reliably predict competitors' reactions to your plans by only studying your own plans; write competitors' plans, not just your own, and build in flexibility.

**Sustaining and knowing the limits**
18. **Success** — Success often leads to arrogance, and arrogance to failure; as brands succeed, egos tend to replace market judgment (see also [[Don't Follow Competitors Down - The $50M Price-Cut Lesson]] for a market-leader-arrogance case).
19. **Failure** — Failure is to be expected and accepted; most companies try to correct problems rather than abandon them and move on.
20. **Hype** — The situation that gets the most press (hype) is usually the opposite of a real marketing success in the making; genuine revolutions often arrive quietly.
21. **Acceleration** — Successful marketing programs are usually built for the long term; chasing short-term fads (trend-surfing) undermines durable brand-building.
22. **Resources** — Without adequate funding, even the best idea won't get off the ground; money is the oxygen that lets an idea catch fire in the mind, but money alone (without the other 21 laws) cannot buy a position.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Before launching any campaign, run it against the checklist: are we trying to be first in a *new* category rather than better in an old one; do we own one simple word; are we fighting for a word a competitor already owns; have we sacrificed enough to stand for something specific?
- If you're not the category leader, position explicitly relative to the leader (law of the ladder / the opposite) rather than claiming generic superiority.
- Treat any urge to extend a strong brand name onto a new, unrelated product line as a warning sign, not an opportunity — check against [[The Line-Extension Trap - Why One Name Can't Own Two Things]].
- Distrust press hype as a signal of real market traction; look instead for quiet, sustained category-level shifts.
- Budget for the law of resources realistically — a brilliant, correctly-positioned idea still needs enough money to blast into the mind; underfunding a correct strategy fails as surely as overfunding a wrong one.

## Notable examples
- Lindbergh vs. Bert Hinkler (first vs. second solo Atlantic flight); Hertz/Avis/National ladder in rent-a-cars; Heineken vs. Michelob (new category vs. me-too import); Volvo/safety, Domino's/home delivery, Federal Express/overnight; Wang's failed pivot from word processors to computers; New Coke winning blind taste tests yet losing the market to Coca-Cola Classic; Atari abandoning "video games" only for Nintendo to take the category; BMW losing focus by chasing Mercedes into large sedans, then recovering by re-emphasizing "driving."
