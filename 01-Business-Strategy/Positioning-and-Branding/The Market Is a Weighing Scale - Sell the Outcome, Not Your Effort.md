# The Market Is a Weighing Scale - Sell the Outcome, Not Your Effort

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[01-Business-Strategy/Business Strategy Index|Business Strategy]]

## One-line summary
No prospect cares about you, your company, or how hard you've worked — the market is an emotionless weighing scale that transacts on value, so every pitch must be framed entirely around the prospect's outcome.

## Context
The foundational reframe most founders resist: they build and sell from *their* point of view (our story, our effort, our mission) when the buyer is running a single silent calculation — "how does this change my situation?" Getting this wrong means building things no one asked for; getting it right is what separates who makes money from who stays broke.

## Key insights
- **The market has no emotions.** It doesn't reward you for caring, for tenure, or for effort — only for solving a problem the buyer actually has. Sympathy is not a transaction.
- **"Nobody cares about you" is a feature, not an insult.** Once you truly accept it, you stop wasting words on your credentials and mission and spend them on the prospect's exact situation — and conversion follows.
- **Sell the outcome in the buyer's own terms.** Not what your product *is*, but what it *does for them* — ideally how it makes them look and feel to the people around them.
- **The shift is a lever, not a tweak.** The same seller, same product, moving from self-centred to outcome-centred framing can go from worst on the team to best.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Audit any pitch, page, or ad: count sentences about *you* vs. about *the prospect's result*. Cut the former hard.
- Lead with the concrete outcome and a number ("we wire you $500 for something you already own and would throw away"), not with your company narrative.
- Reduce the prospect's effort to near zero in the ask — the ink-cartridge pitch worked partly because all they had to do was read a serial number.
- Before building anything, state the specific problem it solves for a specific person; if you can't, you're building for yourself.

## Notable examples
- Suby's first sales job cold-calling to buy back empty ink cartridges: pitching his company's mission ("saving the turtles") got him hung up on all day. Switching to "I'll wire you $500 and you just read me the serial number" made him the #1 salesperson at every company he worked at.
