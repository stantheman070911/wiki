# Real-World Proof and the Overkill Bias - Why the Best Get to Teach

## One-line summary
People don't buy education, coaching, or expertise from whoever can teach it — they buy from whoever has the most extreme real-world proof, because of an "overkill bias" that assumes someone who succeeded at the hardest version will easily handle their easier one.

## Principle
When many people can technically deliver the same thing, buyers default to the one with the most demonstrated real-world success. Humans carry an **overkill bias**: if a jacket keeps you warm on Mount Everest, you assume it'll keep you warm from your car to the door; so you'd rather learn a basic skateboard trick from Tony Hawk than from any of the thousands of people equally capable of teaching it. Demonstrated competence at an extreme confers authority over everything below it — and that authority is brand. This is why, in expert and education businesses, **you are capped by how good you actually are**: your real achievement sets a ceiling on the brand, credibility, and premium you can command.

## Scenario
Use this for experts, coaches, consultants, course creators, and personal brands deciding how to earn authority — and for anyone tempted to skip the doing and jump straight to teaching or selling advice.

## Key insights
- **Proof beats pedagogy.** The quality of your teaching matters far less than whether you've visibly done the hard thing. The same course from an unproven author and from a proven operator are not the same product to the buyer.
- **The achievement is the marketing.** You could distill everything Warren Buffett has ever said into a better-than-Buffett investing course — but the one thing you forgot to build was Berkshire Hathaway. Attention and belief follow the receipt, not the curriculum.
- **You get paid for the proof, then for the teaching.** Cordon Bleu can charge more for its culinary education because its graduates are more successful and more employable — the brand's track record confers value onto the student, lowering their perceived risk. The credential becomes a stamp in itself.
- **Your competence is the ceiling.** Because proof drives brand, credibility, and pricing power, how good you genuinely are directly correlates with how large a brand and premium you can build. You can't out-market your way past your own results for long.
- **The unglamorous path is the moat.** Education is fast, easy, and cheap to start — which is exactly why it's crowded and copyable. The durable edge is the thing competitors won't do: be patient, do the work, and become genuinely one of the best first. Then when you teach, people listen immediately and never question your price or ability.

## Tactics / how to apply
1. Before building an expert or education offer, accumulate a visible, extreme, real-world result in the domain — the harder and more measurable, the more overkill authority it confers.
2. Lead every pitch with the proof (the exit, the record, the roster of successful graduates), not the syllabus.
3. Position the credential as risk reduction for the buyer — show that your students/clients go on to succeed, which lets you charge more for the same teaching.
4. If you lack proof, spend the season doing the thing rather than teaching it; treat that as brand investment, not delay.
5. Resist the temptation to compete on teaching polish alone against someone with a bigger receipt — you'll lose to their proof.

## Notable examples
- **Tony Hawk teaching an ollie:** thousands could teach it, but given the choice everyone picks the best-known practitioner — overkill bias in action.
- **The Mount Everest jacket:** proof at the extreme (warm on Everest) sells the easy case (warm to your car).
- **Warren Buffett vs. a "better" investing course:** the missing ingredient isn't better content, it's an \$89B-profit track record built over decades.
- **Acquisition.com's business content** got disproportionate attention only after a \$46.2M exit — same teacher, far more belief once the receipt existed.
- **Cordon Bleu:** a strong reputation for producing employable chefs lets it charge premium tuition, because the brand confers value onto graduates.
