# Distribution Beats Product - The Best-Known Wins, Not the Best

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[01-Business-Strategy/Business Strategy Index|Business Strategy]]

## One-line summary
The money isn't in your goods and services, it's in the selling of them — distribution is a terminal monopoly on a market, and it's usually the best-*known* product that wins, not the best product.

## Context
A hard pill for product-obsessed founders: the market rarely rewards the finest thing built in isolation. Selling ability and distribution compound into a flywheel that a superior-but-unsold product can't beat, which is why sales must be designed before the product, not after.

## Key insights
- **Sales trumps all, and it compounds.** If you can sell better than anyone, you make the most money → you hire the best talent → they deliver the best results and build the best goods → which you then sell. The engine starts with selling, not with the product.
- **Distribution is a terminal monopoly.** Owning the channel and the attention is a moat competitors can't out-product you around; the best-known option wins the default.
- **The execution/distribution founder beats the product founder every day.** Two founders — one product-obsessed toiling in his room, one obsessed with execution and distribution — the distribution one wins repeatedly.
- **Design the sale before the thing.** You should already know exactly how you'll sell it *before* you develop it — never build first and then ask "how do I sell this?"

## Tactics / how to apply
- Before building, write the go-to-market: the offer, the channel, and the pitch. If you can't, don't build yet.
- Weight your own time toward the compounding levers — recruiting great people and selling — rather than defaulting to product polish.
- Treat "best-known" as a goal in its own right: invest in distribution and showmanship, not only in features.
- Package and present the product for maximum showmanship (the framing, the demo, the one-line promise), because presentation is part of the sale.

## Notable examples
- Apple / Steve Jobs: arguably the best products in the world, driven by one of the greatest salespeople ever. Jobs framed the iPod as "a thousand songs in your pocket," reportedly spent ~80% of his time on recruiting, ~10–15% on sales and marketing, and only ~5% on actual product — the cash from selling let him hire Jony Ive to build the best products.
