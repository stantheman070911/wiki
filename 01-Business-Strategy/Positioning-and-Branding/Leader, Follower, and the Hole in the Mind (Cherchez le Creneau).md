# Leader, Follower, and the Hole in the Mind (Cherchez le Creneau)

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[01-Business-Strategy/Business Strategy Index|Business Strategy]]

## One-line summary
What works for a leader doesn't work for a follower: leaders get in first and then defend, while followers must find an open hole in the mind (or create one by repositioning the competition) rather than attacking a position head-on.

## Context
Position strategy depends entirely on where you already stand on the mental ladder. Ries and Trout give distinct playbooks for the brand that owns the top rung and the also-ran that doesn't.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
**Positioning a leader — get there first, then defend.** Leadership itself is the position; the leader's job is to reinforce "we're the original / the leader" and to defend the rung:
- Cover competitive moves fast (block promising new positions with your own brands before a follower can own them).
- Prefer multibrand entries to line extension — a new name for each new position (rather than stretching the leader's name).
- Embrace competition; the leader stays on top by acting, not resting on share.

**Positioning a follower — cherchez le creneau ("look for the hole").** Find and fill a gap the leader doesn't occupy — which requires thinking in reverse, against the "bigger and better" grain (if everyone goes east, go west). Common holes:
- **Size** — Volkswagen "Think small" owned the small-car hole (other small cars existed; none had *preempted* the position).
- **High price** — own the premium hole first (a quality product built to last); price becomes the positioning attribute.
- **Low price** — the discount hole (useful where the buyer bears the risk).
- **Gender, age, time of day, distribution** — Nyquil owned "the nighttime cold remedy" instead of fighting Contac and Dristan in daylight.

**Repositioning the competition.** When no hole is left, create one by moving a competitor in the mind. To sell a *new* idea you often must displace an *old* one: Tylenol repositioned aspirin (raising doubts about aspirin's side effects) rather than praising itself. Failing to reposition the incumbent is fatal — Nuprin ($35M) and Advil ($40M) launched the same ingredient but never repositioned Tylenol, and both stalled. "Coming to grips with the competition is the main problem in most marketing situations."

## Key insights
- **You must think as much about competitors' positions as your own.** Prospects choose among options; your rung relative to theirs matters more than your intrinsic merit.
- **Don't go over an obstacle — go around it.** A head-to-head assault on an owned position (IBM in computers) is hopeless; select a position no one has a firm grip on.
- **The hole must actually be open in the mind.** The against-position only works if the creneau isn't already preempted.
- **Reposition by telling the truth about the incumbent**, not by exaggerating yourself — you shift the prospect's existing perception of the competitor.

## Tactics / how to apply
- If you lead: name yourself the original/leader, block emerging positions with separate brands, and react quickly to challengers.
- If you follow: list the holes (size, price, gender, age, time-of-day, distribution) and pick one the leader can't easily occupy.
- If no hole exists: reposition the incumbent by surfacing a real weakness the prospect will accept, opening space for you.
- Never launch against a dominant brand without a plan to move *it* in the mind first.

## Notable examples
- VW Beetle (size), Nyquil (nighttime), premium/discount price holes.
- Tylenol repositioning aspirin; Avis "We're No. 2"; 7Up "the uncola."
- Nuprin and Advil failing by not repositioning Tylenol.
