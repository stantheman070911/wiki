# Multi-Unit Expansion - The Founder-Replacement Test and Why Franchising Is Expensive Capital

## One-line summary
Don't open the next location until the current one is maxed and runs without you — prove it with the founder-replacement test (open a unit per quarter with a team that isn't you) — and understand that franchising is the most expensive capital there is, so only reach for it when you actually need capital you can't otherwise raise.

## Principle
Multi-unit growth fails when founders scale problems instead of solutions. The gate is not ambition but repeatability: a single unit at capacity, running without the founder, that can be duplicated on a predictable cadence. Franchising and licensing feel like acceleration but are financing decisions — you give away almost the whole unit for a royalty, making them the priciest capital available.

## Scenario
Use this for any owner of a physical, location-based, or multi-branch business tempted to open location two or three, and for anyone weighing franchising or licensing as a growth path.

## Diagnosis
- The first location isn't maxed on demand or pricing, yet you want a second.
- You'd be personally running the new unit (you *are* the operating system).
- Adding location two made the business *less* profitable, and the proposed fix is location three.
- You're considering franchising/licensing mainly to move faster, not because you lack capital.
- The current unit still depends on the founder for day-to-day decisions.

## Why It Happens
Founders confuse a bigger footprint with scaling. A new location run by the founder isn't leverage — it's the same job in a new building. Overexpansion is often *undertalent* in disguise: it worked when a great operator was in place and broke when they left. Franchising looks attractive because someone else funds the buildout, but that's exactly the point — it's capital, and the most expensive kind, because you surrender nearly all the unit economics for a small royalty off the top.

## Recommended Action
1. First, max the current unit: pull pricing, hours, and demand levers before adding capacity ("nail it, then scale it").
2. Apply the founder-replacement test: put a general manager in place, then behave as if you're "on vacation" for 3–6 months. If the flagship dips at all, you're not ready to open the next one.
3. Set the per-quarter cadence bar: you've earned the right to expand when you can open a location per quarter with a team that isn't you and that you're not involved in day-to-day.
4. Instrument tracking and data before scaling, because every decision made without data carries higher stakes as you grow.
5. For multi-unit privately-held businesses, staff each location with a boots-on-ground operator who has a profit share (see [[Compensating Leaders - Reverse-Engineer OTE, Profit Pools, and Phantom Equity]]).
6. Prefer margin improvement or M&A of already-stood-up units over franchising; only franchise/license once you clear the per-quarter bar and genuinely want the arms-length capital.
7. Recognize that founder or original-owner "magic" doesn't automatically transfer to a new or acquired location — each new site under-performs the flagship until the specific behaviors that created that magic are identified, documented, and actively trained into the new site's team, not merely assumed to travel with the brand name.
8. When expansion speed itself is the temptation (multiple partnership or acquisition offers arriving at once), resist rushing into unvetted deals purely to hit a location-count milestone — the fastest way to five locations is not the fastest way to fifty, and hasty early partnerships can structurally cap or damage the very expansion they were meant to accelerate.

## Example from the AMA
A veneers business doing $2.4M across two locations (the second struggling) wanted to open a third; Hormozi showed it could reach ~$25M within the *existing* two locations' capacity and said not to open a third until the first is maxed. A pizza shop owner got the litmus test verbatim: train the GM, step out for 3–6 months, and if the flagship dips, don't open number two. A dietitian group weighing franchising heard the capital argument in full — franchising is "the most expensive form of capital you can get" because "you're giving literally almost all the business away and you're getting a royalty" — and earned a conditional green light: licensing is fine "once you can open up a location per quarter with a team that's not you that you're not involved in at all."

A chiropractic-chain breakdown (Raymond) surfaced the founder-magic dilution problem directly: each new or acquired location performed below the flagship because it lacked the original owner's personal touch, and the fix was to isolate exactly what that touch consisted of operationally and build it into training rather than treating it as an unrepeatable personal quality. An eyelash-salon-chain breakdown (Annie) illustrated the expansion-speed trap — facing pressure to accept multiple new location or partnership opportunities quickly, the caller was warned that "the fastest way to five locations isn't the fastest way to fifty," and that rushing into partnerships without proper vetting to hit a short-term location count can create structural problems that slow or cap the long-run expansion.

## Caveats / Exceptions
- Franchising/licensing can be right for capital-intensive expansion or genuine geographic reach you can't otherwise fund — the point is to price the trade honestly.
- In a fixed-price industry, improving margins (see [[Compete on Efficiency When You Can't Control Price]]) can equal several new locations without expansion risk.
- M&A of existing practices can be faster than franchising if you can convert an acquired unit to your metrics quickly.
- Overexpansion is far harder to undo than to avoid; an extra year spent nailing the model often shaves years off the back end.
