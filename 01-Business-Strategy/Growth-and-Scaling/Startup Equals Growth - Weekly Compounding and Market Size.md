# Startup Equals Growth - Weekly Compounding and Market Size

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[01-Business-Strategy/Business Strategy Index|Business Strategy]]

## One-line summary
A startup is not merely a new or technological company but a company designed for rapid growth, which requires both something many people want and a way to reach and serve them.

## Context
Treating every small business as a startup obscures the strategic constraint that shapes venture companies. Growth is not a later optimization; it determines market choice, financing, organization, and daily priorities from the beginning.

## Key insights
- **Growth is the defining property.** Technology, venture funding, and an exit are common consequences, not the definition.
- **Rate changes behavior.** A target such as 5–7% weekly growth gives founders a decision rule: actions that move the metric matter now; attractive distractions do not.
- **Compounding makes small starts compatible with huge outcomes.** The first market may be narrow, but it must sit on a path into a large or rapidly expanding market.
- **Demand creates growth more than spending does.** When a product hits an urgent need, usage can grow with a small team; hiring is often an effect of growth, not its cause.
- **Growth has two multipliers.** Outcome size depends on the rate of growth and how long that rate can continue.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
Model the company as **initial active users × weekly growth multiplier × number of compounding weeks**, constrained by retention and eventual market size. Review one primary growth measure weekly, diagnose why it moved, and choose the next week's work accordingly.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Define one active-user or revenue metric that represents delivered value.
- Set a weekly target appropriate to the stage and calculate the absolute additions required.
- Work backward from the target into concrete user conversations, launches, and product changes.
- Separate growth-causing work from scale theater such as premature hiring or broad publicity.
- Reassess whether the first niche connects to a much larger future population.

## Notable examples
At 10% weekly growth, 100 users become roughly 14,000 in a year and about two million in two years. The actions used later will change, but early manual acquisition can start the same compounding curve.
