# Growth Is Not a Strategy - When Expansion Destroys Value

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[01-Business-Strategy/Business Strategy Index|Business Strategy]]

## One-line summary
Growth is the *outcome* of a successful strategy, not a strategy in itself — chasing growth directly, especially through acquisition or entry into a "growing" commodity market, routinely destroys shareholder value even as revenue and market-share headlines improve.

## Context
Direct case study in what happens when a company treats growth as a goal it can "engineer," rather than as the byproduct of a coordinated, focused strategy — the reverse side of [[Focus - Winning a Segment by Coordinating Policy Around It]], using Crown Cork & Seal's post-1989 acquisition spree as the primary evidence.

## Key insights
- **Growth in a commodity industry is a trap, not an opportunity.** Growing demand pulls in new capacity investment from every competitor; once growth in demand slows, profits vanish for firms without a real competitive advantage — the industry acts like "an economic black hole," absorbing more cash than it ever returns to ordinary participants.
- **Acquisitions almost always destroy value unless you can buy below fair value or add more value than any other buyer could.** Buyers typically pay a ~25% premium over market value plus fees; absent one of those two conditions, expansion by acquisition is a wealth transfer to sellers and advisors, not value creation.
- **"Growth" is frequently a euphemism for unresolved problems.** CEOs reach for acquisition-fueled growth for reasons that have nothing to do with strategy: administrative costs are (wrongly) assumed to fall with size, displaced executives get moved to new divisions instead of let go, larger-firm leaders are paid more, and empire-building is simply more fun than managing what already exists.
- **Advisors have their own incentive to manufacture growth rationales that don't survive scrutiny.** In the Telecom Italia–Cable & Wireless merger discussion, the investment banker's justification escalated from "economies of scale" (false — the businesses operated in unrelated regions) to the meaningless "economies of mass" ("the combined company will have more cash flow") — a purely arithmetic non-argument for a multi-billion-dollar deal.
- **Losing your original coordinated advantage while pursuing growth is the real cost, not just deal overpayment.** Crown Cork & Seal's traditional edge (short-run flexibility, escaping captive-supplier dynamics — see [[Focus - Winning a Segment by Coordinating Policy Around It]]) was left behind, unreplaced, when leadership chased PET-plastics growth by acquisition; the acquired scale never substituted for the abandoned focus.
- **Healthy growth looks structurally different: it's a symptom, not a target.** It shows up as market-share gains happening *simultaneously* with superior profit rates, and it's the reward for real innovation, efficiency, or expanded capability — not something a leader can order into existence by "wanting to grow bigger."

## Framework / model (if applicable)
Before treating any "growth strategy" as legitimate, test it against three questions:
1. **Source** — is the projected growth driven by genuinely expanding demand for a differentiated capability, or by substitution/commoditization dynamics that will hit a hard ceiling?
2. **Price** — for any acquisition-driven growth, can you argue credibly that you're buying below fair value, or that you specifically can add more value to the target than any other buyer? If not, assume value destruction.
3. **Focus cost** — does pursuing this growth require abandoning or diluting the coordinated policy set that produced the company's original advantage? If so, the growth is likely trading a real edge for a larger, less profitable footprint.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Treat "we need to grow" as a symptom requiring diagnosis, not an actionable strategy on its own — ask what specific advantage or capability is supposed to generate that growth, per [[The Kernel of Good Strategy - Diagnosis, Guiding Policy, Coherent Action]].
- Before approving an acquisition, require an explicit answer to "why us, why now" that isn't just "bigger cash flow" or "market leadership" — vague scale arguments (economies of mass, global footprint) are a red flag independent of deal size.
- When entering a fast-growing but commoditizing market, model what happens to margins once growth in demand slows and existing capacity investment has to be absorbed — most of the "growth story" profit is illusory if it's being reinvested in new capacity rather than distributed.
- Watch for a company redefining "focus" downward to accommodate a growth push (as Crown's CFO did) — it's an early tell that the original coordinated advantage is being quietly abandoned.

## Notable examples
- **Crown Cork & Seal under William Avery (1989–2001)**: twenty acquisitions in eight years, including a $9B rollup of CarnaudMetalBox, chased PET-plastics growth; the substitution-driven growth hit its ceiling almost exactly as Crown became the world's largest container maker, and stock fell from $55 to $5 as return on capital collapsed from 15.3% toward negative territory.
- **Telecom Italia / Cable & Wireless merger proposal (1998)**: Morgan Stanley's rationale shifted from "economies of scale" (indefensible — unrelated regional markets) to "economies of mass" (pure cash-flow arithmetic, not a value argument) under direct questioning; the board rejected the deal within two days of the meeting where this was exposed.
- **Contrast — Crown under John Connelly (pre-1989)**: revenue grew only 3.1%/year over the prior decade, yet produced an 18.5%/year shareholder return (vs. 8.6% for the S&P 500) — proof that healthy, advantage-driven growth requires far less top-line expansion than engineered growth-by-acquisition to produce superior returns.
