# The Seven Levels of Business Growth - From Side Hustle to Exit

## One-line summary
Each revenue level requires a different founder job, asset base, and organization; growth stalls when a business keeps using the skills and structure of the previous stage.

## Context
Revenue is only a rough marker, but it reveals recurring transitions: learning to create value, escaping self-employment, delegating delivery, becoming distinctive, formalizing assets, professionalizing management, and eventually managing quality of earnings and exit options.

## Key insights
- **$0–10K: prove agency.** A side hustle creates stories, confidence, and the habit of spotting a problem, proposing a solution, and getting paid.
- **$10K–100K: escape the solo-operator trap.** Selling personal time resembles employment with several bosses; deeper technical skill can make this trap more comfortable without making it scalable.
- **$100K–500K: delegate supply and own demand.** The founder concentrates on positioning, relationships, marketing, and sales while others deliver the outcome.
- **$500K–1M: add influence.** Intellectual property, a clearly defined customer, and a credible figurehead make the company more than a local commodity.
- **$1M–5M: formalize owned assets.** Brand, IP, channels, products, systems, culture, and dedicated sales and marketing leadership reduce borrowed advantage and founder fragility.
- **$5M–10M: cross the desert.** Generalists and informal coordination stop working before the company can comfortably afford a full executive structure; capital, data, specialization, and difficult personnel changes become necessary.
- **$10M+: optimize ownership.** The founder manages leaders, quality of earnings, acquisitions, valuation, and possible exit rather than individual transactions.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
At every level ask four questions: **What is the founder's highest-value role? What must now be delegated? Which assets must the company own? What organization can the current economics support?** Do not hire the next stage's structure until the model can fund it, but do not expect the current structure to produce the next stage's result.

The central strategic fork is between a **lifestyle boutique**—roughly 4–12 people, high flexibility, founder-led demand—and a **performance business**—roughly 30+ people, executive-led operations, proprietary assets, and an explicit exit path.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Identify the current stage from actual behavior and organization, not aspirational revenue.
- Write a stop-doing list for the founder at the next threshold.
- Before $1M, prioritize demand generation and a clear IP–ICP position over complex infrastructure.
- Between $1M and $5M, replace borrowed audiences and personalities with assets the company controls.
- Before crossing the 12-person threshold, decide deliberately whether the desired end state justifies the desert.
- Above $10M, segment revenue by recurrence, margin, predictability, and strategic value rather than celebrating the total alone.

## Notable examples
Priestley describes losing access to prominent partners during the financial crisis and discovering that the attention, reputation, and market access had belonged to those partners rather than to his company. The next stage required formalizing assets the business itself owned.
