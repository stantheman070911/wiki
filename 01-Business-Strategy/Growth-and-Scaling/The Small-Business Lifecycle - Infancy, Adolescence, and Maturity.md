# The Small-Business Lifecycle - Infancy, Adolescence, and Maturity

## One-line summary
Small businesses move from founder-does-everything Infancy into delegation-stressed Adolescence, but they reach Maturity only by adopting a designed, founder-independent operating model—not merely by surviving long enough.

## Context
Growth changes what a business needs from its owner. Failure occurs when the owner tries to preserve the role, control, and habits of an earlier stage after demand and organizational complexity have moved on.

## Key insights
- **Infancy fuses the founder and the business.** The owner performs nearly every task, customers depend on personal skill, and increased demand is met by working longer. The stage ends when individual effort can no longer absorb the workload.
- **Adolescence begins with help but often substitutes abdication for delegation.** The owner hands unwanted work to a capable employee without defining the result, method, authority, standards, or reporting relationship, then intervenes after surprises appear.
- **Growth eventually exceeds the owner’s comfort zone.** The Technician’s limit is personal output; the Manager’s is the number of people and decisions that can be supervised; the Entrepreneur’s is the management capacity that can be aligned to the vision.
- **Three common reactions preserve the underlying defect.** The owner shrinks back to doing everything, accelerates until uncontrolled growth breaks the company, or survives through continuous personal overexertion.
- **Maturity is a perspective, not an age.** A mature company begins with a clear picture of the finished business and behaves according to that model before it has the size, staff, or reputation associated with it.
- **Preparation is the owner’s growth job.** Anticipate the processes, people, capital, technology, space, and management capacity required at successive benchmarks; growth without structural preparation scales chaos.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
| Stage | Operating reality | Founder’s necessary transition | Failure signal |
|---|---|---|---|
| Infancy | Founder is the production system | Separate the business from personal labor | More hours, falling quality, no capacity |
| Adolescence | Employees enter an undefined system | Delegate explicit outcomes and build management | Rework, interference, blame, key-person dependence |
| Maturity | Present operations are shaped by a future model | Develop the business continuously against that model | Strategy displaced by today’s emergencies |

Maturity does not require completing Infancy and Adolescence badly first. The company still encounters their operational challenges, but a future-state design changes how it responds.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Diagnose the stage from operating behavior rather than revenue, company age, or headcount.
- Before hiring, define the outcome, standards, authority, information flow, and training the role requires.
- Write capacity benchmarks for demand, staff, cash, space, and technology before the current setup is overwhelmed.
- If the founder steps away, observe which promises fail. Treat those failures as missing systems rather than proof that only the founder can do the work.
- When considering contraction or another burst of growth, test whether the move fixes the operating model or merely returns the owner to a more familiar level of control.

## Notable examples
Sarah delegates much of her pie shop to Elizabeth but never constructs a real owner–employee agreement or management system. When Elizabeth leaves, the business collapses back into founder labor. The problem is not simply misplaced trust; it is a relationship and organization left undefined because the owner avoided managerial work.
