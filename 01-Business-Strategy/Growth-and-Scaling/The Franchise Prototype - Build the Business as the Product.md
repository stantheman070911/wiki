# The Franchise Prototype - Build the Business as the Product

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[01-Business-Strategy/Business Strategy Index|Business Strategy]]

## One-line summary
Design the business as a tested prototype that delivers a distinctive, predictable result through systems, so customers receive the promise and ordinary employees can reproduce it without founder heroics.

## Context
The “franchise” in this model is a design constraint, not necessarily a legal expansion strategy. Pretend the current operation must become the model for 5,000 identical units: anything that depends on the founder’s memory, taste, discretion, or presence has not yet become part of the business itself.

## Key insights
- **The business is the product.** The commodity is what the customer buys; the business is the repeatable mechanism that creates the total experience and result.
- **A prototype converts assumptions into evidence.** New methods are tested in a live operating environment before they become standards.
- **The system runs the business; people run the system.** Good design does not eliminate human judgment or development. It gives ordinary people tools, boundaries, and feedback that let them produce extraordinary consistency.
- **Founder independence is a design requirement.** A company that performs only when a particular person is present is people-dependent, not systems-dependent.
- **Predictability creates trust.** A strong first experience that changes arbitrarily on the next visit is worse than a modest experience delivered reliably, because the customer cannot make an informed decision to return.
- **Documentation makes the method transferable.** Operations manuals state the purpose of the work, the steps, and the standards for both process and result.
- **The prototype serves every stakeholder.** It must create consistent value for customers, employees, suppliers, and lenders—not merely make production efficient.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
Design the prototype against six constraints:

1. Provide consistent value beyond stakeholder expectations.
2. Make each role operable at the lowest skill level consistent with the required result.
3. Make order visible in the environment and work.
4. Document all recurring work in operations manuals.
5. Deliver a uniform and predictable customer experience.
6. Use a coherent visual and physical code so the entire business communicates one promise.

The test is not “Could another expert run this?” It is “Could a properly selected and trained ordinary person reproduce the promised result from the system?”

## Tactics / how to apply
- Run the founder-removal test: list every result that degrades when the founder is absent and convert each dependency into a process, standard, training mechanism, or information system.
- Treat the current unit as a laboratory. Change one operational variable, measure the result, and only then make it standard.
- Build manuals around outcomes and critical checkpoints, not exhaustive descriptions of every possible action.
- Define the customer promise in observable terms—timing, presentation, accuracy, follow-up, and recovery—then make the operation capable of keeping it every time.
- Train employees to operate and improve the system; do not ask them to compensate indefinitely for a missing one.
- Delay replication until the prototype sustains acquisition, delivery, management, and economics without hidden founder labor.

## Notable examples
Gerber uses McDonald’s as the canonical prototype: Ray Kroc treated the restaurant—not the hamburger—as the product sold first to franchisees. Details were tested, documented, trained, and controlled so a customer could receive the same promise across locations and the operation could work in the hands of people other than its creator.
