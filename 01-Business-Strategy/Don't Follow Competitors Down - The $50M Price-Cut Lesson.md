# Don't Follow Competitors Down - The $50M Price-Cut Lesson

## One-line summary
When a competitor undercuts you, reacting by cutting your own price or copying their model usually destroys your economics without changing customer behavior; focus on the customer instead, and let rivals copy the model that doesn't make money.

## Principle
Competitive panic is expensive. The market leader who reacts to a discounter by matching them trades a healthy business for a broken one — and often the price change doesn't even move retention. The durable move is to keep your attention on the customer and your own economics; a rival copying a money-losing model is doing you a favor, not a harm.

## Scenario
Use this when a cheaper or flashier competitor appears, poaches testimonials or customers, or triggers the urge to relaunch, discount, or restructure. It applies to any category with visible rivals and any founder feeling "at war."

## Diagnosis
- A competitor undercuts you or offers more for less and your instinct is to match.
- You're contemplating a price cut, value-dump relaunch, or model change *in reaction* to them.
- You assume the rival is a genius (rather than possibly running on flashy funding that will bust).
- You're watching the competitor more than the customer.
- You haven't checked whether the reactive move would actually change churn or willingness to pay.

## Why It Happens
Ego reframes a business threat as a fight to be won, and fights invite overreaction. Cutting price feels decisive but frequently changes nothing about cancellation behavior once you're already above the "this is a lot of money" threshold — so you simply make less. Copying a competitor's model imports their (often worse) economics onto your larger base, multiplying the damage. Meanwhile the customer never asked you to react; they asked to be served.

## Recommended Action
1. When undercut, first ask whether matching would actually change customer behavior — usually it won't above a willingness-to-pay threshold.
2. Refuse to copy a competitor's model reflexively; a rival replicating a money-losing model is a gift ("let him figure that out for himself").
3. Assume competitors aren't morons — but also consider their business may be flashy, VC-funded, and unprofitable; two smart-money groups failing to make a model work is data.
4. When rivals go quantity, go quality — double down on community, service, and the things that actually retain.
5. Keep the eye on the customer: "you will never go out of business focusing on the customer."
6. Compute the lifetime cost of a reactive discount before making it; a small monthly cut can compound into an enormous lost exit multiple.

## Example from the AMA
A motocross-training founder feared a copycat replicating his low-margin single-day tour model; Hormozi's read was to welcome it — "he's going to copy the model that makes no money." He then told the story of his own costliest strategic error: as market leader, a cheaper one-on-one competitor poached his top testimonials, so he relaunched to his base with more value at a *lower* price, cutting ~$500k/month of top line (~$6M/year in profit). Churn didn't change at all — the price move was above the threshold that mattered — and after the eventual sale, that mistake cost him roughly $50M. The competitor's unprofitable model killed itself. His distilled lesson: don't follow competitors down; focus on the customer and you'll win.

## Caveats / Exceptions
- This is not an argument against ever changing price — it's against *reactive* changes that don't alter customer behavior.
- Sometimes a competitor genuinely has a better model; assess honestly rather than assuming either genius or fraud.
- Focusing on the customer still requires real differentiation (see [[Decommoditize Before You Discount - Make the Difference Obvious]]).
- A legitimate quality-vs-quantity stance must be backed by actual quality, not slogans.
