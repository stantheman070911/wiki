# Maximize Net Cash, Not ROAS - The Percentage Lies

## One-line summary
You can't pay overhead with ROAS — optimize for the largest absolute pile of net free cash flow, not the highest return-on-ad-spend ratio, even if that ratio (and other vanity metrics) has to fall as you scale.

## Context
Founders instinctively defend a high ROAS and refuse to let it drop, so they cap ad spend and cap the business with it. The ratio is a vanity metric; the bank balance is the score. Reframing from percentage to absolute dollars is what unlocks aggressive, profitable scaling.

## Key insights
- **The ratio is not the goal; net cash is.** Would you rather spend $5k/month at 5x ROAS, or $50k/month at 3.5x ROAS? The second returns far more absolute net cash even though the percentage is "worse."
- **"Money soldiers."** Every dollar of ad spend is a soldier sent out; all that matters is that soldiers return to camp with *more* soldiers — not the percentage growth of any one platoon. If ROAS alone were the goal, every guru posting 1,000% ROAS would be a billionaire.
- **Self-imposed metric caps become the real constraint.** "I don't want to go over $200 per booked call" is an arbitrary limit that throttles growth — if a higher cost-per-call unlocks 150 more leads and more net profit, the cap is the problem.
- **Scale is a capital-allocation question, not a ratio-defense question.** Decide how big you want to build, then choose the plays (more team, new geography, new category) that deploy the capital to get there.
- **Expect vanity metrics to dip on the way up.** CPL, cost-per-booked-call, and ROAS can all worsen while net free cash flow rises — that trade is usually correct.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Report and decide on **absolute net cash per period**, not ROAS %. Make ROAS a diagnostic, never the objective function.
- Before refusing to raise spend, ask: does the incremental spend still return more net cash after all costs? If yes, raise it — the ratio falling is fine.
- Delete arbitrary metric ceilings (max CPL, max cost-per-call); replace them with a break-even/contribution threshold tied to actual unit economics (see [[CAC, LTV, and Payback Period - The Three Numbers That Decide If You Win]]).
- When surplus cash appears, prefer reinvestment (team, offices, new markets) over consumption early — that's what turns one Ferrari's worth of money into five.

## Notable examples
- Business owners bragging about a 5x ROAS at $5k/month who won't scale to $10–15k because the ratio slips to 4x — leaving large net profit on the table.
- Suby: he could afford five Ferraris for years before buying one, because he reinvested that money into the business, team, offices, and new markets — compounding one Ferrari's worth into five.
