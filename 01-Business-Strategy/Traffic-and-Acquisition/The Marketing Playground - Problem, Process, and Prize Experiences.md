# The Marketing Playground - Problem, Process, and Prize Experiences

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[01-Business-Strategy/Business Strategy Index|Business Strategy]]

## One-line summary
Replace a pressure funnel with a set of useful experiences that help buyers understand their problem, learn a credible process, and verify the prize before choosing to buy.

## Context
Modern buyers move through many nonlinear touchpoints and resist being pushed from awareness to purchase. A marketing playground gives them several positive ways to progress according to their current question.

## Key insights
- **Buying readiness changes the information needed.** A problem-aware person needs diagnosis and empathy; a process-aware person needs principles and proof; a solution-ready person needs confidence in the product and outcome.
- **Problem content should be clinical.** Symptoms make the issue recognizable, causes make it intelligible, treatments make it actionable, and prognosis sets a realistic time horizon.
- **Process content teaches the path without forcing the product.** Books, workshops, cases, podcasts, and benchmarks show the customer how change occurs.
- **Prize experiences reduce decision risk.** Trials, demonstrations, calculators, customer events, consultations, and explainer videos let buyers inspect the likely outcome.
- **Multiple entrances outperform one rigid funnel.** Buyers can begin with the experience most relevant to their awareness, then move naturally to another.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
Build three experience zones:

1. **Problem:** diagnosis quiz, mini-course, discussion group, or content structured as symptoms → causes → treatments → prognosis.
2. **Process:** book, case study, workshop, podcast, or assessment explaining the method and best practices.
3. **Prize:** trial, one-to-one discussion, ROI calculator, customer experience, demonstration, or unboxing-style explanation of what purchase delivers.

Every experience should be valuable on its own and offer a clear but optional next step into the adjacent zone.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Interview customers to capture their pre-purchase language before writing problem-aware material.
- Map every existing asset to problem, process, or prize and identify the missing zone.
- Avoid introducing the product while the customer is still trying to name the problem.
- Use assessments differently at each stage: diagnosis, benchmark against the process, or readiness/ROI confirmation.
- Link experiences laterally as well as forward; a buyer may revisit the problem after learning about the process.
- Measure assisted conversion paths and time-to-readiness, not only the last touch.

## Notable examples
A lead-generation software company can use a course to explain why demand is failing, a blueprint to teach its methodology, and a free trial to let the buyer experience the solution. No single asset has to perform the entire sale.
