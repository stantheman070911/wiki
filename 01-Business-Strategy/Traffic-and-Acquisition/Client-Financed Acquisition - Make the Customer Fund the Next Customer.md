# Client-Financed Acquisition - Make the Customer Fund the Next Customer

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[01-Business-Strategy/Business Strategy Index|Business Strategy]]

## One-line summary
If a customer pays you more than it cost to acquire *and* fulfill them within the first 30 days, a credit card's interest-free window funds the next customer — removing cash as the ceiling on growth, and turning "scale" from a funding problem into a pure efficiency problem.

## Context
Most businesses have lifetime gross profit greater than acquisition cost (a good business) but not within the *first purchase* — so profit trickles in over months while ad spend goes out today, and that cash-flow gap, not the unit economics, is what actually caps growth. This entry names the mechanism that closes the gap and the two ratios used to judge whether ads are the problem or the business model is. It is the paid-ads-scaling counterpart to [[The Money Model - Sequencing Offers to Fund Growth]] (which builds the same idea from the offer-sequencing side) and complements [[Maximize Net Cash, Not ROAS - The Percentage Lies]].

## Key insights
- **The 30-day rule.** Any business can get ~30 days of interest-free money on a credit card. If 30-day cash collected exceeds CAC + cost to fulfill, you clear the card and recycle it endlessly — this is how Hormozi scaled every company past $1M/month within 12 months with no outside funding.
- **Judge ads by LTGP:CAC, not ROAS.** Compare **lifetime gross profit** (all a customer ever spends, minus cost to deliver) against **cost to acquire**. Below ~3:1, businesses struggle to scale; at/above 3:1 they take off. It's an observed pattern, not a law.
- **CAC between competitors is closer than you think.** Winners and losers in the same industry pay *similar* amounts to acquire a customer; the difference is how much they *make* per customer. So a "bad ads" complaint is often a bad *business model* (low LTGP).
- **Diagnose ads vs. model with the industry average.** If your CAC is within ~3× the industry average, the leverage is in raising LTGP (business model). If it's above ~3× average, the leverage is in the ads themselves.
- **Costs approach zero; revenue approaches infinity.** Past a point, shaving another dollar off CAC is harder than making another dollar of LTGP — so once acquisition is efficient enough, stop optimizing cost and grow value.
- **Don't confuse a sales problem with an advertising problem.** If engaged leads have the problem you solve and the money to spend but aren't buying, the ads work — the sales process is broken. Fixing the wrong one is expensive.

## Framework / model
### The mechanism
```
30-day cash collected  >  CAC + 30-day cost to fulfill   →  card cleared → recycle → repeat
```
When the first purchase alone doesn't clear it (e.g., a $115/mo membership at ~$10 gross/mo vs. $30 CAC → 3.3:1 lifetime but only $10 in month one), **close the gap with an immediate upsell**: a $100 100%-margin upsell taken by 1 in 5 adds ~$20 average → $30 in month one = break-even by day 30. Every later month is profit that funds more acquisition.

### The three phases of paid-ad spend
1. **Track money** — set up attribution before spending a dollar; otherwise you get cleaned out.
2. **Lose money** — expect to lose more often than you win; cap each new-ad test at ~2× the 30-day cash a customer generates, kill fast if it produces no leads by ~1× that cash.
3. **Print money** — once break-even or better, reverse the budget from your *customer-capacity* goal (not from a spend cap), padding ~20% for the efficiency loss at scale.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Engineer the money model so first-30-day cash clears CAC + fulfillment — usually via a fast upsell or a paid front door — *before* trying to scale spend.
- Report LTGP:CAC, not ROAS %; make ROAS a diagnostic only (see [[Maximize Net Cash, Not ROAS - The Percentage Lies]]).
- Benchmark your CAC against the industry average to decide where to work: within 3× → fix the model (raise LTGP); above 3× → fix the ads.
- Before blaming ads, run the one-question check: do engaged leads have the problem and the money? If yes and they're not buying, fix sales, not spend.
- Scale by choosing a customer-volume target and backing out the daily budget, rather than defending a ratio.

## Notable examples
- Hormozi grew from ~$400K/month (June) to $780K (July) to past $2.5M/month by testing new audiences and adding an affiliate layer — sustained only because 30-day cash kept clearing the card.
- A portfolio company spent 12 weeks and ~$150K on paid ads with the *right* leads on the phone who wouldn't buy — an advertising *diagnosis* that was actually a *sales* problem; giving up mid-fix cost an estimated ~$1M in enterprise value.
