# The Fishing Is Best Where the Fewest Go - Underpriced Channels and the Contrarian Move

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[01-Business-Strategy/Business Strategy Index|Business Strategy]]

## One-line summary
When every competitor is funnel-hacking the same playbook in the same channels, cut-through comes from doing the opposite — advertising where none of them are, where attention is still underpriced.

## Context
Copying the biggest competitors' funnels and ads feels safe but guarantees you compete on their turf, at their prices, with their disadvantages amplified for a smaller player. The contrarian move — go where the fishing is best because the fewest go — turns a resource disadvantage into an attention advantage.

## Key insights
- **Crowded channels are expensive and undifferentiated.** When thousands of competitors bid the same keywords and clone the same funnels, cost-per-click is bid up and your message blends in.
- **The fishing is best where the fewest go.** An underused channel offers cheap attention and sky-high cut-through simply because you're the only one there.
- **Do the opposite, don't copy.** Sometimes the best move is not to imitate the biggest competitors but to do the exact reverse of what they're doing.
- **A resource disadvantage can be flipped.** Not being able to compete in the expensive channel is the reason to find a cheaper, emptier one — the constraint forces the edge.
- **A distinctive identity amplifies an empty channel.** Being memorable (an outrageous name) compounds the cut-through you already get from being the only advertiser there.

## Tactics / how to apply
- List where all your competitors advertise; then deliberately look for a channel none of them use and test it small.
- Negotiate creatively into premium inventory you can't afford at rate-card: offer a lower price for *unsold* remnant spots ("run me only when you can't sell it").
- Don't wait for polished production — a "blind read" on a cheap mic that runs is worth more than a perfect ad that never ships.
- Measure cut-through, not just CPC: an emptier channel can deliver far more qualified leads per dollar even at an unglamorous production quality.

## Notable examples
- Suby's King Kong launch: late to a market with thousands of agencies; "SEO agency" clicks cost ~$36 and big firms spent ~$150k/month on Google Ads. He couldn't compete there. Instead he wired a radio station $7,000 to run his remnant drive-time spots at $150 each (vs. $500 rate-card) whenever they couldn't sell the inventory — recorded on a cheap Blue Yeti as a blind read. The spots ran and produced ~700 leads in the first month, because he was the only digital-marketing agency on radio — and the outrageous name "King Kong" (vs. generic "best web results" competitors) amplified the cut-through.
