# The Creative Volume Engine - Ads Are an At-Bats Game

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[01-Business-Strategy/Business Strategy Index|Business Strategy]]

## One-line summary
Paid acquisition is a numbers game played with creative: you need enough daily spend to get regular at-bats and enough new creative to feed the machine, because at low volume a working funnel just looks like bad luck.

## Principle
Winning ads are discovered, not authored. You cannot know in advance which hook or angle will convert, so the constraint is the *rate at which you can test*. Two levers set that rate: daily spend (how many at-bats you buy) and creative volume (how many different shots you take). Under-resourcing either one starves the engine and hides the fact that the model already works.

## Scenario
Use this when a business has a proven offer and positive unit economics but growth is slow, lumpy, or "inconsistent" — three dead weeks then one great week. It is especially relevant for high-ticket funnels with high CAC, for founders spending a few hundred to a couple thousand dollars a day, and for any creative-led acquisition (Meta, TikTok, YouTube).

## Diagnosis
- Results feel volatile: a stretch of nothing, then a cluster of sales in a day.
- Daily spend is far below CAC, so a single sale takes several days to materialize.
- The team ships a handful of new creatives per week, not per day.
- Media buyers optimize the pixel on the final purchase, which never accumulates enough events to teach the algorithm.
- The founder judges the month by whether it broke even rather than by at-bats and learning.

## Why It Happens
When CAC is $3,500 and spend is $1,300/day, you buy roughly one customer every three days — the same conversion rate feels like chaos because the sample is tiny. **Insufficient volume feels like volatility.** With too few creatives, the algorithm has nothing fresh to serve and fatigue sets in fast. And optimizing toward a rare event (the close) gives the pixel too weak a signal; a mid-funnel event you can generate by the thousand teaches it far faster.

## Recommended Action
1. Raise daily spend toward CAC so you can win at least one customer per day and read results on a daily cadence.
2. Treat the business as an experiment for reps first and profit second until the model is nailed; then scale.
3. Manufacture creative volume cheaply: film winners on an iPhone with a teleprompter app, walk-and-talk your best hooks, and turn winners into static images. An hour can yield 20–30 ads; a real baseline is closer to 100/week (industrial engines run hundreds per day).
4. Once a hook wins, reuse it and vary only the "meat" and the background rather than starting from scratch.
5. Optimize the pixel on a high-volume qualified event (e.g. the application step) with a separate thank-you page for qualified traffic, not on the final sale.
6. Separate media buying (the easy part — one buyer can run it) from creative production (the real constraint).

## Example from the AMA
A fitness-coaching business co-founded with a well-known athlete was spending $1,300/day at a $3,500 CAC and producing about five ads per week, then wondering why revenue was erratic. Hormozi's fix was structural: push spend toward CAC (~$3,600/day) for one sale a day, lift creative from ~5/week to ~100/week, and optimize the pixel on the qualified-application page. He contrasted the volume directly — "we put out 250 new ads per day … you're putting out five per week." A separate caller building AI video ads was told the market doesn't want a few beautiful $18k commercials; it wants 100 pieces of creative at ~$50 each, because advertisers "don't know and I don't know which one's going to work."

## Caveats / Exceptions
- Volume cannot rescue a broken offer or a funnel that leaks before the creative ever matters; fix decommoditization and the sales motion first.
- Spending to CAC is only prudent once the model is proven; do not scale losses.
- More creative is not more *random* creative — reuse winning hooks and iterate rather than reinventing every asset.
- Brands with large existing awareness may not need more top-of-funnel recognition, only more creative and spend.
