# CAC, LTV, and Payback Period - The Three Numbers That Decide If You Win

## One-line summary
Every business reduces to three numbers — customer acquisition cost (CAC), lifetime value (LTV), and payback period — and whoever can afford to spend the most to acquire a customer, and get that money back the fastest, wins the market.

## Context
Suby built a $250M agency and generated $7.8B in client sales across 1,000+ niches; this is the unit-economics model he says every one of those businesses ultimately runs on, whether they've measured it or not. It's the finance-side counterpart to offer-construction frameworks like [[The Value Equation and the Grand Slam Offer]] — this entry is about whether the resulting numbers actually work.

## Key insights
- **Nothing happens until something is sold.** Most owners avoid customer acquisition because it's confrontational — easier to tweak a website or a logo than ask strangers for money — but acquisition is the one activity that builds enterprise value; everything else is downstream of it.
- **He who can spend the most to acquire a customer wins.** Ad costs only rise over time. A business with a higher LTV can outbid every competitor for the same customer, buy up the available inventory of attention, and simply outmuscle the market — so raising LTV is worth infinitely more than shaving a few points off cost-per-lead.
- **All customer acquisition is paid.** Referrals and organic content feel free but aren't — you're paying with time and human capital instead of ad spend. Only paid acquisition is a scientific, repeatable equation: put a dollar of input in, get a predictable number of leads out.
- **Cash flow, not ROAS, is the oxygen of the business.** You cannot pay rent or payroll with a return-on-ad-spend percentage — only with cash that has actually come back. A healthy LTV:CAC ratio on paper means nothing if the cash trough between spending and recouping is too deep to survive.
- **Most businesses spend their time on the wrong side of the ledger.** The default is to spend 80% of effort trying to lower CAC and 20% trying to raise LTV. The biggest businesses invert this — they spend roughly 80% of their effort on retention, upsells, and increasing what a customer is worth, because a small LTV increase compounds into a much larger allowable CAC.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
**The three numbers:**
1. **LTV (lifetime value)** — total revenue over the last 12–36 months ÷ number of customers in that period, adjusted for typical profit margin. Example: $250,000 revenue ÷ 110 clients = $2,279 LTV.
2. **CAC (customer acquisition cost)** — every cost tied to converting attention into a customer (agency fees, sales team, media spend).
3. **Payback period** — how long it takes to recoup the CAC in cash, not just on paper (revenue can trickle back over 90/180/360-day windows even when the lifetime LTV:CAC ratio looks healthy).

**Target ratios:**
| LTV:CAC | Read |
|---|---|
| 1:1 | Losing proposition — not making money |
| 3:1 | The sweet spot — profitable and still worth spending aggressively |
| 5:1+ | Usually a sign of *under*-spending — could acquire more customers before hitting diminishing returns |

Rule of thumb: be willing to spend up to ~33% of LTV to acquire a customer.

**Shortening payback period:**
- E-commerce: target ~90 days; the fastest lever is a one-click post-purchase upsell (discounted add-on, expedited shipping, extended warranty) that lifts day-one ROAS.
- Service business: front-load setup fees, deposits, and paid upsells rather than relying on total contract value trickling in over time.
- Target: recoup CAC within a single credit-card billing cycle (~30 days, interest-free) so financing ad spend never becomes the bottleneck.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Calculate your actual LTV and CAC before touching an ad account — most owners are optimizing blind.
- Compute your theoretical maximum CAC (LTV × 0.33) and treat anything below it as room to scale spend, not a number to protect.
- Track cash-flow payback period separately from ROAS/ROI — they answer different questions, and only payback period tells you if the business can survive its own growth.
- Add a one-click upsell to the immediate post-purchase flow as the single fastest lever on payback period.
- Redirect discretionary time from CAC-reduction tactics (cheaper clicks, better targeting) toward LTV-raising ones (retention, upsells, higher-margin backend offers) once CAC is at a workable ratio.

## Notable examples
- The $250,000 revenue ÷ 110 clients = $2,279 LTV worked example.
- "Money soldier" analogy: every ad dollar is a soldier sent to war whose only job is to come back with more soldiers — the payback period is how long the trip takes.
- SaaS businesses with 5-year payback periods still raising venture capital because the LTV:CAC ratio is healthy even though the cash-recoupment window is long — illustrating why payback period and LTV:CAC are tracked as separate numbers.
- Businesses that raise ad spend from $10K/month to $50K/month and see 30-day ROAS drop, but need to be evaluated on lifetime ROAS instead of first-purchase ROAS before concluding the spend increase failed.
