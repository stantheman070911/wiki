# Buy Bigger - Why Small Acquisitions Break and the Founder Is Usually the Constraint

## One-line summary
Small businesses look cheap to buy and easy to grow, but they cap out because the founder who could only reach a few million is usually the constraint — so acquirers who want scale learn to buy bigger businesses with better talent, and to spend their own time generating demand.

## Principle
Acquisition math that looks obvious ("buy for nothing, take it from $5M to $50M") ignores that a business is limited by its people, and a founder who plateaued at a few million typically is the ceiling. Buying bigger businesses buys better founders and teams; it's why sophisticated capital gravitates upmarket despite the higher entry price. And the acquirer's own highest-leverage act is generating demand, not babysitting portfolio fires.

## Scenario
Use this for anyone acquiring and holding businesses, roll-up operators, or a founder building a holding company and deciding where to spend attention across a portfolio of small companies.

## Diagnosis
- You keep playing babysitter to acquired founders and fighting the same day-to-day fires.
- Portfolio companies are small and each has a hundred problems at once.
- Acquired founders aren't driving 10x thinking — they're stuck in the day-to-day.
- You assume the constraint is deals/capital when it's the caliber of people you bought.
- You're reinventing the wheel that professional acquirers already learned.

## Why It Happens
Small companies come with a founder-shaped ceiling: the person who capped at $3M often lacks the ambition or ability to reach $30M, and someone who already made their money is hard to re-motivate. Good founders instinctively ask "what one thing 10xes this?"; mediocre ones get sucked into the day-to-day and stagnate for years — so a portfolio of small acquisitions is really a portfolio of "who" problems. Winners either win or die; they won't quietly stay flat, which is exactly the profile you need and rarely get at the small end.

## Recommended Action
1. Bias toward bigger acquisitions: an operator who reached $20M ARR is usually a better entrepreneur than one who capped at $3M.
2. Treat the constraint as a "who" problem — find winners inside each business or install a genuinely good operator (rare and worth paying for).
3. Be willing to exit or hand back companies that aren't winners; don't subsidize non-winners with your attention.
4. Match every hire to the business's actual constraint (supply vs demand), not to a generic org chart.
5. Spend your own time on the highest-leverage act — generating demand/building brand — which pulls in talent, deal flow, and everything else.
6. Prefer buy-and-hold where capable founders roll and stay; remove founders only when you can replace them with someone better.

## Example from the AMA
A buy-and-hold software group ($12M across four companies) asked how to balance growing existing businesses with new acquisitions. Hormozi said the acquisition side is straightforward (generate deal flow); the hard part is that small acquired businesses "have like a hundred problems that are wrong all at the same time," and "usually it is the founder that's the problem." He shared that of 24 acquisitions he made in 2020–2023, he sold or gave back his shares in 19 because the founders "just weren't winners," and that he now spends most of his time looking for winners. His conclusion: the reason professional acquirers "just buy bigger" is that bigger businesses come with better founders and teams. He also framed his own job as mostly demand generation, "the highest leverage thing I can do."

## Caveats / Exceptions
- Bigger deals cost more and carry their own integration risk; "buy bigger" is about talent quality, not size for its own sake.
- Removing a founder can tank a business if you can't replace them with someone genuinely better (the classic PE failure).
- Some small businesses hold a rare winner worth backing hard; the point is to find winners, wherever they are.
- Founders who've already made their money may still be great operators if the mission re-engages them — assess motivation directly.
