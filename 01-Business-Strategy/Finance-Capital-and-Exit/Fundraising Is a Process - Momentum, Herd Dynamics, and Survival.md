# Fundraising Is a Process - Momentum, Herd Dynamics, and Survival

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[01-Business-Strategy/Business Strategy Index|Business Strategy]]

## One-line summary
Raise money as a concentrated, time-bounded process: demonstrate facts rather than sell enthusiasm, create parallel investor momentum, accept workable terms promptly, and never let fundraising consume the company.

## Context
Investor interest is highly social and path-dependent. Sequential, open-ended fundraising lets every investor wait for someone else while the startup loses time and bargaining power.

## Key insights
- **Conviction should rest on the company.** The strongest pitch is evidence—growth, engaged users, a formidable team—not rhetorical pressure.
- **Investor opinion is recursive.** One credible commitment raises the probability of another, while visible hesitation produces the opposite cascade.
- **Run a batch, not a queue.** Parallel conversations create comparable decision windows and prevent months of serial rejection.
- **Optimize for sufficient capital and good partners, not the theoretical maximum valuation.** A clean close returns founders to product work.
- **Deals are background processes until signed.** Depending emotionally or operationally on an unsigned deal damages morale and negotiating behavior.
- **Raise by milestones.** Each round should buy enough progress to reach the evidence required for the next financing gear or profitability.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
Use a four-phase process: **prepare evidence and target list → open conversations in a tight window → create explicit decision deadlines and close committed investors → stop fundraising and execute the milestone**. Maintain a no-funding survival branch throughout.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Describe the company in one compelling, accurate sentence.
- Lead with what has been built, what users do, and the rate of change.
- Schedule likely investors close together and communicate real commitments without bluffing.
- Ask directly what remains unresolved and whether the investor intends to proceed.
- Do not treat verbal interest, introductions, or draft documents as cash.
- End the process once the round is sufficient; fundraising has severe opportunity cost.

## Notable examples
Convertible notes enabled “high-resolution fundraising”: different investors could close at different times and prices, rewarding decisive participants while breaking the deadlock created by everyone asking who else was investing.
