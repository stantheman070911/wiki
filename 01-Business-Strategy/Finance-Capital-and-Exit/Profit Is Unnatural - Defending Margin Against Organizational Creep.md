# Profit Is Unnatural - Defending Margin Against Organizational Creep

## One-line summary
Profit does not preserve itself: organizations naturally spend available surplus, so margin requires a deliberate owner, explicit constraints, and proof before recurring cost is added.

## Context
As revenue grows, every department can produce reasonable arguments for more people, tools, and programs. Individually defensible expenses accumulate until the business consumes its own margin.

## Key insights
- **Revenue growth does not guarantee profit growth.** Without discipline, costs rise to meet available cash.
- **Profit needs an active defender.** Someone must repeatedly reject expenses that do not create sufficient incremental return.
- **Hiring pain is not proof of need.** A role should relieve a measured constraint, not merely make the current discomfort disappear.
- **Temporary profit is not automatically distributable.** Service and digital businesses often need to reinvest in scarce talent and durable brand, but reinvestment still requires a return thesis.
- **Industry averages can normalize waste.** A mediocre benchmark is not a physical limit on margin.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
Before adding a recurring expense, require four answers:

1. What binding constraint does this remove?
2. What measurable output should improve?
3. What is the smallest reversible test?
4. When will the expense be removed if the output does not appear?

Assign one leader explicit responsibility for preserving the gap between revenue and cost rather than assuming every function will self-regulate.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Review recurring expenses separately from one-time experiments.
- Track contribution margin as revenue grows; flag expenses growing faster than the output they support.
- Use automation, process simplification, or stronger individual contributors before adding layers of coordination.
- Create sunset dates for experimental tools, contractors, and roles.
- Reinvest deliberately in talent and brand while retaining a written expected-return case.
- Keep a zero-based question in reviews: “If we did not already pay for this, would we start today?”

## Notable examples
Hormozi relays a billionaire mentor's observation that businesses slowly eat their own profits because people keep finding reasons to spend. The practical countermeasure is a person who repeatedly holds expenses down while revenue rises, preserving the widening spread rather than allowing headcount to absorb it.
