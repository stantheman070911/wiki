# Wealth Creation - Measurement, Leverage, and Compressed Work

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[01-Business-Strategy/Business Strategy Index|Business Strategy]]

## One-line summary
Wealth is created by making things people want; startups let small groups measure their contribution more directly, use technology as leverage, and compress decades of ordinary output into a few intense years.

## Context
Money transfers claims on existing value, while wealth creation increases what people can use. Understanding the distinction clarifies why equity, risk, and concentrated work can produce unusually large outcomes without requiring a zero-sum explanation.

## Key insights
- **Wealth is useful capability, not currency.** Products and services make people richer when they improve what can be done with available time and resources.
- **Measurement changes effort.** Small teams make individual contribution more visible than large organizations, allowing rewards to track results more closely.
- **Leverage multiplies a contribution.** Software and other reproducible technology let one piece of work serve many people at low marginal cost.
- **Startups compress work.** Founders exchange higher intensity and risk now for a chance to produce the value of a much longer conventional career.
- **Equity aligns reward with total value created.** The rational amount to give an investor or employee depends on whether their addition increases the remaining stake's expected value.
- **Large fortunes increasingly come from building.** As technology lowers startup costs and expands reach, creating scalable companies displaces inheritance and resource extraction among the largest outcomes.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
Analyze an opportunity through **value per user × number of users × degree of personal contribution × ownership retained**. Then account for probability, time, dilution, and the opportunity cost of concentrating years into the attempt.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Ask what concrete capability the work creates, not only how it will be monetized.
- Choose environments where performance can be observed and rewarded.
- Seek tools or systems that reproduce good work across many users.
- Evaluate equity exchanges with the equation: will the new party raise the value of the company enough that your smaller percentage is worth more?
- Preserve runway so the high-intensity period has time to reach evidence.

## Notable examples
Viaweb's small team built software that served many merchants, combining direct measurement of a few people's work with software replication. The same structure explains why technology founders can create and capture more value than equally hardworking people in low-leverage settings.
