# Default Alive or Default Dead - Runway, Growth, and the Fatal Pinch

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[01-Business-Strategy/Business Strategy Index|Business Strategy]]

## One-line summary
Know early whether current cash, expenses, and revenue growth lead to profitability; if they do not, cut burn or fix growth before slow growth and short runway become an irreversible fatal pinch.

## Context
Vague optimism about the next funding round prevents founders from confronting a deteriorating trajectory. Survival improves when facts and hopes are written separately and a fallback begins while choices still exist.

## Key insights
- **Default alive/dead is a trajectory, not a mood.** Hold expenses constant, extend recent revenue growth, and ask whether cash lasts until profitability.
- **The fatal pinch combines three conditions:** default dead, slow growth, and too little time to change either.
- **Fundraising is never guaranteed rescue.** Even fast-growing companies can spook investors; a precise survival plan must exist independently.
- **Overhiring is the common accelerant.** Founders mistake the large teams of successful companies for the cause of success when they are usually its effect.
- **Fix appeal before adding capacity.** More people rarely repair a moderately appealing early product and often make rapid evolution harder.
- **Ramen profitability changes bargaining power.** Covering founders' living costs removes an immediate dependency on investors and preserves time to learn.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
Each month calculate **cash remaining ÷ net monthly burn = runway**, then project revenue using a conservative recent growth rate. Maintain three dates: the estimated profitability date, the date fundraising must succeed, and the earlier date at which the survival plan automatically begins.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Put default status on the monthly operating review; do not wait until the final year of runway.
- Write a no-funding plan with named cuts and a trigger date.
- Treat hiring as a consequence of proven demand or a clearly identified bottleneck.
- Before adding headcount, test whether founders can improve growth through product revision, direct sales, or manual service.
- Minimize fixed costs until customers demonstrate that the company has made something they want.

## Notable examples
Airbnb waited four months after YC fundraising to hire its first employee. The founders remained overloaded, but their labor was directed at evolving the product rather than scaling an uncertain model.
