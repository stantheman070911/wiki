# How Money Is Made - Value, Return on Time, and Money Rules

## One-line summary
Money is voluntary value exchange; your earnings equal value created × ability to negotiate × how replaceable you are, and the discipline of getting rich (once) comes down to a handful of money rules about time, speed, and spending.

## Context
This consolidates Hormozi's "money rules" — the principles he says separate losing all his money twice from keeping it after $100M net worth.

## Key insights
- **Money is made by voluntary exchange.** You give someone value first; they voluntarily give money back (anything else is theft). Both parties say thank you — both believe they're better off.
- **The earnings equation.** Career earnings = (value you create in total) × (your ability to negotiate your share) × (scarcity — how few others can create that value). All three are required; if someone will do it cheaper, your position disappears. Uniqueness comes from your *skill stack*, which is your moat.
- **"Passive income is a lie."** Everything is return-on-time; there's no truly active or passive, only how much you make per hour. Stop hunting scalable/passive shortcuts and instead multiply the active activity already earning far above your hourly average.
- **Buy time like the rich, buy stuff like the poor.** The average person spends ~$1,500/mo on ~96 hours of chores; if you earn >$15/hr, buying that back and reinvesting the high-energy hours makes you money twice (earnings + faster skill growth).
- **"If you can't buy it twice, don't buy it once."** Price purchases in the post-tax hours of work they cost (Hormozi's Smoothie King "two hours per lunch" realization); saving doesn't make money but lets you look further out and take bigger, money-making risks.
- **You should only have to get rich once.** Don't bet what you have and need for something you don't have and don't need.
- **Automate investing, add friction to spending.** Skim investment off the top before you see it; make spending manual (write checks, delete shopping apps, don't save cards). Invest first, spend the rest (Switzerland's high millionaire density as the "different rules" case).

## Framework / model (if applicable)
**"It's easier to make a million than $100k."** $100k assumes trading time for wages, capping you. A million forces a different *solution set* — more skill in less time (walk one block vs. drive five). Thinking bigger changes your tools.

**The 10-year, 10x contract.** Would you make no money for 10 years to add a zero (or two) to your net worth on year 10? Most say yes — so "act like it": make reasonable compounding bets and winning becomes inevitable rather than a game of chance.

**Partnership rule.** A partner must bring one of three things you lack — money, experience, or time/energy — and must *clearly contribute* it. Partnerships guarantee the largest decrease in your earning power (you pay in equity, the most expensive currency). Hormozi had nine failed partnerships, often from insecurity/fear of going solo.

**Closing maxim:** "Money loves speed, wealth loves time, poverty loves indecision." By the time you have all the information, the opportunity is gone — decide fast, correct later, because deciding (not doing) is what bogs people down.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Compute your real hourly rate (annual income ÷ ~2,000, or ÷ ~8,000 for per-waking-hour) and outsource anything below it.
- Check your bank account before social media each morning — "what gets measured gets managed" (Hormozi did this daily until ~$20M net worth).
- Manage time before money: the best returns on money come from those who best invest their time.
- Vet every partnership against the money/experience/time-and-energy test and clear contribution.

## Notable examples
- Hormozi's nine simultaneous income streams (chiro agency, dental agency, gym turnarounds, six gyms) fed his ego, not his bank account — spreading seven ways, not multiplying. Cutting to one thing (Leila's prompt) was the turnaround.
- Realizing a $500 sale took 30 minutes while a month of grocery/prep/cleaning cost $500 and far more time — the case for buying time back.
