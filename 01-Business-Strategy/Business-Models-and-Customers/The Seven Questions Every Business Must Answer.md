# The Seven Questions Every Business Must Answer

## One-line summary
Missing any one of seven questions is enough to doom a business, no matter how important the underlying need — the 2000s cleantech bubble (thousands of companies, $50B+ invested, most bankrupt) shows what happens when founders answer zero of them well, while Tesla shows what happens when a company answers all seven.

## Context
Thiel uses the cleantech bubble as a diagnostic case study precisely because the *macro* thesis (the world needs new energy) was true, and it still produced a historic wave of failures — proof that being right about a sector-level need is not the same as having a viable company, and that the checklist matters more than the conviction.

## Key insights
- **The seven questions, stated plainly.** *Engineering* — can you create breakthrough technology instead of an incremental improvement? *Timing* — is now the right time to start this specific business? *Monopoly* — are you starting with a big share of a small market? *People* — do you have the right team? *Distribution* — do you have a way to not just create but deliver the product? *Durability* — will your market position be defensible 10–20 years out? *Secret* — have you identified a unique opportunity that others don't see?
- **Cleantech failed almost all seven, systematically.** *Engineering*: Solyndra's cylindrical solar cells were only ~1/π as efficient as flat ones per unit area — a worse starting point than the incumbent, not a 10x-better one. *Timing*: SpectraWatt's CEO compared 2008 solar to 1970s microprocessors, but silicon solar cells dated to 1954 with only linear efficiency gains since — there was no exponential inflection to catch. *Monopoly*: cleantech pitches opened with "energy is a trillion-dollar market" as if that were good news, when huge markets mean brutal competition, not easy wins — and founders inflated their own market share by choosing whichever denominator (their city, their country, global solar, all renewables, all power generation) made the percentage look best that week. *People*: Founders Fund's own (only half-joking) screening rule was to pass on any cleantech CEO who wore a suit to a pitch meeting — real technologists dress like technologists, and executives who look like salesmen are usually worse at both sales and tech than they appear. *Distribution*: Better Place raised and spent $800M+ on a confusing electric-car-plus-battery-swap product nobody could clearly explain how to buy, and sold roughly 1,000 cars before its 2013 bankruptcy. *Durability*: nearly every failed solar company blamed cheap Chinese competition after the fact, but the trend (China's manufacturing cost advantage, and separately the fracking boom that crashed natural gas prices 70%+ by 2013) was visible in advance to anyone who asked "what stops a bigger, cheaper competitor from wiping me out?" *Secret*: cleantech companies mostly justified themselves with a conventional truth everyone already agreed on (the world needs cleaner energy) rather than a specific, non-obvious insight about how to win.
- **Tesla answered all seven ("7 for 7").** *Technology*: integration, not any single component, was the 10x win — the Model S was rated higher than any car Consumer Reports had reviewed. *Timing*: Musk read the 2010 window for a $465M DOE loan as one-time-only (correctly — Solyndra's collapse politicized the subsidy question 18 months later) and moved before it closed. *Monopoly*: started in the tiny, high-margin luxury EV sports-car niche (~3,000 Roadsters at $109K) before expanding to the larger luxury-sedan market. *People*: an engineer-salesman CEO built a team he explicitly called "Special Forces." *Distribution*: Tesla chose to own its entire sales and service chain rather than depend on independent dealerships — higher upfront cost, more control and brand strength. *Durability*: speed plus head start plus founder-still-in-charge (unlike every legacy automaker) compounds the lead. *Secret*: Tesla's insight was that cleantech was a fashion/status phenomenon as much as an environmental one, and built a car people wanted to be seen in, not just a car that felt virtuous.
- **The "social entrepreneurship" framing made things worse, not better.** Treating a cleantech company's goal as simultaneously "socially good" and "financially good" let founders substitute a broad, universally-applauded goal (clean energy) for a specific, differentiated plan — but broad, agreed-upon goals produce undifferentiated products by definition (this is the same trap as [[Monopoly vs. Competition - Why All Happy Companies Are Different]]'s "competitive lies"). Doing something genuinely different is what's good for society *and* what lets a business capture value — the two aren't in tension, they're the same move.
- **Getting 5 or 6 of 7 right can still work; the cleantech bubble's distinguishing failure was going to market with essentially zero good answers** and expecting the macro need alone to carry the company.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
**Use the seven questions as a pre-mortem checklist for any new business, not just cleantech or energy.** Write one sentence per question with your actual, specific answer (not "the market is huge" as a monopoly answer, not "we're passionate" as a people answer). A checklist with several one-line non-answers is the same red flag whether the sector is solar panels, SaaS, or a restaurant.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Before funding or joining a venture, force a written one-line answer to each of the seven questions; treat any answer that restates the macro opportunity ("energy/AI/healthcare is huge") rather than the specific plan as an unanswered question.
- Distrust founder teams that dress or present in a way optimized to look fundable rather than to look like people doing the actual work — treat mismatched signaling as data, not noise.
- When assessing durability, explicitly ask "what stops a well-resourced competitor (often from a lower-cost country or an adjacent incumbent) from replicating this in 3–5 years?" — if there's no answer, that's the durability question failing, regardless of current traction.
- Separate "is this sector important" from "is this specific company's plan good" — a true macro thesis is necessary but nowhere near sufficient.

## Notable examples
- More than 40 solar manufacturers went bankrupt or shut down in 2012 alone; Solyndra is the most famous case but far from the only one (Evergreen Solar, Abound Solar, Energy Conversion Devices, Q-Cells, SpectraWatt all cited).
- MiaSolé's CEO told Congress his company was one of several "very strong" competitors in thin-film solar, then minutes later predicted it would become the world's largest thin-film producer within a year — a live example of the monopoly-question self-contradiction; the company was later acquired for hundreds of millions less than investors had put in.
