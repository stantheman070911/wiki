# The Lifestyle Boutique Operating Model - Team, Product Ecosystem, and Campaigns

## One-line summary
A profitable lifestyle boutique combines a 4–12 person role-complete team, founder-led demand, a four-part product ecosystem, a simple operating cadence, and three campaign horizons without crossing accidentally into a larger-company structure.

## Context
A solo expert with assistants still owns a job, while a 13–25 person company often has more coordination cost than its economics can support. The lifestyle boutique is a deliberately bounded operating model optimized for profit, flexibility, and manageable complexity.

## Key insights
- **Four to twelve people is a design constraint.** Below four, the founder remains indispensable to delivery; above twelve, functional groups and management layers begin to form.
- **Founder-led growth is a role, not a failure to delegate.** The founder pitches, publishes, develops products, raises profile, and forms strategic partnerships while a general manager runs daily operations.
- **Every business has demand, supply, and operations.** Demand needs marketing and sales; supply needs product/delivery and customer success; operations needs finance, data/IT, and coordination.
- **Profit lives in the ecosystem.** A gift earns attention, a product for prospects builds trust, a core offer creates transformation, and a product for clients maintains the result or relationship.
- **Campaigns need three time horizons.** The repeatable week supplies baseline revenue, quarterly spotlights create event-driven spikes, and an annual big message compounds brand.
- **Simplicity preserves lifestyle.** One communication channel, a small tool stack, disciplined file storage, and a sleep-at-night dashboard outperform enterprise complexity at this scale.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
Design four linked layers:

1. **Team:** founder/KPI, general manager, marketing, sales, product/delivery, customer success, finance, and IT/data—combining roles until economics support specialization.
2. **Products:** gift → product for prospects → core offer → product for clients.
3. **Cadence:** quarterly reset → Monday three-to-six priorities → Friday done/not-done debrief.
4. **Campaigns:** perfect repeatable week → quarterly spotlight → annual big message.

As a financial floor, test whether the model can support roughly $125,000 in annual revenue per core team member; replace the heuristic with a proper scenario model before hiring.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Reverse-engineer the desired 4–12 person company instead of incrementally repairing today's founder-dependent job.
- Hire operational leadership from demanding small-unit environments when the role requires coordination more than prestige credentials.
- Make the path between all four products explicit and assign each team function to a customer transition.
- Keep all team communication in one agreed channel and all reusable assets in a shared filing system.
- Build a small dashboard around cash, pipeline, delivery risks, customer health, and near-term milestones.
- Stop at twelve people unless the owner deliberately chooses the capital, executive team, and stress of crossing to a performance business.

## Notable examples
Priestley notes that complex, low-margin restaurant units are routinely run by young general managers. Many expert-service businesses overestimate the credentials needed to operate their simpler daily system and keep the founder trapped unnecessarily.
