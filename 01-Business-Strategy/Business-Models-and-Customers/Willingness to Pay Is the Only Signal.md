# Willingness to Pay Is the Only Signal

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[01-Business-Strategy/Business Strategy Index|Business Strategy]] → [[01-Business-Strategy/Business-Models-and-Customers/Business Models and Customers Map|Business Models and Customers]]

## One-line summary
Someone *saying* they need your product is not the same as them *having* the problem — the only signal that a market is real is a credit card, so build for whoever has the biggest pain and will actually pay, and treat downloads, signups, and views as vanity until they convert to revenue.

## Context
Natalie Barbou built Rella (a tool for the creator economy). Version 1.0 was "for everyone" on social media — influencers, managers, agencies — and it nearly killed the company: a "Frankenstein" product no one understood, low willingness to pay, high churn, $25k left in the bank. The pivot to a single paying avatar (social-media managers/teams) took Rella from ~$3,500/month to $1M ARR in 11 months, then $2M. The lessons are about how to find a real market.

## Key insights
- **"I need this" ≠ having the problem.** Influencers told her "this is exactly what I need," then never used or paid. Enthusiasm in a demo is not demand. The test is: put it in the market, ask for a credit card, and see if they pay. "If you're not making money as a business, you have a hobby."
- **Ask two questions to find the market:** *who has the biggest problem?* and *who is willing to pay to solve it?* Their intersection is your avatar. For Rella it was social-media managers, whose pain was deep enough that they paid despite bugs.
- **Make the customer's pain 10× bigger, not the product 10× better.** First-time founders obsess over building something 10× better than competitors; usually you can't, and changing mass behavior is too slow for your runway (only the iPhone/ChatGPT-tier products do it). Instead, find a pain so deep that people use a *glitchy* product over their current tools — that's your white space.
- **Go with the behavioral current, not against it.** Don't try to change where behavior is; find where behavior already is, price it, and go there — "like going with a current." Trying to convert influencers who wouldn't pay was swimming upstream; social-media managers were already paying-shaped.
- **Vanity metrics lie; paid conversion is truth.** Downloads, signups, and app-store rank "don't matter truly." The only number that matters is free-trial → paid. Money is the growth metric that tells you whether to pivot.
- **Test the pain before you build.** Waitlists, customer interviews, watching how they work day-to-day — and be honest about whether "they'd use this" is real or your own delusion.

## Application
- Before scaling anything, force the willingness-to-pay test: ask for the card. Track free-to-paid conversion, not signups.
- Name one avatar by the intersection of *biggest problem* × *will pay*; when in doubt, follow the money to the segment already paying for adjacent solutions.
- Judge product-market fit by whether people tolerate an imperfect product because the pain is severe — not by demo enthusiasm.
- Kill vanity dashboards; put revenue (or paid conversion) as the single north-star metric that triggers persist-or-pivot decisions.
