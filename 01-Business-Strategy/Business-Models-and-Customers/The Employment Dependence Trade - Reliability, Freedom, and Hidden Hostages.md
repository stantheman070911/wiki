# The Employment Dependence Trade - Reliability, Freedom, and Hidden Hostages

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[01-Business-Strategy/Business Strategy Index|Business Strategy]] → [[01-Business-Strategy/Business-Models-and-Customers/Business Models and Customers Map|Business Models and Customers]]

## One-line summary
Employment exchanges autonomy for mutual dependability: the organization buys priority and compliance while the worker buys income stability and protection from market volatility.

## Context
The employee-contractor choice is not merely a wage comparison. Each structure allocates availability, switching rights, training investment, reputation, and tail risk differently.

## Key insights
- **Employment purchases reliability.** An employee is expected to remain available when a more attractive short-term offer appears; a contractor retains more freedom to reallocate effort.
- **The bargain is mutual.** Stable pay, benefits, training, and identity reduce the worker's market risk in exchange for schedule, policy, and managerial control.
- **Dependence creates hidden leverage.** Mortgages, status, family obligations, specialized company knowledge, and fear of future employers can make formal freedom economically costly to exercise.
- **Company-specific value can become a trap.** Skills and relationships that are highly valuable inside one firm may have little outside market value.
- **Contracting transfers a different tail risk.** Lower fixed cost can conceal the cost of emergency unavailability, weak institutional memory, or a supplier exercising its option to leave.
- **Critical judgment needs independence.** People whose survival depends on pleasing a hierarchy may avoid decisions that are right for the system but dangerous to their position.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
Choose the relationship by four requirements:

1. **Priority:** Must the person respond when plans change?
2. **Specificity:** How much company-specific learning is required?
3. **Continuity:** What is lost when the person exits?
4. **Independence:** Does the role need to challenge the organization without fear?

High priority, specificity, and continuity favor employment; modular output and valuable independence favor contracting. Critical oversight roles need structural protection whichever form is used.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Compare the cost of nonavailability and knowledge loss with the contractor's apparent savings.
- Avoid making one employee's household security the mechanism that suppresses legitimate dissent.
- Protect whistleblowing and independent risk functions from the hierarchy they must challenge.
- Help employees maintain portable skills, documented achievements, and external professional relationships.
- For contractors, define availability, substitution, continuity, and emergency obligations explicitly.
- For personal career design, calculate runway and transferable capability before treating independence as freedom.

## Notable examples
Taleb's airline thought experiment shows a contractor accepting a better offer despite a penalty, leaving the airline unable to operate; employment would have purchased dependable availability. Conversely, the employee who cannot risk a mortgage or reputation may remain silent about a dangerous decision, placing someone else's skin in the organization's game.
