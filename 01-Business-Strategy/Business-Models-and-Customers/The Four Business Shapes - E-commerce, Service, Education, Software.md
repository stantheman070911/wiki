# The Four Business Shapes - E-commerce, Service, Education, Software

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[01-Business-Strategy/Business Strategy Index|Business Strategy]] → [[01-Business-Strategy/Business-Models-and-Customers/Business Models and Customers Map|Business Models and Customers]]

## One-line summary
Almost every business is one of four shapes — e-commerce, service, education/info, or software — and each shape has a characteristic growth curve and one defining hard problem that is a *feature* of the model, not a bug; the winning move is to recognize your shape, stop assuming something is broken, and pour disproportionate resources into the single constraint that shape hands you.

## Principle
Pick any business apart into three chunks — **attraction** (getting interest), **conversion** (the transaction), and **delivery** (fulfilling the promise) — and the mix of people, capital, and time across those chunks determines its shape. The four shapes trade off against each other on the same axes: how fast revenue scales, how capital-intensive it is, how sticky the revenue is, and how much the outcome depends on the founder. Understanding your shape tells you which problems to expect, which are unavoidable, and where the enterprise value is actually created.

The four shapes and their signatures:

1. **E-commerce** — scales revenue fast, but is capital-intensive and constrained by cash flow, supply chain, and distribution; the moat is brand. Growth rate is capped by the free cash flow the business throws off. See [[The E-commerce Shape - Scale Fast, Then Fight Cash, Supply, and Brand]].
2. **Service** — slow, steady, people-heavy growth; talent is the bottleneck, so it is permanently supply-constrained. Easiest to start, hardest to scale, least risky (you can always cut headcount and stay profitable), and high cash flow. See [[The Virtuous Cycle of Service Businesses]].
3. **Education / info / consulting** — scales fastest of all (near-vertical) because you productize a skill once and resell it, but it hits a hard ceiling: near-zero retention (you graduate people), and you create your own competitors by teaching them. Fast, easy, and cheap to start — which is exactly why it's crowded. See [[The Education Continuity Problem - Sell Consumable Inputs, Not the Black Box]].
4. **Software / SaaS** — slowest, most capital-hungry start (long, unprofitable runway to product-market fit), but once PMF and retention exist it is the most scalable, highest-margin, stickiest, and most valuable of the four. See [[The Software Shape - Survive to Product-Market Fit, Then Retain Forever]].

## Scenario
Use this to diagnose which model you are actually in (and therefore which problems are normal), to choose a model before starting, or to understand a portfolio where different revenue lines are different shapes. It's the top-level map that the four shape-specific entries hang off.

## Key insights
- **Features, not bugs.** Every shape's biggest complaint is its defining characteristic: e-commerce owners fight cash flow and supply chain; service owners can't find good people; educators fight retention and copycats; software founders burn cash for years before PMF. None of these mean your business is broken — they mean you correctly identified the model. Hormozi sees $30M, $50M, $100M businesses still mistaking a core feature for a flaw.
- **The hard problem is where the money is.** What's hard for you is hard for your competitors. Conquer the shape's defining constraint and you unlock disproportionate enterprise value, because few others solve it. So quantify it: "If solving X adds $50M of enterprise value, am I willing to spend $500k and real focus on it?" Then fund the constraint deliberately instead of treating it as an annoyance.
- **Brand is the cross-cutting moat.** In all four shapes, brand is the durable defense: it lifts click-through (interest), repurchase (retention), and premium (price) — which means lower CAC, higher LTV, and higher gross profit simultaneously. Anyone can copy your product down to the thread count; they can't copy the brand.
- **Match the shape to your temperament.** Willing to suffer for years, detail-obsessed, disregards others' opinions → software. Promotional, hates details, loves selling → e-commerce or education. Exceptionally skilled and a natural teacher who loves people and building culture → service. Culture is just brand pointed inward: brand governs customer behavior, culture governs employee behavior.
- **Your first business is almost never your last.** Life is long; you can change shape later. Hormozi has owned all four and done well in each — the question is which is right for you *right now*.

## Tactics / how to apply
1. Break your business into attraction / conversion / delivery and count where the people, capital, and time concentrate — that reveals your shape.
2. Name the one defining constraint of that shape and write it down as "the problem I get paid to solve," not "what's wrong with us."
3. Estimate the enterprise value unlocked by solving it, then size your investment (money, hires, founder attention) against that number rather than treating the constraint as overhead.
4. Deliberately fund the constraint and shut out the rest — depth on the core problem beats duct-tape across everything.
5. If choosing a model from scratch, match the shape to your tolerance for suffering, your relationship to detail, and whether you actually like leading people.
6. In a multi-line business, classify each revenue line separately; an investment/holding vehicle is not itself a shape — it's a container whose value depends on the businesses inside it.

## Notable examples
- **Acquisition.com is a blend.** The advisory practice is a pure white-collar *service* business (analyst → associate → director career paths, multi-year apprenticeship, Ivy-League/operator talent). ACQ AI is *software*. The books and bundle were *education/media*, which is why a book launch could do enormous revenue "0 to $100M in 60 hours" — a promotional engine on top of an audience with a history of good free experiences. A Leila apparel drop was *e-commerce*. The portfolio also holds an insurance company and a teeth-whitening chain (both service-shaped).
- **An investment firm is a vehicle, not a business** — it makes no money unless the businesses it contains do; capital just concentrates leverage so few people making a few large decisions can create value in excess of the money in.
