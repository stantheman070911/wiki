# Survive vs Thrive - Intent, Interruption, and Turning Emergencies Into Lifetime Value

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[01-Business-Strategy/Business Strategy Index|Business Strategy]] → [[01-Business-Strategy/Business-Models-and-Customers/Business Models and Customers Map|Business Models and Customers]]

## One-line summary
Emergency ("survive") customers arrive via high-intent search, get price-shopped, and never come back; to build lifetime value you flip them into ongoing ("thrive") buyers and add interruption traffic that pays a premium to feel better rather than to stop feeling bad.

## Principle
There are two demand modes. **Survive** buyers act in pain, search with high intent, compare several vendors, and buy once. **Thrive** buyers want to be better than baseline, don't wake up shopping for you, and must be *interrupted* — but because they aren't comparing, they tolerate a premium and buy for the long haul. A business that only serves survive demand has an LTV ceiling; growth comes from converting emergencies into recurring relationships and from adding interruption traffic.

## Scenario
Use this when a business is inbound-only, price-shopped, and stuck on retention despite acceptable CAC — mobile IV therapy, restoration and emergency home services, urgent care, repair. The tell is that CAC is fine but LTV is thin because every sale is a one-off pain purchase.

## Diagnosis
- Leads come almost entirely from high-intent search (Google/PPC, "near me now").
- You get price-shopped on speed or a small discount ($50 off, "there within the hour").
- Repeat and referral rates are low; each transaction is a single emergency.
- Margins are pressured because you compete against several vendors the buyer is comparing in real time.
- The offer is framed as rescue (fix the crisis) rather than prevention (stay ahead of it).

## Why It Happens
Intent traffic is, by definition, comparison traffic: the buyer typed the query into six tabs, so you compete on price and speed. Pain purchases end when the pain ends. Interruption traffic — someone scrolling who feels generally "off" — has no comparison set, so a 30% premium and a differentiated bundle survive. Prevention framing ("stay ahead of it") converts a one-time rescue into an ongoing subscription because the value cycle never ends.

## Recommended Action
1. Keep the emergency channel (PPC/SEO) but stop letting it be the only sale — add a backend offer after every emergency job.
2. Add interruption traffic (e.g. Meta) whose messaging targets the thrive customer who wants to feel better, not the one who should feel less awful.
3. Flip pain into ongoing with a hook that reframes the transaction: "Do you want today for free? That's what most people do" → then explain the membership that credits today's visit.
4. Convert packs into memberships but frontload cash by having members prepay the first quarter or year, then step down (12 → 6 → 3 months).
5. Charge two ethically distinct prices: a higher one on interruption/thrive traffic (add one differentiating widget), a standard one on intent/survive traffic.
6. Turn every customer into an ad (UGC testimonials) to feed the interruption channel.

## Example from the AMA
A mobile IV-drip business had a $278 Google CAC, ~$454 LTV, and could not decommoditize its "$50 off within the hour" emergency offer. Hormozi reframed it as an LTV problem: get out of the survive (emergency) business and into thrive (ongoing wellness). Run Meta to interrupt people who "feel like [ __ ] in general," flip walk-ins with "want to make today free? that's what most people do," move from six-packs to prepaid memberships, and price interruption traffic 30% higher because it isn't price-shopped. A restoration company losing insurance-covered work was told the same shape: keep answering emergencies, but add a backend membership pitch ("let's make sure this doesn't happen again") rather than hitting the "Armageddon button" and switching the whole model to subscriptions.

## Caveats / Exceptions
- Selling a one-time solution to someone in pain is far easier than selling a recurring solution to someone who feels fine — the membership must ride on the back of the emergency, not replace it.
- Venture-backed "home-services membership" pure plays rarely work profitably; the reliable pattern is emergency-first, membership-on-the-back (HVAC visit → membership).
- Interruption traffic is colder and converts differently; expect a different sales motion than high-intent leads.
- Prevention claims must be honest; do not manufacture fear to sell subscriptions.
