# Community Quality Is the Offer - Qualification, Curation, and Network Effects

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[01-Business-Strategy/Business Strategy Index|Business Strategy]] → [[01-Business-Strategy/Business-Models-and-Customers/Business Models and Customers Map|Business Models and Customers]]

## One-line summary
A durable community is defined as much by who is excluded as by what the host provides, because qualification turns membership itself into the product and eventually the acquisition engine.

## Context
Communities often optimize for member count or monthly recurring revenue, then discover that weak-fit members increase moderation, reduce trust, and make the room less valuable to everyone else. Curation is not merely enforcement; it is product design.

## Key insights
- **“Who is here?” is part of the offer.** Qualified peers can be more valuable than another lesson, call, or template.
- **Permissive entry creates hidden delivery costs.** Poor-fit members require more support, weaken culture, and reduce referrals and retention.
- **Early curation compounds.** A strong initial cohort teaches later members how the room behaves and makes self-policing possible.
- **Exclusion is not moral judgment.** “Not a fit for this room” can be true without calling the person bad.
- **The mature model moves from broadcast to network.** At first the host supplies most value; eventually members create value for one another and attract more people like themselves.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
The community quality cycle:

1. Define observable admission criteria tied to the promised peer group.
2. Admit enough members to establish activity, then raise the bar as demand grows.
3. Remove members whose behavior damages the shared standard, even when they pay well.
4. Identify the most active, valuable members and refine marketing around their common traits.
5. Shift value creation from host-to-members toward member-to-member exchange.
6. Protect the resulting network through continued admission and removal discipline.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Publish concrete fit criteria such as role, revenue, experience, life stage, or current problem.
- Add an application or qualifying question before payment when peer quality is central.
- Define behaviors that trigger coaching, warning, or removal.
- Review the most active members quarterly and use their language in positioning.
- Measure member-to-member replies, referrals, retention, and useful introductions—not only content consumption.
- Do not confuse early-stage need for activity with permanent open admission; alternate quantity and quality as the community matures.

## Notable examples
Hormozi compares an unqualified community to a party where everyone is admitted: the gathering becomes strange because no boundary defines it. He notes that removing members paying $3,000 per month can still be rational when they damage the “garden” and reduce the value experienced by everyone else.
