# The SMB Service Barbell - Go Cheap or Go Proven, the Middle Is a Dead Zone

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[01-Business-Strategy/Business Strategy Index|Business Strategy]] → [[01-Business-Strategy/Business-Models-and-Customers/Business Models and Customers Map|Business Models and Customers]]

## One-line summary
If your customers are small businesses, the mid-price band ($1.5k–$3k/month) is a dead zone where rising CAC and structural SMB churn eat the margin — the only two models that work are dirt-cheap and nearly automated, or expensive and reserved for already-proven operators.

## Principle
Small businesses are inherently volatile customers: independent of how good your service is, they churn because they themselves are fragile, undercapitalized, and often badly run. Any vendor pricing in the middle of the market inherits that volatility at a cost structure that can't absorb it. The fix is a barbell — serve SMBs so cheaply and with such low delivery cost that churn doesn't matter, or serve only the SMBs sophisticated enough to already have proof, budget discipline, and a working sales process.

## Scenario
Use this whenever a business sells services or software to small and medium businesses (marketing agencies, done-for-you providers, local-business SaaS) and notices CAC creeping up, margins compressing as revenue grows, or clients blaming the vendor for problems that are really the client's own business dysfunction.

## Diagnosis
- Average customer price sits in the $1,500–$3,000/month range and average tenure is short (4–6 months).
- CAC keeps rising even though sales skill and process haven't changed.
- Clients frequently cite their own poor sales or operations as the reason they can't get value from the service.
- The founder feels pulled to add nurture, hand-holding, or "customer success" work that starts to resemble consulting the client's whole business.
- Growth requires constantly refilling a leaky bucket rather than compounding an existing base.

## Why It Happens
SMBs fail and change direction at a structurally higher rate than larger, better-capitalized businesses, so any vendor serving them inherits that base rate of churn regardless of service quality. At the same time, CAC only rises over the life of a channel. The combination means margins compress relentlessly in the middle of the price spectrum: too expensive to treat as a high-volume, low-touch commodity, too cheap to fund the extra support SMBs actually need to succeed. Founders instinctively respond by adding more service (nurture calls, done-with-you support) to compensate for the client's shortcomings, which raises delivery cost without changing the underlying retention math — because the retention problem was never really about the client's experience, it was about who the client is.

## Recommended Action
1. Diagnose whether you are structurally in the dead zone: if average revenue per SMB customer sits between roughly $1,500 and $3,000/month, treat that as a warning band, not a stable operating point.
2. Choose a side of the barbell deliberately. Low road: price under ~$400–500/month, make delivery close to free (templated, largely automated, a narrow and visible outcome like local map ranking), and win on volume and sales velocity rather than retention.
3. High road: raise price and qualify hard for SMBs that already have a proven model, know their own metrics, and have functioning sales — i.e., businesses further along than "SMB" in spirit even if small in revenue.
4. Do not try to fix client churn by adding uncompensated hand-holding; if the client's own business is the constraint, no amount of service-side effort recovers the LTV.
5. If you're currently stuck in the middle, decide explicitly which direction to move rather than defaulting to "provide more value" at the same price point.

## Example from the AMA
A digital-marketing agency serving Australian SMBs (cleaning companies, yard-work businesses) had grown from $0 to $500k in four months at roughly $450/week (~$2k/month), a textbook dead-zone price. Hormozi projected the trajectory forward: CAC would keep rising, churn would keep eating margin as clients (whose businesses were themselves unstable) failed regardless of the agency's performance, and the founder would be tempted to add sales/nurture support for clients who "don't know how to run their business" — all of which "is just wrong." His prescription: go to ~$300–400/month with a narrow, near-zero-cost-to-deliver service (e.g., local map ranking, review management/SEO) to get 30–40-month stick rates on volume, or go up-market to serve businesses that already have proven sales processes and can pay premium prices. "Drop low, go high, and the middle is just a dead zone where everyone dies."

## Caveats / Exceptions
- This applies specifically to vendors whose customer base *is* SMBs; a premium/barbell strategy for your own offer tiers (see [[Barbell Pricing and the Unscalable Premium Offer]]) is a related but different decision.
- "Cheap and automated" only works if delivery cost is genuinely near zero; a cheap offer with real delivery cost just relocates the dead zone.
- Moving up-market requires actually qualifying prospects on sophistication (existing sales process, known metrics), not just raising the sticker price.
- Some founders can profitably serve the middle if they've solved distribution so cheaply that CAC stays structurally low — the dead zone is a default, not a law of physics.
