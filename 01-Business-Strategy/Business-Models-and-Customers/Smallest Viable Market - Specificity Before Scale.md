# Smallest Viable Market - Specificity Before Scale

## One-line summary
Choose the smallest group large enough to sustain the work, delight it so completely that members spread the change, and expand only after earning that foundation.

## Context
“Everyone” feels safe because it avoids choosing, but it produces generic products, vague messages, and no accountability. The smallest viable market reverses the sequence: establish fit and density within a specific psychographic group before seeking reach.

## Key insights
- **A market is chosen, not found.** The organization decides which people it can serve unusually well and accepts that others may correctly decline.
- **Psychographics usually matter more than demographics.** Worldview, desires, fears, and status orientation explain behavior better than age or income alone.
- **Minimum viable means sufficient, not tiny.** The group must be small enough for specificity and large enough to support the economics and intended impact.
- **Exclusion improves the work.** Saying “It's not for you” protects the promise from being diluted for incompatible preferences.
- **Density beats scattered reach.** A concentrated group can see one another adopting the idea, creating proof, connection, and local network effects.
- **The first audience shapes the future.** Early customers provide examples, language, norms, and referrals, so selecting them is a strategic design decision.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
Define a smallest viable market with five choices:

1. **Change:** What observable transition should the work enable?
2. **People:** Which shared worldview makes that change urgent and meaningful?
3. **Minimum:** How many successful customers are needed for economic and operational viability?
4. **Promise:** What specific, truthful result can be delivered consistently to this group?
5. **Propagation:** Why would successful members tell peers, and how does each additional member improve the experience or its social proof?

Expand to an adjacent group only when the original market has a repeatable result, a clear story, and enough density to remain well served.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Replace a broad demographic avatar with a worldview statement: “They believe ___, seek ___, and avoid ___.”
- Calculate the customer count needed from price, contribution margin, service capacity, and desired operating scale.
- Recruit a small cohort, observe the language members use after success, and use it to sharpen the promise.
- Identify incompatible expectations and state them plainly in qualification, onboarding, and positioning.
- Track cohort success, retention, referrals, and customer language before adding reach.
- When moving beyond early adopters, redesign the bridge for a more conservative audience instead of assuming the original “new and risky” story will transfer.

## Notable examples
The Grateful Dead built a durable career around a comparatively small group of devoted fans rather than maximizing mass-market appeal. Facebook crossed one local chasm at a time—starting with dense, status-aware campus networks—before becoming a mass platform. Both examples show how connection inside a defined group can precede scale.
