# The Five Advantages of a Perfect Business

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[01-Business-Strategy/Business Strategy Index|Business Strategy]] → [[01-Business-Strategy/Business-Models-and-Customers/Business Models and Customers Map|Business Models and Customers]]

## One-line summary
Businesses are easier to grow and far more profitable when they score well on five continua — sticky, expensive, expansion, air, and unique — and if you can only optimize one, make it retention.

## Context
This is an S-tier ranking for opportunity vehicles: a way to diagnose whether a "level-10 skill set is trapped in a level-2 opportunity." Few businesses have all five; even one beats most competitors. All five are continuums, not binaries.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
**1. Sticky (revenue retention) — the most important.** Without retention you're forever in the sales business ("be in the resale business, not the sales business" — John Paul DeJoria).
- *Logo retention* (how many customers remain; can't exceed 100%, decays via structural/involuntary churn and voluntary churn) vs *net revenue retention* (can exceed 100% if stayers spend more).
- Get NRR > 100% with a clear upgrade path (e.g. $9 → $99 = 11×; even with 20% leaving the low tier, a few upgrades more than replace it), so the business grows even acquiring no one new.
- **The churn curve** (from hundreds of thousands of memberships): month 1 is worst (>20%), another drop ~month 3 (~10%), then ~month 6 it settles near ~2%/month. Takeaway: make the first 30 days awesome and *just get people to month 6*.
- *Compounding:* Company B (100 new/yr, zero churn) reaches 300 loyal customers while Company A (churns everything) must buy 2× the customers at rising cost — same revenue, far worse economics and stress.

**2. Expensive (high gross margins).** Penny-to-make, sell-for-a-dollar. High gross margins → higher net margins, faster cash conversion, more to reinvest, ability to pay people better. Low-margin: groceries, farming, restaurants (food is elastic/commoditized). High-margin: media, information, education, software, pharma, supplements. Lever: decommoditize.

**3. Expansion (a growing market).** Easiest growth is riding a growing industry; you supply demand-generation skill, so you just need to avoid a headwind. Shrinking: newspapers, formal education (~-6%/yr), tobacco, alcohol, brick-and-mortar retail, clerical. Growing: energy, AI, healthcare, cybersecurity, e-commerce, alternative education (>20% CAGR — the Skool bet).

**4. Air (operational scale = low operational complexity + low capex).** Few variables to manage to expand a unit (a podcast ad-read scales; 100 restaurants don't). Low capex → less dilution, faster self-funded growth (Buffett prefers cash-generative over cash-hungry businesses). High ROIC makes you a magnet for capital; if you can't raise money, the core economics — not your pitch — are the problem.

**5. Unique (a moat).** Low barriers → competition → price erosion (e.g. SMMA). Moats, weakest to strongest: **capex as a barrier** (the shovel/power-plant effect — spend to make entry harder for others); **trade secrets/patents** (patentable = new + non-obvious + useful); **brand** (Hormozi's favorite — Revlon vs CVS generic off the same belt converts more at a higher price). Coke ties all five: capital with great returns, patent + brand, sticky habit, cheap to make, still expanding.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Score your business 1–10 on each of the five; don't treat them as yes/no.
- If you can improve only one thing, fix retention first, then backfill margins, market, operations, and moat.
- When retention is unclear, identify activation signals manually before automating onboarding; see [[Activation Before Automation - Manual Onboarding to Fix Churn]].
- Build an explicit upgrade path so cheaper customers can spend more (drives NRR past 100%).
- Concentrate retention effort on the first 30 days and the path to month 6.
- If early in your career and your industry has *no* retention, consider switching vehicles.
- To raise capital for a capex-heavy model, fix the deal economics (payback in year 1 beats payback in year 3), not just the pitch.

## Notable examples
- Skool: community + consumable content drives month-over-month payment; retention data above.
- Coca-Cola as the near-complete five-advantage business (Buffett's long hold).
