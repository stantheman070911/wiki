# The Hedgehog Concept - Three Circles and the Economic Denominator

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[01-Business-Strategy/Business Strategy Index|Business Strategy]] → [[01-Business-Strategy/Business-Models-and-Customers/Business Models and Customers Map|Business Models and Customers]]

## One-line summary
A durable strategy is a simple organizing concept discovered at the intersection of what you can become best at, what drives your economic engine, and what your people genuinely care about.

## Context
Complex plans do not create strategic clarity. The useful simplification comes after deep understanding: a company identifies the few activities it should organize around and gains an equally clear view of what it should stop pursuing.

## Key insights
- **The Hedgehog Concept is an understanding, not an aspiration.** Wanting to be the best, declaring a goal, or naming a core competence does not establish that you can actually become the best.
- **Competence can become a trap.** A profitable activity performed for decades may still be incapable of supporting greatness; history and sunk cost do not make it strategically right.
- **The economic denominator reveals the engine.** "Profit per X" forces a choice about the unit whose improvement most strongly compounds the whole model.
- **Passion is discovered, not installed.** Do work the organization can care deeply about; slogans cannot manufacture durable commitment to an unloved activity.
- **Growth follows coherence.** Growth itself is not a Hedgehog Concept. Relentless consistency inside the three circles produces growth as a consequence.
- **Clarity is iterative.** The good-to-great companies took about four years on average to reach their concept through repeated questioning, debate, decisions, and autopsies.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
**The three circles:**

1. What can we become the best in the world at—and what can we never be best at?
2. What single denominator, such as profit per customer, employee, visit, region, or risk unit, best captures our economic engine?
3. What work or purpose are we deeply passionate about?

The intersection becomes a short decision rule. A standing **Council** of roughly five to twelve respected people cycles through: questions → debate → decisions → autopsies → better questions. It seeks understanding rather than consensus; the accountable executive still decides.

## Tactics / how to apply
- List current businesses and capabilities, then separately mark which have a credible path to world-class performance.
- Test several "profit per X" denominators and model how optimizing each would change behavior across the system.
- Distinguish passion for the mechanics of the work from passion for the purpose it serves; either can satisfy the third circle.
- Create a standing cross-functional Council with enough expertise and trust for vigorous, non-defensive debate.
- Use every material decision and postmortem to refine the three circles rather than treating strategy as an annual event.
- Reject opportunities that cannot pass all three circles, even when they promise immediate growth.

## Notable examples
Walgreens organized around convenient drugstores and profit per customer visit, allowing dense store clusters and drive-through pharmacies to reinforce one another. Abbott accepted that it could no longer become the best pharmaceutical researcher and shifted toward cost-effective health care through diagnostics and nutrition. Wells Fargo exited global banking ambitions and focused on running a western bank like a business, measured by profit per employee.
