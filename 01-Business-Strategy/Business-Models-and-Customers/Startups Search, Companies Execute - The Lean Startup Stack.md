# Startups Search, Companies Execute - The Lean Startup Stack

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[01-Business-Strategy/Business Strategy Index|Business Strategy]] → [[01-Business-Strategy/Business-Models-and-Customers/Business Models and Customers Map|Business Models and Customers]]

## One-line summary
A startup is not a smaller version of a large company: a company *executes* a known, repeatable business model, while a startup *searches* for one — and confusing the two is why founders were told for decades to behave like big companies and fail.

## Context
This is Steve Blank's foundational distinction, the one that launched the Lean Startup movement. Writing his memoirs after the Epiphany IPO, he noticed the pattern no one had named: turn-of-the-century VCs told startups to do everything a large company does — hire sales/marketing/bizdev, build the product serially, ship, and there was no concept of a *pivot* (you just fired an executive). The best startups quietly ignored that advice. The reason was that a century of management tools (org design, hiring, product management) had been built for *execution*, and there were no tools — not even language — for *search*.

## Key insights
- **Execution vs. search is the core split.** Large companies run known business models; startups look for them under conditions of extreme uncertainty. The whole point of Lean is to build a management stack for search equivalent to the MBA stack for execution.
- **Give founders permission to change.** Before this reframe, there was no word for pivot and no license to change direction; "no business plan survives first contact with customers," and a plan gets shelved right after funding — so what replaces it must be a search process, not a static document.
- **Your product is not your company.** The product is one part of the business model. Demand creation, revenue model, costs, key activities, resources, partners, and channels are the rest — and great teams grabbing your demo out of your hands is not the same as having a business.
- **VCs bet on the searcher, not the spreadsheet.** In an innovation cluster, investors back passion, vision, and the ability to assemble a team and search, because they know honest failure isn't the end (see [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Mindset-and-Identity/Founders Are Artists - Calling, Cargo Cults, and the Drive to Create|Founders Are Artists]]).

## The Lean Startup stack
Three components make up the search toolkit:
1. **Business Model Canvas** (Alexander Osterwalder) — one page listing the nine things a startup must worry about (customers, value proposition, channels, revenue, costs, key activities, resources, partners, relationships). "Who are the customers / what are you building for them" together is *product-market fit*. It captures ~85% of what a startup needs to reason about — the checklist of hypotheses.
2. **Customer Development** (Blank) — get out of the building and test those hypotheses against hundreds/thousands of real prospects to inform the vision (see [[03-Tactics-and-Playbooks/Sales-and-Lead-Generation/Get Out of the Building - Customer Development Informs the Vision|Get Out of the Building]]).
3. **Agile Engineering** — build minimum viable products iteratively (championed as the practitioner complement by Eric Ries, the first customer-development practitioner at IMVU), so the product evolves with the validated learning rather than via multi-month waterfall.

## Application
- Name which mode you're in: are you *searching* for a repeatable, scalable business model, or *executing* one you've already validated? Use search tools (canvas + customer development + MVPs) until the model is proven, then switch to execution tools.
- Treat your business plan as a set of hypotheses on the canvas, not a document — expect to pivot when contact with customers disproves one.
- Don't mistake a working demo for a company; walk the whole canvas (demand, revenue, costs, resources) before declaring product-market fit.
- Reserve execution-style hiring (full sales/marketing orgs) for after the model is found, not before.
