# Organic Startup Ideas - Live in the Future and Notice What Is Missing

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[01-Business-Strategy/Business Strategy Index|Business Strategy]] → [[01-Business-Strategy/Business-Models-and-Customers/Business Models and Customers Map|Business Models and Customers]]

## One-line summary
The best startup ideas are noticed rather than invented: become expert at a changing frontier, build what you or nearby users urgently need, and disable the filters that hide tedious or unfashionable problems.

## Context
Trying to “think of a startup” produces plausible-sounding solutions without authentic demand. Organic ideas arise when knowledge of a domain makes a missing tool feel obvious.

## Key insights
- **Start with a need, not a market label.** Ask what you wish someone would build for you and whether you would use it if someone else made it.
- **Live at a leading edge.** People inside a rapidly changing field notice gaps before outsiders recognize that the field exists.
- **Urgency matters more than theoretical population.** A narrow group with an acute need is a stronger beachhead than millions who might be mildly interested.
- **Schlep blindness conceals value.** Tedious regulation, payments, logistics, and messy real-world work repel builders, leaving useful problems underexplored.
- **Competition can validate demand.** A crowded market is promising when you can state precisely what incumbents overlook; no competition may mean no need.
- **Ideas often enter from the side.** Replacements initially serve a small or disdained group and only later reveal that they have displaced an incumbent category.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
Screen an idea with four questions: **Who urgently needs it? How do we know firsthand? What has changed that makes it possible now? What schlep or unfashionable detail causes others to ignore it?** A strong answer identifies both a credible first user and a path into a growing future.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Keep a log of gaps, workarounds, and “why doesn't this exist?” moments without forcing them into business ideas immediately.
- Work like a consultant for one demanding user until the true problem becomes concrete.
- If you lack firsthand need, enter the user's environment long enough to acquire it.
- Seek groups that are small today but representative of how many people will behave tomorrow.

## Notable examples
Dropbox emerged from Drew Houston's own need; Airbnb from its founders' need for rent; Stripe from programmers' frustration with payments. Stripe's unattractive schlep was also its protection from casual competitors.
