# The AI-Ready Business Redesign - Speed to Value and Experience Moats

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[01-Business-Strategy/Business Strategy Index|Business Strategy]] → [[01-Business-Strategy/Business-Models-and-Customers/Business Models and Customers Map|Business Models and Customers]]

## One-line summary
Redesign an AI-exposed business from the customer's desired outcome backward, use agents to compress speed to value, and defend the result with a narrow reputation plus exclusivity, community, and real experiences.

## Context
Adding AI to an inherited workflow preserves assumptions that automation may have made obsolete. The more useful exercise is to declare the old operating model finished and reconsider the audience, offer, roles, systems, and customer journey from zero.

## Key insights
- **A clean-sheet question exposes legacy decisions.** If the company started today, it might choose a different customer, offer, CRM, team structure, delivery method, and pricing model.
- **Speed to value is the redesign target.** Begin with the outcome customers seek and ask for the fastest AI-enabled path to a useful result, not merely the fastest way to perform the old tasks.
- **Agents expand the feasible offer.** Imagine a large pool of capable specialists available at negligible marginal cost; this reveals research, analysis, drafting, and guidance that can be embedded for every customer.
- **Code alone is a weakening moat.** When competitors can reproduce features quickly, defensibility shifts toward reputation, audience fit, proprietary context, and relationships.
- **Experience moats reinforce one another.** Exclusivity filters the participants, community lets them access one another, and memorable real-world experiences make the relationship difficult to substitute.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
Use a three-part redesign:

1. **Kill the legacy model:** reconsider concept, audience, offer, sales motion, roles, and systems without sunk-cost protection.
2. **Rebuild for speed to value:** list every component of the desired outcome, then decide what AI can complete, accelerate, personalize, or quality-check.
3. **Install the moat:** define a narrow game where the company can be top-tier, then add an **Exclusivity–Community–Experience** triangle.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Hold a reset workshop whose opening assumption is that no current workflow is automatically retained.
- List customer outcomes rather than internal departments; assign agents and humans against each outcome.
- Use AI for the repeatable first 80%, while reserving judgment, accountability, relationship, and high-stakes tailoring for people.
- Set meaningful entry criteria so the community itself becomes part of the offer.
- Create customer-to-customer value and real experiences that a copied software feature cannot reproduce.
- Revisit pricing after speed and scope change; selling the old unit of labor can hide the new value created.

## Notable examples
Priestley describes converting a hands-on book-coaching process into an AI-guided product and using agents to give every accelerator client forms of marketing and strategic assistance that previously would have been too expensive to deliver broadly.
