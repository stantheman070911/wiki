# ICP Qualification Through Segmented Churn

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[01-Business-Strategy/Business Strategy Index|Business Strategy]] → [[01-Business-Strategy/Business-Models-and-Customers/Business Models and Customers Map|Business Models and Customers]]

## One-line summary
Your ideal customer is defined by who actually stays, so segment churn by channel, avatar, and behavior, find the cohort that retains, and then point all messaging and pricing at that cohort instead of averaging good customers together with the ones who were always going to leave.

## Principle
Blended churn is a lie of averages. A "17% monthly churn" business often has one cohort that stays for years and another that quits in three months; the blended number hides both. The job is to isolate the retaining cohort — the true ICP — and re-engineer acquisition around it, because a business that only speaks to its ICP compounds while one that serves everyone spins its wheels.

## Scenario
Use this when churn is the binding constraint, when a business serves multiple avatars or acquisition channels, or when a founder feels "too far down market." It applies to SaaS, agencies, coaching, memberships, and any recurring-revenue model with mixed customers.

## Diagnosis
- Overall churn is high, but you sense some customers stick forever and others vanish fast.
- You sell to several avatars, price bands, or lead sources without tracking them separately.
- Retention data isn't cut by cohort (first-3-months vs 3-months-plus) or by segment.
- The offer and content speak to a broad audience, so unqualified buyers keep entering.
- You keep trying to fix product or scale spend before knowing *who* actually stays.

## Why It Happens
When you serve everyone, your data mixes signal with noise and you optimize toward the average, which pleases no one. Bad-fit customers churn for structural reasons (they don't have the problem often enough, can't afford it, or lack the prerequisites to succeed), and no product tweak fixes a fit problem. Whatever slice of the audience you speak to is the slice you attract; vague messaging attracts unqualified buyers whose churn then swamps the metric.

## Recommended Action
1. Instrument churn by three cuts: acquisition channel, avatar (a discriminating variable — e.g. transaction volume, revenue, team size), and in-product behavior (activation actions).
2. Define the ICP as the segment that retains, using hard qualifiers (e.g. "$750k+ in commissions, has a training system, 10+ people downstream").
3. Track ICP churn separately from non-ICP churn and drive the ICP number toward a target (sub-3–5% monthly) before scaling.
4. Rewrite messaging, offer, and pricing to speak only to the ICP — the audience you name is the audience you get.
5. Keep serving "minnows" as short-term cash flow at a lower price and a less-frequent billing cadence, but don't let them define the model.
6. Don't scale acquisition until the ICP churn is solved; cutting churn by two-thirds alone can triple the business.
7. Build the avatar definition across three distinct buckets rather than one: demographics (who they are — role, company size, industry), quantifiables (hard numbers that predict fit — revenue, transaction volume, team size), and behaviors (what they do — existing systems in place, prior spend on similar solutions, engagement patterns). A retaining ICP is usually defined by a combination across all three, not any single variable.

## Example from the AMA
A done-for-you content agency selling to insurance salesmen had 17% monthly blended churn. Hormozi surfaced the split: best clients (over $750k in personal commissions, with a training system and 10+ downline) stayed a year-plus, while others left in three months. His plan: qualify to that ICP, retarget all messaging to it, price minnows at a lower quarterly rate for cash flow, and — critically — track ICP vs non-ICP churn separately, scaling only once ICP churn dropped under ~3% a month. A real-estate SaaS losing ~100 of 150 weekly signups was told to segment by channel, avatar, and activation action to discover which avatar (e.g. higher-volume agents or teams) actually retained, then aim ads at exactly that avatar.

A financial-advisor-focused lead-generation agency provided the three-bucket avatar breakdown directly: Hormozi had the founder separate demographic traits (advisor tenure, firm size), quantifiable thresholds (assets under management, existing marketing spend), and behavioral signals (whether the advisor already ran any lead-gen system) to isolate which combination of the three actually predicted retention, rather than qualifying on a single loose criterion like "financial advisor."

## Caveats / Exceptions
- A new avatar experiment needs time (often a month) before LTV:CAC is trustworthy — early "easy to acquire" euphoria isn't proof they'll stay; some cheap-to-acquire segments are cheap because they're miserable to serve.
- Minnows can be a legitimate short-term cash engine; the error is confusing them with the model.
- Qualifiers should be observable and predictive of retention, not arbitrary gatekeeping.
- Some churn is a delivery or activation problem, not a fit problem; segmentation tells you which.
