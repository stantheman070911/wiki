# The Paid Front Door - Warm Cold Traffic Before the Core Offer

## One-line summary
When cold traffic will not buy the core offer, add a small paid front door that proves value, filters for buyers, gathers qualification data, and turns strangers into warmer prospects before the real sale.

## Principle
Do not send cold traffic through the same sales motion that works for warm traffic. A warm audience already trusts you; a cold audience needs a smaller buying decision, more education, and a clearer proof step before the high-ticket ask.

## Scenario
Use this when a business has capacity and a working offer, but growth stalls because warm audience demand is tapped out and cold funnels break even or worse. It is especially relevant for high-ticket coaching, services, technical services, local home services, and expert-led products where trust is the main missing ingredient.

## Diagnosis
- Warm direct-response campaigns still work but fatigue quickly.
- Cold VSLs, webinars, or call funnels generate leads but few qualified buyers.
- Many booked calls cannot afford the offer or do not understand why they should trust it.
- The business judges the funnel by total leads or show-up rate instead of qualified conversion.
- The sales team is forced to do too much trust-building inside one call.

## Why It Happens
Cold traffic has not yet absorbed the context that warm traffic already has: who you are, why the problem matters, why your method works, and whether the prospect is the right buyer. A free front end often attracts the most curious and least qualified people. A small paid commitment filters out a large share of non-buyers while creating behavioral momentum: buyers buy more.

## Recommended Action
1. Replace the free event or direct call push with a low-ticket paid front door, often $49-$99.
2. Make it a real workshop, assessment, audit, trial, or first step that delivers a concrete win.
3. Sell the core offer at the end only after value, authority, and fit have been established.
4. Add one or two high-signal qualification fields before the front door purchase or booking.
5. Track qualified conversion, not just raw lead conversion.
6. Feed qualification data back into ad optimization, messaging, and sales prioritization.
7. Make the front door feel absurdly valuable, even if it only breaks even or loses money.

## Example from the AMA
A poker coaching business had a $5,000 five-day offer, 200k YouTube subscribers, and strong warm conversion, but cold webinars flopped. Hormozi diagnosed that cold traffic was being pushed through a warm motion. His fix was a $49-$99 paid workshop, expanded to about three hours, with real value before the ask. He also told the founder to collect average poker buy-in as a qualification field so the business could optimize around qualified buyers rather than raw registrants.

The same pattern appeared in other calls: a cloud-services expert was advised to sell a paid improvement patch or assessment before managed services; a genetic-testing business could let affiliates keep the front-end test profit and monetize downstream; an outdoor-lighting company could offer a small driveway or walkway install rather than a vague free sales pitch.

## Caveats / Exceptions
- A paid front door does not fix a weak core offer. It only improves trust, qualification, and sequencing.
- Free can still work when the audience is already warm or when volume and qualification are solved elsewhere.
- The front door must be a genuine value delivery mechanism, not a disguised pitch. If the customer feels tricked, it damages the back-end sale.
- For very simple, low-ticket products, adding a paid intermediate step can add friction without enough benefit.
