# Goldilocks Pricing - Anchors, Decoys, and the Middle Option

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[01-Business-Strategy/Business Strategy Index|Business Strategy]]

## One-line summary
Present a deliberately structured good–better–best range so buyers can judge value comparatively; the target offer becomes the safe, sufficient middle rather than an isolated price requiring an absolute judgment.

## Context
Customers rarely know the “true” value of an unfamiliar offer. When shown one option, they must evaluate it against a vague memory of alternatives. A coherent range supplies the comparison and lets the seller define what cheap, appropriate, and premium mean in the category.

## Key insights
- **Price is interpreted in context.** The same target price feels different depending on the option shown before and beside it.
- **The extremes give the middle meaning.** A low option frames minimum sufficiency; a premium option frames maximum scope and makes the target easier to justify.
- **A decoy clarifies comparison.** An intentionally dominated alternative can make the preferred option’s advantage visible, but a deceptive or unusable decoy destroys trust.
- **The middle reduces two fears at once.** Buyers avoid the quality risk associated with the cheapest option and the excess associated with the most expensive.
- **Tier gaps should tell a story.** Random bundles create confusion; each step should correspond to a distinct customer, outcome, or level of support.
- **Anchoring cannot rescue weak value.** The target still has to solve the problem credibly; context changes perception, not product-market fit.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
Design a **Good–Better–Best ladder**:

1. Choose the offer you most want qualified customers to buy.
2. Create a credible entry option that solves a narrower version of the problem.
3. Create a premium option with materially greater speed, certainty, access, customization, or status.
4. Make the target visibly closer in price to the entry tier than to the premium tier while delivering a meaningful step up in value.
5. Name each tier by customer or outcome, not by arbitrary metals.
6. Track mix, margin, refund rate, and customer fit—not only conversion.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Never present a high-consideration offer without at least one relevant comparison.
- Use a comparison table that highlights the few differences that determine fit.
- Explain who should deliberately choose the lowest and highest tiers; this makes the middle recommendation more credible.
- Keep the entry tier genuinely usable and the premium tier genuinely deliverable.
- Test order and spacing as well as price; the first number seen becomes an anchor.
- Review whether the middle attracts the right customers or merely the most customers.

## Notable examples
Panasonic added a higher-priced microwave beside two existing models; the former premium product became the middle option and its sales increased. Bartlett also describes a property agent who showed one inferior, one target, and one extremely expensive property, allowing the target to feel like the obvious balance.
