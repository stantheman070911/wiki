# Van Westendorp Price Sensitivity Analysis

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[01-Business-Strategy/Business Strategy Index|Business Strategy]]

## One-line summary
Ask prospective buyers four price questions, plot the answers, and you get a defensible price *range* — the band between "too cheap to be credible" and "too expensive to consider" — instead of guessing what to charge on a new offer.

## Context
Most founders set price by feel, by copying competitors, or by what they think they "deserve" — all weak signals. The Van Westendorp method (a 1970s market-research technique) turns pricing into data. Hormozi runs it most times he launches a new offering; it's the concrete tool behind the softer advice to "charge more" and to price by what the customer will pay, not out of your own wallet.

## The four questions
Survey real prospects (ideally in your target segment) with exactly these:

1. At what price would this be **so expensive** you wouldn't even consider it?
2. At what price would this be **so cheap** that you'd doubt it could be any good?
3. At what price would it be **expensive, but you'd still consider it** — the edge where you'd have to think, but end up buying?
4. At what price would it be a **bargain** — a clearly good deal?

## Reading the plot
Plot the responses as cumulative curves and two decision points emerge:

- **Point of marginal cheapness** — below this price, most people think it's *too cheap to be believable* (price is signalling low quality). This is your floor.
- **Point of marginal expensiveness** — above this price, most people think it's *too expensive to consider*. This is your ceiling.
- The **usable price band** sits between them. Within it you can find the price that yields the most sales, and — after weighing gross margin — the price that yields the most money. A higher-friction, human sales process can hold a higher point in the band than a fully automated self-serve checkout, so choose the point to match your sales motion.
- **Double gaussians = two customers.** Sometimes the curve has two humps because you're actually pricing for two different segments. Don't average them — segment them.

## Key insights
- **Price is a value signal, in both directions.** Too high loses the sale; too *low* also loses it, because cheapness reads as "can't be any good." The floor is as real as the ceiling.
- **Collect customer stats alongside the four answers.** Then you can slice the curve — rich customers vs. poor customers, one use-case vs. another (e.g. home services vs. other) — and price each segment precisely instead of using one blunt number.
- **This is how consumer goods are priced.** The same method used to price a new shampoo works for a service, a course, or a software tier.
- **AI collapses the analysis time.** What used to be a manual scatter-plot exercise can now be run by pasting the raw survey data into an LLM and asking for the analysis in minutes — including the segmented cuts.

## Application
1. Before launching a new offer, survey a batch of real prospects with the four questions above; capture a few demographic/firmographic stats per respondent.
2. Derive the point of marginal cheapness (floor) and marginal expensiveness (ceiling); price inside that band.
3. Pick the point in the band that fits your sales motion — nearer the top if a person sells it with friction, nearer the sales-maximising point if it's self-serve — then sanity-check against gross margin.
4. Re-run sliced by segment; if you see a double hump, treat them as two offers/prices, not one compromise.
5. Combine with premium anchoring: this tells you the *believable* range; a deliberately high anchor still has a place above it (see Barbell Pricing).
