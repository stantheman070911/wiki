# Compete on Efficiency When You Can't Control Price

## One-line summary
In industries where price is fixed by insurers, regulators, or a commodity market, you can't win from the top line, so you win from the bottom up — drive cost of delivery down and let margin expansion do the work of several new locations.

## Principle
When price is externally set, the only controllable variable is efficiency. You cannot raise revenue per unit, so competitive advantage and profit growth come from lowering the cost to deliver. A modest margin gain compounds enormously: improving the bottom line on existing volume can equal opening several new units, with none of the expansion risk.

## Scenario
Use this for insurance-reimbursed services, regulated trades, commodity products, and any business where the customer or a third party dictates the rate and you compete on how cheaply and consistently you can deliver.

## Diagnosis
- Your price is set by insurers, a regulator, or a commodity spot rate — not by you.
- Margins are thin and you keep trying to grow the top line you can't move.
- Competitors offer the same output at the same price, so the fight is on cost, not value.
- You have unexamined cost of delivery (COGS, routing, labor structure, tooling).
- You're eyeing expensive expansion before harvesting available margin.

## Why It Happens
A fixed price caps top-down strategy. If you can't charge more, the arithmetic of profit runs entirely through cost. Delivery cost is usually full of slack — inefficient routing, low utilization, manual work that could be tooled or offshored — so bottom-up improvement is both available and defensible. Because margin gains multiply against your entire existing volume, they often dwarf the profit a risky new location would add.

## Recommended Action
1. Accept the price as fixed and redirect all strategic attention to cost of delivery.
2. Open the hood on unit economics: COGS, labor model, route/asset utilization, and the manual steps that could be systematized, offshored, or automated.
3. Hunt for structural efficiency plays — e.g. route density for a mobile service, higher per-visit value at lower visit frequency, offshore/AI operating leverage for a service.
4. Quantify the leverage: a ~10% margin improvement on a thin-margin unit can be a large percent gain in bottom line — "that's like you opening six more locations."
5. Bank the freed cash flow, then decide which few tasks you dislike you can hire out of once the margin covers it.

## Example from the AMA
A dietitian group faced fixed insurance reimbursement (~$150/hour). Hormozi's counsel: "in the insurance business you can't control price, so you have to control efficiency — that's how you win from the bottom up rather than top down." He showed that lifting a ~15% margin by ~10 points was a ~67% improvement in bottom line — "that's like you opening six more locations" — achievable in about six months by driving COGS down internally. An exhaust-hood cleaning business heard the same theme applied through route density and less-frequent, higher-priced visits to raise gross margins at a fixed customer price.

## Caveats / Exceptions
- Efficiency-led competition still needs a defensible reason-to-choose-you; don't let cost focus become a race to the bottom on quality.
- Some "fixed" prices have adjacent unregulated segments (cash-pay, premium tiers) worth testing.
- Cost-cutting must not degrade the retention-driving parts of delivery; cut waste, not value.
- Margin harvesting funds growth; it is a means to reinvest, not an end in itself.
