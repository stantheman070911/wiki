# Give Them What They Want to Get Them What They Need - The Defined-End Offer

## One-line summary
People buy a defined, time-bound result — a challenge, a program, a fix — not an open-ended relationship, so lead with a concrete finish-line offer (the "ham"), then use the time you've bought to sell them the ongoing solution they actually need (the "garlic").

## Principle
Wants and needs diverge. Nobody buys "a lifestyle," "ongoing tutoring," or "continuity"; they buy a 6-week challenge, a 21-day detox, a 3-week program, a specific score. A defined-end offer is a sales motion: it converts far more easily than open-ended continuity, and it earns you the time and momentum to educate the buyer into the longer commitment they truly require.

## Scenario
Use this when continuity or long-term services sell poorly cold, when a defined-end product outperforms a "just buy the ongoing thing" pitch, or when marketing the real (ongoing) solution confuses prospects. It applies to coaching, fitness, tutoring, memberships, and expert services.

## Diagnosis
- Prospects say they "just want" the short/defined thing, not the ongoing program.
- A time-bound offer (challenge, bootcamp, trial program) converts better than continuity.
- You try to explain the full ecosystem/nuance before the customer has bought anything.
- Continuity churns early because people never wanted an open-ended commitment up front.
- You're marketing what they *need* instead of what they *want*.

## Why It Happens
A defined end-point makes the purchase legible: the buyer can picture the finish line and the risk feels bounded. Open-ended commitments feel like exposure, so they resist. Once inside a program, though, the customer experiences momentum and realizes the short fix won't fully solve the problem — and now you have time and trust to sell the long-term solution. Explaining the ecosystem before the first purchase just adds friction to a decision they haven't yet agreed to make.

## Recommended Action
1. Package the entry as a concrete, time-bound outcome (a challenge, a program, a defined result), not "ongoing X."
2. Market the thing they want; deliver — and only *then* explain — the thing they need, once they're in.
3. Use the program window to build momentum and educate toward the deeper problem.
4. Bundle the defined-end offer against a longer package (e.g. a year) as the natural upsell.
5. Attach a compelling guarantee to the defined outcome (e.g. "hit the target score or I keep coaching you free").
6. If a defined-end offer was working and you swapped it for a "buy continuity" ad, go back — continuity is rarely sold directly.
7. Remember that timing is part of the offer itself: the right offer pitched at the wrong moment in the buyer's journey is functionally still the wrong offer, so match not just what you offer but when you offer it to where the prospect actually is.
8. If a single, expensive front-end offer isn't converting, consider inserting a smaller, cheaper defined-end step ahead of it — a bounded diagnostic, audit, or assessment — to earn the larger commitment afterward rather than asking for the full ask immediately.

## Example from the AMA
A tutoring business found that everyone who came through a 3-week summer program "just wanted ongoing tutoring," so the founder switched ads to sell ongoing tutoring directly — and conversion collapsed. Hormozi told the ham-and-garlic parable (you wrap the medicine the dog needs in the ham it wants) and explained that no one ever bought a gym membership saying "I want a lifestyle" — they buy a 6-week challenge or 28-day fix. The fix: return to the defined-end offer, bundle it against a one-year package, and add a score-based guarantee. A fitness "ecosystem" operator got the compressed version: "market the thing they want, then give them the thing they need — don't try to explain the thing they need before you give them the thing they want."

A cocktail-consulting business (Ben) had been trying to sell a single cold $30k engagement directly, with weak conversion. Hormozi's fix illustrated both new points at once: restructure the funnel to lead with a much smaller, defined-end front-end offer — a $5–6k pricing audit with a clear, bounded deliverable — that earns trust and momentum before the larger $30k engagement is ever pitched, on the principle that "the right offer at the wrong time is still the wrong offer." Pitching the full offer cold, before the prospect had a defined reason to trust the scope of the larger commitment, was the wrong-time mistake being corrected.

## Caveats / Exceptions
- The defined-end offer is a genuine value delivery, not a bait-and-switch; the front-end result must be real.
- This differs from a paid low-ticket front door (which warms cold traffic and qualifies); here the point is packaging continuity behind a discrete outcome.
- The upsell must be earned inside the program through momentum, not forced at signup.
- Guarantees must be honest and bounded to a realistic outcome.
