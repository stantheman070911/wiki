# The Virtuous Cycle of Price - Why Premium Pricing Serves Customers Better

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[01-Business-Strategy/Business Strategy Index|Business Strategy]]

## One-line summary
Charging a premium is not just a profit decision but a delivery decision — a high price funds the margin, attracts the committed clients, and creates the investment that together produce better customer results, while cutting price sets off the opposite death spiral.

## Context
The instinctive way to widen the gap between price and value is to lower price, and for most businesses that is the wrong move: price can only fall to zero but can rise without limit, and there is no strategic benefit to being the *second*-cheapest in a market. This entry isolates the causal loop Hormozi uses to argue premium pricing is the more *ethical* choice, not merely the more lucrative one. It is the "why charge more" companion to the [[The Value Equation and the Grand Slam Offer]] (how to justify it) and [[Barbell Pricing and the Unscalable Premium Offer]] (where to sit on the price spectrum).

## Key insights
- **Price and value are different things, and the gap between them is the whole game.** People buy to get a deal — value received must exceed price paid. The goal is not the cheapest price but the widest favorable gap: sell $100,000 of perceived value for $10,000, not $10 of value for $9.
- **Cutting price triggers a downward spiral.** A low price lowers the client's emotional investment, lowers perceived value ("it can't be good if it's this cheap"), lowers results (uninvested clients don't do the work), attracts the worst and least-satisfiable clients, and destroys the margin needed to deliver an exceptional experience. Everyone loses.
- **Raising price triggers the reverse.** Higher price raises emotional investment, raises perceived value, raises results (invested clients follow through), attracts the best and easiest-to-satisfy clients, and multiplies margin — which can be reinvested in people, experience, and scale.
- **Price itself is a value signal.** In the blind wine study, identical wine was rated better when labeled more expensive. A high price doesn't just capture value; within limits it *creates* perceived value and lends the offer an aura of "there must be something entirely different going on here" — the psychology of a category of one.
- **"Those who pay the most pay the most attention."** If your product requires the customer to do something to get the result, their investment predicts their adherence. Pricing so it "stings a little" is therefore in the customer's interest, not against it.
- **Charge far more than it costs, not more than it's worth.** The moral line is not "never charge a lot" but "never charge more than the value delivered." Aim for a price many multiples of your cost to fulfill (think up to 100×), while keeping it a genuine steal for the buyer.

## Framework / model
### The two directions of the price loop
| Move | Emotional investment | Perceived value | Client results | Client quality | Margin to reinvest |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Cut price** | ↓ | ↓ | ↓ | worst / least satisfiable | destroyed |
| **Raise price** | ↑ | ↑ | ↑ | best / easiest to satisfy | multiplied |

The loop is self-reinforcing in both directions: margin funds a better product, a better product justifies a higher price, a higher price attracts better clients, better clients get better results, better results become the proof that justifies charging even more.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Stop pricing off the market average. Copying competitors prices you *at* the bottom of a commoditized field — and those competitors are usually broke. Price off delivered value instead.
- Raise price only *after* sufficiently increasing value, so the buyer still gets an obvious deal. Premium price + premium value keeps the gap wide.
- Aim to be the most expensive option in the market, not slightly above average — the discontinuity is what creates the "category of one" lure and monopoly-style margins.
- Reinvest the extra margin visibly into the customer experience, better people, and results delivery — that is what keeps the cycle spinning rather than pocketing it and coasting.
- Have the conviction to ask for a premium by earning it through reps: you can charge a year's salary for a result only once you have delivered that result enough times to *know* the client will succeed.

## Notable examples
- Hormozi's Gym Launch entered a market of $500/month competitors (with a single $5,000 high-end player) at **$16,000 for a 16-week engagement** — 3× the highest and 32× the lowest priced competitor — then upsold ~35% into a $42,000/year agreement. Clients still got a deal: the average member's topline grew ~$239,000 in 11 months against the $42,000 fee.
- A portfolio photography business raised average ticket from $300 to $1,500 (5×) while spending *less* time per customer with *higher* satisfaction; the price move 38×'d weekly profit ($1,000 → $38,000/week).
- The blind wine tasting (identical wine, three price labels) rated in strict order of price — evidence that price directly moves perceived value.
