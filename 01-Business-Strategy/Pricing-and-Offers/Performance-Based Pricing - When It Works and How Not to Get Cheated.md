# Performance-Based Pricing - When It Works and How Not to Get Cheated

## One-line summary
Performance pricing is always best on paper and often worst in practice; it only works when you can trust the client to pay, which means selling to accountable enterprises or controlling the money flow yourself — otherwise you've built an incentive to be cheated.

## Principle
Getting paid a share of results aligns incentives beautifully in theory. In practice it hangs entirely on one risk: will the client honestly report and remit? The whole viability of the model reduces to that single question. If you can't guarantee payment — through client quality or through structural control of the cash — a performance deal quietly rewards the client for underreporting.

## Scenario
Use this when an agency or service provider is moving from flat retainers to revenue-share or pay-for-results, especially in lead-gen, email, ads, and outbound where attribution and collection are contestable.

## Diagnosis
- You're switching to performance because one big "whale" made it look lucrative.
- Your customer base is SMB or mid-market, where contracts carry little weight.
- You can't see the client's true numbers (their CRM, their store, their revenue).
- Attribution is disputable ("did your lead close, or did they?").
- You're relying on trust and contracts rather than on visibility or control.

## Why It Happens
Performance economics are strongly predicated on prospect quality. Enterprises with assets, obligations, and reputations tend to honor contracts; smaller businesses often don't, and "US contracts mean basically nothing" when enforcement costs exceed the claim. When you can't see the numbers, you've set up an incentive system that rewards the client for cheating — and if someone must choose between cheating themselves or cheating you, they'll cheat you.

## Recommended Action
1. Gate performance deals on payment confidence: only offer them to clients you're highly confident can pay and will track (e.g. $10M+ ARR, venture-backed, real obligations).
2. If you must serve smaller clients on performance, control the money flow — receive payment and remit their share (the reason Shopify email agencies can run on percentages: clear tracking, and no one spins up a second store to cut you out).
3. Where you can't control cash, demand absolute transparency (direct CRM/e-commerce integration) before agreeing to a percentage.
4. When neither trust nor control exists, price to a north-star proxy instead (e.g. a rate per million emails or per verified action) rather than a share of their reported revenue.
5. Consider a two-part structure: a higher one-time fee to prove the result (find message-market fit), then a recurring/performance tier for scale.
6. Remember the risk you're truly underwriting is collection — solve that and you've solved the business; a payment-guaranteed deal can even go zero-dollars-up-front.

## Example from the AMA
A high-volume cold-email agency ($2.2M) wanted to switch to performance at scale after landing one big "whale." Hormozi warned that performance is "always the best model on paper, not always the best model in practice," strongly predicated on prospect quality: enterprises adhere to contracts, SMBs don't. He cited his own performance-based software (Alan), which only worked because "I controlled the flow of payments," and Shopify email agencies that can do percentages because tracking is clear and clients won't open a second store to cut them out. His guidance: only go performance with clients you're confident can pay and track; the entire business reduces to solving collection — "you solve that, you solve the business."

## Caveats / Exceptions
- One whale succeeding is not proof the model generalizes to your typical (smaller) customer.
- Controlling the money flow adds operational and trust burden; it's a feature, not a nuisance.
- Custom per-client pricing is a last resort; it fits when you have few, high-value customers.
- Even with enterprises, define attribution windows and reporting up front to avoid disputes.
