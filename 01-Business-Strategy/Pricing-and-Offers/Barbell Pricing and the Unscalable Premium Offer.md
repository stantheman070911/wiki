# Barbell Pricing and the Unscalable Premium Offer

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[01-Business-Strategy/Business Strategy Index|Business Strategy]]

## One-line summary
Sell something very expensive to a select few or very cheap to everyone — the middle is where businesses die — and when starting, the easiest premium is to sell your own time one-on-one, unscalably, because the cash and the anchor it creates fund everything else.

## Context
A business is an arbitrage between the cost of getting a customer and what you make from them. Where you sit on the price spectrum decides how hard that arbitrage is. Hormozi argues the extremes win and, counter to most founders' instincts, that an unscalable high-ticket offer is the best starting move.

## Key insights
- **The barbell: extremes win, the middle dies.** Either sell extremely expensive to a select few or super cheap to the masses. To serve the masses cheaply you first need enough money — which the premium end provides (Tesla: Roadster → S → Model 3).
- **Sell your time 1:1, even though it doesn't scale.** It doesn't have to scale. Reasons it's superior early: you learn far more from a few high-value clients (and they shift your beliefs about the market); everyone already earns per hour (income ÷ ~2,000), so "selling time is for poor people" is a limiting belief; more delivery flexibility for fast iteration/beta-testing; you can cap the time; ~100% margin; and it lifts the whole brand.
- **When you have demand, cut supply.** Contracted, fixed supply forces the price up (the two most powerful concepts in business are leverage and the supply/demand curve).
- **Confront the price for the anchor to work.** State the high price plainly ("working with me 1:1 is $10k"), let the prospect fully consider it, then shut up. If they balk, offer the scalable version with ~90% of the elements. Even buyers who never take the premium absorb its value by association.
- **The anchor math.** With a $100 and a $1,000 offer, if 10 of 100 buyers take the premium, revenue roughly doubles and that increment is ~100% margin — the expensive thing can produce ~3× the profit of the cheap thing.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
**Three frames to build the premium offer:**
1. **10×/100× frame** — "If someone paid me $100k instead of $1k, what would I include?" List it, cross out anything with real hard costs, keep the rest, and offer it at $1k–$10k.
2. **Word-of-mouth-only frame** — if the only way to get new customers were referrals from this one client, what would the offer/experience need to be? Build that.
3. **Remove-the-unscalable frame** — take out everything unscalable but make it worth 10× as much anyway; how?

**The value drivers (what makes something worth a premium):**
- **Dream outcome** — described for the *right* avatar (not your $100 buyer). Articulate their pain better than they can (mine the reviews of books they buy for exact pain language); "pain and persuasion only exist in the specific, never the vague."
- **Perceived likelihood of achievement** — 1:1/unscalable delivery raises it; reputation acts as an implied guarantee.
- **Speed / latency** — the strongest single persuasion lever: "latency beats magnitude." Halve delivery time; sell the wealthy on time saved, not money saved; grant priority.
- **Effort / ease** — walk the customer journey and delete friction point by friction point; higher price funds the more turnkey (and lets you pay vendors more to prioritize your customers).

## Tactics / how to apply
- List a premium price wherever you sell; if unlisted, say it out loud when you offer — and confront it, don't soften it.
- Add a $1,000 and a $10,000 tier above your current scalable offer, even if few buy.
- Pick the *premium* avatar deliberately; don't ask what your $100 buyer would pay $1,000 for.
- Use the three frames to design the offer; use the four value drivers to justify the price.
- Harvest the premium clients for case studies, marketing material, and a worldview-shifting network.

## Notable examples
- Hormozi's early gym: one referred client did 5×/week 90-minute sessions at ~$125/hr ≈ $45k/month cash, funding reinvestment in the business.
- Tesla's Roadster → Model S → Model 3 descent from premium to mass.
