# Price, Churn, and EPC - Optimize the Whole Revenue System

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[01-Business-Strategy/Business Strategy Index|Business Strategy]]

## One-line summary
A pricing decision is good only when its combined effects on conversion, retention, revenue, and acquisition economics improve earnings per visitor—not when it optimizes churn in isolation.

## Context
Price changes several variables at once: who buys, how many buy, what they expect, whether payment clears, how long they stay, and how much cash each click produces. Treating one of those variables as the objective can make the whole system worse.

## Key insights
- **LTV, not churn, is the retention objective.** A lower price may reduce cancellations yet still lower lifetime revenue.
- **EPC is the end-to-end scoreboard.** Earnings per click incorporates traffic, conversion, price, and retention into one comparable output.
- **Price and qualification must match.** High-ticket churn often reflects a weak prospect-value match rather than an inherent penalty for charging more.
- **The pricing curve is usually flatter than founders imagine.** A large price increase can create only a small net gain after conversion, expectations, financing, and failed payments change.
- **Product quality dominates marginal price tuning.** A well-qualified customer buying a strong product can support many viable price points; a weak product cannot be rescued by arithmetic.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
Evaluate a price test through the full chain:

1. **Traffic:** How many qualified visitors reach the offer?
2. **Conversion:** What percentage buys at the new price?
3. **Collected revenue:** What clears after failed payments, financing, refunds, and chargebacks?
4. **Retention:** How long do customers stay and how does usage affect value?
5. **EPC:** Divide total collected lifetime revenue by the clicks that produced it.

A lower price that cuts churn 20% but cuts revenue per customer 50% is usually a loss unless conversion or another downstream variable more than offsets it.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Run price tests on matched cohorts and measure collected revenue, conversion, churn, and EPC over the same time horizon.
- Segment results by customer qualification; aggregate churn can hide a poor-fit cohort.
- Record expectation, payment-failure, refund, and support-load changes alongside revenue.
- Reject tests that improve a local metric while reducing total contribution per visitor.
- Prioritize order-of-magnitude constraints before repeatedly tuning a price that moves profit only a few percent.

## Notable examples
Hormozi contrasts a hypothetical 50% price reduction with only a 20% churn improvement: the retention chart looks better, but the economics deteriorate. Conversely, raising a launch price from $6,000 to $9,000 would not automatically add 50% revenue because conversion, expectations, and card approvals would also change.
