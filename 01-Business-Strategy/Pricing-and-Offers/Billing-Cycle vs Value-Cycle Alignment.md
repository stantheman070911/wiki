# Billing-Cycle vs Value-Cycle Alignment

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[01-Business-Strategy/Business Strategy Index|Business Strategy]]

## One-line summary
Bill on the cadence at which the customer actually receives value; when someone only needs the product a few times a year, monthly billing manufactures churn, so match the invoice to the value cycle with quarterly or annual terms.

## Principle
Churn is often a billing-design artifact, not a satisfaction problem. If the value arrives quarterly but the invoice arrives monthly, the customer faces a purchase decision in every month where they got nothing — and some will cancel. Aligning the billing cycle to the value cycle removes those needless decision points and stabilizes retention while pulling cash forward.

## Scenario
Use this for subscriptions and memberships where usage is intermittent or seasonal — tools a customer only needs when they transact, services tied to episodic events, or any product whose value shows up in bursts rather than continuously.

## Diagnosis
- Customers churn in the months they didn't actively use the product.
- Real-world usage is periodic (e.g. a few times a year), but you bill every month.
- Annual plans, when offered, retain dramatically better than monthly.
- You're optimizing the product to fix churn before checking whether billing cadence is the cause.

## Why It Happens
Every invoice is a fresh purchase decision. When value delivery and billing are misaligned, you hand the customer repeated "should I keep paying for this?" moments precisely when they have the least evidence of value. Matching cadence to value delivery means each invoice lands next to a fresh experience of worth, so the renewal decision is made in a favorable frame — and prepaid terms give the customer runway before the next decision at all.

## Recommended Action
1. Find the natural value cycle: how often does the customer actually get paid-off value? (e.g. one transaction per quarter → quarterly.)
2. Set the default billing cadence to that cycle — quarterly or annual — rather than defaulting to monthly.
3. Run the "remove the option" test: try annual-only for a week; you aren't changing price, only removing a cadence, so it's the easiest test there is.
4. Keep a monthly option deliberately to preserve a fast feedback loop on churn while you fix the product; once monthly churn is genuinely solved, push everyone to annual for better economics.
5. For lower-tier/on-ramp customers, use a longer cadence (quarterly) to give more runway without forcing another purchase decision.

## Example from the AMA
A real-estate-focused SaaS at $60/month was losing ~100 of 150 weekly signups, largely because many agents only sell around four houses a year and "don't need this every month." Hormozi's move was to align billing to the value cycle — quarterly (one house per quarter) or annual — because "we want the value cycle to be aligned with the billing cycle." He advised keeping monthly available as an honest signal ("it keeps you honest") for a faster feedback loop, then pushing annual once churn was truly fixed. The same logic drove converting IV-therapy packs into prepaid memberships and offering an exhaust-hood service semi-annually at a higher per-visit price.

## Caveats / Exceptions
- Longer cadences hide churn signal; keep a monthly cohort while you're still diagnosing retention.
- Aligning cadence doesn't fix a genuinely weak product — it removes false churn, not real dissatisfaction.
- Annual-only can suppress top-of-funnel conversion for some audiences; test rather than assume.
- Prepaid terms improve cash flow and stickiness, but over-long commitments can raise the barrier to the first sale.
