# Levels of a Personal Brand and the Five Archetypes

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[02-Social-Media-Strategy/Social Media Strategy Index|Social Media Strategy]]

## One-line summary
A personal brand is how the world sees you when you're not in the room; it exists on four levels (0–3), and most people should deliberately aim for Level 1 — an extension of what you do that doesn't define you — via one of five low-lift archetypes.

## Context
Promoting yourself is now permanent cultural infrastructure — in an era of AI resumes, remote work, and thinner mentorship, a personal brand is how you steer out of being an anonymous blip. This entry maps the ladder so you can pick the altitude you actually want before you start.

## Key insights
- **Personal brand vs. creator are different goals.** A *creator* wants to be employed as a creator; a *personal brand* is meant to help your career or future ventures. Decide which you're building before you start — you can change later.
- **The "views are down" / flattening theorem.** More creators plus sharper algorithms (geography, interest, overlapping interest) fragment the market, so universal content competes against far more relevant niche content. That is the *opportunity* for small brands: a niche of 1–2k genuinely interested people can meet your objective.
- **The 3–10k-view sweet spot.** For most personal brands, consistently landing ~3–10k views per video is the target — reaching invested people, not chasing scale. Going much higher usually means you've drifted outside what you're trying to achieve. (Goal-dependent: true creators chasing reach need more.)
- **Entertain or give value — always.** No one cares about you; they care about themselves. Content that only talks about you has a smaller addressable market.
- **A business-linked personal brand needs leverage skills beyond posting.** Pitching, publishing, productizing, profile-building, and partnerships determine whether attention turns into authority and commercial opportunity.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
**The four levels:**
- **Level 0** — everyone online has some brand by default.
- **Level 1** — a personal brand that *extends* what you do but doesn't define you; not trying to live off it. **Aspire here if you're in business at all.**
- **Level 2** — the brand becomes part of your life and defines you online/offline; you make some money and consider going full-time (the hardest jump — many creators don't actually want it).
- **Level 3** — larger than life; influences trends and is referenced by others (the Gary V / guru tier; formerly reserved for authors like Seth Godin or Tim Ferriss).

**The five Level-1 archetypes:**
1. **Carousel dumper** — rapid-succession carousels of work/life (strong on Instagram; some creative directors ride this to real traction).
2. **Documentarian** — posting milestones (a raise, a project), often on LinkedIn.
3. **Hobbyist** — content about a passion tangential to your work; powerful with alignment (a realtor into interior design; a cinematographer into cars building a de-facto portfolio).
4. **Casual poster** — low-production yapping/skits (native to much of Gen Z; harder for older generations).
5. **Journeyman** — documenting a specific goal over a time frame ("learn to draw in 60 days").

## Tactics / how to apply
- Before posting anything, choose your target level and whether you're building a brand or becoming a creator; write down *why* (career leverage, distribution, attracting a partner).
- Pick one Level-1 archetype that fits your temperament and existing life, and default to it so content becomes a habit, not a second job.
- Judge success by view *quality* against your goal, not raw views; a few thousand of the right people can be the future hires, clients, or collaborators you want.

## Notable examples
- Creative directors with ~70k followers who essentially just "carousel dump" cool work.
- A cinematographer making good-looking car content builds a portfolio that proves craft without anyone questioning the passion.
