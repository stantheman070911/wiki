# Kaizen and the Progress Principle - Small Wins, Course Corrections, and Motivation

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Frameworks and Mental Models Index|Frameworks and Mental Models]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Focus-Execution-and-Systems/Focus Execution and Systems Map|Focus, Execution, and Systems]]

## One-line summary
Make improvement a recurring team process: define the standard, invite small changes from the people closest to the work, implement and broadcast visible wins, and correct small deviations before they compound.

## Context
Large breakthroughs are rare, difficult to schedule, and psychologically distant. Small improvements are easier to propose and repeat; collectively they improve the system while giving people the sense of forward motion that sustains motivation.

## Key insights
- **Innovation is an operating cadence, not an event.** The durable advantage comes from many implemented ideas, not occasional executive inspiration.
- **Small does not mean insignificant.** Tiny gains and tiny misses compound with repetition and time.
- **The frontline sees the friction.** Improvement systems work when the people doing the task are expected and empowered to refine it.
- **A suggestion needs coaching and ownership.** Raw complaints become useful ideas when a supervisor helps shape them and the originator participates in implementation.
- **Visible progress is intrinsically motivating.** People are most engaged when they feel they are making headway in meaningful work.
- **Early wins restart stalled teams.** Scaling a distant goal down into achievable milestones reduces helplessness and procrastination.
- **Course correction must be frequent.** A small deviation left unexamined becomes a different destination; check-ins keep work and relationships aligned.
- **Pay can crowd out intrinsic improvement.** Recognition, autonomy, meaning, and implemented ideas often sustain participation better than piece-rate rewards for suggestions.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
Use the **Standard–Improve–Implement–Broadcast loop**:

1. Define the current best-known standard and make it observable.
2. Ask every team member for one small, practical improvement within a fixed cadence.
3. Coach the idea until it is testable; require a solution and an owner, not an anonymous complaint.
4. Let the originator help implement the change.
5. Measure the result and adopt useful changes into the new standard.
6. Broadcast the progress and repeat.

Support the loop with regular one-to-one and team check-ins that ask: What is slightly off course? What is blocking progress? What is the smallest useful correction now?

## Tactics / how to apply
- Add one improvement proposal and one removed friction point to every recurring team review.
- Give supervisors responsibility for developing ideas, not merely approving or rejecting them.
- Break annual goals into milestones small enough to produce evidence of movement every week.
- Maintain a visible change log showing the idea, owner, result, and updated standard.
- Praise implemented learning and useful effort, not only dramatic outcomes.
- In relationships and teams, address a one-degree misalignment while it is still a sentence, not after it becomes a crisis.

## Notable examples
Toyota’s kaizen system made improvement part of every worker’s job and used supervisors as idea coaches. At the NUMMI plant, largely the same workforce and equipment produced radically better quality once authority, training, and improvement routines changed. British Cycling applied marginal gains across equipment, hygiene, sleep, and training, creating both performance improvement and a shared narrative of progress.
