# The E-Myth Owner-Role Triad - Entrepreneur, Manager, and Technician

## One-line summary
A business owner must deliberately alternate among three jobs—imagining the future, creating order, and producing today’s work—because letting the Technician dominate turns a business into a demanding job.

## Context
Technical competence often triggers an “entrepreneurial seizure”: a skilled worker assumes that knowing how to perform the work means knowing how to build a business that performs it. The two are different disciplines. A durable company needs the owner to design the enterprise, manage its operating logic, and ensure technical execution without confusing any one role for the whole.

## Key insights
- **The Entrepreneur asks what could be.** This role lives in the future, identifies opportunities, and defines the business as a designed response to a customer need.
- **The Manager asks how to make it orderly.** This role converts vision into plans, standards, priorities, and predictable coordination.
- **The Technician asks what must be done now.** This role performs the craft and values direct control, visible progress, and practical methods.
- **Each role is necessary; dominance is the problem.** Vision without management creates chaos, management without vision preserves an orderly irrelevance, and technical work without either creates founder dependence.
- **The fatal assumption confuses a craft with an enterprise.** Understanding baking, consulting, medicine, or software development does not automatically confer the skills required to acquire customers, manage cash, design roles, or build a delivery system.
- **The founder’s calendar reveals the real hierarchy.** If urgent production consumes every available hour, entrepreneurial and managerial work have been subordinated regardless of what the organization chart says.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
Use three recurring questions to activate the appropriate role:

1. **Entrepreneur:** What business and customer experience are we trying to create in the future?
2. **Manager:** What structure, standards, information, and sequence would make that future predictable?
3. **Technician:** What must be executed now, and what does excellent execution look like?

The roles should form a loop: vision sets direction, management builds the environment, technical work produces evidence, and that evidence informs the next version of the vision and system.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Audit two weeks of work and label each block E, M, or T. A founder trapped in technical work will see the imbalance immediately.
- Reserve recurring time for future work and system design before operational demand fills the calendar.
- When tempted to solve a recurring failure personally, ask whether the business needs better execution, a clearer management system, or a redesigned model.
- Define the technical work separately from the business that sells and delivers it; document the customer, promise, economics, roles, and operating method around the craft.
- Hire or train to restore missing roles, not merely to remove whichever task currently feels unpleasant.

## Notable examples
Gerber’s running example is Sarah, an excellent pie baker whose love of the craft becomes exhaustion after she opens a shop. Customers are buying Sarah’s personal performance rather than the shop’s reliable capability, so her supposed business is a job that cannot function without her.
