# Managerial Output and Leverage

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Frameworks and Mental Models Index|Frameworks and Mental Models]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Focus-Execution-and-Systems/Focus Execution and Systems Map|Focus, Execution, and Systems]]

## One-line summary
A manager's output is the output of the teams under their supervision or influence, so managerial productivity rises by choosing timely activities that multiply other people's performance.

## Context
Managerial calendars are filled with information, decisions, coaching, and interruptions, which makes busyness easy to confuse with contribution. The correct unit of value is not what the manager personally completes but what the organization produces differently because of the manager's work.

## Key insights
- **Output belongs to the system, not the manager's task list.** A design manager's output is working designs; a school leader's output is educated students; a know-how manager's output includes neighboring teams improved by their expertise.
- **Activities are inputs.** Meetings, reports, decisions, nudges, and reviews matter only through their effect on organizational output.
- **Leverage is impact per managerial activity.** High leverage comes from affecting many people, changing one person's work for a long time, or supplying a unique piece of knowledge that unlocks a large group.
- **Timing changes leverage.** Defining a planning process before two hundred people begin is far more valuable than clearing confusion after they start; intervening immediately when a valued employee plans to quit can preserve an otherwise lost option.
- **Negative leverage is often systemic.** Waffling, unprepared meetings, pessimism, and meddling can suppress the output of many people well beyond the time the manager spends.
- **Delegation transfers execution, not accountability.** Shared context makes delegation high leverage; follow-through at the lowest-value stage distinguishes monitoring from abdication.
- **Time is the manager's hardest resource constraint.** Calendar choices visibly communicate priorities and determine which potential output receives leadership attention.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
**Managerial output equation:**

> Managerial output = output of supervised organizations + output of neighboring organizations under the manager's influence.

Raise productivity through three moves:

1. **Increase activity rate:** reduce setup, batch similar work, and standardize recurring responses.
2. **Increase leverage per activity:** teach, clarify, decide, or design systems that affect many future hours of work.
3. **Change the activity mix:** deliberately replace low- or negative-leverage work with a small number of high-leverage interventions.

Use four questions on each candidate activity: Who will it affect? For how long? Is the manager's knowledge uniquely needed? Does the timing make the intervention more or less valuable?

## Tactics / how to apply
- Define three to five observable outputs for your team and for the adjacent teams your work materially influences.
- Review the prior week's calendar and label each block negative, low, medium, or high leverage; remove, delegate, or batch the bottom tier.
- Keep a discretionary inventory of improvement projects so spare capacity goes to system improvement rather than meddling.
- Delegate work you understand well enough to monitor. Review rough drafts, early indicators, or decision logic before value accumulates.
- Match monitoring frequency to the person's experience with that specific task, then reduce inspection as evidence of competence grows.
- Treat information gathering as a layered system: rapid informal signals, validating reports, and periodic deep analysis should overlap rather than substitute for one another.
- Visit the work directly; short, in-context conversations often transfer more information per minute than formal office appointments.

## Notable examples
Grove's two-hour new-employee orientation affected roughly two hundred people at once and modeled Intel's values through both content and behavior. A performance review uses only a few managerial hours but can redirect a person's work for a year. Conversely, a manager who delayed a decision effectively gave a red light to an entire organization, illustrating how inaction can have high negative leverage.
