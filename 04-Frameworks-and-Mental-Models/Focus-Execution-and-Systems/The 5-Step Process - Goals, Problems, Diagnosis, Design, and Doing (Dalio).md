# The 5-Step Process - Goals, Problems, Diagnosis, Design, and Doing (Dalio)

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Frameworks and Mental Models Index|Frameworks and Mental Models]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Focus-Execution-and-Systems/Focus Execution and Systems Map|Focus, Execution, and Systems]]

## One-line summary
Personal and organizational evolution happens through one iterative loop — set clear goals, surface the problems blocking them, diagnose those problems to their root cause, design a plan around the diagnosis, and then push through to execution — and doing all five steps well, in strict sequence without blurring them, all but guarantees getting what you want.

## Context
This is Dalio's general-purpose engine for achieving anything, introduced in Life Principles and reused verbatim as the backbone of Work Principles' management chapters (diagnosing problems, designing machines, executing). No one is naturally strong at all five steps, which is why the chapter spends as much time on humility and delegation as on the steps themselves.

## Key insights
- **1) Have clear goals.** Prioritize — you can have almost anything you want, not everything. Don't confuse goals with desires: a desire is a first-order temptation (a nicer car, looking smart in an argument) that can actively undercut the goal. Reconcile the two into what you truly want, and don't mistake the trappings of success for success itself.
- **2) Identify and don't tolerate problems.** Treat painful problems as improvements screaming at you, not as things to avoid because facing them is unpleasant. Be specific — don't confuse a cause with the problem itself, and don't let small and big problems blur together. Develop a "fierce intolerance of badness" once a problem is named.
- **3) Diagnose problems to their root causes.** Spend real time (15 minutes to an hour for a real diagnosis) establishing "what is" before jumping to "what to do about it." Root causes are described in adjectives — traits, character, design flaws — not verbs (specific actions); knowing what someone (including yourself) is like predicts what to expect from them going forward.
- **4) Design a plan.** Go backward before going forward — replay how you actually got here. Think of the problem as an output produced by a machine, and think of the plan like a movie script: visualize specifically who does what, in what order, through time. Write the plan down so it's measurable. Designing a good plan takes surprisingly little time relative to diagnosis and execution — "designing precedes doing," and rushing past it is the most common failure mode.
- **5) Push through to execution.** Great planners who don't execute go nowhere — good work habits are underrated. Establish clear metrics, ideally tracked by someone other than the person doing the work, so completion isn't self-graded.
- **Weaknesses don't matter if you find a solution for them.** Identify at which of the five steps you personally tend to fail, then either build the skill yourself or get help from someone strong where you're weak — there are only two paths to success: have what you need yourself, or get it from others through humility.

**Mental-map × humility matrix.** Plot two axes: quality of your mental map of a situation (vertical) against your degree of open-minded humility in seeking better answers (horizontal). The best outcomes sit at the intersection of both being high — but critically, humility alone (genuinely seeking a better answer from someone else) can outperform having a good mental map alone. This is why Step 2 and Step 3 in particular benefit more from asking the right people than from personal brilliance.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Before diagnosing anything, write down the goal in one sentence and check it isn't secretly a disguised desire.
- When a problem surfaces, force yourself to name it specifically (who, what, how big) before proposing any fix — resist the urge to skip straight to solutions.
- Give diagnosis a real time-box (15–60 minutes) rather than rushing to the plan; the plan-writing step itself should be comparatively fast once the diagnosis is solid.
- Identify which single step of the five you personally tend to skip or rush, and build a standing check (a person, a checklist) specifically for that step.

## Notable examples
- Dalio frames the whole process as directly transferable between a single person's career and an entire organization — Work Principles' chapters on managing, diagnosing, and designing (Chs. 10–13) are this same five-step loop applied at the machine/organization level rather than the individual level.
