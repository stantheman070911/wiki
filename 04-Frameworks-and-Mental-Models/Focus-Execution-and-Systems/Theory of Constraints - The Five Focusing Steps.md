# Theory of Constraints - The Five Focusing Steps

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Frameworks and Mental Models Index|Frameworks and Mental Models]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Focus-Execution-and-Systems/Focus Execution and Systems Map|Focus, Execution, and Systems]]

## One-line summary
Improve a system by finding the constraint that governs its goal, exploiting it, subordinating everything else, elevating it only when necessary, and repeating without letting old rules become the next constraint.

## Context
Complex organizations present many visible problems, but their interdependence means only a small number of leverage points determine overall performance. The Theory of Constraints turns ongoing improvement into a focused cycle instead of a portfolio of disconnected local projects.

## Key insights
- **Every system has a constraint.** If nothing limited performance, output toward the goal would be infinite; the practical task is to find the current limiting condition.
- **The constraint may be physical or managerial.** A machine can expose the limit, but policies, measurements, scheduling rules, market demand, and resistance to change often become the deeper constraint.
- **Exploit before investing.** Protect the constraint from breaks, defects, low-priority work, bad sequencing, and avoidable setups before buying capacity.
- **Subordination is the hard organizational move.** Non-constraints must operate in service of the constraint even when that lowers their local utilization or conflicts with existing incentives.
- **Improvement moves the constraint.** A solution that remains after conditions change can become harmful inertia; the system must be diagnosed again after each breakthrough.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
1. **Identify** the system's constraint—the condition that currently limits progress toward the goal.
2. **Exploit** the constraint—use its existing capacity as effectively as possible without major investment.
3. **Subordinate** everything else—align releases, priorities, policies, measures, and support resources to the exploitation decision.
4. **Elevate** the constraint—add capacity, redesign the process, change the market offer, or replace the governing policy if the limit still binds.
5. **Repeat and resist inertia**—when the constraint breaks, return to step one and re-examine every rule created for the previous state.

## Tactics / how to apply
- State the system's goal and the few global measures that reveal movement toward it before naming a constraint.
- Look for persistent queues, missing inputs at synchronization points, lost constraint time, or an external limit such as insufficient demand.
- Build an exploitation plan that eliminates avoidable loss: protect quality before the constraint, prioritize current demand, cover breaks, reduce changeover time, and offload suitable work.
- Translate subordination into explicit operating rules for upstream releases, downstream quality, staffing, and incentives.
- Elevate only after exploitation and subordination prove insufficient; otherwise capital spending can conceal a policy problem.
- After improvement, ask what changed, what old rule is now invalid, and where the new constraint moved.

## Notable examples
Bearington first identified heat treatment and the NCX-10 as bottlenecks. It gained capacity through better staffing, quality checks, full furnace loads, old machines, outside vendors, and alternative routings; then it subordinated material release to the bottlenecks. The constraint later shifted to market demand, and old rules—red/green priorities and make-to-stock orders—had to be removed because they had become sources of inertia.
