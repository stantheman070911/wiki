# Planning Backward from the Gap

## One-line summary
Planning converts the future gap between environmental demand and current momentum into a small set of actions that must begin now, then uses measurable milestones for early correction.

## Context
Today's shortfall usually reflects a planning failure in the past. A plan creates value only when it changes current commitments, capacity, or sequencing early enough to shape the future—not when it produces a polished document after choices have effectively closed.

## Key insights
- **Demand comes before feasibility.** First state what customers and the environment will require; prematurely editing demand to fit present capacity conceals the real gap.
- **Current momentum is a forecast, not a snapshot.** Include work already in the pipeline, its expected completion dates, and realistic attrition to estimate where doing nothing different will lead.
- **Separate need, capability, and commitment.** Ask what must happen, what can happen, and only then decide what will happen.
- **Strategy is the chosen action set.** Tactics are the next level's implementation details; both must be traceable to the gap they are intended to close.
- **The output of planning is changed behavior.** Decisions, starts, stops, resource moves, and monitoring routines are the deliverable; the bound plan is incidental.
- **Plans need a review horizon.** Implement the portion that lies before the next planning cycle, allow evidence to accumulate, and replan the rest.
- **Focus requires explicit exclusion.** Every yes consumes finite capacity and is therefore a no to another possible commitment.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
**Demand–momentum–gap:**

1. **Project environmental demand:** define customers, vendors, competitors, internal dependencies, and what they will require at the chosen horizon.
2. **Forecast present momentum:** inventory capabilities and work in process; estimate what will emerge, when, and with what failure rate if nothing changes.
3. **Calculate the gap:** compare demand and momentum using the same units and dates.
4. **Generate actions:** distinguish what is needed from what is feasible; evaluate alternatives for effect and timing.
5. **Commit strategy:** choose a limited action set, resources, owners, and explicit work to stop.
6. **Create milestones:** translate objectives into dated, observable key results that reveal whether progress is on pace.
7. **Review and replan:** use feedback before the next horizon rather than treating forecasts as promises.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Express demand and pipeline output in the same unit—for example activated customers, completed designs, or staffed operating hours.
- Work backward from the date the capability must exist and identify the latest safe start for each dependency.
- Add a loss factor to project portfolios instead of assuming every initiative will finish and contribute as planned.
- Limit each planning period to a few objectives; if everything is an objective, the system provides no focus.
- Make key results unambiguous about quantity and date, but do not use them mechanically as a performance contract when conditions or valuable opportunities change.
- Publish a stop list with every new plan so resources are genuinely reallocated rather than layered onto an already overloaded system.
- Evaluate the process by what started, stopped, or changed in the following weeks—not by the quality of the presentation.

## Notable examples
Grove distinguishes today's gap from today's decision: a car that has run out of fuel needed a decision earlier, not a more urgent reaction now. At Intel, planning for a Philippine expansion translated a future capacity need into dated studies, a location decision, ratification, and construction actions. Columbus's voyage illustrates that key results can be completed while the stated objective is missed, so milestones guide judgment rather than replace it.
