# More, Better, New - The Scaling Priority Sequence

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Frameworks and Mental Models Index|Frameworks and Mental Models]]

## One-line summary
To scale marketing (or almost any function), exhaust the options in order — do *more* of what works, then do it *better*, and only try something *new* when more and better are tapped out — because most operators invert this and waste 80% of their effort chasing new when the return on more is still sitting on the table.

## Context
Hormozi's default answer to "how do I scale?" is that entrepreneurs reach for novelty far too early. He watched a portfolio CEO making 15 ads a month ask how to grow, when Hormozi at that stage was making 35 a *week*. The sequence below is the priority order he applies to advertising, but it generalizes to sales headcount, content, and product.

## The sequence
1. **More.** When starting or scaling, more is almost always the answer. The people ahead of you aren't doing 2–3× your volume — often 100×. "Volume negates luck": if you don't want to depend on one of five ads being a winner, make more ads. This is the "create flow, monetize flow, then add friction" stage — run water through the machine to see where it breaks.
2. **Better.** Shift to optimization only when the return on effort for *better* exceeds one incremental unit of *more*. Adding the 2nd salesperson to a team of 1 can double output for the same effort; adding the 11th to a team of 10 is the same effort for a 10% gain — at that point a 20–25% throughput lift from a headline test or CTA tweak is the better return. Optimization only pays once real volume is flowing through.
3. **New.** Do new last, because **the cost of change is guaranteed but the return on something new is not.** Only reach for a new channel/tactic when more and better are genuinely exhausted — e.g. a local business already saturating its radius on one platform. The vast majority of entrepreneurs want to spend 80% of their time on new; they should spend ~90% on more and better of what already works, and touch new only when nothing is working (as at the very start, when everything is new).

## Key insights
- **Growth is an after-effect of inputs, not a goal.** You identify the constraint, relieve it, and volume grows into the next bottleneck — "all businesses grow until their point of constraint and then grow no further."
- **The 10× question gates the sequence.** Ask "what would stop me from 10×-ing this right now?" If nothing, do it. If something breaks first, isolate that constraint, fix it, then add volume — repeat.
- **New is a real investment with a real lag.** A new channel can take 3–12 months to pay back; mark *progress* (clicks → opt-ins → right-fit leads → booked calls), not day-one dollars, and treat it like investing half a year's profit to double the business — often a great return, but only judged on the right timescale.
- **A second working channel more than doubles enterprise value** because it also removes single-channel fragility.

## Application
- When you feel stuck, first ask "am I actually doing enough volume?" — usually the honest answer is no; do more before anything else.
- Switch to optimization only when one more unit of volume is higher-effort/lower-return than a sharpening tweak (the team-of-10 threshold).
- Before opening a new channel, confirm more and better are genuinely tapped; then fund it as a multi-month investment and track leading indicators, not first-month ROI.
- Keep re-asking the 10× question to surface the next constraint rather than diffusing effort across novelty.
