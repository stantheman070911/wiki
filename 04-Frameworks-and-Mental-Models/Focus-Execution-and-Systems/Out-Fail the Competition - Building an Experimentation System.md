# Out-Fail the Competition - Building an Experimentation System

## One-line summary
Increase the rate of bounded, measurable experiments: when downside is reversible and learning is shared, more failures produce faster feedback and create more chances for a rare winner to pay for the portfolio.

## Context
“Fail fast” is empty unless the organization changes decision rights, incentives, measurement, and knowledge sharing. Teams do not experiment because a slogan permits it; they experiment when the cost of trying is bounded and the social cost of an honest failure is lower than the cost of inaction.

## Key insights
- **Failure is useful only when it creates feedback.** Unmeasured repetition is not experimentation.
- **The target is experiment velocity, not recklessness.** Reversible, low-consequence decisions should move quickly; irreversible decisions still require deliberation.
- **Long-tailed returns justify many misses.** In business, one successful experiment can repay many small failures, so evaluating each attempt in isolation produces excessive caution.
- **Bureaucracy taxes learning speed.** Layers of approval turn inexpensive two-way-door decisions into slow, expensive commitments.
- **Incentives reveal the real culture.** If failed experiments harm reputation, compensation, or promotion, employees will protect themselves regardless of stated values.
- **Reward execution of a sound test, not an uncontrollable result.** The team controls hypothesis quality, test design, speed, and learning—not market response.
- **Failure is intellectual capital.** Sharing a rejected hypothesis prevents duplicate loss and gives the next experiment a better starting point.
- **A failure-rate metric needs guardrails.** Measure valid experiments and useful learning, not raw errors or avoidable negligence.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
Build the system around five levers:

1. **Remove bureaucracy:** delegate reversible decisions to small, high-judgment teams.
2. **Align incentives:** recognize well-run experiments regardless of outcome.
3. **Promote the behavior:** elevate leaders who model intelligent risk and remove managers who punish it.
4. **Measure accurately:** track hypotheses tested, cycle time, learning quality, adoption, and downside.
5. **Share the failure:** publish concise experiment records so knowledge compounds across teams.

Before testing, classify the decision: **one-way door** (hard to reverse; slow down) or **two-way door** (reversible; decide and test quickly).

## Tactics / how to apply
- Require each experiment to state the hypothesis, success measure, maximum downside, owner, and review date.
- Set an explicit budget and blast radius for reversible tests.
- Hold a weekly experiment review focused on learning and next actions, not blame.
- Track decision-to-result cycle time and reduce approvals that add no risk control.
- Maintain a searchable experiment log including negative results.
- Celebrate teams that stop a weak idea early because evidence changed—not only teams whose tests win.

## Notable examples
Booking.com built an experimentation platform and normalized thousands of concurrent tests to discover customer behavior rather than settle product arguments by authority. Amazon treats invention and failure as inseparable, using the asymmetric payoff of rare winners such as AWS to justify a portfolio of bounded experiments.
