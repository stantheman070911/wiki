# The One Value Prop - Find It With Two Questions

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Frameworks and Mental Models Index|Frameworks and Mental Models]]

## One-line summary
Ask your customers two questions — what one thing, if removed, would make this worthless to you, and what one thing would make you stay forever — and the answers hand you the single value proposition to build the entire business around; then relentlessly cut everything that doesn't serve it.

## Context
Sharran Srivatsaa scaled Teles Properties from ~$300M to $3.4B in topline sales in 5 years and now leads acquisition.com. The turnaround wasn't clever tactics — the messaging was simply wrong, so he sat down with all 30 agents and asked one question. Every one gave the same answer: "Being here saves me at least one day a week." That became the entire value prop — "come to us because our job is to save you at least a day a week; what would you do with another 52 days a year?" — and 10×'d the business with nothing else changed. (His scaling diagnostic — Traffic/Systems/Skills, 1-1-1, the universal front door — is covered in [[01-Business-Strategy/Growth-and-Scaling/少即是多的擴張系統：Traffic-Systems-Skills 診斷、1-1-1 與萬能前門|少即是多的擴張系統]]; his optionality/exit and decision frameworks are in the Chinese decision/finance entries.)

## Key insights
- **We fear talking to our customers.** Because we're smart, we *assume* they want all the extras — the calls, the Loom videos, the written reports, the hand-delivered baskets. They don't. They usually want one thing. Assumption is the enemy; ask.
- **The two questions that find the value prop:**
  1. *What is the one thing that, if I took it away, this would no longer be valuable to you?* → what to keep and build around.
  2. *What is the one thing I could add that would make you stay forever?* → where to invest next.
  They tell you what to eliminate and what to double down on — which simplifies the business.
- **Build every decision around the one value prop.** Once "save agents one day a week" was the prop, every proposal was filtered: does this help agents save a day a week? A $10M ad budget — no. So it wasn't done. The prop simplifies the pitch *and* the operations.
- **The curse of capability.** Smart, capable operators take on complex things *because they can*, and build a tangled web of offers. "To do great things, we must do fewer things." Complexity isn't linear — it's exponential ($100M is far more than 100× the complexity of $1M).
- **Grow by subtraction.** When Sharran's team invests in complex businesses, they cut and cut — "you have 90% of your time back and only 10% of revenue dropped." Project forward: what would the $10M or $100M version look like? That instantly reveals what's important and what to cut.
- **The universal front door.** One simple thing can become the entryway to everything (Mailchimp's first 5,000 contacts free became its whole model → a multi-billion sale). Find the one thing and make it the front door.

## Application
- Interview your best customers with the two questions verbatim; look for the answer that repeats. That repeated answer is your value proposition.
- Rewrite your messaging as that one prop, stated as the outcome the customer feels ("save you a day a week"), not your features.
- Filter every initiative through "does this serve the one prop?" — kill what doesn't, even if it's clever.
- When the business feels complex, ask "what would the 10×/100× version look like?" and grow by subtracting toward it.
