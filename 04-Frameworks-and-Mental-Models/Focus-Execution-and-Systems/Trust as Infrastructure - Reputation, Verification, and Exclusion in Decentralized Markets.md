# Trust as Infrastructure - Reputation, Verification, and Exclusion in Decentralized Markets

## One-line summary
When central enforcement is weak, commerce depends on identity, reputation, collateral, verification, and credible exclusion that make future opportunity more valuable than a one-time fraud.

## Context
Decentralization removes some gatekeepers but also removes guarantees they supplied. A functioning market therefore needs an enforcement stack that helps strangers verify counterparties, contain losses, and penalize defection.

## Key insights
- **Trust is not optimism.** It is confidence produced by evidence, aligned incentives, limited exposure, and enforceable consequences.
- **Persistent identity turns behavior into an asset.** A reputation matters only when actions can be linked reliably to the same actor over time.
- **Future access can enforce present conduct.** Exclusion from a valuable network, market, or profession can be a stronger penalty than a small fine.
- **Verification and privacy must coexist.** Parties may need proof of identity, authority, funds, or performance without exposing every unrelated detail.
- **Trust systems can be captured.** Reputation scores, certifiers, and dispute processes become new centers of power and need appeal, audit, and anti-collusion mechanisms.
- **High-trust systems fail safely.** They still use staged commitments, collateral, limits, and redundancy because a good history never eliminates tail risk.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
Build a five-layer **trust stack**:

1. **Identity:** Can the actor be linked to prior behavior?
2. **Verification:** What evidence proves capability, ownership, or delivery?
3. **Alignment:** What does each party lose if it defects?
4. **Containment:** How is exposure capped before trust is established?
5. **Enforcement:** Can restitution, exclusion, or legal remedy actually occur?

Missing layers should reduce the size and reversibility of the transaction.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Start new counterparties with small, reversible commitments and increase limits only after verified performance.
- Use milestone payments, escrow, collateral, warranties, and audit rights where quality appears after delivery.
- Record objective delivery history instead of relying on testimonials alone.
- Make sanctions proportional and portable enough that repeated defection is costly across the relevant network.
- Give participants a way to challenge false or manipulated reputation data.
- Preserve an external legal remedy for high-impact failures even when most disputes are settled privately.

## Notable examples
Medieval trade fairs used officials who notarized contracts, seized goods from defaulters, and could exclude dishonest merchants from future fairs. The book anticipates cryptographic identity and verifiable reputation playing a similar role in digital markets where participants may never meet.
