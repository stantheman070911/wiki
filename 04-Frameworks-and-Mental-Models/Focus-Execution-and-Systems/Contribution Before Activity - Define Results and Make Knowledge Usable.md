# Contribution Before Activity - Define Results and Make Knowledge Usable

## One-line summary
Define a role by the result others need from it—not by its activities, authority, or expertise—then shape its output, relationships, and meetings so that contribution can actually be used.

## Context
Specialized knowledge is incomplete by itself. It becomes productive only when another person can understand and apply it, which makes usability and coordination part of the specialist’s responsibility rather than administrative overhead.

## Key insights
- **Activity answers “What do I run?”; contribution answers “Why am I on the payroll?”** The second question lifts attention from effort and status to the performance of the whole organization.
- **Contribution has three dimensions:** direct results, the values and standards the organization must preserve, and the people and capabilities needed for tomorrow. Neglecting any one eventually weakens the institution.
- **A role’s contribution changes with the role.** Continuing the behaviors that earned a promotion can cause failure when the new position requires a different balance among results, standards, and people development.
- **Specialists own the usability of their output.** Expertise is not an excuse for jargon. The producer must learn who will use the work, what they need to understand, when they need it, and in what form.
- **Productive relationships are organized around mutual contribution.** Warmth without results is not good human relations; clear expectations, communication, teamwork, and individual development follow from asking what colleagues need from one another.
- **Meetings are contribution tools.** Their format should follow a declared purpose—decision, information, report, exploration, or coordination—and they should end once that purpose is achieved.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
**The contribution contract:**

1. **Expected result:** What material outcome should the organization expect from this role over the next 18–24 months?
2. **Three-part test:** What direct result, standard/value, and future capability must the role create?
3. **Users:** Who must use this output to produce the next result?
4. **Usability:** What do those users need, when, and in what form?
5. **Reciprocity:** What contribution do you require from them?
6. **Operating occasions:** Which meetings, reports, or presentations are necessary to make the exchange happen?
7. **Evidence:** What external or downstream change will show that the contribution worked?

## Tactics / how to apply
- Rewrite role descriptions from verbs such as “manage,” “oversee,” or “prepare” into explicit results and users.
- Ask key peers: “What contribution from me lets you make yours, and when and how do you need it?”
- Before producing a report, identify the decision it should enable; remove information that is precise but irrelevant to that decision.
- Open each meeting by stating its intended contribution and format. Do not let an information meeting become a free-form brainstorm or let a decision meeting end without a decision owner.
- Close meetings by restating the conclusion, owner, deadline, and required communication.
- Revisit the contribution contract after a promotion, structural change, or major shift outside the organization.

## Notable examples
- A government science publication became more polished under a professional writer but lost its scientific readers: the predecessor had written *for* young scientists and recruitment, while the replacement wrote *at* them.
- A bank’s agency department redefined itself from accurate clerical processing into a relationship channel to the senior financial executives who bought the bank’s other services.
- A retail-chain president concluded that only he could make management development real, then repeatedly checked whether managers had followed through on development commitments.
