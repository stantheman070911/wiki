# The Business Teardown - Hormozi's Diagnostic Method

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Frameworks and Mental Models Index|Frameworks and Mental Models]]

## One-line summary
A repeatable procedure for diagnosing any business: run a fixed intake, sanity-check the numbers before trusting them, triage supply vs. demand, lock the avatar and the channel, find the single binding constraint, then prescribe only the two or three highest-leverage moves — sequenced light-and-fast first.

## Context
This is the meta-methodology reverse-engineered from a run of live Hormozi consulting sessions across wildly different businesses (a railing e-commerce store, a personal-styling service, a garbage-collection route, a travel-hedging course, a lash-salon chain, a Thai restaurant, a chiropractic chain, a music-law practice, a couples-coaching program, and more). The businesses differ; the diagnostic sequence he applies is almost identical every time. The value is the sequence, not any one company's answer. See the companion [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Focus-Execution-and-Systems/The Diagnostic Pattern Library - Condition, Root Cause, Fix, Scaling Strategy|Diagnostic Pattern Library]] for the recurring if-A-then-B relationships this method surfaces.

## The intake (ask in this order)
He gathers the same facts every time, in roughly this order, before proposing anything:
1. **What do you make?** Revenue, profit, net margin, and **LTV:CAC** — the one ratio he treats as the core economic signal.
2. **What do you want to have happen?** The goal, forced into a specific number and timeframe ("5 million" → "would tripling this year be OK?").
3. **Who do you help?** The avatar(s), and what **percentage** each is of the business (mix by segment, income, custom vs. off-the-shelf).
4. **How do you help them?** The offer and delivery mechanics (onboarding, fulfillment, what recurs).
5. **How do you make money?** Price points, billing model, front-end/back-end, what's recurring vs. reoccurring.
6. **How do you get customers?** Every channel and the **percentage of customers from each** — cross-referenced against where the owner spends their *time*.
7. **Sales velocity.** Leads/month, show rate, close rate, churn.
8. **What's the problem / what's holding you back?** Their stated constraint (which is often a symptom, not the root cause).

## Questions to help understand a business
The eight-item intake is the spine; below is the fuller question bank he draws from, grouped by what each cluster is trying to expose. Pull the ones that fit the business in front of you.

**1. Financial health and current state** — establish the ground truth of the numbers first.
- "How much money do you make?" / "What's the current state of the business?"
- "What are your net margins?"
- "How old is the business?"
- "Do you have very high fixed costs in this business?"
- "How do you come up with pricing?" (cost-plus vs. value-based)

**2. Goals and vision** — the owner's dream state, and whether they want to scale, keep, or exit.
- "What's the goal?" / "What do you want to have happen?"
- "Do you want to sell the business, or just make money with it?"
- "Why is solving this important — what happens if you don't fix it?"

**3. Understanding the avatar** — is the audience too broad, and are they high-value?
- "Who do you help?" / "Who do you serve?"
- "What's your dream customer?"
- "Is there an income level associated with this?"
- "Is there a specific life event that triggers people into this buying cycle?"
- "Who tends to be attracted to the message — the woman or the man?"

**4. Customer acquisition and marketing** — hunt for single-channel dependency and lead-flow efficiency.
- "How do you get customers?" / "Where do you find them?"
- "What percentage of customers come from each source?"
- "What's your sales velocity per month?"
- "What's the door-open rate?" (for door-to-door)
- "What stops you from just spending more on ads?"
- "Do you have prices on the site?"

**5. Identifying constraints (the 'heartburn' questions)** — pinpoint exactly where it's breaking.
- "What's the problem? What's holding you back?"
- "Is this a supply- or demand-constrained business?"
- "If we double the lead flow, can you handle that?"
- "If you had to wave a magic wand, what's giving you the most heartburn right now?"
- "Why the rush of opening new locations before you had the money and manpower?"

**6. Product and offer structure** — how value is delivered, and whether recurring/upsell exists.
- "How do you help them?" / "How do you make money?"
- "What percentage of people go from the core offer to the upsell?"
- "What percentage of customers are new versus returning?"
- "If they decide they want to come back, how does that work?"
- "If you eliminated [the time-consuming task], how much time would you get back?"

**7. Sales and operational process** — the click-to-close mechanics and how the team is run.
- "Walk me through the sales process — from click to close."
- "How are you actually facilitating the transaction?"
- "How do you teach your people right now?"
- "What's the incentive for the staff to stay?"
- "Do you have abandoned-cart tracking?"

## The diagnostic sequence
- **Sanity-check the numbers first.** Before optimizing, find the anomaly. A nonsensical LTV:CAC (1:1, 41:1) means "we have numbers but they're not useful" → a data/attribution problem to fix *before* any spend decision. Never optimize on data you haven't validated.
- **Triage: supply-constrained or demand-constrained?** His "first triage question." Demand-constrained (can handle more customers) → attack lead flow / conversion / spend. Supply-constrained (more demand than you can serve) → pare or productize delivery, raise price, or add capacity. (See [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Focus-Execution-and-Systems/Demand or Supply|Demand or Supply]].)
- **Make the two recurring decisions: avatar and channel.** Most stuck small businesses are spread across several avatars and several channels. Force a choice: **one avatar, one channel, one product to ~$1M**, then expand. Pick the avatar you have the most of, that is least price-sensitive and highest-margin; pick the channel already producing the most customers for the least effort.
- **Find the single binding constraint.** "All businesses grow until their point of constraint, then grow no further." Ask **"what would break first if you 10×'d?"**, relieve exactly that, and grow into the next constraint. Growth is an after-effect of relieving constraints, not a goal.
- **Fix leaks before adding traffic.** "Why pour more into a leaky bucket?" Retention/conversion first, then volume.
- **Reason from the fewest need-to-believe.** Plan on the fewest assumptions possible — every added assumption sharply lowers the odds it works. The "ramrod": *if only one thing were true, what would stop this from working?* — then put all effort on that one lever.

## Prescription discipline
- **Give only 2-3 things**, not 20 — "so you have a high likelihood of actually doing it." Leverage means finding the few moves with the highest output.
- **Sequence light-and-fast before heavy.** Do the high-impact, near-zero-effort moves first (a headline change, turning up spend, a menu reprint), then the heavier work (hiring, new systems). Respect dependencies: build the system → drive traffic to it → add long-term nurture in parallel.
- **Think in triples (the "rule of 3 and 10").** Systems break when they roughly triple; plan in 3× steps rather than one giant leap, because each order-of-magnitude change breaks something new.
- **Have the founder sell early.** Founder-selling keeps you close to the customer, gives the fastest feedback on offer/avatar/pricing, converts better (highest conviction), and preserves cash. Only hand it off once you literally can't take the calls — by then revenue has already grown enough to fund the fix.
- **Watch for the six small-business mistakes** he repeatedly flags: (1) too many avatars / no focus, (2) over-expansion before nailing the model, (3) mis-set compensation, (4) mispricing (usually under-pricing), (5) single offer with no front-end/back-end, (6) selling out of your own wallet (charging from what *you'd* pay, not the customer's value).

## Application
- Run the eight-question intake verbatim on any business before forming an opinion; capture channel and revenue *by percentage*.
- Validate the numbers and resolve anomalies before acting; if attribution is broken, fixing it is step one.
- Answer supply-vs-demand, then force the avatar and channel choices; collapse toward one-avatar/one-channel/one-product until ~$1M.
- Name the single binding constraint via the 10× question; prescribe only 2-3 moves and order them fast-and-light first, heavy later, dependencies respected.
