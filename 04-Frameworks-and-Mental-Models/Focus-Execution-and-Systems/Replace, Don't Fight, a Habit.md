# Replace, Don't Fight, a Habit

## One-line summary
Break a bad habit by preserving its cue and purpose while substituting a less harmful routine and reward; suppression keeps attention fixed on the behavior and drains limited self-control.

## Context
“Stop doing it” is not an executable behavior. A habit already solves something—relief, stimulation, belonging, or escape—and removing it without a replacement leaves both the cue and the unmet need intact.

## Key insights
- **A habit is a loop, not an isolated action:** cue → routine → reward. Change is easier when each component is observed separately.
- **Suppression can rebound.** Trying not to think about a behavior keeps the behavior cognitively active and can increase the chance of performing it.
- **The brain responds better to a positive action.** “Chew gum when the urge arrives” gives the cue a new route; “do not smoke” leaves no route at all.
- **The replacement must serve the same job.** A substitute that does not provide some version of the original reward will not survive the cue.
- **Self-control is sensitive to load.** Stress, sleep deprivation, deprivation-heavy plans, and several simultaneous changes make relapse more likely.
- **Environment carries more weight than intention.** Removing the old routine from reach and putting the replacement directly in its place reduces the number of decisions required.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
Use the **Replace the Loop protocol**:

1. Record the cue: time, place, emotion, people, and preceding action.
2. Name the actual reward being sought—not merely the behavior.
3. Choose a small replacement routine that can start immediately under the same cue.
4. Remove access to the old routine and make the substitute visible and frictionless.
5. Change one loop at a time until the new response becomes the default.
6. Protect sleep and lower avoidable stress during the early repetition period.

## Tactics / how to apply
- For one week, log each occurrence without trying to change it; look for the repeating cue and reward.
- Write the new behavior as an implementation rule: “When X happens, I will do Y for Z minutes.”
- Make the replacement smaller than your ambition: a short walk can replace the first five minutes of stress scrolling.
- Keep the desired action in sight and the undesired action out of immediate reach.
- Treat a lapse as loop data: identify which cue, reward, or environmental condition the substitute failed to handle.

## Notable examples
Bartlett’s father smoked only in his car. Once he recognized the car as the cue, he removed the cigarettes and put miniature lollipops in the same container. He retained the reach-and-reward sequence while replacing the harmful routine.
