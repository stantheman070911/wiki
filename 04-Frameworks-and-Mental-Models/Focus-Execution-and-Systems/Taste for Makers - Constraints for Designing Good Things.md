# Taste for Makers - Constraints for Designing Good Things

## One-line summary
Good design is not arbitrary preference: it is simple, timeless, structurally correct, suggestive, and repeatedly revised by a maker whose taste stays ahead of the current execution.

## Context
Makers need a usable standard for choosing among many possible forms. Taste supplies that judgment, but it develops through close study, imitation, production, and correction rather than declarations of originality.

## Key insights
- **Good design is simple because the problem is understood.** Ornament often disguises unresolved structure; removing it forces the core to work.
- **Timelessness is a stress test.** If a design depends on current fashion, it may optimize social signals rather than function.
- **Good work solves the right problem.** Precision applied to a mistaken specification produces polished failure.
- **The best objects are suggestive.** They provide strong primitives and leave room for users to combine them in unforeseen ways.
- **Copying what you genuinely like is productive.** Beginners imitate surfaces, then gradually discover the principles behind their preferences and form an independent taste.
- **Revision is normal.** Early versions are not proof of talent but material for comparison against the standard.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
Review a design through six lenses: **function, simplicity, coherence, durability, user freedom, and ease of revision**. When two options remain, prefer the one that explains more with fewer arbitrary decisions and can evolve without breaking its conceptual core.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Collect examples you admire and name the specific property each gets right.
- Remove decorative elements one at a time; restore only those with a clear job.
- Test the design against real use rather than creator intention.
- Compare with older successful work to detect fashion-dependent choices.
- Give one accountable designer authority over coherence while inviting evidence from many reviewers.

## Notable examples
Programming languages, paintings, mathematical proofs, and physical products share recurring design virtues despite different media. This cross-domain recurrence is evidence that taste tracks properties beyond personal preference.
