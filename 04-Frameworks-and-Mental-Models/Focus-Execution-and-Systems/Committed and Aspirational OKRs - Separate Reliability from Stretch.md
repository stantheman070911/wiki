# Committed and Aspirational OKRs - Separate Reliability from Stretch

## One-line summary
Label must-deliver commitments separately from possibility-expanding aspirations so teams can protect reliability without lowering ambition or punishing intelligent failure.

## Context
One score cannot mean the same thing for a service-level promise and a moonshot. When the two are mixed, teams either sandbag to protect themselves or divert resources from essential work toward a goal they never credibly promised to deliver.

## Key insights
- **Committed OKRs are promises.** Teams plan and reallocate resources to reach 100 percent; a miss requires prompt escalation and a learning-focused postmortem.
- **Aspirational OKRs describe a desired future before the path or resources are known.** An average near 70 percent can indicate appropriately difficult stretch, not poor performance.
- **The labels change operating behavior.** Calling a commitment aspirational weakens urgency; calling an aspiration committed creates defensiveness and priority inversion.
- **Ambition must still be credible.** A goal no one believes can be influenced destroys morale instead of stimulating invention.
- **Capacity should reveal tension.** Commitments should consume most, but not all, available resources; commitments plus aspirations should exceed current capacity and expose the demand for investment.
- **Stretch needs guardrails.** Exponential growth in one metric can degrade quality, safety, satisfaction, or trust unless counter-results protect the system.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
Use a two-basket portfolio:

1. **Commitment basket:** Required operational promises with known owners, resources, plans, and a target score of 1.0.
2. **Aspiration basket:** High-value future states that require discovery, invention, or additional resources, with expected misses and wider score variance.

Fund and sequence the commitment basket first. Use remaining capacity to pursue aspirations by explicit priority, carrying forward only those that remain relevant.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Mark every OKR as committed or aspirational when it is written, not after results arrive.
- If a commitment lacks resources or conflicts with another priority, escalate immediately rather than silently accepting the miss.
- Draft aspirations from the desired future or customer need, not as a timid increment above current performance.
- Ask whether the combined portfolio credibly consumes somewhat more capacity than the team has; if not, the aspiration may be business as usual.
- Keep a relevant aspiration visible across cycles so chronic under-resourcing or weak understanding cannot disappear through deletion.
- Add quality and human-impact key results to any stretch metric that could be optimized destructively.

## Notable examples
- Google treated product and service promises as 1.0 commitments while using roughly 0.7 as a healthy result for ambitious goals such as Chrome adoption.
- YouTube stretched toward one billion daily hours of watch time, then recognized that watch time alone was insufficient and that satisfaction, quality, and social responsibility also needed protection.
- Bono's ONE Campaign needed both discipline and "more red": structure stopped the organization from spreading itself across too many causes, while accepted misses preserved its appetite for transformative goals.
