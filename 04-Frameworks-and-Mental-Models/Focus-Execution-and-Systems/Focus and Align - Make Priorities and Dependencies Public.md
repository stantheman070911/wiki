# Focus and Align - Make Priorities and Dependencies Public

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Frameworks and Mental Models Index|Frameworks and Mental Models]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Focus-Execution-and-Systems/Focus Execution and Systems Map|Focus, Execution, and Systems]]

## One-line summary
Shared goals improve execution when leaders choose very few priorities, explain why they matter, and expose every cross-team dependency without turning alignment into rigid top-down cascading.

## Context
Organizations lose capacity when priorities multiply, contributors cannot connect their work to the mission, or teams assume another group will deliver an unstated dependency. The first two OKR superpowers—focus and alignment—make those tradeoffs and obligations visible.

## Key insights
- **Priority means exclusion.** A long goal list is a record of wishes; three to five top objectives create a usable decision rule for allocating time and resources.
- **Leadership conviction is operational.** Leaders must model the system, repeat why each top-line goal matters, and visibly accept the same transparency expected from everyone else.
- **Alignment is a network, not only a cascade.** Top-down direction gives coherence, bottom-up goals contribute autonomy and local information, and horizontal commitments connect the functions that must deliver together.
- **Roughly half bottom-up preserves initiative.** Frontline contributors often see opportunities and threats before the center does; negotiated key results are stronger than mechanically inherited ones.
- **Transparency creates a social contract.** Public objectives let colleagues see why a request matters, where their work fits, and who is blocking whom.
- **Every dependency must appear twice.** If Team A needs Team B, both teams should record the contribution; awareness without an explicit commitment is fragile.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
Build alignment across three directions:

1. **Vertical intent:** Leaders publish the mission-linked outcomes and the tradeoffs they imply.
2. **Bottom-up ownership:** Contributors propose goals and negotiate key results close to the work.
3. **Horizontal contracts:** Teams record shared milestones, owners, timing, and dependencies in each participant's goals.

The result is not complete cascading. It is a visible network in which central priorities guide distributed judgment.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Begin each cycle by selecting the few objectives with the greatest leverage and publishing a stop-doing list alongside them.
- Use all-hands communication to explain the purpose of each company objective, then let teams translate it into outcomes appropriate to their work.
- Review cross-functional goals by asking: Who else must act, is their commitment recorded, and what happens if their milestone slips?
- Elevate a departmental objective to a company objective when it requires broad lateral support.
- Name a single owner for each key result while preserving collective responsibility for the objective.
- Notify every stakeholder when a shared goal is revised, transferred, or dropped.

## Notable examples
- Intel's Operation Crush translated a strategic emergency into aligned objectives across sales, marketing, engineering, and support rather than treating the response as one department's campaign.
- MyFitnessPal discovered that a partner team's unacknowledged dependency could threaten a shared launch; explicit cross-team OKRs converted assumed help into planned capacity.
- Intuit used public goals to connect globally distributed functions and replace siloed handoffs with horizontal collaboration.
