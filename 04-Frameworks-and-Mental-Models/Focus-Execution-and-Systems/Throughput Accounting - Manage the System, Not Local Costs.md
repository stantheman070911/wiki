# Throughput Accounting - Manage the System, Not Local Costs

## One-line summary
Judge an operating decision by whether it increases throughput, reduces inventory, or reduces operating expense for the whole system—not by whether it makes one resource look efficient.

## Context
Traditional cost accounting distributes overhead across products and rewards local utilization, which can make excess production look productive and inventory look like an asset. Throughput accounting reconnects operational decisions to the business goal of making money now and in the future.

## Key insights
- **Throughput is created only by sales.** Producing something that remains in work-in-process or finished-goods inventory has not generated money.
- **Inventory is money trapped in intended sales.** It consumes cash, space, handling, coordination, and risk of obsolescence; adding notional labor value to it can hide those liabilities.
- **Operating expense is the money spent turning inventory into throughput.** Labor and most plant costs do not change merely because a non-constraint performs another setup or stays busy.
- **The constraint sets the economic value of time.** An hour lost at a binding constraint is an hour of system output that cannot be recovered elsewhere; an hour saved at a non-constraint may be only idle capacity renamed as savings.
- **Local efficiency can reduce global profit.** Running every resource at full utilization creates queues and finished goods when the constraint or market cannot absorb the output.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
Use three operational measures together:

1. **Throughput (T):** the rate at which the system generates money through sales.
2. **Inventory (I):** the money invested in things the system intends to sell.
3. **Operating expense (OE):** the money the system spends to turn inventory into throughput.
4. **Desired direction:** increase T while simultaneously reducing I and OE; when trade-offs are necessary, test the decision at the system level rather than assigning fictional savings to a department.

## Tactics / how to apply
- For any proposed action, write its expected change to T, I, and OE in actual cash terms; reject allocated-cost changes that do not alter cash or system output.
- Value a constraint hour using the throughput it enables or the system expense it governs, not the resource's isolated hourly rate.
- Do not release work merely to keep people or machines active. Release it only when the constraint and customer demand require it.
- When spare capacity exists, evaluate incremental business using incremental material, freight, and truly variable costs plus constraint consumption—not an average product cost loaded with fixed overhead.
- Track lead time, due-date performance, work-in-process, throughput, and cash alongside financial reports so accounting conventions cannot conceal operational improvement.

## Notable examples
The Bearington plant increased throughput, cut inventory, and improved delivery while reported product cost initially rose because smaller batches created more setups. The expense base had not risen; the accounting model treated idle non-constraint time as if it were a scarce cost and later recorded inventory reduction as a loss because less "value added" remained capitalized on the balance sheet.
