# The Framework for Making Frameworks - Distill by Commonality

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Frameworks and Mental Models Index|Frameworks and Mental Models]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Focus-Execution-and-Systems/Focus Execution and Systems Map|Focus, Execution, and Systems]]

## One-line summary
To turn any repeated task into a reusable framework: ingest a mass of real examples, list everything each one did, keep only the few factors they all share, delete the rest, and name the result to make it memorable.

## Context
Hormozi is known for framework-ing everything; asked for his method, he gives a repeatable process. The point of a framework is to avoid re-making the same decisions or re-solving the same problems — so anything you must do over and over is a candidate for distillation.

## Key insights
- **Frameworks exist to stop re-deciding.** If you have to do something repeatedly, build a template so you don't re-litigate the same choices each time.
- **Signal is what the winners have in common.** Individual winning examples each do many things; most of those things are noise. The transferable causes are the handful of factors present across *all* of them.
- **Delete to the core.** The work is subtractive: gather everything, then strip away everything that isn't shared, until only the common few remain.
- **Naming is the last, easy step.** Make it sticky with alliteration or an acronym so it can be recalled and taught.
- **Definitions must survive counterexamples.** Start from observable facts, propose the simplest definition, then deliberately search for a case that the definition labels incorrectly; keep revising until it stops breaking.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
**The framework-making process:**
1. **Ingest a mass of data** — collect many real instances of the outcome you want (e.g. Hormozi watched the intros of his top 35 YouTube videos; interviewed 20 gyms with sub-3% monthly churn).
2. **List everything each did** — write out all the actions/elements in every instance, however many (one had five, another seven, another twenty-plus).
3. **Find the commonalities** — circle what appears across all of them.
4. **Delete everything else** — reduce to only the shared few.
5. **Name it** — same-letter or acronym for memorability.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Pick a task you repeat (writing intros, retaining customers, closing calls) and gather 15–35 real examples of it done well — ideally your own, so the pattern fits your context.
- Transcribe every element of each example without filtering, then look only for what *all* share; discard the rest even if it feels valuable.
- Pressure-test the distilled few: would removing any one break the result? If not, cut it.
- Give the final set a memorable name so you and your team apply it without re-deriving it.
- For abstract ideas, explain them as if to an observer who cannot infer intention or “read between the lines.” Define every necessary term with behavior or evidence that two people can independently observe.

## Notable examples
- **Promise, Proof, Path** — derived by watching his top 35 video intros and keeping the three elements they all shared. (See [[Promise, Proof, Path - Engineering the Video Intro]].)
- **The Five Horsemen of Retention** — at Gym Launch, Hormozi had industry-average churn; he called ~20 gyms with under 3% monthly churn, each doing 20+ things, and found all 20 shared only five — which became the retention framework.
- **Discipline as a falsification test** — defining discipline as merely “action” fails because many pleasurable actions require no discipline. A useful definition must distinguish doing what is immediately reinforcing from persisting through unrewarded or aversive action.
