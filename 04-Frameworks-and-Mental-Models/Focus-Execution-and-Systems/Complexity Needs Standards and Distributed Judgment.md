# Complexity Needs Standards and Distributed Judgment

## One-line summary
Complex systems become reliable when a few critical standards and communication routines constrain failure while local experts retain authority to adapt to conditions no central plan can predict.

## Context
A complicated problem can be decomposed and repeated from a known design; a complex problem changes as people act and cannot be fully specified in advance. Pure command-and-control becomes a bottleneck, while unconstrained autonomy fragments the system.

## Key insights
- **Simple, complicated, and complex problems require different controls.** A recipe can solve a simple task; expertise and coordination solve a complicated one; complex work additionally requires adaptation because outcomes and interactions are not fully repeatable.
- **More expertise does not automatically create system reliability.** Optimizing each component can still produce an incoherent whole when interfaces, timing, and information flow fail.
- **Centralize goals and critical constraints; decentralize response.** Leaders define what must be protected and how the system will stay connected, then push decisions to the people closest to changing facts.
- **Standards should preserve capacity for judgment.** The right protocol secures known essentials and clears attention for novelty; the wrong one attempts to prescribe every move.
- **Communication is part of the control system.** Scheduled cross-checks make distributed authority coherent by updating the shared picture and exposing conflicts between specialties.
- **Reliability is institutional, not heroic.** The modern professional ideal adds disciplined procedure and teamwork to selflessness, skill, and trustworthiness.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
**The minimum-specification system:**

1. **Classify the work:** determine what is repeatable, what requires specialist sequencing, and what remains genuinely unpredictable.
2. **Standardize the known criticals:** encode the few safety, quality, or commitment conditions that cannot be left to memory.
3. **Create communication checkpoints:** require the relevant experts to compare information before commitment and at important transitions.
4. **Define local decision rights:** give the edge of the system permission to act when conditions diverge from plan.
5. **Keep a common goal and measures:** let distributed decisions vary while outcomes and non-negotiable constraints remain visible.
6. **Learn from recurring failure:** investigate patterns, revise the standards, and keep the system adaptive.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Separate every operating procedure into non-negotiable conditions, recommended defaults, and areas of local judgment.
- Move approval authority toward the role with the freshest information, while requiring rapid reporting of decisions that affect other parts of the system.
- Add cross-functional review points around interfaces—design to build, sales to delivery, production to release—where independent specialties can invalidate one another's assumptions.
- Audit incidents for system interactions rather than stopping at the person who made the final visible mistake.
- Treat tools and automation as components that also introduce failure modes; verify how people, technology, and handoffs operate together.
- Revise standards when evidence changes and publish a date or version so yesterday's solution does not harden into doctrine.

## Notable examples
Modern construction abandoned the all-knowing Master Builder. A project runs through specialized schedules plus mandatory consultations when the unexpected appears. During Hurricane Katrina, centralized command structures seized up, while Walmart set common priorities and measurements and authorized local managers to improvise relief. Both cases paired shared constraints with distributed judgment rather than choosing one at the expense of the other.
