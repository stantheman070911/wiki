# OKRs - Define Objectives Through Sufficient Key Results

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Frameworks and Mental Models Index|Frameworks and Mental Models]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Focus-Execution-and-Systems/Focus Execution and Systems Map|Focus, Execution, and Systems]]

## One-line summary
An OKR turns a consequential direction into an observable promise by pairing one concrete objective with the complete set of measurable outcomes required to achieve it.

## Context
Teams often mistake intentions, task lists, launches, or metrics for goals. Objectives and key results separate the desired change from the evidence that the change occurred, making priorities easier to communicate, coordinate, and revise.

## Key insights
- **The objective is the what.** It should be significant, concrete, action-oriented, unambiguous, and valuable enough to justify scarce attention.
- **Key results are the evidence-bearing how.** Each one should name a specific, time-bound, verifiable outcome rather than an activity such as helping, analyzing, consulting, or participating.
- **Necessary is not sufficient.** If every key result could be completed while the objective still fails, the set is incomplete; add the hard result that closes the logical gap.
- **Pair quantity with quality.** A volume target without a guardrail invites gaming, corner-cutting, or damage elsewhere in the system.
- **Keep the set small.** Three to five objectives per cycle, with no more than about five key results each, force a real choice about what not to do.
- **Do not confuse a launch with value.** "Ship X" is weak unless the goal is the shipment itself; usually the stronger result states the user or economic effect the launch must produce.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
Use the **Objective–Key Result sufficiency test**:

1. **Objective:** State the valuable end condition in one crisp line.
2. **Outcomes:** List the measurable changes that would prove progress.
3. **Evidence:** Give each result a number, date, and credible source of verification.
4. **Sufficiency:** Ask whether scoring 100 percent on every result necessarily fulfills the objective.
5. **Guardrails:** Pair speed or quantity with quality, safety, cost, or satisfaction where goal gaming could cause harm.
6. **Value test:** Ask who would notice or benefit if the objective reached 100 percent.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Rewrite activity verbs into end-user or business outcomes: replace "analyze latency" with a dated, published latency result and the improvement it must enable.
- Use real milestone dates instead of placing every key result on the final day of the cycle.
- Define ambiguous denominators and windows explicitly: distinguish all-time users from seven-day active users, for example.
- Assign one owner to each result even when the objective is collective.
- Reject any objective that can score perfectly without producing direct user, operational, or economic value.
- Add supporting key results to every team whose contribution is material; an unrecorded dependency is not a commitment.

## Notable examples
- Google contrasts the activity "launch Foo 4.1" with the outcome "improve sign-ups by 25 percent"; the latter makes the reason for shipping testable.
- Intel paired output goals with quality measures so a team could not hit a production number by degrading reliability.
- The Ford Pinto and Wells Fargo cases show why a single aggressive quantity target without counterbalancing quality or ethics can produce rational but destructive behavior.
