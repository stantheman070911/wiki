# Soft Skills Are Observable Behavior Chains

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Frameworks and Mental Models Index|Frameworks and Mental Models]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Focus-Execution-and-Systems/Focus Execution and Systems Map|Focus, Execution, and Systems]]

## One-line summary
Soft skills become teachable when abstract labels such as empathy, rapport, or leadership are decomposed into observable behaviors that can be practiced, reinforced, and measured.

## Context
Managers often give feedback using words everyone recognizes but no one can execute: “be empathetic,” “build rapport,” or “show leadership.” The skill feels mysterious because its success criteria have not been operationalized.

## Key insights
- **A skill is an adaptive chain of behaviors.** Complexity comes from the number and sequencing of actions, not from an unteachable essence.
- **Labels describe; they do not instruct.** “Empathy” may include asking about feelings, recapping, acknowledging, pausing, and changing vocal delivery.
- **Soft skills are harder to define, not less definable.** They usually contain more success conditions than a narrow technical task.
- **Observation separates signal from mythology.** An expert may perform well without knowing which behavior caused the result.
- **Immediate feedback accelerates learning.** Correct one behavior at a time, repeat it immediately, and then reconnect the chain.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
To operationalize an abstract skill:

1. Name the outcome the label is supposed to produce.
2. Observe several people achieving that outcome.
3. List what they visibly say or do before, during, and after it.
4. Separate constants from variables.
5. Turn each behavior into a drill with pass/fail criteria.
6. Practice the full sequence under varied conditions and reinforce immediately.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Ban abstract feedback unless it is followed by a behavioral example.
- Record real conversations and annotate the exact action preceding a useful response.
- Translate vocal “tone” into controllable dimensions: volume, speed, pitch, articulation, and pauses.
- Train one link at a time before rehearsing the entire interaction.
- Ask whether the definition survives counterexamples; revise it when it predicts the wrong thing.

## Notable examples
Hormozi reduces rapport to asking questions until a genuine similarity is found and then naming the connection. He reduces vocal tone to a few controllable variables: remain audible, clear, and slow enough to understand; vary pitch and pauses to solicit response and attention.
