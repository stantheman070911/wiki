# The Greener-Pasture Fallacy - Fix the Business You Have Before You Start the Next One

## One-line summary
When a business hits a hard constraint, founders are frequently tempted to start a new, unrelated venture rather than solve the constraint in front of them — the new venture feels like progress but is usually an escape from a harder, more valuable problem.

## Principle
A stalled or constrained business generates real discomfort, and starting something new produces the feeling of momentum without requiring the founder to face the specific, often unglamorous fix the original business needs. The new venture is rarely actually a better opportunity in expectation — it's an unconstrained, unexamined idea that looks better only because it hasn't yet accumulated the visible scar tissue of the current business. The discipline is to exhaust the fix for the business you have before diverting founder attention, capital, or team bandwidth into a new one.

## Scenario
Use this when a founder whose core business has hit a plateau, a churn problem, or a supply/demand constraint proposes launching an unrelated new product, service, or company instead of addressing the constraint directly.

## Diagnosis
- The core business has an identifiable, specific constraint (a supply bottleneck, a churn problem, a founder-dependency issue) that hasn't been directly addressed.
- The proposed new venture is unrelated or only loosely related to the core business's actual constraint.
- The founder's reasoning for the new venture centers on excitement or a market opportunity, not on data showing the core business's ceiling is truly structural and unfixable.
- Team, capital, or founder time would be split between the working core business and the new idea rather than concentrated on removing the core constraint.
- A similar pattern has occurred before — the founder has a history of starting new initiatives when the current one gets hard.

## Why It Happens
Fixing a known constraint is specific, often tedious work with a real chance of failure that's attributable to the founder — a churn problem might mean confronting a flawed sales process or a bad hire. Starting something new offers a blank slate where nothing has gone wrong yet, so it feels safer even though it isn't: the founder is trading a known, addressable problem for an unknown set of new ones, while leaving the original constraint to keep degrading the core asset.

## Recommended Action
1. Before greenlighting a new venture, name the core business's specific current constraint (see [[Demand or Supply - The One-Constraint Diagnosis, Three Functions, and the Ticking Time Bomb]]) and confirm whether it has actually been attacked directly, not just tolerated.
2. Apply the [[The Change Cost Filter - Stay, Scale, or Burn It Down]] risk-adjusted bar to the new venture — it must clear a materially higher expected-value threshold than simply fixing the current constraint, not just feel more exciting.
3. If the new idea is a genuinely lower-touch extension of the core (a natural adjacent product), evaluate it as a supply-scaling lever (see [[The X-Factor Dilution Problem - Four Ways to Scale Past Yourself]]) rather than a separate business.
4. If the founder's real motivation is escaping a hard but solvable problem, name that directly — the new venture should be shelved until the core constraint is either fixed or proven structurally unfixable.
5. Where multiple businesses already exist, apply the same discipline in reverse: consolidate attention onto the one with the most upside rather than continuing to split it (see [[The Direct-Response Doom Loop and the One-Brand Bet]]).

## Example from the AMA
Several callers illustrated this pattern from different angles. A hair-extensions business founder wanted to build a separate SaaS product rather than address gaps in her core business. A CFO-advisory founder wanted to launch a course business instead of fixing retention in the advisory practice. A direct-response e-commerce operator running four capped brands kept looking for a fifth opportunity rather than concentrating on the one brand with real potential. And a couple running "Coupleneur" built an entire new low-touch product line instead of resolving the supply constraint limiting their core, higher-value offering. In each case, Hormozi's redirect was the same: identify and fix the specific constraint in the existing business first, since the new venture's appeal was mostly the absence of a known problem, not a genuinely superior opportunity.

## Caveats / Exceptions
- Not every new idea is an escape — some founders correctly recognize a business is structurally capped (see [[The TAM Illusion - Saturated Markets, the Puddle-to-Ocean Path, and Five Directions to Move]]) and a new venture is the right call after genuinely exhausting the current one.
- A new venture that directly leverages the same supply, brand, or customer base as the core business is a different, often legitimate case of scaling (see [[The X-Factor Dilution Problem - Four Ways to Scale Past Yourself]]), not a distraction.
- Founder burnout on a specific problem is real; sometimes the right move is bringing in help to fix the constraint rather than either abandoning it or personally starting something new.
