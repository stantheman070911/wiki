# Mirroring and Voice - Slow the Negotiation and Keep Them Talking

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Frameworks and Mental Models Index|Frameworks and Mental Models]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Persuasion-and-Influence/Persuasion and Influence Map|Persuasion and Influence]]

## One-line summary
Use a calm voice, repeat a counterpart's last one to three important words, and leave silence so they elaborate without feeling interrogated.

## Context
Negotiators often lose information by talking too much, rushing to fill silence, or reacting to surface demands. Mirroring and deliberate vocal delivery slow the exchange, build rapport, and make the other person feel safe enough to reveal the thinking underneath a position.

## Key insights
- **The listener directs while the speaker reveals.** A short mirror transfers the conversational burden back to the counterpart without creating the pressure of a new question.
- **Delivery changes meaning.** A positive, playful voice is the best default; a slow, downward-inflected late-night-FM voice communicates calm control when stakes rise.
- **Silence completes the mirror.** The pause gives the counterpart room to inspect, clarify, or correct what they just said.
- **Mirrors reduce cognitive load.** Reusing the counterpart's own words is easier for them to process than a complex rebuttal and signals attention without premature agreement.
- **Speed is usually the enemy.** Slowing down reduces mistakes and makes hidden assumptions easier to notice.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
Use **Hear–Mirror–Pause–Harvest**:

1. **Hear:** listen for the last one to three words, or the most important phrase, rather than planning a response.
2. **Mirror:** repeat those words with a curious or neutral upward inflection.
3. **Pause:** remain silent for at least four seconds; do not rescue the other person from the silence.
4. **Harvest:** listen for new facts, emotions, constraints, and contradictions, then mirror or label the most useful thread.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Begin difficult conversations with a relaxed, unhurried cadence and a genuine smile when appropriate; the smile changes the sound of the voice.
- When a claim is vague, mirror its most loaded phrase instead of challenging it: “Too expensive?” or “Needs board approval?”
- Use the late-night-FM voice for boundaries, high emotion, or facts that must land without sounding combative.
- Avoid overusing the direct, assertive voice; it invites a status contest and should be reserved for rare boundary-setting moments.
- After every mirror, count slowly before speaking. If the counterpart continues, return fully to listening.

## Notable examples
In a bank-hostage negotiation, Voss's team used a calm voice, mirrors, and long pauses to keep the lead robber talking. The seemingly small repetitions encouraged him to disclose that the supposed group of robbers did not exist, changing the team's understanding of the situation.
