# Guarantee Execution - How, the Rule of Three, and Hidden Players

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Frameworks and Mental Models Index|Frameworks and Mental Models]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Persuasion-and-Influence/Persuasion and Influence Map|Persuasion and Influence]]

## One-line summary
An agreement is real only when the implementation is credible, the commitment survives three tests, and every person able to block execution is accounted for.

## Context
A verbal “Yes” can be counterfeit, aspirational, or unsupported by the people who must act. Negotiators therefore need to design the path from consent to execution, not merely celebrate the apparent close.

## Key insights
- **“Yes” is nothing without “How.”** Value appears only when an agreement is carried out.
- **Implementation questions create ownership.** People work harder on a solution they have explained in their own words.
- **The Rule of Three tests conviction.** Reaching the same commitment three different ways makes false agreement and unresolved hesitation harder to hide.
- **Incongruence is data.** A mismatch among words, tone, and body language indicates doubt that should be labeled, not ignored.
- **The table is larger than the room.** Behind-the-table stakeholders can implement, alter, or kill a deal regardless of the visible decision maker's enthusiasm.
- **Deal killers may matter more than deal makers.** One unbriefed operator can defeat an otherwise sound plan.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
Use **Commit–Reaffirm–Operationalize–Map**:

1. **Commit:** capture the first explicit agreement.
2. **Reaffirm:** summarize or label it until the counterpart confirms, “That’s right.”
3. **Operationalize:** ask how success will be measured, what happens off track, and who does what by when.
4. **Map:** identify everyone affected, their motivation, their authority, and their ability to block execution.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Ask “How will we know we are on track?” and “How will we address it if we get off track?”
- Treat “I’ll try” and “You’re right” as warnings that ownership is missing; return to implementation questions.
- Vary the three commitment checks: direct agreement, an accurate summary, and a calibrated implementation question.
- Label nonverbal hesitation: “I heard yes, but it seems like something still does not fit.”
- Ask “How on board are the people not on this call?” and “What will this change create for the rest of the team?”
- Confirm that the operational handoff preserves every mechanism required by the plan; never assume support travels automatically through a hierarchy.

## Notable examples
During a Louisiana prison siege, negotiators created a walkie-talkie ritual to prove surrendering inmates would be safe. An officer outside the negotiation confiscated the radio and nearly triggered violence. The agreement itself was sound, but one hidden implementation player almost destroyed it.
