# Risk Reversal - The Guarantee Toolkit

## One-line summary
The single biggest objection to any purchase is risk, so the highest-leverage conversion move is to reverse it with a guarantee — and a guarantee only has teeth when it names a concrete "or-what": if you don't get X in Y time, we will Z.

## Context
Reversing risk can lift conversion 2–4× on the same offer, which is why experienced marketers spend as much time crafting the guarantee as the deliverable. This is the reusable decision tool for *which* guarantee to use and how to structure it; it sits alongside [[Scarcity and Urgency - Engineering Demand with Supply and Deadlines]] and the bonus-stacking material as the offer-enhancer layer of [[The Value Equation and the Grand Slam Offer]].

## Key insights
- **A guarantee needs an "or-what."** "You'll get 20 clients guaranteed" is weak; "20 clients in 30 days or you get your money back *plus* your ad spend" has teeth. The conditional Z is what makes it credible and shifts risk from buyer to seller.
- **Do the math, not the emotion.** A stronger guarantee that lifts sales 30% while doubling refunds from 5%→10% still nets ~23% more collected sales. For a guarantee to *lose* money, the added refunds must fully offset the added sales — rare. So default toward stronger guarantees and let arithmetic, not fear, decide.
- **Guarantees can attract bad customers.** People who buy *only* because of the guarantee may not do the work. Tying the guarantee to the actions required for success both improves results and screens for commitment.
- **Watch guarantees on high-cost fulfillment.** If each sale carries heavy delivery cost, a refund means eating both the refund *and* the fulfillment cost — prefer conditional, anti-, or performance guarantees there.
- **Not having a guarantee is itself attention-worthy.** Because guarantees are so standard, boldly declaring "all sales final — because once you see this, you can't un-see it" (an anti-guarantee) can raise perceived value rather than lower it.
- **Name it.** "30-day money-back guarantee" is vanilla; a named, vivid guarantee ("the Club-a-Baby-Seal guarantee") sells harder for the same terms.

## Framework / model
### The four families of guarantee
| Type | Structure | Best fit |
|---|---|---|
| **Unconditional** | Pay first, refund for any reason ("informed decision," no questions) | Low-ticket B2C; highest volume, some refunds |
| **Conditional** | Refund/continued work *only if* the buyer does defined actions and still fails | Most B2B and results-based services; screens for effort |
| **Anti-guarantee** | "All sales final," with a credible reason-why (exposure that can't be un-seen) | Consumable, exclusive, or easily-copied deliverables; high-ticket customization |
| **Implied / performance** | Rev-share, profit-share, pay-per-result — no result, no payment | Trackable outcomes; perfect interest alignment |

### The strongest conditional variants
- **Service guarantee (Hormozi's favorite):** keep working for free until the result is achieved — eliminates *time* risk while never exposing the fee. Add conditions (attend calls, submit data) to keep it fair.
- **Outsized refund:** double/triple money back, or a fixed payout larger than price, gated on heavy consumption conditions (used on high-margin offers).
- **Delayed-payment / first-outcome:** don't bill (or keep paying their ad spend) until the customer hits their first defined win — focuses the whole team on activation.

### Stacking
Combine an unconditional short-window guarantee with a conditional longer-window one (e.g., 30-day no-questions + 90-day conditional triple-money-back), or two sequential outcome guarantees. Stacking compounds perceived safety and future-paces the result.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Identify the buyer's single biggest fear ("what do they *not* want to happen if they pay?") and reverse *that* specific fear into the guarantee.
- Match guarantee type to price point and fulfillment cost: unconditional for low-ticket B2C; conditional/service for high-ticket or high-delivery-cost B2B; anti-guarantee for easily-copied or consumable goods; performance where outcomes are measurable.
- Make the guarantee's conditions the exact actions your best customers already take — the guarantee then sells *and* engineers results.
- Give a vivid name and state it boldly, even the anti-guarantee.
- If a customer wants their money back, give it — "don't take blood money." Refund rates below ~5% are the signal that your product is strong enough to guarantee at all.

## Notable examples
- A $2,997 course guaranteeing "if you spend $X on ads and don't profit, I'll buy your store for $25,000" reportedly generated ~$3M in extra sales while paying out only ~10 refunds — the guarantee produced ~$2.75M net.
- Hormozi's weight-loss satisfaction guarantee ("I'll write you a check the day you tell me we suck") saw **2 refunds out of ~4,000 sales** over 3.5 years.
- Agencies switching from retainer to a *performance* (implied) guarantee reportedly moved from ~$20K to ~$200K/month by aligning pay with client results.
