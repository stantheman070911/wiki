# Practical Value - The Psychology of Deals and the Rule of 100

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Frameworks and Mental Models Index|Frameworks and Mental Models]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Persuasion-and-Influence/Persuasion and Influence Map|Persuasion and Influence]]

## One-line summary
People share genuinely useful "news you can use" to help others — and whether a deal *feels* good depends not on its real size but on reference points, framing, and the Rule of 100.

## Context
The fifth STEPPS driver (see [[The STEPPS Framework - Six Drivers of Word of Mouth]]) and the easiest to apply — almost anything saves time, money, or effort. Sharing useful information is a modern barn-raising: it helps the *receiver* (unlike Social Currency, which flatters the sender) and strengthens bonds. It explains why hikers discuss vacuum cleaners and why an 86-year-old's corn-shucking clip hit 5M views. The subtle part is that "value" is perceived, not absolute — so the same discount can seem great or trivial depending on how it's set up.

## Framework / model
Perceived value follows Kahneman & Tversky's **prospect theory**:

- **Reference points.** People judge a price against what they *expect* to pay, not in absolute terms. A grill cut $350→$250 feels like a great deal; the same grill cut $255→$240 doesn't — even though $240 < $250. Infomercials ("you'd expect to pay $200… only $39.99!") and "regular price" tags exist to set a high reference point. Merely labeling an item "SALE" at an *unchanged* price lifted demand >50%.
- **Diminishing sensitivity.** The same change matters less the farther it is from the reference point. Almost everyone drives 20 min to save $10 on a $35 clock radio; almost no one does it to save $10 on a $650 TV — identical savings, different felt magnitude.
- **The Rule of 100** — a shortcut for framing a discount as a percentage vs. a dollar amount:

> [!note] Rule of 100
> **Under $100 → show the percentage.** On a $30 shirt, "20% off" beats "$6 off."
> **Over $100 → show the dollars.** On a $2,000 laptop, "$200 off" beats "10% off."
> Pick whichever frame produces the bigger-looking number.

## Key insights
- **Scarcity raises perceived value here too.** Limited time, quantity limits ("limit 3 per customer," +50% sales), and restricted access ("exclusive member rate") all make a deal feel better — but if *everything* is always on sale, the sale price becomes the new reference point and the discount stops working.
- **Narrow can out-share broad.** Content with a *small* obvious audience (Ethiopian food, water polo) is often shared *more* than broad content, because it snaps a specific friend to mind and compels the pass-along. Niche relevance beats reach.
- **Package the value to travel.** Vanguard's tight one-topic *MoneyWhys* emails get forwarded; so do "5 ways to…" listicles. Make value easy to see (a bell/sign showing how much a shopper saved) so it can be talked about.
- **Truth caveat.** The drive to help means *useful-seeming-but-false* info spreads fast too (the discredited vaccine–autism fraud). Verify before passing on.

> [!warning] Two different "Rule of 100"s
> Berger's Rule of 100 (above) is about **discount framing** — the $100 price threshold that flips percentage vs. dollar presentation. It is *unrelated* to Alex Hormozi's Rule of 100 (do 100 primary lead-generation actions a day) in [[The Core Four and the Rule of 100 - Lead Generation Fundamentals]]. Same name, different concept — don't conflate them.

## Application
- Set a credible high reference point before revealing your price; keep a visible "regular price."
- Use the Rule of 100 to choose the discount frame; below $100 lead with %, above $100 lead with $.
- Add honest scarcity (time, quantity, or access limits), but don't make "on sale" the permanent state.
- Target content at the narrowest audience that genuinely fits; package useful knowledge into short, forwardable, single-topic bundles.
