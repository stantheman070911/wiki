# Reciprocation and Commitment - Persuasion's Two Deepest Levers

## One-line summary
Two of the most powerful compliance forces are the rule of reciprocation (we feel obligated to repay what we receive) and the drive for consistency (we align later behavior with earlier commitments) — both work automatically, and both can be triggered by a small first move.

## Context
Cialdini's first two principles are the deepest because they exploit near-universal social wiring: the debt of a gift and the pressure to match your own prior word. Understanding them is essential for both ethical persuasion and self-defense.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
**Reciprocation — the old give and take.** We're obligated to repay favors, gifts, and concessions; the rule is so strong that even an *uninvited* or *unwanted* first gift creates indebtedness, and an unequal repayment (repaying far more than received) is common because being indebted feels worse than overpaying.
- *Free samples, the Hare Krishna flower:* give first, then ask; the gift creates the debt that drives the "yes."
- **Rejection-then-retreat (door-in-the-face):** make a large request sure to be refused, then retreat to the smaller one you actually wanted. Your concession triggers *their* obligation to concede in return (Boy Scout: $5 tickets refused → a $1 chocolate bar bought). It also rides the contrast principle, and — crucially — produces *more* responsibility for and satisfaction with the final deal, so the target follows through.

**Commitment and consistency — hobgoblins of the mind.** Once we take a stand, we face internal and external pressure to behave consistently with it. Compliance pros secure a small initial commitment that then justifies larger, aligned requests.
- **Foot-in-the-door:** a tiny "safe-driver" window sticker later made homeowners agree to a huge ugly "Drive Carefully" billboard on their lawn — the small act shifted their self-image.
- **Commitments stick when they are active, public, effortful, and freely chosen.** Written and witnessed commitments bind hardest; effortful initiations (hazing, hard-won membership) raise loyalty; a choice made without heavy outside pressure is internalized as "who I am."
- **The lowball:** get the decision on an attractive basis, then remove the original reason (raise the price, drop the incentive). The commitment often survives because people grow their own new legs to support it.

## Key insights
- **A small first move sets the trajectory.** Both levers are seeded by something minor — a free sample, a signature, a checkbox — that reorganizes later behavior.
- **The obligation is felt, not reasoned.** People comply to discharge a debt or protect self-consistency, often against their own interest.
- **These build durable relationships when used honestly** (genuine gifts, real commitments) and backfire when counterfeited.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Give real value first (useful content, a sample, help) before asking — the WIKI's "give-only content" model is reciprocation at scale.
- Open with a larger legitimate request; if declined, retreat to the true ask (and let the concession do the work).
- Secure a small, voluntary, ideally written/public commitment aligned with the larger goal you're building toward.
- Make desired behavior effortful and self-chosen so it's internalized, not merely complied with.
- **Defense:** if a "gift" or an initial deal is really a compliance device, redefine it as a sales tactic — you owe nothing for a trick — and re-ask whether you'd make the same commitment fresh, knowing the original reason is gone.

## Notable examples
- Rejection-then-retreat: the Boy Scout $5→$1 sequence; Cialdini's own repeated purchases.
- Foot-in-the-door: tiny sticker → giant lawn sign.
- Lowball: the car priced attractively to win the decision, then quietly raised.
