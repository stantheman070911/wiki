# Triggers - Top of Mind, Tip of Tongue

## One-line summary
People talk about and choose whatever is top of mind, so linking a product or idea to frequent cues in the everyday environment keeps it being mentioned long after the initial excitement fades.

## Context
The second STEPPS driver (see [[The STEPPS Framework - Six Drivers of Word of Mouth]]) — and its most counterintuitive. Interesting content wins *immediate* word of mouth but not *ongoing* word of mouth; triggers drive the ongoing kind. That's why Cheerios out-talks Disney World: people see cereal (and are cued to mention it) every morning, while a theme-park trip is exciting but rarely re-triggered. Accessible thoughts also drive *action*, not just talk: NASA's Mars mission lifted Mars-bar sales; voting inside a school raised votes for school funding; French vs. German music in a store shifted which wine people bought.

## Framework / model
A **trigger** is an environmental cue that makes a related idea accessible; a product's set of triggers is its **habitat**.

- **Consider the context.** Design or phrase the message so the target's everyday environment will cue it. A dining-hall *tray* slogan ("Each and every dining-hall tray needs five fruits and veggies a day") lifted actual veg consumption 25%, even though students *hated* it — while a clever, un-triggered slogan changed nothing. Budweiser's "Wassup?" (a common greeting) beat GEICO's cavemen (rarely seen).
- **Grow the habitat.** Forge a *new* link to a prevalent cue by repeated pairing. Kit Kat + coffee turned a $300M brand into $500M; "Boston Market for dinner" (not just lunch) lifted buzz 20%. Even a rival can be a trigger — the "poison parasite" antismoking ad made every Marlboro cowboy cue "emphysema."

## Key insights
- **Frequency × strength of link.** Pick a *frequent* cue (coffee, not hot chocolate; "weekends," not "holidays" for Michelob). But over-linked cues dilute: "red" triggers nothing specific because it's tied to everything, whereas "peanut butter" strongly cues "jelly." Prefer a fresh, distinctive, high-frequency link.
- **Trigger near the point of action.** A cue at the wrong moment wastes itself — the bath-mat ad triggers in the shower (where you can't buy one); "Man Drinks Fat" triggers exactly when you reach for a soda. Reusable grocery bags need a *leaving-the-house* trigger (the shopping list), not a *parking-lot* one.
- **Why negative buzz can help.** For unknown products, even a bad review raised sales ~45% — the mention itself acts as a trigger that the thing exists.

## Application
- Audit your audience's day: which sights, sounds, times, or habits already occur near where you want them to talk or buy?
- Attach your idea to the most frequent of those cues, and pair it repeatedly until the link sticks.
- Prefer a distinctive, uncrowded cue over a generic one; place the trigger where the desired action can actually happen.
- If you need durable (not just launch-day) word of mouth, favor a frequently-triggered hook over a merely interesting one.
