# High-Arousal Emotion - When We Care, We Share

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Frameworks and Mental Models Index|Frameworks and Mental Models]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Persuasion-and-Influence/Persuasion and Influence Map|Persuasion and Influence]]

## One-line summary
Sharing is driven by physiological *arousal*, not by whether an emotion is positive or negative — so evoke high-arousal feelings (awe, excitement, amusement, anger, anxiety) and avoid low-arousal ones (sadness, contentment) that make people power down.

## Context
The third STEPPS driver (see [[The STEPPS Framework - Six Drivers of Word of Mouth]]). Analyzing ~7,000 *New York Times* articles for what made the Most E-Mailed list, Berger found the surprise: it isn't positivity. *Awe* boosted sharing (+30%), but so did *anger* and *anxiety*; *sadness* reduced it (−16%). The unifying variable is **arousal** — a state of activation and readiness for action. High-arousal emotions "kindle the fire" and drive people to *do* something (including share); low-arousal emotions deactivate. This is why science articles go viral disproportionately: they evoke awe.

## Framework / model
**The arousal map** — classify the emotion you're evoking on two axes; share-worthiness follows the *arousal* row, not the valence column:

| | High arousal → **shares** | Low arousal → **suppresses** |
|---|---|---|
| **Positive** | Awe, Excitement, Amusement (humor) | Contentment |
| **Negative** | Anger, Anxiety, (Disgust) | Sadness |

- Humor spreads not because "funny = viral" but because *amusement is high-arousal*.
- Contentment feels good yet rarely gets shared — it relaxes rather than activates.
- **Arousal transfers across sources.** Participants made to jog in place (physical arousal, no emotion) were 2× more likely to share an unrelated article — so *any* activation, even situational, boosts transmission.

## Key insights
- **Focus on feelings, not features.** Information alone rarely changes behavior (teens know smoking is bad). Even "unemotional" products have a core feeling — use the Heaths' **Three Whys** (ask "why does this matter?" three times) to drill from function to emotion. Google turned search into the tear-jerking "Parisian Love"; make global warming about dying polar bears, not statistics.
- **"Make them mad, not sad."** Negative emotion is usable *if* it's high-arousal. Disgust ("Man Drinks Fat"), anxiety (BMW's *The Hire* films, +12% sales), and anger all drive sharing; sadness doesn't.
- **Arousal is a double-edged sword.** High-arousal anger snowballs fastest online (Motrin's "babywearing" backlash; "United Breaks Guitars" — 3M views, ~$180M stock drop). Monitor for arousing negativity ("pissed off," "angry") and defuse it early, before it spreads.
- **Catch people already aroused.** Ads placed in exciting/anxious moments (game shows, crime dramas, the gym) get more discussion because ambient arousal spills over.

## Application
- Identify the *high-arousal* emotion your idea can genuinely evoke, and lead with it instead of facts.
- On the positive side, inspire (awe) or amuse; on the negative side, provoke anger/anxiety/disgust — never settle for merely sad.
- Add arousal to lift sharing: making a story angrier or an ad funnier both increase transmission.
- Place your message where the audience is already activated, and watch high-arousal *negative* chatter about you so you can address it fast.
