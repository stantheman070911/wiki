# Start With Agreement - Keep the Other Mind Open

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Frameworks and Mental Models Index|Frameworks and Mental Models]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Persuasion-and-Influence/Persuasion and Influence Map|Persuasion and Influence]]

## One-line summary
When changing a mind, begin with genuine common ground and demonstrate understanding before presenting disagreement; receptivity is a prerequisite for evidence to work.

## Context
Opening with “you are wrong” turns a shared problem into a contest between people. Once identity and status feel threatened, even strong evidence is discounted. The first job of persuasion is therefore not rebuttal but keeping the other person cognitively and emotionally engaged.

## Key insights
- **Healthy conflict pits both people against the problem.** Unhealthy conflict makes each person the other’s problem.
- **Agreement increases receptivity.** Research described by Bartlett found that agreement increased confidence in a partner and cognitive openness, while disagreement reduced the weight given to the partner’s view.
- **Feeling heard comes before being persuadable.** Listening for the motive, evidence, or value beneath a position creates the credible basis for “I understand.”
- **Common ground is not capitulation.** You can validate a concern or goal without endorsing the conclusion drawn from it.
- **The opening determines whether the rest is processed.** A logically superior argument has little value if its first sentence triggers defense and closes attention.
- **“That's right” is evidence of accurate understanding.** It signals that the other person recognizes their own worldview in your summary; “You're right” is often only a polite way to end the exchange.
- **A useful summary combines content and emotion.** Paraphrasing captures the facts and logic, while labels name the feelings and constraints that give those facts meaning.
- **Safe refusal can precede genuine agreement.** Giving the other person room to say “No” protects autonomy and makes later commitment more credible.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
Use **Hear–Bridge–Reframe–Test**:

1. **Hear:** let the other person complete the case; use mirrors and labels to uncover facts, emotions, and constraints.
2. **Bridge:** identify a belief, motive, constraint, or desired outcome you genuinely share.
3. **Reframe:** summarize their position through both paraphrase and labels until the response is effectively “That's right.”
4. **Test:** introduce the difference as another route toward the shared objective, then ask what evidence or next experiment could resolve it.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Ban “I disagree,” “you’re wrong,” and rebuttal-by-interruption from the opening of difficult conversations.
- Start with a truthful bridge: “We both want…,” “I agree that…,” or “I can see why that evidence leads you there.”
- Separate the person’s observation from their inference; agree with accurate observations before challenging the inference.
- Ask the other person to correct your summary before presenting your own view.
- Prefer a “No”-oriented opening when commitment pressure is high: “Would it be a bad idea to look at another route?”
- Treat “You're right” or vague assent as incomplete; ask a calibrated implementation question to test whether agreement has become ownership.
- If the exchange becomes repetitive, pause and restate the shared objective; repeated arguments usually mean the real concern has not been heard.

## Notable examples
Bartlett uses polarized political arguments to illustrate the failure mode: presenting more hostile evidence can reduce belief rather than strengthen it. The people most able to change our minds are often those with whom we already share substantial trust and agreement.

In a kidnapping negotiation described by Voss, the negotiator repeatedly summarized the captor's grievances and objectives until the captor replied “That's right.” The phrase did not end the conversation; it marked the point at which the captor felt accurately understood and became open to movement.
