# Public - Observability and Behavioral Residue

## One-line summary
People imitate what they can see, so making a product, choice, or behavior more observable — and leaving visible traces after use — drives it to catch on: built to show is built to grow.

## Context
The fourth STEPPS driver (see [[The STEPPS Framework - Six Drivers of Word of Mouth]]). "Monkey see, monkey do" has a hidden condition: you can only copy what's visible. People rely on **social proof** — using others' behavior to resolve uncertainty — which is why we pick the busy restaurant, tip more when the jar is seeded, and why MBA students' varied ambitions converge on banking/consulting. Social proof can even override life-and-death judgment: 97% of offered kidneys are refused, and because each patient sees only that "many before me passed," ~1 in 10 refuses a kidney they should have taken. But **behavior is public and thoughts are private** — so private preferences (like students quietly disliking binge drinking) get overridden by visible behavior. The design job is to make the private observable.

## Framework / model
Steve Jobs flipped the Apple laptop logo — sacrificing the owner's orientation so the logo faced *everyone else* right-side-up when open — because observability drives imitation. Three tools:

### 1. Make the private public
Create public signals for private choices, actions, and opinions. Movember turned an invisible cause (men's health) into visible moustaches — walking billboards that raised >$174M. "I Voted" stickers surface a private act. Correcting false norms works too: Arizona cut heavy drinking ~30% by publishing the *true* norm (most students drink little), making private agreement visible.

### 2. Design products that advertise themselves
Every use should transmit passive social proof. Hotmail's "Get your free e-mail" signature drove 8.5M signups in a year; Apple's *white* earbuds made iPod adoption visible against everyone's black cords; "Sent from my iPhone," big logos, Louboutin's red soles, and distinctive shapes/sounds all self-advertise — advertising without an ad budget.

### 3. Create behavioral residue
Physical traces that persist *after* use. Livestrong's **yellow** bands (a rarely-worn, highly visible color) sold 85M+; Lululemon makes reusable bags people won't throw away; gym bags and mugs beat makeup cases as swag because their use is public. Choose residue that's seen often and from far away.

## Key insights
- **Observability amplifies real effects.** Neighbors' car purchases influence yours more in sunny, drive-everywhere Los Angeles/Miami than in rainy, subway-riding Seattle/New York — because you can *see* what they drive.
- **To prevent a behavior, reverse the logic — make the public private.** Anti-drug ads *increased* teen drug use by signaling "lots of kids do this"; "many visitors have stolen wood" nearly *doubled* theft. Highlight the desired norm instead ("please leave the wood, to preserve the forest"), and don't broadcast the wrongdoing minority.

## Application
- Ask: "Can others see when someone uses this or supports this?" If not, invent a visible signal, symbol, or residue.
- Bake self-advertising into the product (signatures, distinctive color/shape/sound, prominent marks) so each use recruits the next user.
- Give away residue that stays visible long after purchase, and in colors/forms that stand out.
- When discouraging a behavior, never cite how many people already do it — publicize the healthy majority instead.
