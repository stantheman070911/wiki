# Storytelling for Business - The Four Pillars

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Frameworks and Mental Models Index|Frameworks and Mental Models]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Persuasion-and-Influence/Persuasion and Influence Map|Persuasion and Influence]]

## One-line summary
People buy on emotion, not logic, and stories are how you create emotion — so a business pitch or piece of content should get attention, make them feel, teach them something, and leave them wanting more, all in service of a clear message you decided on first.

## Context
Ben Watkins is a TV showrunner (Amazon's *Cross*) who came up as an actor and screenwriter. His thesis: storytelling is a learnable skill and the real reason many succeed (Taylor Swift, Steve Jobs, Obama — three industries, same skill). "The only way to get money is to tell the right story"; on Shark Tank, 99% of the time what's really bought is the story. A journalist reselling $129 of eBay junk with professionally-written stories attached made ~$8,000 — the story elevates value because it taps emotion.

## Principles of messaging (decide before you tell)
Before any story, fix four things as your north star, then make every choice against them: **what are you saying, why are you saying it, who is it for, and what are you promising the audience.** Without this anchor you ramble.

## The four pillars of a good story
1. **Get their attention.** Three levers: **surprise** (the brain relaxes on patterns and perks up when one breaks); **emotion** (if you cause the feeling, they focus on you); and **call them by their name** — the most powerful word is someone's name; make the content feel like it's speaking directly to them ("Just Do It"; Apple's "here's to the crazy ones"). Do at least one in the first ~10 seconds.
2. **Make them feel something — the three C's.** Get **content, character, and circumstance** all eliciting emotion at once (Titanic: two characters you care about + a love confession + a sinking ship). Don't state a fact — make them feel like the one using the product. "I don't want you to see my resume; I want you to see me."
3. **Teach them something.** Take a world they know and show a part they didn't — value, not empty calories. (The caveman telling the bear story is really teaching how to survive a bear.) In an oversaturated feed, teaching is what makes it stick.
4. **Leave them wanting more.** Not cliffhangers — **open questions**. Establish a question the audience *needs* answered, and make everything before it promise the answer will be worth it (the curiosity gap, as wide as possible).

## Pitching with story
- **Infect them with the purpose.** Don't just tell the story — get them emotionally attached to you as the teller and to the project's *why*. The test: the first thing they do leaving the room is call their boss/spouse/coworker to talk about your pitch.
- **Let them own it.** When they offer an idea, riff on it (you needn't even use it) — that gives them ownership, and now they'll fight for the project.
- **Read the room; find the overlap.** Assume they're in a bad mood, bored, running out of time, on their sixth pitch — and make your delivery flip that (be the best part of their day). Research who they are and surface a genuine overlap at the right moment to speed the connection.
- **Mastery is what you leave out.** A good storyteller paints the picture; a master knows what to *omit* (Twain: "I'd have written a shorter letter if I had more time"). Sort details into two buckets: those that keep momentum, and those you *withhold* so they lean in and ask. When they've bought emotionally, move on — don't over-explain.

## Application
- Write your message north star (what/why/who/promise) before drafting anything.
- Open with surprise, emotion, or the reader's "name" in the first 10 seconds; layer content + character + circumstance to make them feel; teach one non-obvious thing; end on an open question.
- In a pitch, sell the *purpose* and let them co-own it; read their state and adapt; cut every detail that doesn't build momentum or bait a question.
