# Bend Their Reality - Loss, Fairness, Deadlines, and Anchors

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Frameworks and Mental Models Index|Frameworks and Mental Models]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Persuasion-and-Influence/Persuasion and Influence Map|Persuasion and Influence]]

## One-line summary
Negotiated value depends on emotional reference points, so shape the frame around loss, fairness, time, and anchors instead of treating price as an objective fact.

## Context
People do not evaluate an offer in isolation. They compare it with an anchor, interpret it through a personal idea of fairness, and become more impulsive as deadlines approach. A negotiator who understands these predictable distortions can change how an unchanged offer feels without relying on blunt pressure.

## Key insights
- **No deal is better than a bad deal.** Splitting the difference can combine two incompatible positions into the worst practical result rather than a fair one.
- **Deadlines create imagined consequences.** They often make a party rush and concede even when the date is arbitrary or flexible.
- **Fairness is emotional leverage.** “We just want what is fair” can destabilize a counterpart; the proper response is to ask where the process became unfair rather than concede reflexively.
- **Loss aversion is stronger than equivalent gain.** A proposal becomes more compelling when the counterpart sees what inaction puts at risk.
- **Anchors define the comparison.** Low expectations, relevant ranges, exact numbers, noncash terms, and unexpected gifts can all change perceived value.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
Use **Frame–Anchor–Expand–Protect**:

1. **Frame:** surface emotional drivers and the concrete loss created by inaction.
2. **Anchor:** use an accusation audit, a defensible reference range, or let a better-informed counterpart name the first monetary number.
3. **Expand:** move beyond price to terms that are inexpensive to one side and valuable to the other.
4. **Protect:** resist artificial deadlines, fairness attacks, and compromise that violates the actual objective.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Before discussing numbers, identify what the counterpart is really buying: security, recognition, time, certainty, or a desired future state.
- If told an offer is “fair,” mirror the word and ask for the evidence behind that claim.
- When you lack market information, let the other party anchor first and prepare emotionally for an extreme opening.
- If you must indicate price, cite a credible bolstering range whose low end is your real target.
- Use precise numbers to signal calculation and brainstorm nonmonetary terms before moving the price.
- Reveal a deadline when doing so encourages mutual movement; hiding it can create an avoidable impasse.

## Notable examples
In Haiti, a family faced a $150,000 ransom demand. By learning that the kidnappers wanted weekend party money, stalling toward their Friday deadline, using calibrated questions, and finishing with the precise figure of $4,751 plus a low-value gift, the negotiators safely closed far below the original anchor.
