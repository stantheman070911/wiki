# Calibrated Questions - How and What Create the Illusion of Control

## One-line summary
Ask carefully designed “How” and “What” questions that make the counterpart solve your problem while experiencing autonomy and control.

## Context
Direct assertions create a target for resistance, especially when both sides feel powerless. Calibrated questions remove the showdown, invite the other party's intelligence, and constrain the discussion toward a useful destination without sounding like a command.

## Key insights
- **Negotiation is coaxing, not overcoming.** A solution is more durable when the counterpart articulates it as their own.
- **Open-ended is not enough.** A calibrated question has a specific direction and is designed around the problem the counterpart must examine.
- **“How” asks for help.** It can communicate “No” without attacking pride and creates forced empathy for your constraints.
- **“What” makes the problem discussable.** It gathers more than a fact and often converts an accusation into neutral cause-finding.
- **“Why” usually creates defense.** Use it only when you intentionally want the counterpart to defend a position that favors your goal.
- **Self-control is part of the tool.** An accusatory tone converts a calibrated question back into a disguised attack.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
Build questions with **Direction–Calibration–Delivery–Follow-through**:

1. **Direction:** define what the counterpart must think about for the conversation to advance.
2. **Calibration:** begin with “What” or “How” and remove answerable-by-yes verbs such as can, is, are, do, and does.
3. **Delivery:** use a respectful, genuinely curious tone and allow time for thought.
4. **Follow-through:** mirror, label, or summarize the answer; do not rush to fill the information gap with your own proposal.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Convert “You cannot leave” into “What do you hope to achieve by leaving?”
- Convert “Your demand is impossible” into “How am I supposed to do that?”
- Use “What is the biggest challenge you face?” early to uncover the other side's actual problem.
- Ask “How does this affect the rest of your team?” to discover hidden decision makers and deal killers.
- When verbally attacked, pause and ask a calibrated question instead of counterattacking.
- Prepare three to five questions before the conversation, but remain flexible as new information changes the target.

## Notable examples
A consultant whose client kept assigning work without paying summarized the situation and asked, “How am I supposed to do that?” The client apologized, disclosed an internal accounting problem, provided a new contact, and paid within forty-eight hours.
