# Social Currency - We Share What Makes Us Look Good

## One-line summary
People share things that improve how others see them, so to get talked about you must mint social currency — make the sharer look remarkable, high-achieving, or like an insider.

## Context
The first STEPPS driver (see [[The STEPPS Framework - Six Drivers of Word of Mouth]]). Just as clothes and cars signal identity, *what we talk about* signals it too — so we preferentially share things that make us look sharp, funny, or in-the-know, and suppress what makes us look bad. Self-disclosure is intrinsically rewarding: it lights up the same brain circuits as food and money, and people will forgo cash to do it. The design job is to attach that impression-management payoff to your product.

## Framework / model
Three levers for minting social currency:

### 1. Find the inner remarkability
Remarkable = worthy of remark: novel, surprising, extreme, or pattern-breaking. Sharing remarkable things makes the teller seem remarkable, so remarkable brands get ~2× the word of mouth.
- **Break an expected pattern** (JetBlue's frills in a budget category; a $100 cheesesteak; black toilet paper at a party).
- **Use mystery or controversy** (*The Blair Witch Project*: was it real?).
- Remarkability can be *imposed on anything* — a blender became remarkable via *Will It Blend?*.

### 2. Leverage game mechanics
Rules, levels, and feedback that let people mark achievement — and, crucially, compare against others. People will choose to earn *less in absolute terms to rank higher relative to others* (the Harvard salary study). Status is relational, so people broadcast it — and broadcast your brand along with it (frequent-flier tiers, Foursquare badges, a Delta Platinum member bragging on Facebook).
- **Quantify performance** where no metric exists ("gamify" it).
- **Make status legible and visible** — give a tangible symbol people can display; use intuitive tiers (Olympic gold/silver beats confusing "sapphire vs. platinum" cards).

### 3. Make people feel like insiders
Scarcity (limited supply/time) and exclusivity (access gated by criteria or *knowledge*) make things feel more desirable and make the people who get in feel special — so they tell others. Rue La La (invite-only flash sales) outsold the identical, open SmartBargains; Please Don't Tell (secret bar) never advertises; the McRib's manufactured scarcity revived a failed product unchanged.

## Key insights
- **Even a "boring" or leftover product qualifies** — the McRib (largely a shaped pork patty) became sought-after purely through scarcity.
- **Relax the velvet rope over time.** Use scarcity/exclusivity to build demand, then widen access; soften refusals to "no, but…" so gatekeeping doesn't read as snooty.
- **Social incentives beat monetary ones.** Paying people to refer *crowds out* the intrinsic motivation to share; the buzz then scales with the payment, not with genuine enthusiasm. Fantasy football, Foursquare, and airline tiers all run on status alone.

## Application
- Ask: "What about this makes the person who shares it look good?" If nothing, engineer remarkability or a status symbol.
- Add discrete, visible achievement markers people can show off — and make sure your brand rides along when they do.
- Grant early insiders scarce or invitation-only access; give them something to reveal (Please Don't Tell hands you its card *as you leave* — "just in case you want to share the secret").
- Don't default to cash rewards for referrals; harness the desire to look good instead.
