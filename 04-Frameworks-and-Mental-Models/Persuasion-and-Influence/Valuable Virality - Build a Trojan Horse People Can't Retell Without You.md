# Valuable Virality - Build a Trojan Horse People Can't Retell Without You

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Frameworks and Mental Models Index|Frameworks and Mental Models]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Persuasion-and-Influence/Persuasion and Influence Map|Persuasion and Influence]]

## One-line summary
People transmit information wrapped in stories, so build a narrative people want to share — but make your product so integral to it that the story can't be told without mentioning you. Virality is worthless unless it's *valuable*.

## Context
The sixth STEPPS driver (see [[The STEPPS Framework - Six Drivers of Word of Mouth]]). People think in narratives, not facts; a story is a **Trojan Horse** whose entertaining shell carries a hidden payload — a lesson, or information about your brand. Stories are also more persuasive than claims: they work as *proof by analogy* ("it happened to someone like me"), and listeners are too caught up in the plot to counter-argue. This entry focuses on the pitfall the metaphor hides — most viral content fails its sponsor because the brand isn't woven in.

## Framework / model
**Valuable virality** = shareability × the brand being *integral* to the narrative. Two failure modes vs. the win:

| Outcome | Example | What happened |
|---|---|---|
| **Viral but worthless** | "Fool in the pool" (GoldenPalace.com Olympic streaker); Evian "Roller Babies" (50M views) | People shared the *stunt*, not the sponsor — the brand was detachable, so buzz didn't transfer (Evian sales fell ~25%). |
| **Valuable virality** | Panda cheese ("Never say no to Panda"); Blendtec *Will It Blend?*; Dove "Evolution"; Subway/Jared | You literally can't retell it without the brand — the product is load-bearing in the plot. |

The test: *if you removed the brand, would the story still make sense and still be worth telling?* If yes, your Trojan Horse is empty. The Panda ad would be equally funny with any product — but the brand is named in the punchline, so it survives every retelling.

## Key insights
- **Retelling sharpens toward critical details.** Allport & Postman's Telephone studies: ~70% of details vanish in the first few retellings, and what survives is what's *central* to the point. So your brand must be a central detail, not an "extraneous" one, or it gets stripped out.
- **Stories smuggle information as idle chatter.** A Lands' End customer-service story carries five useful facts about the brand without sounding like an ad; the Jared story teaches that Subway is healthy/tasty even when the topic isn't weight loss. This is the "psychological cover" that lets people talk about you without feeling like a walking billboard.
- **Latch onto what people already want to discuss.** Dove "Evolution" rode an existing, emotional, semi-taboo conversation (unrealistic beauty norms) and embedded the brand in it — >16M views, double-digit sales growth.

## Application
- Draft the narrative first, then ask: is the brand *removable*? If yes, rewrite until the product is essential to the plot or the punchline.
- Make the desired takeaway a *critical* detail (the one that survives retelling), not a logo tacked on at the end.
- Wrap useful or emotional information inside a concrete story so it travels under the guise of conversation.
- Anchor to a topic people already want to talk about, and hide your message inside it.
