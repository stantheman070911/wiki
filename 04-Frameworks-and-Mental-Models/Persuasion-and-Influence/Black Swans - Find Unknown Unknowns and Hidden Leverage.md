# Black Swans - Find Unknown Unknowns and Hidden Leverage

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Frameworks and Mental Models Index|Frameworks and Mental Models]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Persuasion-and-Influence/Persuasion and Influence Map|Persuasion and Influence]]

## One-line summary
Treat inconsistencies and apparently irrational behavior as clues to unknown information that can transform both the negotiation and its leverage.

## Context
Preparation is built from known facts and anticipated uncertainties, but decisive information often sits outside both parties' initial models. A Black Swan is an unknown unknown whose discovery changes what is possible, valuable, or urgent.

## Key insights
- **Prior experience guides but can also blind.** A pattern remains an assumption, not a law; every case must be allowed to contradict the template.
- **Counterparts often do not recognize what matters.** The game-changing fact may seem innocuous even to the person revealing it.
- **“Crazy” is a diagnostic signal.** Irrational-looking behavior commonly reflects bad information, a hidden constraint, or an interest you have not discovered.
- **Leverage is perceived and fluid.** It belongs to the side that can provide a desired gain, impose or withhold a feared loss, or invoke the counterpart's own norms.
- **Worldview supplies normative leverage.** Values, identity, status, professional rules, and “religion” determine what the counterpart experiences as legitimate.
- **Unguarded moments reveal more.** Beginnings, endings, interruptions, meals, and face-to-face incongruence expose information that formal scripts conceal.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
Search with **Anomaly–Hypothesis–Leverage–Test**:

1. **Anomaly:** mark every fact, pause, contradiction, missing demand, or decision that does not fit the current story.
2. **Hypothesis:** consider whether the party is ill-informed, constrained, serving another interest, or protecting a value or identity.
3. **Leverage:** classify what the fact may create—positive, negative, or normative leverage.
4. **Test:** use face time, calibrated questions, labels, third-party information, and a second listener to validate the hypothesis.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Enter every negotiation expecting at least three potentially decisive unknowns.
- Ask why the counterpart is communicating this fact now, not merely what the statement says.
- Review recordings or notes with a backup listener whose only job is to notice subtext and anomalies.
- When something makes no sense, label a tentative interpretation so the counterpart can correct it.
- Seek direct interaction for high-stakes ambiguity; email removes tone, body language, and productive unguarded moments.
- Use negative leverage sparingly and indirectly. Label the consequence rather than threaten autonomy.
- Prepare a one-sheet with a high-end goal, situation summary, accusation audit, calibrated questions, and valuable noncash terms.

## Notable examples
A real-estate broker described a fully occupied building as a cash cow, yet the owner wanted to sell. Instead of accepting the contradiction, a student labeled the apparent doubt about the market. The broker corrected him and revealed that the owner urgently needed cash to cover troubled properties elsewhere, transforming the buyer's leverage.
