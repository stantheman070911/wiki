# People Like Us Do Things Like This - Culture and Status as Behavior

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Frameworks and Mental Models Index|Frameworks and Mental Models]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Persuasion-and-Influence/Persuasion and Influence Map|Persuasion and Influence]]

## One-line summary
Behavior spreads when an action becomes a credible signal of what people like us do and where a person belongs or stands within that group.

## Context
People rarely choose from isolated facts. They interpret choices through culture: the norms of a reference group, the identities they seek to affirm, and the relative status a choice may confer or threaten.

## Key insights
- **Culture supplies the default.** “People like us do things like this” compresses belonging, precedent, and expected behavior into a practical decision rule.
- **The relevant “us” is specific.** A message spreads through the reference group whose approval matters, not through an abstract general public.
- **Status is relative and contextual.** It is a perceived position within a group, has inertia, and is learned through local signals; it is not identical to wealth.
- **People pursue different status modes.** Some seek affiliation, closeness, and belonging; others seek dominion, rank, and control. The same offer can signal opposite things to each.
- **Symbols make culture visible.** Language, design, rituals, uniforms, badges, and public behavior let people recognize insiders and infer what a choice means.
- **Normalization is horizontal.** Peers often make a behavior safe before authorities or mass messaging can make it persuasive.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
Map a behavioral culture through four elements:

1. **Us:** Whose membership or respect does the audience care about?
2. **Norm:** What do members already believe people like them do in this situation?
3. **Status movement:** Does the action promise affiliation, dominion, safety, respect, or protection from isolation?
4. **Signal:** What observable story, symbol, ritual, or behavior makes adoption legible to peers?

The intended change becomes easier when the signal truthfully connects the new behavior to an identity the group already values.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Ask customers which peers they compare themselves with and whose judgment would make a choice feel safe or risky.
- Identify whether the audience primarily seeks affiliation or dominion in this context; do not substitute one status story for the other.
- Design an honest, visible artifact of participation—a shared phrase, ritual, badge, container, interface behavior, or customer story.
- Seed change with credible members of a local group whose behavior is observable and imitable.
- Make insider language useful but avoid manufacturing contempt for outsiders; durable belonging does not require dehumanization.
- Track whether customers repeat the intended identity story without prompting. If they do not, the signal is not carrying the culture.

## Notable examples
Water Health International used brightly colored clean-water containers that made adoption visible in a village. Early adopters talked about the benefit, children saw germs projected at school, and respected neighbors carried the containers. Clean water gradually shifted from a novel purchase to what responsible community members did.
