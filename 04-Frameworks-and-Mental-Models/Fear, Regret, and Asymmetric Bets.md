# Fear, Regret, and Asymmetric Bets

## One-line summary
Fear only exists in the vague; specify it, play out plan B, and make your fear of regret bigger than your fear of rejection — then take maximum shots while your downside is near zero.

## Context
Hormozi took six months to quit his consulting job. This entry captures how he dissolves fear and why the 20s are the moment for the most aggressive bets.

## Key insights
- **Fear lives in the abstract, not the specific.** Break a fear into its next two or three concrete steps and the "huge amorphous fear" collapses; in the developed world the real downside is usually just the opinions of people you don't care about.
- **Play out plan B.** Hormozi's worst case was couch-surfing, then reapplying to his old job or gaining a story for business school — "that doesn't actually sound that bad."
- **Fear of regret must exceed fear of rejection.** "Losers fear rejection; winners fear regret." At 85 you won't wish for fewer crazy stories — you'll wish you'd taken more shots.
- **Potency = short gap between decision and action.** You can measure how powerful someone is by the distance between deciding and doing. Move deadlines from end-of-week → end-of-day → end-of-hour → now.
- **Asymmetric bets in your 20s.** With little to lose, every attempt is a scratch-off ticket paid for in time: worst case you get a "no" and learn, best case you win. Take as many shots as possible because the downside is ~zero.
- **"Greatness rejects all first-time applicants."** Failure is a prerequisite; the second attempt takes twice the emotional effort but half the actual effort (you move from uninformed optimism to informed realism).

## Tactics / how to apply
- When afraid, write the fear out step by step and pre-mortem plan B until the vague dread becomes a manageable list.
- Name the specific person whose judgment is stopping you; usually it's one or two voices, and usually not worth surrendering your life to.
- Compress decision timelines deliberately; when no new information will change the call, decide now and correct later (deciding, not doing, is what bogs people down).

## Notable examples
- Hormozi nearly didn't sell Gym Launch for $46M because one colleague might think the number unimpressive — realizing he was handing that person control over his life broke the paralysis.
- Six months of nightly "I'm going to quit" before actually quitting — the cost of a large decision-to-action gap.

## AMA refinements (Ask Me Anything)
A later Q&A added three nuances to the same framework:
- **Regret is usually a fiction.** "We regret when we imagine the upside we didn't get without also considering the downside we didn't suffer to get it." The girl or the business that "got away" is remembered as pure upside minus the trade-off you'd actually have paid — so most regret evaporates once you price the full cost.
- **The eternal questions are personal, not solvable.** How much risk is right, how much to invest vs. consume today, how long to push before pivoting — there's no universal answer because they're 100% personal. Use regret minimization (85-year-old you) and the worst-case test: if you can live with the worst case, take the swing; if not, don't.
- **Other people's money doesn't reduce the real risk.** When tempted to re-attempt a failed venture "with OPM this time," note that the true risk isn't your capital — it's losing the good thing you already have to being split and unfocused. Go all in and accept the trade, or don't do it.
