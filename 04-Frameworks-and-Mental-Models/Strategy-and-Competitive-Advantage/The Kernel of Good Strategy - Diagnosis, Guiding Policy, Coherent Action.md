# The Kernel of Good Strategy - Diagnosis, Guiding Policy, Coherent Action

## One-line summary
A strategy only has substance if it contains a diagnosis of the real challenge, a guiding policy for tackling it, and a set of coherent actions that carry the policy out — strip out any of the three and what's left is a goal, a slogan, or a wish, not a strategy.

## Context
Rumelt's central claim is that most things labeled "strategy" — vision statements, growth targets, lists of initiatives — aren't strategy at all. The kernel is his diagnostic minimum: the bare-bones logical structure every real strategy shares, regardless of industry, scale, or whether the "organization" is a corporation, a school district, or a single grocery store.

## Key insights
- **A strategy is not a goal, a vision, or ambition.** Strategy is a coherent response to a high-stakes challenge — the bridge between broad goals ("be the best") and specific, actionable objectives.
- **Diagnosis comes first, and it's a judgment, not a fact.** A diagnosis replaces the overwhelming complexity of a real situation with a simpler story that names what's critical and, by implication, what to ignore. Different diagnoses of the same facts (e.g. Starbucks' 2008 slowdown as "market saturation" vs. "eroding differentiation") point toward entirely different guiding policies — and none can be proven correct in advance.
- **The guiding policy is a direction, not a destination.** Like a highway guardrail, it channels action without specifying every move. "Provide customer solutions" (Gerstner's IBM) rules out a thousand alternatives without dictating exactly what IBM would build.
- **Coherent action is the part most "strategies" skip.** A strategy that stops at the guiding policy is unfinished — it must translate into a specific, coordinated configuration of resource commitments and policies that reinforce rather than cancel each other out.
- **Coordination itself is a source of advantage**, separate from any specific resource or capability. Most organizations don't coordinate their actions around a single design; they pursue multiple, often conflicting, objectives simultaneously. A leader who imposes real coherence on action is already ahead of most competitors.
- **Centralized coordination is costly and should be used sparingly** — only when the gains from aligning multiple parts around one purpose outweigh the losses from de-specializing people who would otherwise focus narrowly on their own function.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
**The Kernel = Diagnosis + Guiding Policy + Coherent Action.**
1. **Diagnosis** — name and simplify the challenge; identify which aspects of a messy situation are actually critical.
2. **Guiding policy** — choose an overall approach for dealing with the obstacles named in the diagnosis, one that draws on or creates some source of leverage.
3. **Coherent action** — design the specific, coordinated resource commitments and policies that carry out the guiding policy; the "punch" of the strategy, not an implementation afterthought.

Test any strategy document by asking, for each element: is this a diagnosis, a policy, or an action — or is it just a goal restated with more syllables?

## Tactics / how to apply
- Before recommending or evaluating any course of action, force an explicit answer to "What's going on here?" — write the diagnosis down as a sentence, separate from the recommendation.
- Distinguish "goal" (an overall value or desire — freedom, growth, being the best) from "objective" (a specific, actionable target the diagnosis and policy justify). Reject strategy documents that only contain goals.
- Check coherence directly: list the current major actions and policies side by side and ask whether they reinforce each other or quietly work against one another (e.g. buying premium brands while also chasing platform-sharing economies of scale, as Ford did with Jaguar and Volvo).
- Reserve centralized, imposed coordination for cases where decentralized decision-making structurally can't capture the gain — e.g. where costs and benefits of a choice fall on different units, or benefits only exist if multiple parties move together.

## Notable examples
- **Steve Jobs's 1997 Apple turnaround**: diagnosis = an unfocused product line bleeding cash with no coherent logic; guiding policy = shrink to a defensible core; coherent action = cut desktop models from fifteen to one, cut five of six national retailers, cut manufacturing to one core process — each cut reinforcing the others.
- **Lou Gerstner at IBM (1993)**: the dominant diagnosis was "IBM is too integrated for a fragmenting industry, so break it up." Gerstner's competing diagnosis — IBM's problem was failing to use its unique cross-domain integration, not having it — led to the opposite guiding policy (integrated customer solutions) and saved the company.
- **George Kennan's 1946 "long telegram"**: diagnosed the USSR as an ideologically expansionist but realist power, yielding the guiding policy of containment — a single diagnosis that shaped decades of downstream U.S. actions (NATO, the Berlin Airlift, Korea).
