# Strategy as Design - Coordinating Actions Under Constraint

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Frameworks and Mental Models Index|Frameworks and Mental Models]]

## One-line summary
Under real constraints, strategy behaves less like a decision (pick from a list of options) and more like engineering design (find the configuration where every part is tuned to every other part) — and the tighter the constraint or the fiercer the competition, the more that tight coordination matters, while abundant resources let you get away with sloppier integration.

## Context
Extends [[The Kernel of Good Strategy - Diagnosis, Guiding Policy, Coherent Action]]'s "coherent action" element with a specific model of *how* coherence gets engineered, drawn from Rumelt's own early career as a JPL systems engineer designing the Voyager spacecraft under a hard weight budget — plus his account of why strong resource positions (patents, brands) let firms get away without doing this work, and what happens when they eventually have to.

## Key insights
- **Design is finding the configuration, not choosing from a menu.** Hannibal's envelopment at Cannae (216 BC) wasn't selected from a list of battle plans — it was a novel, purpose-built coordination of cavalry, feigned retreat, and heavy-infantry flanks orchestrated in time and space against a specific, anticipated Roman response pattern.
- **Resources and tight integration are partial substitutes.** With abundant resources (a 3,000-lb weight budget at JPL vs. 1,200 lbs), subsystems can be bolted together with minimal coordination. Constrained resources force every subsystem to be reshaped around every other — cutting power-unit weight forces a smaller radio, which forces a more focused antenna, which forces better pointing sensors, which forces more attitude-control fuel. The tighter the constraint, the more the whole must be engineered as one integrated system.
- **A powerful strategic resource (a patent, a dominant brand) buys strategic simplicity** — Xerox's plain-paper-copier patent meant the "strategy" really was just "build factories and sell machines," no clever coordination required. But resource-driven simplicity breeds laxity: Xerox let Canon build superior paper-handling technology and enter personal copiers, printers, and fax machines because tight, ongoing design work wasn't needed to keep profits flowing — until the patent-driven moat eroded.
- **This creates a predictable arc: success → resource accumulation → laxity → decline → vulnerability to a design-driven upstart.** Look at the tightly-integrated newcomer (young Microsoft, young Wal-Mart, young Nvidia), not the resource-rich incumbent, to learn what real design-type strategy looks like — mature incumbents increasingly coast on accumulated resources rather than ongoing coordination.
- **Order imposed on chaos decays without active maintenance.** Even a brilliantly designed system (Sloan's 1921 GM brand-price-tier architecture) requires continuous defense against internal pressure to blur its boundaries — division heads pushing into adjacent price points erode the design from within unless leadership actively resists it (see [[Inertia and Entropy - Three Kinds of Organizational Stuckness]]).

## Framework / model (if applicable)
**Design-type strategy = adroit configuration of resources and actions that yields advantage in a challenging situation.**
- Given a fixed resource bundle, greater competitive challenge → greater need for tight, clever integration of the parts.
- Given a fixed level of challenge, higher-quality or more abundant resources → lower need for tight integration.
Use this trade-off explicitly: when resources are scarce and competition is fierce, invest in coordination, not just capability; when a strong resource position exists, be alert that it's substituting for design work that will eventually need doing anyway.

## Tactics / how to apply
- When facing a hard constraint (budget, headcount, time), map the interactions between decisions explicitly rather than optimizing each piece independently — ask what changing one parameter forces to change elsewhere, the way cutting spacecraft power weight cascaded into antenna, sensor, and fuel decisions.
- If your business currently runs on a strong resource-based moat (patent, brand, network effect), deliberately audit whether the underlying coordination and design skill is being maintained — or whether the moat is quietly substituting for strategic work that will be needed the moment the moat narrows.
- To find models of real design-type strategy, study the young upstart entering a market, not the mature incumbent's current playbook — the incumbent's current simplicity is usually a symptom of resource-driven laxity, not strategic insight.
- When defending a coordinated design (a coherent brand/price architecture, a focused product line), expect continuous internal pressure to blur its edges for local unit gain — active, ongoing leadership defense is part of the design, not a one-time setup task.

## Notable examples
- **Voyager spacecraft design (JPL, 1960s)**: at a 1,200-lb weight budget, virtually every subsystem decision (power, antenna, sensors, fuel) had to be reconsidered jointly; at 3,000 lbs, subsystems could be bolted together with minimal coordination — the same mission, radically different design intensity, purely as a function of the constraint.
- **Paccar's heavy-truck strategy**: quality positioning (Kenworth/Peterbilt), owner-driver-focused dealer networks, build-to-order manufacturing, and premium pricing form one integrated design that has produced a 16% average ROE (vs. 12% for competitors) over twenty years without a single unprofitable year since 1939 — a Frankenstein's-monster mashup of any one piece with generic competitors elsewhere wouldn't work.
- **Xerox and Canon**: Xerox's plain-paper patent bought decades of strategic simplicity; Canon built around Xerox's patent with a genuinely coordinated design (reliable desktop copiers instead of centralized high-volume machines), eventually taking the market Xerox's resource-driven laxity had left undefended.
