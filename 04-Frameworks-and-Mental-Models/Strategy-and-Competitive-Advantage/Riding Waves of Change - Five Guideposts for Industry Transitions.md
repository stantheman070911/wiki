# Riding Waves of Change - Five Guideposts for Industry Transitions

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Frameworks and Mental Models Index|Frameworks and Mental Models]]

## One-line summary
Industry transitions ("waves of change") upend competitive high ground largely independent of any one company's will, and while there's no formula for predicting them precisely, five recurring guideposts — rising fixed costs, deregulation, predictable forecasting biases, incumbent response patterns, and attractor states — help a leader see ten percent more clearly than rivals, which is often enough to win.

## Context
Extends [[Strategic Leverage - Anticipation, Pivot Points, Concentration]]'s anticipation concept to industry-level dynamics rather than individual rival behavior — Cisco Systems' rise (riding four simultaneous waves: microprocessors, corporate networking, IP standardization, and public Internet adoption) is Rumelt's central case for how a small player exploits exogenous change that incumbents can't easily follow.

## Key insights
- **Most eras aren't actually more turbulent than the past — but predictable biases distort how people read change.** Comparing 1875–1925 (electricity, telephone, radio, automobile, modern branding) to any modern fifty-year window shows the modern era isn't uniquely disruptive; the skill is in reading the specific wave correctly, not assuming constant acceleration.
- **Guidepost 1 — Rising fixed costs** force industry consolidation: as R&D costs escalate (color film vs. black-and-white, jet engines vs. piston engines), only the largest players can cover them, and weaker firms exit or get acquired.
- **Guidepost 2 — Deregulation** exposes hidden subsidies. Regulated pricing almost always subsidizes some buyers at others' expense (rural airline routes subsidized by long-haul fares); newly deregulated firms chase what *used to be* the profitable segment long after the subsidy vanishes, because regulated firms never actually knew their own true costs.
- **Guidepost 3 — Predictable forecasting biases** recur: analysts rarely predict a trend will peak and decline (durable-goods demand always eventually saturates once early adopters are covered); "convergence" stories default to a "battle of the titans" narrative that's often wrong (AT&T and IBM both "converged" into the same pit, dug by the microprocessor, instead of fighting each other); and standard advice defaults to "copy the current biggest winner," which fails when that winner's advantage was itself an artifact of soon-to-vanish conditions (airlines told to copy Delta's hub-and-spoke model right as the subsidies underlying it disappeared).
- **Guidepost 4 — Incumbent response** is structurally resistant to change that threatens accumulated skills and positions — see [[Inertia and Entropy - Three Kinds of Organizational Stuckness]] for the mechanics of why.
- **Guidepost 5 — Attractor states** describe how an industry "should" evolve given technology and demand structure, independent of any single firm's preference — Cisco's "IP everywhere" vision described an attractor state (efficiency-driven, not share-driven), which is why it proved more durable than rivals' "intelligent network" visions built around proprietary protocols and captured value.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
When assessing an industry transition, check each guidepost in turn:
1. **Fixed costs** — is R&D or capital intensity escalating in a way that will force consolidation?
2. **Deregulation** — is a hidden subsidy about to be removed, and who has been unknowingly relying on it?
3. **Forecast bias** — is the standard prediction assuming continued growth past a natural saturation point, a "battle of titans," or "copy the current leader" — and does the underlying logic actually support that?
4. **Incumbent response** — which existing players have accumulated skills or positions that make them structurally slow to respond, and where does that create an opening?
5. **Attractor state** — independent of any single company's wishes, what configuration does the underlying technology and demand structure actually favor, and what accelerants (demonstration effects) or impediments (regulatory friction) speed or slow the move toward it?

## Tactics / how to apply
- Before accepting a "this era is uniquely fast-changing" framing, compare the claimed disruption against a genuinely disruptive fifty-year historical window (1875–1925) — most of the time the comparison deflates the urgency narrative and clarifies what's actually new.
- When a regulated or subsidized market is about to open up, map who has been benefiting from a hidden cross-subsidy and assume they'll defend the old fare/pricing structure longer than is rational — that lag is often an exploitable opening.
- Distrust "become like the current market leader" advice during a transition — check whether the leader's advantage depended on conditions (subsidy, technology assumption, distribution structure) that are themselves about to change.
- Define your own point of view on the industry's attractor state — the efficient configuration given technology and demand — rather than reactively matching competitors' moves; look for accelerants (in-your-face demonstration effects, like Napster made MP3 file-sharing suddenly obvious) that will speed the move there.

## Notable examples
- **Cisco Systems (1988–2000)**: rode four waves simultaneously — the shift to software-centric value (microprocessor era), rising corporate network demand, the free, vendor-neutral IP protocol displacing proprietary competitors (IBM's SNA, DEC's DECnet, Apple's AppleTalk), and the explosion of public Internet use — capturing two-thirds of the corporate networking market from incumbents who each clung to a proprietary protocol.
- **U.S. airline deregulation (1978 onward)**: United's CEO committed $3B to new long-haul fleet based on regulated-era assumptions about where "the bucks are"; Continental's own "planning model" turned out to be a cost-plus-markup formula built for negotiating with the Civil Aeronautics Board, not a real market forecast — long-haul fares fell 27% (1984–85) exactly as short-haul fares, no longer subsidized, rose 40%.
- **NEC's "C&C" (computers and communications) forecast (1977)**: a textbook "battle of titans" convergence prediction that pushed both AT&T and IBM toward a confrontation neither ultimately had — both were undercut simultaneously by the same underlying wave (the microprocessor) rather than by each other.
