# Build for the Height - Time Horizon Sets the Foundation

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Frameworks and Mental Models Index|Frameworks and Mental Models]]

## One-line summary
How tall you intend to build decides how deep you must dig — so the fastest way to a $10M business is *not* the fastest way to a $100M business, and simply extending your time horizon changes the foundational decisions in a way that becomes a durable competitive advantage.

## Context
Hormozi does a physical exercise with entrepreneurs: "build the tallest tower you can with these blocks." Give someone 5 seconds and they stack fast and shallow; give them 5 years and unlimited blocks and they build a completely different base. The height you're planning for silently rewrites every foundational choice — and most founders build a one-story foundation, then get angry when they can't put ten stories on top of it.

## The model
- **Time horizon determines foundation depth.** A hundred-story building needs a deeper dig and different materials than a one-story building. The same is true of a company: the decisions that get you to $1M fast are different from — often opposed to — the decisions that get you to $100M. "The fastest way to build a $10M business is not the fastest way to build a $100M business" (and $1M ≠ $10M, either).
- **The plateau-and-rebuild trap.** Founders want the *speed* of a one-story foundation and the *height* of a ten-story building. They skyrocket to the first plateau on a shallow base — and then the correct, painful move is two steps back: tear up the foundation and re-lay it before it can go higher.
- **Obsess over the factory, not the prototype.** Committing to build for the long term (Hormozi decided to run his holding company "forever") immediately changes the decision set — you start building the machine that builds the thing, i.e. the durable foundations, rather than the next quick win. (This is the same insight as [[01-Business-Strategy/Growth-and-Scaling/The Factory Is the Product - Manufacturing as the Moat|The Factory Is the Product]], viewed through time horizon.)
- **Long-horizon choices become the moat 7 years later.** Musk building Tesla's batteries and charging network from scratch, or Bezos deciding logistics — trucks and warehouses — would be Amazon's competitive advantage: these looked slow and expensive up front and produced moats no one can now unseat. The durable advantage was a *byproduct* of thinking over a longer time horizon.

## Focus and patience are the antihuman advantages
- **Focus and patience are enduring competitive advantages precisely because they're antihuman** — and anti-Instagram. They're hard to sustain because they're boring: "I've got no announcement this month because I'm doing boring stuff like hiring."
- **You can't witness focus or patience — only the win.** "People only see the choices you made, not the options you had," so focus looks like luck. Patience is invisible for the same reason: in a marathon, spectators show up for the start and the finish; no one watches the middle. Because almost no one can *observe* these traits, almost no one imitates them — which is exactly why they stay a competitive edge.

## Application
- Decide the height *first*. Name the real end-state (a $100M durable company vs. a $1M lifestyle business) before choosing tactics, because the two demand different foundations.
- If you've plateaued on a shallow base, accept the two-steps-back move: rebuild the foundation rather than forcing more height onto it.
- Extend the planning horizon deliberately — ask "what would I do if I were running this for 50 years?" — and let the foundational decisions (owning the hard parts, hiring, systems) follow.
- Treat focus and patience as strategy, not virtue: expect boring months, and don't trade the invisible middle for a visible announcement.
