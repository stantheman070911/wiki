# Bad Strategy - Fluff, Avoidance, and Goals Mistaken for Strategy

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Frameworks and Mental Models Index|Frameworks and Mental Models]]

## One-line summary
Bad strategy isn't the absence of a plan — it's an identifiable, recurring pattern (fluff, refusal to name the real problem, goals dressed up as strategy, and either scattershot or unreachable objectives) that leaders default to because it avoids the genuinely hard work of choosing.

## Context
Companion diagnostic to [[The Kernel of Good Strategy - Diagnosis, Guiding Policy, Coherent Action]]: where the kernel defines what good strategy must contain, this entry defines the recognizable failure modes to screen for — plus the three deeper reasons leaders keep producing them even when they know better.

## Key insights
- **Bad strategy has its own logic; it isn't just "no strategy."** It actively avoids analyzing obstacles, mistakes strategy work for goal-setting, and hides the avoidance behind confident language.
- **Fluff is jargon that restates the obvious and sounds like expertise.** "Our fundamental strategy is customer-centric intermediation" translates to "our bank's strategy is being a bank." Test any strategic statement by asking whether it survives being said in plain words.
- **Failure to face the problem** means never actually naming the obstacle. If the challenge isn't defined, the strategy can't be evaluated or improved — International Harvester's 1979 "Corporate Strategic Plan" detailed market-share targets across five divisions but never mentioned the company's crippling labor-relations dysfunction, the actual reason its margins were half its competitors'.
- **Mistaking goals for strategy** — a CEO's "20/20 plan" (20% growth, 20% margin) is not a strategy; it's a financial aspiration with no theory of leverage attached. A strategy answers "what has to happen for this to be true?" — a bare performance target doesn't.
- **Bad strategic objectives come in two forms**: a **"dog's dinner"** — a sprawling, unprioritized list of everything anyone wanted included (one city's plan had 47 "strategies" and 178 "action items," one of which was "create a strategic plan") — or **"blue sky"** — an objective that just restates the desired end state without any theory of how to get there (demanding "transformational leadership" from principals trapped in a system that won't let them change the color of the office paper).
- **Three deeper causes keep producing bad strategy**: (1) *unwillingness to choose* — when leaders can't or won't set aside some interests in favor of others, the group compromises on vague language that offends no one (see the DEC "Boxes/Chips/Solutions" Condorcet-paradox deadlock); (2) *template-style strategy* — the vision/mission/values/strategies fill-in-the-blanks format, descended from academic theories of charismatic leadership, that substitutes a formula for actual analysis; (3) *New Thought* — the 19th-century positive-thinking movement's residue in modern "shared vision" literature, the belief that holding a picture of success in mind is itself a strategic act.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
**Four hallmarks of bad strategy** — screen any strategy document for:
1. **Fluff** — jargon masking an absence of content.
2. **Failure to face the challenge** — no explicit diagnosis of the real obstacle.
3. **Mistaking goals for strategy** — financial or aspirational targets presented as if they were a plan.
4. **Bad strategic objectives** — either a "dog's dinner" (too many, unprioritized) or "blue sky" (restates the challenge instead of bridging to it).

## Tactics / how to apply
- Read any strategy statement aloud and substitute plain words for its jargon; if the substance disappears, it's fluff.
- Ask "what is the specific obstacle this addresses?" of every strategic document. If no one can answer without restating the goal, there's no diagnosis.
- Separate "goal" language (growth, being the best, profitability) from "objective" language (specific, resourced, sequenced targets) in any plan you're asked to evaluate or write.
- When a group can't converge on a strategy, suspect an unwillingness to choose rather than a lack of good options — look for whether every faction's preferred option is still technically "on the table."
- Distrust plans built from a vision/mission/values/strategies/initiatives template unless each section is backed by a specific diagnosis; the template's neutrality is a symptom, not a feature.

## Notable examples
- **Chad Logan's "20/20 plan"**: revenue growth of 20%/year and 20% margin, backed only by "the will to win" and a Jack Welch quote — no identified point of leverage anywhere in the industry or the firm.
- **Los Angeles Unified School District (2006)**: superintendent David Brewer's "high-priority schools" focus was a genuine diagnosis with real focus, but individual "strategies" beneath it (e.g. "build leadership teams that share common beliefs, values, and high expectations") were blue-sky restatements with no theory of how a bureaucratic, union-bound system would produce them.
- **DEC's 1992 "Boxes vs. Chips vs. Solutions" deadlock**: three executives each preferred a different strategic direction in a cyclical, un-resolvable preference pattern (Condorcet's paradox); forced to reach consensus, the group settled on "DEC is committed to providing high-quality products and services and being a leader in data processing" — pure fluff, and the company failed to pivot until it was too late.
