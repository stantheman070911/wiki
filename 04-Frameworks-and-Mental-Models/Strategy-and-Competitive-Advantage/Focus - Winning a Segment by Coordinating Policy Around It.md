# Focus - Winning a Segment by Coordinating Policy Around It

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Frameworks and Mental Models Index|Frameworks and Mental Models]]

## One-line summary
Focus means two things at once — coordinating your policies so they interact and reinforce each other, and aiming that coordinated power at a specific target — and a smaller player can out-earn a larger one by using focus to escape captive-buyer dynamics that trap the volume leaders.

## Context
Rumelt's extended Crown Cork & Seal case study shows that a company's real strategy is often invisible even to its own executives and outside analysts, and only becomes legible once you compare its policies against the industry norm and ask what target those deviations are coordinated around — a concrete method for reverse-engineering strategy, not just a positioning label.

## Key insights
- **A company's stated strategy and its actual strategy often diverge.** Crown Cork & Seal's own self-description ("hard-to-hold applications — aerosols and soft drinks") wasn't wrong, but it missed the real mechanism: a coordinated bundle of technical assistance, rapid response, smaller plants, and excess capacity, all pointed at *short production runs* — the underlying reason those short-run customers were profitable.
- **Focus identification works by contrast, not description.** List a company's policies that differ from the industry norm, then ask what common target those specific deviations serve — Crown's technical-assistance policy targeted smaller businesses (who lacked in-house canning expertise); its rapid-response policy targeted rush orders; both converged on "shorter runs" as the actual strategic focus.
- **Focus changes bargaining power structurally, not just through differentiation.** Major can makers accepted becoming captive single-customer producers (two suppliers per big brewer, direct commoditized competition) because of the fixed cost of changeover between long production runs. By deliberately choosing shorter runs, Crown flipped the relationship — one plant, several customers, no captivity — capturing a far larger share of the value it created even with higher unit costs.
- **Focus produces higher profit only when it restructures the buyer/seller power relationship**, not merely because a niche exists. Short runs meant more costly changeovers and higher prices for Crown, but the strategic payoff came from avoiding the captive-supplier trap that kept the big three can makers stuck at 4–5% ROA.
- **Losing focus while still calling it "focus" is a common failure mode.** Crown's own CFO, under new CEO William Avery's acquisition-driven growth push into PET plastics, dismissed concerns about losing focus by redefining the word to mean only "restriction to an industry" ($150–200B of a $300B market) — missing that the deeper meaning was the coordinated policy-and-target logic, which the growth strategy had abandoned. Crown's stock fell from $55 to $5 in the resulting years.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
**Reverse-engineering a company's real focus:**
1. List every policy that differs from the industry norm (not just the ones the company advertises).
2. For each distinctive policy, ask "what specific kind of customer, order, or situation does this serve?"
3. Look for the common target those individual answers converge on — that convergence point, not the marketing description, is the actual focus.
4. Check whether the focus changes the buyer/seller power structure (captive vs. non-captive), not just whether it serves a smaller market — that's what determines whether focus produces superior profit, not just superior fit.

## Tactics / how to apply
- When analyzing a competitor (or your own company), don't accept the stated strategy at face value — build the policy-deviation table and look for the convergence point before concluding what the real focus is.
- Evaluate any proposed "focus" strategy by asking whether it changes bargaining power (e.g. escapes a captive-supplier or commodity dynamic) — a niche that doesn't restructure power relationships is just a smaller market, not a strategic advantage.
- Watch for "focus" being redefined downward (to mean only "we operate in a bounded industry") as an early warning sign that a company is drifting from a real coordinated-policy focus toward unfocused growth-by-acquisition.
- When a company pursuing growth starts adding businesses that don't share the original coordinated-policy logic, expect the original margin advantage to erode even as revenue and "market leadership" claims grow.

## Notable examples
- **Crown Cork & Seal under John Connelly (1960s–1989)**: coordinated short-run focus (technical assistance, rapid response, smaller non-captive plants) produced a 19%/year average shareholder return over 35 years versus the industry's 4–5% ROA norm.
- **Crown under William Avery (1989–2001)**: acquisition-driven growth into PET plastics abandoned the coordinated short-run focus in favor of scale; stock fell from $55 (1996) to $5 (2001) as industry overcapacity and price competition — entirely predictable via standard Five Forces analysis — eroded margins.
- **The captive can-supplier structure**: major can makers (Continental, National, American) accepted 4–5% ROA as the price of long production runs for major beverage brands; the structural trap (two dedicated suppliers per brand, essentially indistinguishable products) is a textbook case of *why* a focus-driven escape from captivity is valuable, not just differentiating.
