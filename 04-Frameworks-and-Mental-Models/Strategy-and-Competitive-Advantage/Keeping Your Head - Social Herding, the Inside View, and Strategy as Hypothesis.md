# Keeping Your Head - Social Herding, the Inside View, and Strategy as Hypothesis

## One-line summary
Good strategy is an educated, testable hypothesis about what will work — refined through real-world feedback the way a scientist refines a theory — while bad strategy collapses into an unfalsifiable closed loop when leaders substitute the crowd's confidence (social herding) or the belief that "our situation is different" (the inside view) for independent judgment about the actual facts.

## Context
The book's closing argument: strategy work is ultimately a discipline of thought, not just a structural template. This entry pairs Rumelt's "strategy as scientific hypothesis" model with his account of why smart, experienced people (Wall Street analysts, Fed chairs, Treasury secretaries) repeatedly fail to apply it under social pressure — directly relevant to evaluating any strategy that leans on "the market/the crowd/every expert agrees."

## Key insights
- **A strategy is a hypothesis, not a guarantee — and asking for a "sure thing" strategy is like asking a scientist for a guaranteed-true theory.** Good strategy work happens at the edge between the known and unknown, tested first against existing knowledge and then against real-world results, exactly like the scientific method — this reframing itself unblocks engineers and executives who reject "strategy" as vague because they're implicitly demanding deductive certainty from an inherently inductive process.
- **Anomalies — facts that don't fit received wisdom — are where the strategic opportunity lives, not an annoyance to explain away.** Howard Schultz's 1983 Milan trip surfaced a genuine anomaly: Italians treated *expensive*, high-quality coffee as a mass-market daily ritual, while Americans treated coffee as a cheap commodity — a gap between two societies' beverage economics that became the strategic hypothesis behind Starbucks.
- **A hypothesis gets tested cheaply and iterated, not bet on all at once.** Schultz's original Il Giornale concept (an exact Milan replica — Italian-only menu, opera, no chairs, bow-tied baristas) was itself wrong on several points; the discipline was testing it, observing what customers actually did, and revising repeatedly (paper cups, chairs, nonfat milk) until the hypothesis matched reality — "scientific induction" applied to an actual business.
- **Social herding substitutes the crowd's apparent confidence for independent evidence.** "The market is saying this is one of the biggest profit opportunities of all time" was treated as a trump card against a completed Five Forces analysis showing transatlantic fiber-optic capacity was a near-perfect commodity headed for overcapacity — Global Crossing's stock hit $38B before the same structural facts, ignored by the herd, produced bankruptcy within three years.
- **The inside view is the belief that "this case is different"** — that historical patterns and other people's/nations'/eras' experience don't apply to *your* situation. Fed and Treasury leaders in the mid-2000s explicitly invoked "deep and liquid markets" and superior modern risk-management tools to wave off twenty-eight prior OECD real-estate-credit boom-bust cycles in the preceding fifty years — an un-decidable question *within* the closed loop of rising asset prices, resolvable only by looking outside it, at history.
- **A closed information loop (Gödel's incompleteness applied metaphorically) makes some questions undecidable from inside the system.** When corporate decisions, trader behavior, and stock prices all feed back into each other, "is this industry actually overcapacity?" becomes unanswerable using only the loop's own signals — the fix is deliberately importing outside, non-market evidence (structural industry analysis, economic history) rather than trusting the loop's self-referential confidence.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
**Strategy-as-hypothesis discipline:**
1. Form the hypothesis from an observed anomaly (a fact that doesn't fit the received story), not from ambition or a template.
2. Test it against existing knowledge and logic before committing large resources.
3. Run the smallest real-world test that's actually informative (one espresso bar, not a national rollout).
4. Treat the result as new data, not as vindication or refutation of your ego — revise the hypothesis and retest.

**Bias check before trusting a strategic conclusion:**
- *Social herding test* — is the confidence behind this claim actually independent evidence, or is it "everyone else believes it too"? If the latter, discount it and go find the underlying structural facts yourself.
- *Inside view test* — does this argument rest on "our situation/team/era is different" without a specific mechanism for why? If so, deliberately go find the outside-view base rate (other companies, other eras, other countries) before proceeding.

## Tactics / how to apply
- When a strategic recommendation cites market price, analyst consensus, or "everyone in the industry agrees" as its main support, demand the underlying structural analysis (e.g. a Five Forces breakdown) independent of that consensus before accepting it.
- Test new strategic hypotheses the way Schultz tested Il Giornale — smallest viable real-world experiment, explicit willingness to revise every specific element based on what actually happens, not just whether the top-line metric looks good.
- When someone argues a historical pattern (credit bubbles, real-estate cycles, industry overcapacity) doesn't apply "this time," ask them to name the specific mechanism that's different — a vague appeal to superior modern tools or unique conditions is the inside-view bias, not an argument.
- Watch for closed information loops in your own organization — decisions based on stock price, internal ratings, or customer satisfaction scores that in turn were generated by those same decisions — and deliberately import external, non-circular evidence before trusting the loop's self-confidence.

## Notable examples
- **Howard Schultz and Starbucks (1983–2001)**: an anomaly noticed on a single Milan trip, tested via one small espresso bar (Il Giornale), iteratively revised against real customer behavior over years, becoming a $2.6B business with 4,700 outlets by 2001 — a full cycle of hypothesis, test, anomaly, revision.
- **Global Crossing (1997–2001)**: a UCLA MBA class's Five Forces analysis in 1999 correctly identified every structural weakness (perfect commodity, no differentiation, unpriced overcapacity, sunk costs) that would sink the company — dismissed in real time by consultants and analysts because "that is not what the stock market is saying"; the company reached a $38B valuation before bankruptcy in December 2001, with STM-1 capacity prices falling from $8M to $325,000.
- **The 2008 financial crisis**: Bernanke's 2004 "Great Moderation" speech, Greenspan's 2005 confidence in derivative pricing tools, and Geithner's 2006 praise of risk-transfer innovation all invoked inside-view reasoning against a backdrop of twenty-eight prior OECD real-estate credit-boom-bust cycles in fifty years — outside-view evidence that was available and ignored, not undiscovered.
