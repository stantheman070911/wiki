# Bullseye - Test All Channels, Then Focus on One

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Frameworks and Mental Models Index|Frameworks and Mental Models]]

## One-line summary
Counter channel bias by considering every plausible route to customers, cheaply test the best few, then concentrate on the single channel that can move the current traction goal.

## Context
Teams usually choose acquisition channels from habit, visibility, or peer imitation. The best channel is often underused precisely because competitors and founders overlook it, so channel selection needs a structured search before focused execution.

## Key insights
- **Discovery and optimization are different stages.** Early tests ask whether a channel could work at all; detailed conversion optimization belongs after a core channel has been identified.
- **The search space must stay broad.** The book’s nineteen channels range from search, content, email, and sales to partnerships, offline events, engineering tools, existing platforms, publicity, and community.
- **Tests should answer strategic questions.** For each channel, estimate the cost to acquire a customer, the volume available, and whether the customers are the kind the business wants.
- **Parallel tests reduce delay.** Running a few small middle-ring tests at once is faster and less misleading than fully building one favored channel before comparing it with alternatives.
- **Concentration creates impact.** Once a channel demonstrates the capacity to move the needle, scattered side experiments become a tax; supporting channels should reinforce the core rather than compete with it.
- **A winner is temporary.** Click-through rates decay, audiences saturate, and companies enter new phases, so the Bullseye search must be repeated when the core channel stops producing adequate growth.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
The Bullseye has three rings:

1. **Outer Ring — What is possible?** Brainstorm at least one credible tactic for every channel without dismissing unfamiliar options.
2. **Middle Ring — What is probable?** Select roughly three promising channels and run inexpensive, time-bounded tests that measure acquisition cost, scale, and customer quality.
3. **Inner Ring — What is working now?** Choose the channel that can materially advance the current traction goal, focus resources on it, and optimize its funnel.
4. **Renewal — What changed?** Re-enter the outer ring when saturation, economics, product phase, or market conditions invalidate the current winner.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Build a one-page channel table with one test idea, success threshold, budget, duration, and owner for every candidate.
- Keep early tests below the cost and time needed to fully implement the channel; seek directional evidence, not polished execution.
- Define conversion before launch and trace it to a real customer action rather than clicks or impressions alone.
- Rank results on all three dimensions—economics, available volume, and customer quality—rather than choosing the cheapest lead source automatically.
- After selecting the inner-ring channel, pause unrelated channel work for one operating cycle and redirect it toward the winner.

## Notable examples
Mint first listed the blogs its likely customers read, built relationships with those publishers, and used targeted placements and badges to secure a concentrated launch audience. Evernote repeatedly entered new platforms early, accepting several failed bets because a successful one—such as early Android support—could deliver hundreds of thousands of users before the platform became crowded.
