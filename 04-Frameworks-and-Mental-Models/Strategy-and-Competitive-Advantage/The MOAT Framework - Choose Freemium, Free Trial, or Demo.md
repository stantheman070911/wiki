# The MOAT Framework - Choose Freemium, Free Trial, or Demo

## One-line summary
Choose a self-service or sales-led entry model by matching it to your market strategy, competitive maturity, buyer level, and time-to-value rather than copying another company's freemium or trial tactic.

## Context
Freemium, free trials, and demos are customer-acquisition systems with different economics, not interchangeable website options. The wrong choice can give away scarce demand, overwhelm support, or force a complex product into an experience users cannot navigate alone.

## Key insights
- **Market strategy sets the economic shape.** A dominant strategy wins broadly with a better, cheaper solution; a differentiated strategy serves an underserved niche better at a premium; a disruptive strategy offers an intentionally simpler, cheaper solution to overserved buyers.
- **Ocean conditions change the education burden.** Mature red-ocean buyers already understand the category, favoring product-led evaluation; a complex blue-ocean product may need sales to teach a new problem frame before self-service can work.
- **Audience determines the path to adoption.** Bottom-up users can try, adopt, and champion a product; top-down executives often buy a rollout they will not personally use and require procurement, training, and change management.
- **Time-to-value is the gating variable.** A product-led offer works only when the target user can reach meaningful value quickly with little or no human help.
- **Freemium requires volume and a reason to upgrade.** It fits large markets and simple adoption, but it fails when the free tier satisfies the whole job or support costs outrun conversion.
- **A demo remains rational for high-value complexity.** Small total markets, new categories, high annual contract value, and difficult implementation can justify a high-touch model.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
Score the four MOAT dimensions:

1. **Market strategy:** Dominant, differentiated, disruptive, or a deliberate hybrid?
2. **Ocean conditions:** Are you creating demand in a blue ocean or harvesting understood demand in a red ocean?
3. **Audience:** Does adoption begin with executives in a top-down sale or end users in a bottom-up motion?
4. **Time-to-value:** Can the intended user independently experience the promised outcome fast enough to sustain motivation?

The strongest freemium fit is a large, mature market with bottom-up adoption, simple self-service, and a clear usage-based upgrade path. Trials tolerate more product depth; demos tolerate more education and implementation complexity.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Estimate the reachable market and the ongoing cost of serving non-paying users before considering freemium.
- Test whether the actual buyer is also a capable user; if not, do not expect an executive to self-onboard merely because the product is free.
- Observe new users reaching the first valuable outcome. If success requires extensive guidance, fix the experience or keep a sales-assisted path.
- Use a hybrid to de-risk transition: launch a separate product-led arm, place time-limited trials inside a valuable free product, or fall back to a useful free tier after a trial ends.
- Revisit the choice as the market and product mature; a category that initially needs sales education can later become ideal for self-service.
- Match sales assistance to lifetime value so human touch does not turn a low-price model unprofitable.

## Notable examples
- Canva used a disruptive strategy: fewer capabilities than Photoshop, but a simpler and cheaper route to common design jobs for overserved users.
- Salesforce initially needed sales-led education to overcome objections to cloud CRM; once a category is understood, product-led competitors can exploit the mature demand.
- Slack's bottom-up adoption lets one user invite a team until the product becomes valuable enough for a manager to purchase.
- Vidyard tested a separate freemium product, GoVideo, to build capability and validate the model without immediately disrupting its established revenue engine.
