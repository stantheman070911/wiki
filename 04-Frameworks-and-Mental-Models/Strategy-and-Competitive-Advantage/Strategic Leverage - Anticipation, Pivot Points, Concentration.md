# Strategic Leverage - Anticipation, Pivot Points, Concentration

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Frameworks and Mental Models Index|Frameworks and Mental Models]]

## One-line summary
Strategic leverage comes from anticipating how others will behave, finding the pivot point where a small push produces an outsized effect, and concentrating resources there instead of spreading them evenly across every priority.

## Context
Part of Rumelt's "sources of power" toolkit for building the guiding-policy element of [[The Kernel of Good Strategy - Diagnosis, Guiding Policy, Coherent Action]] — the mechanism by which a guiding policy actually multiplies the effect of limited resources, rather than just describing a direction.

## Key insights
- **Leverage = anticipation + a pivot point + concentration.** Archimedes' lever only moves the earth if you apply force at the right spot; strategy work is finding that spot in a business or competitive situation.
- **Anticipation doesn't require prophecy** — it means reading the predictable "downstream" consequences of trends, routines, and incentives that are already in motion. Toyota's $1B hybrid bet rested on two ordinary anticipations: fuel-economy pressure would grow, and rivals would license rather than build their own competing system.
- **A pivot point is a natural or created imbalance** where a relatively small adjustment unleashes disproportionate force — an imbalance between a rival's position and its underlying capability, or between pretension and reality (Reagan's 1987 "tear down this wall" speech exploited the decades-old gap between Soviet claims of liberalization and the concrete facts of the Berlin Wall).
- **Concentration works because of threshold and salience effects.** Below a certain level of effort, most actions produce no visible result at all (advertising below a spend threshold does almost nothing); above it, results compound. This is why dominating a small segment usually beats spreading equal resources across a big one.
- **Threshold effects also explain why cognitively limited leaders and organizations benefit from having very few priorities** — attention that's split five ways produces less total effect than the same attention concentrated on one or two decisive issues.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
Before committing resources to any initiative, ask three questions in sequence:
1. **Anticipation** — what do we already know, with reasonable confidence, about how competitors, customers, or regulators will behave, based on their existing routines and incentives (not on wishful forecasting)?
2. **Pivot point** — where is the imbalance in this situation — the place where a modest, well-timed action produces effects that don't scale linearly?
3. **Concentration** — are we actually applying enough force at that one point to cross the threshold, or are we spreading the same resources thin across many objectives?

## Tactics / how to apply
- When evaluating a strategic bet, write out the specific anticipated behaviors of rivals or customers it depends on — if you can't name them, the "leverage" is probably wishful thinking, not anticipation.
- Look for imbalances between a competitor's stated position and its actual underlying capability or commitment; these are often exploitable pivot points that don't require you to out-resource the rival, just out-time them.
- Before allocating budget across multiple initiatives, ask whether any of them are below the threshold where they'll have zero visible effect — better to fully fund one initiative past its threshold than half-fund three that never clear theirs.
- Prefer dominating a narrow, well-chosen segment over spreading equal effort across a broad one; bargaining power and visible results both concentrate disproportionately at the top of a segment.

## Notable examples
- **7-Eleven Japan (Noritoshi Murata)**: anticipated that Japanese consumers were unusually bored-prone (200+ soft-drink brands, 70% annual product turnover per store); built a quick-response merchandising system as the pivot point, then concentrated relationships with second- and third-tier manufacturers to exploit it — driving twice the sales-per-square-foot of competitors in China through the same underlying capability.
- **Harold Williams and the Getty Trust (1983)**: rather than simply buying more art with the trust's mandated $65M/year, Williams concentrated the resource on transforming the *study* of art — a pivot point sized to the resources at hand, producing outsized visibility relative to a same-size competitor art museum.
- **Pierre Wack at Shell (1970s)**: anticipated the OPEC oil-price shock years ahead by reading income, population, and development-goal trends in Iran, Iraq, and Venezuela — an anticipation, not a forecast, that let Shell position ahead of the "flood" while rivals like George W. Bush's offshore drilling ventures got caught by the reversal.
