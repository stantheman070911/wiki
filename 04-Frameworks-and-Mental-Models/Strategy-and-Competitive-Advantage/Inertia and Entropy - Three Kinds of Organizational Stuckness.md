# Inertia and Entropy - Three Kinds of Organizational Stuckness

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Frameworks and Mental Models Index|Frameworks and Mental Models]]

## One-line summary
An organization's biggest strategic threat is often not competitors but its own decay — inertia (resistance to changing course) comes in three distinct forms with different fixes, while entropy (the steady loss of order and focus even absent any external change) means that without constant active maintenance, even a brilliantly designed strategy blurs and collapses on its own.

## Context
Names the internal-decay half of what [[Riding Waves of Change - Five Guideposts for Industry Transitions]]'s "incumbent response" guidepost gestures at — the specific mechanisms by which organizations fail to adapt, plus the separate, subtler force (entropy) that erodes even organizations facing no external shock at all.

## Key insights
- **Inertia of routine**: standard operating procedures that were rational under old conditions keep running after those conditions change, because routines filter perception itself, not just action. Continental Airlines' 1980 "Boeing Fleet Planner" wasn't a market forecast at all — it was a cost-plus-markup tool built for negotiating with a price regulator, still being used, unexamined, two years into deregulation, to predict fares that "had to rise."
- **Inertia of culture**: deeply held work norms resist change far more stubbornly than routines, and can't be fixed by hiring smarter people alone. AT&T's Bell Labs (1984) had world-class research talent but zero product-development competence, because decades as a regulated monopoly had built a culture where fundamental research, not shippable products, conferred status — a Bell Labs engineer quoted three years and three million dollars for a demo Rumelt then wrote himself in three weeks. Breaking cultural inertia requires simplification, fragmentation to expose hidden cross-subsidies, triage, and ultimately replacing the "alpha" figures whose behavior sets the group's norms.
- **Inertia by proxy**: a company appears unresponsive to a threat not because it's stuck, but because responding would cannibalize a still-profitable stream sustained by *its customers'* inertia. Regional Bell companies didn't offer early DSL in profitable T1-line markets — not out of incompetence, but because T1 customers were slow to switch, so ignoring cheap DSL competitors was, for a while, the more profitable choice. This kind of inertia can reverse suddenly once the incumbent decides adaptation beats defending the old stream — and the switch to real competition can be devastating (every CLEC that had built a business on the temporary gap failed once it closed).
- **Entropy is decay without any external shock at all** — the tendency of unmanaged structures to lose order, focus, and definition purely through the passage of time, requiring constant active maintenance to resist (much like a garden requires ongoing weeding, not just planting). It's distinct from inertia: entropy happens even when no one is resisting change, simply because no one is actively preserving structure either.
- **The hump chart makes entropy visible and measurable.** Ranking business units by a properly adjusted profit measure and charting cumulative contribution reveals cross-subsidy: a smooth rising curve means no hidden subsidies, but a hump that rises then sags means weak units are being propped up by strong ones, often invisibly, through years of accumulated measurement distortions (Denton's best single outlet generated more profit than its entire 28-outlet chain combined, once true capital and incentive costs were properly allocated).

## Framework / model (if applicable)
**Three types of inertia, three different fixes:**
| Type | Cause | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Routine | Obsolete standard procedures perceived as still-valid | Hire from firms with better methods, use consultants, or redesign procedures directly — perception barriers, not skill barriers |
| Culture | Deeply held work norms enforced by high-status group members | Simplify → fragment → triage → replace the "alpha" figures whose norms the group follows |
| By proxy | Profitable inertia in *customers*, not the firm itself | Recognize the temporary trade-off explicitly; expect sudden, possibly devastating reversal once the firm decides to compete for real |

**Entropy diagnostic**: build a hump chart (units ranked by true, cost-adjusted contribution, cumulative total plotted) — a sustained hump (rise then sag) over time, for separable units, is direct evidence of unmanaged cross-subsidy and decayed focus, independent of any competitive threat.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Before concluding an organization lacks the skill to change, check whether a routine, a culture, or a proxy-inertia dynamic is actually the blocker — each requires a structurally different intervention, and misdiagnosing which one wastes the fix.
- When a company seems irrationally slow to respond to an obviously cheaper competitor, check whether its own most valuable customers are the ones actually inert — the company may be rationally protecting a profit stream sustained by customer stickiness, not blind to the threat.
- To break cultural inertia, don't start with a challenge or incentive scheme — start with simplification (stripping complex cross-subsidizing administration) and fragmentation (breaking the political coalitions that hide inefficiency), then triage unit by unit.
- Build a hump chart for any portfolio of comparable units (stores, products, accounts, regions) using a properly cost-adjusted profit measure, not the standard accounting figure — a persistent hump is worth investigating even in the complete absence of any competitive pressure.

## Notable examples
- **Continental Airlines (1980–81)**: continued to use its regulated-era "Boeing Fleet Planner" cost-plus-markup tool as if it were a competitive market forecast two years into deregulation, predicting long-haul fares "had to rise" right before they fell 27% — a pure inertia-of-routine failure that contributed to a hostile takeover and the CEO's suicide.
- **AT&T / Bell Labs (1984–85)**: Nobel-laureate-producing research culture proved structurally incapable of shipping consumer products (cellular phones required outside Motorola equipment for the first market test; a videotex system needed a subcontractor because Bell Labs couldn't deliver working software) — a pure inertia-of-culture failure that persisted despite senior management's stated desire to compete in computing and communications.
- **Denton's garden-supply chain (1997)**: a properly built hump chart revealed that the top-ranked outlet's gain-to-operating ($1.05M) exceeded the entire 28-outlet chain's total ($0.32M) — pure entropy, with no external competitive shock, fixed within two years by closing the worst outlets and transferring best practices, raising total profit from $100K to over $5M.
