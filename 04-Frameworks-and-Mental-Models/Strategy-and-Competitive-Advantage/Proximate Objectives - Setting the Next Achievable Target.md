# Proximate Objectives - Setting the Next Achievable Target

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Frameworks and Mental Models Index|Frameworks and Mental Models]]

## One-line summary
The more uncertain and complex a situation is, the more a leader's job is to absorb that ambiguity and hand the organization a proximate objective — a target close enough at hand to actually be feasible — rather than a distant, ill-defined goal dressed up as ambition.

## Context
Counters the common belief that dynamic, uncertain situations call for looking further into the future and setting bolder, more distant goals. Rumelt argues the opposite: uncertainty degrades foresight, so it should shrink the objective's time horizon, not expand it. Complements [[The Kernel of Good Strategy - Diagnosis, Guiding Policy, Coherent Action]] by defining what a good guiding policy converts into.

## Key insights
- **A proximate objective is a target the organization can reasonably be expected to hit, even overwhelm** — feasible given current resources and competence, not a restatement of the ultimate ambition. Kennedy's 1961 moon-landing commitment felt audacious but was, given known rocket technology and von Braun's technical assessment, a carefully chosen *feasible* objective — the U.S. had a real, resourced path to it.
- **A leader's core job in an ambiguous situation is absorbing complexity on the organization's behalf**, handing down a simpler, solvable problem. Phyllis Buwalda's 1961 JPL "lunar surface specification" wasn't a claim to know the moon's surface (nobody did) — it was a deliberately chosen, plausible, and *actionable* engineering target that let Surveyor's design proceed instead of stalling in endless "what if" debate.
- **In highly dynamic situations, look-ahead should shrink, not grow.** The right posture resembles two chess masters improving position and creating options move by move, not committing to a ten-move plan — "taking a strong position and creating options" beats trying to see far ahead when the future is genuinely uncertain.
- **Proximate objectives cascade** — a feasible objective at the top of an organization generates its own sub-objectives at each level below it, each proximate to that unit's actual capability. What's proximate for one team or company is often wildly out of reach for another with fewer accumulated skills — captured in the "helicopter pilot" idea that higher-order coordination (landing on a pitching ship deck) is only possible once lower-order skills (basic flight control) have become fully automatic.
- **Bad objectives fail the feasibility test, not the ambition test.** "Win the War on Drugs" and "achieve energy independence" aren't proximate objectives because no one actually has a credible, resourced path from here to there — the vagueness of the "how" is the tell, not the size of the goal.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
When setting or evaluating an objective, ask:
1. **Feasibility** — given current resources, skills, and competence, is there a real, specifiable path to this, or only a hoped-for one?
2. **Absorption** — has the leader actually resolved the ambiguity in the situation into a concrete, workable spec, or just pushed the ambiguity down to the team?
3. **Layering** — does this objective sit on a rung the organization has actually reached (per its accumulated skills), or does it assume competences that haven't been mastered yet?

## Tactics / how to apply
- When a goal feels distant or vague, ask "what is the single feasible objective that, if accomplished, would make the biggest difference right now?" — and push the answer toward something concrete enough to assign and measure, not a restated ambition.
- Before setting an objective for a team, check whether the underlying skills it assumes are already "automatic" for that team (per the layering idea) — assigning distant coordination challenges to a team still mastering basics wastes the assignment.
- In genuinely uncertain situations, favor moves that create options and strengthen position over moves that commit far ahead — treat the situation like an early-game chess position, not a fixed multi-year roadmap.
- When leading through ambiguity, do the work of turning "we don't really know" into a specific, defensible working assumption the team can build against — even an imperfect spec beats paralysis.

## Notable examples
- **NASA's Surveyor program (1961–67)**: Buwalda's lunar-surface spec (hard, grainy, slopes under 15°, small scattered boulders) wasn't verified truth — it was a proximate objective that let Hughes Aircraft's engineers actually build something; five Surveyors landed successfully, and Apollo 12 later touched down 200 yards from Surveyor 3.
- **A regional business school's strategic plan (2005)**: forced to pick one feasible objective instead of a vague "be top-ranked in the region" ambition, the faculty converged on "get students into better jobs" — targeting ten specific employer firms — a proximate objective concrete enough to actually organize the school's committees around.
- **Nestlé's acquisition of Rowntree**: proximate objectives cascaded in time as well as hierarchy — early, modest wins in moving Rowntree's UK-centered brands into new markets built the base of competence and information that let later, more ambitious objectives become proximate in turn.
