# Increase the Surface Area of Luck

## One-line summary
You cannot schedule a lucky break, but you can increase its probability by creating more quality encounters among your ideas, work, people, and emerging opportunities.

## Context
Attributing success entirely to luck encourages passivity, while denying luck produces false certainty. The useful middle position separates the uncontrollable event from the controllable odds of encountering it.

## Key insights
- **Luck is a probability distribution, not a switch.** A specific breakthrough remains uncertain even when its odds improve.
- **Exposure creates opportunity collisions.** Publishing, pitching, testing products, studying trends, and entering relevant rooms let more people and ideas encounter the work.
- **Quality and quantity interact.** More random activity creates noise; more well-targeted repetitions produce useful feedback and a larger chance of an outlier.
- **A portfolio of attempts needs survivability.** Small, affordable bets preserve the ability to keep playing until an exceptional result appears.
- **The result is often power-law distributed.** Many attempts may fail or remain small while one relationship, product, channel, or timing event dominates the return.
- **Retrospective stories hide the denominator.** A visible win may sit behind hundreds of companies, artists, pitches, or experiments that never became famous.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
Think of luck surface area as four multipliers:

**Relevant people met × useful work exposed × experiments completed × time kept in the game.**

If any factor approaches zero, opportunity has fewer ways to find you. Improving the smallest factor is often more valuable than maximizing an already strong one.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Set weekly output targets for pitches, publications, experiments, and substantive conversations.
- Choose rooms and audiences where the desired opportunity is plausible rather than networking indiscriminately.
- Design downside-capped tests so a miss supplies data without ending the game.
- Follow trends closely enough to recognize timing, but demand a concrete action or test from every trend thesis.
- Maintain an opportunity log linking unexpected outcomes back to the activity that made them possible.
- After a win, study the full attempt distribution so luck does not get rewritten as inevitability.

## Notable examples
Priestley cites Richard Branson's repeated emphasis on luck while noting the large number of companies and artists behind the famous successes. The lucky outcomes were real, but so was the deliberate expansion of attempts.
