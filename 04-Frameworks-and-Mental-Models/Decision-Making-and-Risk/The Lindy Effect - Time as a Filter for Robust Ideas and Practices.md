# The Lindy Effect - Time as a Filter for Robust Ideas and Practices

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Frameworks and Mental Models Index|Frameworks and Mental Models]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Decision-Making-and-Risk/Decision Making and Risk Map|Decision Making and Risk]]

## One-line summary
For nonperishable things exposed to real selection, surviving longer is evidence of robustness and often increases expected remaining life.

## Context
The Lindy effect applies to ideas, books, tools, institutions, and practices that do not physically age like organisms. Time repeatedly exposes them to changing users and shocks, removing many fragile alternatives—but survival alone does not prove truth, morality, or present fit.

## Key insights
- **Time is an adversarial filter.** Repeated exposure tests failure modes that a short experiment, credential, or peer review may miss.
- **Nonperishable things can age in reverse.** A book or practice that has remained useful for centuries has crossed more environments than a recent equivalent and may remain relevant longer.
- **Selection must be real.** Lindy evidence is weak when subsidies, coercion, monopoly, archival luck, or institutional protection prevent failure.
- **Survival supports robustness, not universal superiority.** A practice can persist because it serves a narrow environment, coordinates a group, or avoids a hidden risk—not because every explanation attached to it is correct.
- **New and old require different evidence.** Novel methods need testing, reversibility, and small exposure; old methods deserve a presumption of hidden function until their mechanism is understood.
- **Future judgment is stronger than current applause.** Fashion, citation, and peer status are short-window signals; continued voluntary use is harder to manufacture.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
Apply the **Lindy evidence test**:

1. Is the thing nonperishable?
2. How long has it faced meaningful use and variation?
3. Could failure actually remove it?
4. Is survival voluntary or protected?
5. Has its environment changed enough to test robustness?
6. What present condition may invalidate the historical record?

## Tactics / how to apply
- Use old, proven components for load-bearing functions and reserve novelty for bounded, reversible experiments.
- Search classics and long-running practice before treating a behavioral claim as new.
- Compare methods by years of real use and failure exposure, not only by publication recency.
- Investigate why a persistent rule survived before removing it; retain the protective function even if the original story is obsolete.
- Do not use Lindy as an excuse against innovation: new tools become tested only through controlled exposure.

## Notable examples
Taleb contrasts fresh food with old laws and books: biological goods perish, while a repeatedly used nonperishable rule can gain evidence with age. Plumbing and other practiced crafts are filtered continuously by whether they work; a fashionable theory can circulate through peer approval without comparable contact with consequences.
