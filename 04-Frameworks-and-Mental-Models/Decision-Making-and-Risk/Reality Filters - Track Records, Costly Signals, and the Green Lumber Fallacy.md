# Reality Filters - Track Records, Costly Signals, and the Green Lumber Fallacy

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Frameworks and Mental Models Index|Frameworks and Mental Models]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Decision-Making-and-Risk/Decision Making and Risk Map|Decision Making and Risk]]

## One-line summary
When outcomes are measurable, durable performance under real constraints is stronger evidence than polish, credentials, vocabulary, or a persuasive causal story.

## Context
People easily mistake looking like an expert for surviving the work of one. Real selection can elevate practitioners who lack conventional signals, while fields insulated from consequences can reward those who reproduce the expected performance of expertise.

## Key insights
- **Success despite a negative stereotype is informative.** A practitioner who prospers while looking unlike the role had to overcome perception with stronger results.
- **Cosmetic fluency is gameable.** Jargon, credentials, confidence, plans, and presentation can satisfy evaluators without improving the underlying outcome.
- **Relevant knowledge is often tacit.** The green-lumber trader succeeds without knowing an apparently basic product fact because he understands the variables that actually move the trade.
- **Explanation and execution are different skills.** A clear narrative may be useful, but it can also replace evidence when the audience cannot observe performance.
- **Costly signals deserve more weight.** Capital at risk, a falsifiable forecast, repeat customers, retained responsibility, and long-term survival are harder to fake than claims.
- **Track records require adjustment.** Luck, hidden leverage, selection bias, and a favorable environment can produce impressive histories that have not yet met the relevant stress.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
Use an **evidence hierarchy**:

1. Durable outcomes across relevant conditions.
2. Repeated behavior with retained downside.
3. Auditable work samples and referenceable customers.
4. Specific, falsifiable explanations.
5. Credentials and peer reputation.
6. Confidence, vocabulary, appearance, and presentation.

Lower levels can help screen candidates but should not override contradictory evidence from higher levels.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Define the result before evaluating the person; otherwise charisma will define success after the fact.
- Ask for base rates, full histories, failures, and the amount of personal exposure behind a claim.
- Use paid trials or work samples that reproduce the real constraints of the role.
- Prefer references who observed outcomes over people impressed by the candidate's presentation.
- Test whether performance persists without hidden leverage, a monopoly, or one unusually favorable period.
- Reward simple solutions when they work; do not pay for complication merely because it looks more expert.

## Notable examples
Taleb's surgeon who looks like a butcher may be the stronger choice because a successful career despite the appearance implies a powerful reality filter. The green-lumber trader knew less descriptive detail than his bankrupt counterpart but understood the market variables that determined survival.
