# Gap Selling - Current State, Future State, and the Value of Change

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Frameworks and Mental Models Index|Frameworks and Mental Models]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Decision-Making-and-Risk/Decision Making and Risk Map|Decision Making and Risk]]

## One-line summary
A sale becomes compelling when the buyer can see a valuable, urgent gap between a precisely diagnosed current state and a concrete future state that justifies the cost and risk of change.

## Context
Product-led selling starts with what the vendor offers; gap selling starts with why the buyer should change. It treats a sale as diagnosis and change facilitation, making the magnitude of the buyer's problem and desired outcome—not the product's feature list—the basis of value.

## Key insights
- **No problem, no sale.** A stated need is only a hypothesis; the real sale begins with a problem and the consequences it creates.
- **Every sale is a change decision.** Buyers compare the pain, effort, cost, uncertainty, and political risk of changing against the consequences of remaining in the current state.
- **The gap is the value-bearing unit.** A small difference between present and desired outcomes creates little urgency; a large, measurable difference can justify substantial investment.
- **Current state creates the push; future state creates the pull.** Dwelling only on pain leaves the buyer stuck, while describing only an attractive future lacks a credible starting point.
- **Intrinsic motivation gives the outcome meaning.** A numerical goal matters more when the seller learns why that particular goal matters to the buyer or organization.
- **A negative or weak gap is a reason to disqualify.** If the transition risk exceeds the value of the future state, selling harder is not service.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
Use the change equation:

`Future state - Current state = Gap`

Then test whether the gap exceeds the full change cost: price, implementation work, learning, disruption, risk, politics, and emotional resistance. The sale is viable only when the buyer agrees the outcome is valuable, the problem is real, the change is worth making, and the seller can credibly help make it.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Quantify the current result and the target result in the buyer's own units: revenue, time, conversion, waste, risk, capacity, or another operational measure.
- Ask what happens if nothing changes and how the consequences compound over time.
- Ask, “What is driving this change?” after the buyer describes the desired outcome; keep going until the strategic or personal motivation is explicit.
- Include transition costs and stakeholder risks in the comparison instead of presenting the future state as frictionless.
- Before pitching, summarize the current state, future state, gap, and motivation and ask the buyer to validate each.
- Walk away or redirect the buyer when the gap is too small, the solution cannot create the desired outcome, or the change would introduce a larger risk.

## Notable examples
A routine tow is price-sensitive until the stranded driver must reach a once-in-a-lifetime White House dinner in forty minutes. The physical service is unchanged, but the desired outcome makes the gap—and therefore the value of immediate help—dramatically larger.
