# Skin in the Game - Aligning Upside, Downside, and Accountability

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Frameworks and Mental Models Index|Frameworks and Mental Models]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Decision-Making-and-Risk/Decision Making and Risk Map|Decision Making and Risk]]

## One-line summary
Advice and decisions become more ethical and reliable when the people who receive the upside also bear meaningful downside from being wrong.

## Context
Skin in the game is broader than financial ownership. It joins ethics, incentives, information quality, and learning: someone who can transfer harm to others can profit from hidden fragility, repeat errors, and sound convincing without ever being corrected by reality.

## Key insights
- **Symmetry is the core rule.** If you keep the reward, you should bear a meaningful share of the harm; if your decision exposes others, accountability must survive the decision date.
- **Downside reveals information.** An opinion backed by money, reputation, career, or personal exposure contains more evidence than costless commentary.
- **Consequences create learning.** People insulated from their errors can preserve a bad theory indefinitely; systems learn when poor decisions remove authority, capital, or future opportunity.
- **Hidden tail risk is the main abuse.** Agents can collect frequent small rewards while rare losses arrive later, after they have been paid or moved on.
- **Liability should match the causal horizon.** Compensation, warranties, clawbacks, and evaluation periods must last long enough for the relevant failure to appear.
- **Skin in the game is necessary, not sufficient.** Exposure cannot repair incompetence, fraud, or systemic risk by itself; oversight and limits remain necessary when one person's failure can ruin everyone.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
Run the **symmetry audit** on any consequential decision:

1. Who receives the upside?
2. Who bears ordinary losses?
3. Who bears rare or delayed losses?
4. Who controls the decision and its information?
5. Can the decision-maker leave before outcomes are known?
6. What consequence would make learning unavoidable without creating ruin?

Misalignment is greatest when control and reward sit together while delayed downside is dispersed to people with no voice.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Ask advisers what exposure they personally retain and how they are paid.
- Tie variable compensation to durable outcomes; defer part of it beyond the plausible failure window.
- Use warranties, clawbacks, co-investment, error budgets, and explicit decision ownership.
- Keep risk bounded: accountability should encourage care, not create a single failure that destroys the person or system.
- Record forecasts and decision assumptions so later evaluation cannot be rewritten.
- Prefer owner-operators and practitioners when the work requires judgment under uncertainty, while still verifying their record.

## Notable examples
Taleb contrasts hedge-fund owner-operators, who often invest substantial personal wealth alongside clients, with executives who keep years of compensation before a taxpayer-funded failure. Hammurabi's builder rule is an extreme historical attempt to prevent an expert from hiding structural risk that only appears after delivery.
