# Learn How to Make Decisions Effectively - Expected Value, Levels, and Algorithms

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Frameworks and Mental Models Index|Frameworks and Mental Models]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Decision-Making-and-Risk/Decision Making and Risk Map|Decision Making and Risk]]

## One-line summary
Good decision-making is a strict two-step process — learn (synthesize reality accurately, both in the moment and through time) then decide (run the expected-value math, unclouded by emotion) — and most of the value in getting good at it comes from recognizing recurring situation-types ("another one of those") and converting your criteria into reusable principles, or even algorithms, instead of re-deciding everything from scratch.

## Context
Closes out Dalio's Life Principles by turning the preceding chapters (reality, the 5-Step Process, open-mindedness, wiring) into an operational toolkit for the moment of choice itself.

## Key insights
- **The biggest threat to a good decision is harmful emotion, not lack of information.** Decision-making is learning, then deciding — never blur the two into one motion.
- **Synthesizing the situation "at hand":** carefully choose who you get information from; don't believe everything you hear; remember everything looks bigger up close (proximity distorts scale); "new" is systematically overvalued relative to "great"; and don't over-extrapolate from a single data point ("dot").
- **Synthesizing through time:** weigh both the rate of change and the absolute level — a student going from a 30 to a 50 is improving but still failing. Favor being roughly right ("by-and-large") over being precisely wrong; use the 80/20 Rule; be an "imperfectionist" who refuses to over-invest in marginal precision.
- **Navigate levels deliberately.** Reality exists at multiple zoom levels, like a map — track explicitly whether a conversation or decision is happening "above the line" or "below the line," and make sure decisions are made at the right level *and* stay consistent across levels.
- **Make decisions as expected-value calculations**: reward × probability of being right, minus penalty × probability of being wrong. Raising your probability of being right is valuable no matter your starting point — going from 51% to 85% right is roughly 17x more valuable than going from 49% to 51%. Knowing when *not* to bet matters as much as knowing what to bet on, and the best choices have more pros than cons, not zero cons.
- **Prioritize by weighing the value of more information against the cost of delay** — must-dos before like-to-dos, and accept upfront that you won't get to everything.
- **Simplify.** "Any damn fool can make it complex. It takes a genius to make it simple" — Dalio cites Picasso's career-long trajectory toward simplification as the model.
- **Use principles to cut the decision volume.** Recognizing a situation as "another one of those" — a recurring type with a knowable cause-effect pattern — and applying a tested principle instead of re-deciding from scratch reduces the number of decisions you actively need to make by roughly a factor of 100,000 over a lifetime. This only works if you study history broadly (not just your own lifetime) so you can actually recognize the type when it recurs.
- **Convert your principles into algorithms and let a computer decide alongside you.** A toy example: a thermostat rule ("if temp < 68°F and it's not between midnight and 5am, turn on the heat") models the same logic as a serious investment or management algorithm — write down your criteria as you decide, then formalize them.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
**Three types of computer-aided decision systems**, ranked by trustworthiness: (1) **expert systems** — Bridgewater's own approach, where humans specify the causal logic and the computer just executes it faster and without emotion; (2) **mimicking systems** — pattern-copying without understanding *why* the pattern works, which is risky because it breaks silently when underlying conditions change; (3) **data mining** — brute-force pattern-finding, dangerous in markets/economies specifically because widely-known patterns get arbitraged away and because correlation is routinely mistaken for causation. Deep Blue's 1997 chess win shows where pure pattern systems *do* work — bounded, unchanging rule systems — versus economies and markets, which are unbounded and constantly evolving, where they don't.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Before deciding, explicitly separate the "learning" phase from the "deciding" phase — write down what you've learned before you let yourself form a conclusion.
- Run genuinely close calls as an explicit expected-value calculation (reward × P(right) − penalty × P(wrong)) rather than a gut call, especially for consequential, reversible-cost decisions.
- When you notice a decision "feels familiar," name the pattern explicitly ("this is another one of those") and check whether you already have — or should write — a standing principle for it.
- When building any decision-support tool (a spreadsheet rule, a dashboard, an AI prompt), be honest with yourself about whether it's an expert system (you understand and specified the causal logic) or mimicking/data-mining (it just found a pattern) — trust the former far more than the latter, especially in fast-changing domains.

## Notable examples
- Dalio once called an unlisted house he liked, purely because "it never hurts to ask" — the owner not only agreed to sell but offered him a personal loan, illustrating the asymmetric upside of low-cost, high-optionality bets under expected-value thinking.
- The 1971 Nixon gold shock (a currency default Dalio didn't see coming) and his 1982 depression miscall were both, in hindsight, "another one of those" — recurring historical patterns (excess-debt-driven currency devaluations) he could have anticipated had he studied history more broadly rather than trusting only his own lived experience.
