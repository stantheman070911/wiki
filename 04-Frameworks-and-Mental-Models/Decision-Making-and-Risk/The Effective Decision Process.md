# The Effective Decision Process - Principles, Boundary Conditions, Action, and Feedback

## One-line summary
Make few consequential decisions by diagnosing the class of problem, defining nonnegotiable boundary conditions, choosing what is right before compromising, embedding execution, and testing assumptions against direct reality.

## Context
An executive decision is a judgment under uncertainty, not a calculation that discovers a provably correct answer. Speed and volume often signal symptom treatment; durable decisions resolve recurring situations at the level of policy and make most later cases routine applications.

## Key insights
- **Classify before solving.** A recurring symptom, a locally unique instance of a common class, a truly exceptional event, and the first appearance of a new recurring class require different treatment.
- **Default to searching for the generic problem.** Repeatedly treating a pattern as separate incidents produces endless adaptations. But forcing a genuine exception through an old rule is equally dangerous.
- **Boundary conditions define adequacy.** State what the decision must accomplish, its minimum acceptable result, and the constraints it must satisfy. Incompatible conditions mean the proposal is a hope for a miracle, not a decision.
- **Decide what is right before what is acceptable.** Compromise is unavoidable, but without a clear model of the full solution it is impossible to distinguish half a loaf—which still feeds—from half a baby—which satisfies nothing.
- **A decision is not made until it becomes work.** It needs a named owner, action, deadline, affected parties, informed parties, suitable capabilities, and incentives aligned with the new behavior.
- **Feedback requires direct exposure.** Reports and metrics are abstractions. The decision-maker must periodically observe the operating reality and test whether assumptions and results still hold.
- **Disagreement improves judgment.** Competing, reasoned hypotheses reveal hidden assumptions, supply fallback alternatives, and stimulate imagination; consensus without an articulated alternative is a warning.
- **Doing nothing is always an alternative.** Intervene when deterioration or a vanishing opportunity makes action necessary; otherwise compare the risk and benefit of action against inaction, then act fully or decline rather than hedge.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
**The effective decision sequence:**

1. **Classify:** Generic, generic-but-new-to-us, exceptional, or first sign of a new generic condition?
2. **Define:** What underlying problem explains all observed facts, including the awkward ones?
3. **Specify:** What objectives, minimum results, and constraints must the answer satisfy?
4. **Design the right answer:** Solve for the specifications before negotiating acceptability.
5. **Generate disagreement:** Require alternative interpretations, measurements, and courses of action; state what evidence would validate each.
6. **Choose whether to act:** Compare deterioration/opportunity, benefits, costs, and risks with the do-nothing case.
7. **Embed action:** Assign owner, work, deadline, communication, capability, measures, and incentives.
8. **Install feedback:** Write expected results and review dates, then inspect reality directly.
9. **Reopen when reality diverges:** Treat unexplained observations as evidence that the classification, assumptions, or rule may be obsolete.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Write the problem as a pattern rather than an event: “What recurring condition would make this incident predictable?”
- Before approving a proposal, list its boundary conditions and test them for internal conflict and dependence on everything going right.
- Ask every advocate to state what evidence would falsify their view and which measurement best represents the decision—not merely which data are available.
- Assign a dissenting participant to build the strongest credible alternative; postpone unanimous decisions long enough to surface what the group may be missing.
- Add an execution block to every decision record: accountable owner, next action, deadline, people affected, people informed, required capability, and changed incentive or metric.
- Schedule a review when evidence will be meaningful, then go to the work, customer, or operating site instead of relying only on the report produced by the implementation chain.

## Notable examples
- Alfred Sloan treated GM’s conflict between divisional autonomy and central direction as a constitutional design problem, producing decentralization rather than repeatedly mediating personalities.
- The Bay of Pigs plan depended simultaneously on overthrowing Castro and appearing free of U.S. intervention; without an island-wide uprising those boundary conditions were incompatible.
- Sloan reportedly postponed a meeting’s unanimous decision specifically to give the group time to develop disagreement and understand what the decision was really about.
- A discontinued product generated years of excess components because nobody identified the purchasing clerk as someone who needed to know the decision.
