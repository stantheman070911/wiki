# Push or Pivot - The Disproven-Thesis Test

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Frameworks and Mental Models Index|Frameworks and Mental Models]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Decision-Making-and-Risk/Decision Making and Risk Map|Decision Making and Risk]]

## One-line summary
Pivot only when a *foundational assumption* your business was built on has been proven false by real activity; if every original thesis still holds and things are merely slower or harder than you wanted, you push — and "failure" almost always means quit this *tactic*, not quit the business.

## Context
"When do I push and when do I pivot?" is, in Hormozi's words, one of a handful of eternally unanswerable entrepreneur questions — there's no universal answer because it's partly personal. But he offers the sharpest available decision rule, which cuts through the more common mistake: not stubbornly pursuing a dead idea, but *abandoning a live one too early*.

## The rule
- **Pivot when a founding assumption is disproven.** If you based the business on a fundamental thesis and your own business activity has proven that thesis untrue, changing course makes sense.
- **Push when the theses still hold.** If none of your original assumptions are wrong and it's simply "not happening as fast or as easily as I wanted," that is not a reason to pivot — that's the normal cost of building. Push.
- **The bigger risk is quitting the right path.** "There are mistakes of picking the wrong path, but there are more mistakes of abandoning the right path." Discomfort masquerades as evidence; slowness is not disproof.

## Local vs. global failure
- **Kill the tactic, not the mission.** Reframe failure as *local*, not *global*. If you pitch a hundred dog owners on a doggy skateboard and none want it — even after you build it — then quit the doggy-skateboard business. You should **not** quit *business*. The specific idea failed; the enterprise of being an entrepreneur did not.
- **This is what makes failure tolerable.** Figuring anything out *is* a long sequence of local failures — failed headlines, failed episodes, forgetting to turn the mic on. As long as none of them tip into the one global failure ("I'm no longer doing this"), you're fine.

## Guardrail: iterate on feedback, don't just persevere
Pushing is not blind stubbornness. The rule assumes you're in a live feedback loop — getting real market data, taking it, and improving. "As long as you do not quit, you win — you just have to not stop … as long as you get feedback and you take the feedback and you improve." Persistence without feedback is how you end up selling doggy skateboards forever; persistence *with* feedback is how the fog clears.

## Application
- When tempted to pivot, write down the 2–3 foundational assumptions the business rests on. Ask of each: *has my actual activity proven this false* — or is it just slower/harder than hoped? Only a genuine disproof licenses a pivot.
- Separate the failing *unit* from the enterprise: name exactly which tactic/offer/idea died, and confirm the mission is still intact before deciding to quit anything.
- Keep the feedback loop explicit: if you're going to push, define what data you're collecting and how you'll change in response, so "push" doesn't decay into "ignore reality."
