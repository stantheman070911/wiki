# The Contrarian Question and Zero to One - Vertical vs. Horizontal Progress

## One-line summary
The future-defining question isn't "what's a good business idea" but "what important truth do very few people agree with you on" — and the only progress that answers it is vertical (0→1, doing something new), not horizontal (1→n, copying what already works).

## Context
Thiel opens every job interview and this book with the same question because it forces a person past consensus knowledge into a genuinely owned, testable belief. It's the starting filter for deciding whether an idea is worth building a company around at all.

## Key insights
- **The contrarian question's form matters.** A good answer takes the shape "most people believe x, but the truth is the opposite of x" — not a true-but-agreed-upon fact ("our schools are broken"), and not a side taken in an existing debate ("there is no God"). It has to be both intellectually and psychologically hard, because saying it means holding an unpopular, unproven view.
- **Two kinds of progress.** Horizontal/extensive progress = copying things that work, going from 1 to n (globalization is the macro word for this). Vertical/intensive progress = doing new things, going from 0 to 1 (technology is the macro word for this, and it isn't limited to computers — any new, better way of doing something counts).
- **Globalization without new technology is unsustainable.** Copying today's resource-intensive lifestyle to billions more people without new technology is environmentally catastrophic, not prosperity-generating — spreading old ways to create wealth results in devastation, not riches.
- **Progress was never automatic.** After 10,000 years of fitful advance, the world had a burst of relentless technological progress from the 1760s (steam engine) to about 1970 — then it slowed to mostly computers and communications. The task is to both imagine and create new technology, not assume it arrives on its own.
- **Startups are the vehicle for vertical progress.** New technology tends to come from small, mission-bound groups, not big organizations (bureaucracy moves slowly, entrenched interests avoid risk) or lone geniuses (who can create a work of art but not an industry). A startup is "the largest group of people you can convince of a plan to build a different future."

## Framework / model (if applicable)
**Vertical vs. horizontal progress, applied to your own plan:**
1. Ask the contrarian question about your specific market: "what valuable company is nobody building?" Every correct answer is necessarily a secret — see [[Secrets - How to Find What Everyone Else Has Missed]].
2. Classify your plan: are you taking something that already works and making more of it (1→n), or doing something nobody has done (0→1)? Only the latter escapes competition — see [[Monopoly vs. Competition - Why All Happy Companies Are Different]].
3. Treat "not a formula" as the point: because every 0-to-1 innovation is new and unique, no authority (including this book) can prescribe concrete steps. The consistent pattern among successful builders is finding value in unexpected places by reasoning from first principles, not by pattern-matching to what already worked for someone else.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Before pitching or building, write your own answer to "what important truth do very few people agree with you on?" If it's a fact everyone already accepts, or a side of an existing debate, it's not there yet.
- Audit a business plan by asking whether it's fundamentally 1→n (a better/cheaper/faster version of an existing category) or 0→1 (something that didn't exist as a category before). Both can be viable, but only 0→1 can become a durable monopoly.
- Be suspicious of "best practices" and "conventional wisdom" as a starting point for a new venture — they lead to dead ends by definition, since the best paths are new and untried.
- Distrust nimbleness and size as the source of a startup's advantage; the real advantage of small size is the space it creates to think differently.

## Notable examples
- China's 20-year plan to become like the developed world (railroads, air conditioning, cities) is horizontal progress — globalization, not new technology.
- Building 100 typewriters is horizontal progress; inventing the word processor is vertical progress.
- The Founding Fathers, the Royal Society, and Fairchild Semiconductor's "traitorous eight" are examples of small, mission-bound groups that changed the world — the startup pattern, before "startup" was a word.
