# First-Principles Thinking - Reason from Physics, Not Analogy

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Frameworks and Mental Models Index|Frameworks and Mental Models]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Decision-Making-and-Risk/Decision Making and Risk Map|Decision Making and Risk]]

## One-line summary
For anything important, boil the problem down to its fundamental truths and reason up from there, rather than reasoning by analogy ("it's always been done this way") — which only ever yields slight iterations of the status quo.

## Context
Musk credits a physicist's mode of thinking for most of his non-obvious decisions. Analogical reasoning is the mental default (and appropriate for daily life), but for the decisions that matter it traps you inside convention. This entry is that method.

## Key insights
- **Analogy vs first principles.** Most of the time we do something because it's similar to what's been done or what others do — cheap cognitively, but it produces only slight iterations. "For important things, that kind of thinking is too bound by convention or prior experiences."
- **Reason from fundamental truths.** Break a problem down to what you know is physically/economically true, then build the answer up. (SpaceX: instead of "rockets cost $X because they always have," price the raw materials — aluminum, titanium, copper, carbon fiber — and find they're a few percent of the rocket's price.)
- **"It's always been done this way" and "nobody's ever done it" are ridiculous stopping points.** They are signals to dig, not to defer.
- **Obsess over truth; aspire to be less wrong.** Treat your current view as a hypothesis to be improved. You never reach perfect truth — the goal is to be *less wrong* over time by actively seeking disconfirming evidence and welcoming correction.
- **Think in limits.** Push a variable to its physical extreme to reveal what's actually possible (e.g. the theoretical minimum cost or maximum rate), then work back toward it — this exposes how much "impossible" is really just convention.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
**The first-principles procedure:**
1. State the problem and the conventional answer (the analogy you're inheriting).
2. Strip it to fundamental truths — physical constraints, raw costs, hard limits — that you can't argue with.
3. Reason *up* from those truths to a fresh solution, ignoring "how it's done."
4. Pressure-test against the limit case: what does physics say is the floor/ceiling? How far is convention from it?
5. Hold the result as "less wrong," not final — keep seeking evidence that would correct it.

## Tactics / how to apply
- On any high-stakes decision, write the inherited "analogy" answer, then force yourself to derive the answer from raw fundamentals instead.
- Cost or spec something from its physical components rather than from market precedent; the gap is the opportunity.
- When you hit "that's just how it's done," treat it as a flag to investigate the underlying truth, not a reason to stop.
- Frame beliefs as hypotheses; actively hunt the evidence that would prove you wrong, aiming to be less wrong rather than to feel right.

## Notable examples
- SpaceX pricing rockets from raw-material costs (first principles) instead of historical launch prices (analogy).
- Thinking in limits to define the theoretical minimum mass or cost, then engineering toward it.
