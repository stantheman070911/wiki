# When a Partnership Makes Sense - The Five Things a Partner Must Bring

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Frameworks and Mental Models Index|Frameworks and Mental Models]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Decision-Making-and-Risk/Decision Making and Risk Map|Decision Making and Risk]]

## One-line summary
A partnership is only worth it if the other person brings money, time, skill, network, or distribution that you lack; if you're both the same, one of you is unnecessary — and effort you'd both rate differently is where partnerships crack.

## Principle
Partnerships exist to close a gap, not to share company. For a partnership to make long-term sense, the partner must supply at least one of five things you don't have: money, time, experience/skill, network, or distribution. Sameness is redundancy — "if we're both the same, one of us isn't necessary" — and mismatched perceptions of effort are the fault line where partnerships fail.

## Scenario
Use this when someone is weighing whether to partner (versus go solo or take equity/salary), when structuring a founder or sales partnership, and when a younger or earlier-stage operator is deciding how to gain experience.

## Diagnosis
- You're considering partnering with someone who has roughly what you already have.
- The partnership solves a vague "culture" or "companionship" want more than a concrete gap.
- One party would rate the same task's effort very differently than the other.
- You'd get equity contingent on hitting a number you could hit alone anyway.
- You're early and unsure whether to partner, go solo, or join a bigger operation to learn.

## Why It Happens
Partnerships pay for a missing ingredient; when nothing is missing, you've only added a mouth to feed and a vote to split. Effort is perceived, not measured, so two partners will inevitably disagree on who's pulling weight — "I take out the trash and think it's a 10; you think it's a four" — and that gap corrodes trust over time. Rockefeller's observation captures the ordering: it's rare for great businesses to come from great friendships, but common for great friendships to come from great businesses — so start from the business, not the bond.

## Recommended Action
1. Before partnering, name the gap and confirm the partner brings one of the five: money, time, experience/skill, network, or distribution.
2. If you'd both bring the same thing, don't partner — one of you is redundant; structure it as a role with incentives instead.
3. Interrogate contingent equity: if you'd earn the equity only by hitting a number you could hit on your own, you may not need the partner at all.
4. Start from the business, not the friendship — great partnerships tend to grow out of great businesses, not the reverse.
5. If you're early, optimize for learning over earning: joining a company scaling from ~15 to ~100 headcount teaches you what "done right" looks like while someone else carries the risk.
6. First get clear on what you actually want — "figuring out what you want is 99% of it; getting it is the easy part."

## Example from the AMA
An 18-year-old weighing a partnership in a $200k pest-control business (5% equity contingent on driving $2M in revenue) was second-guessing it. Hormozi zoomed out: for a partnership to make sense, "they've got to have money you don't have, skills you don't have, time you don't have, or network or distribution you don't have — those are the things." Since the partner was only "one step ahead, not a hundred," and the equity was contingent on revenue the young man could drive himself, Hormozi suggested he'd learn more joining a business already scaling from ~15 to ~100 people — "the goal is for you to learn, not earn." He cited Rockefeller — "it's rare for great businesses to come out of great friendships, but common for great friendships to come out of great businesses" — and his first boss's line that "figuring out what you want is 99% of it."

## Caveats / Exceptions
- Complementary partners can be extraordinary; the test is genuine complementarity, not similarity.
- Contingent equity can still be fair if the partner supplies something you truly lack (capital, fulfillment, network).
- "Learn not earn" is stage-specific advice for early operators; it doesn't override a clear, high-value opportunity in front of you.
- Even a well-matched partnership needs explicit agreements on roles and effort to survive the perception gap.
