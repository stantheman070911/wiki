# Secrets - How to Find What Everyone Else Has Missed

## One-line summary
Every valuable company is built on a secret — an important truth that's hard to find but not impossible — and most people have stopped looking, which is exactly why there's still room to find one.

## Context
The business version of the contrarian question ("what valuable company is nobody building?") only makes sense if the world still has secrets left; Thiel diagnoses why modern culture has quietly stopped believing that, and gives a method for looking anyway.

## Key insights
- **Secrets vs. mysteries.** A secret is something unknown but knowable with enough effort (Pythagoras had to work to discover the triangle-sides relationship that's now taught to grade schoolers). A mystery is something that may be permanently unknowable (string theory: hard to test, hard for almost anyone to fully understand — possibly impossible, not just difficult). The distinction matters because you can achieve the difficult, but not the impossible — so the first job is sorting which category a question falls into before deciding whether to pursue it.
- **Four reasons belief in secrets has declined.** *Incrementalism* — schooling trains "one small step at a time," and overachieving outside what's asked earns no credit, so people stop reaching past the assigned curriculum. *Risk aversion* — a secret hasn't been vetted by the mainstream, so pursuing one risks being "lonely and wrong," which feels unbearable even to people willing to be "lonely but right." *Complacency* — elites with the most freedom to explore new thinking have the least incentive to, since they can comfortably collect rents on what's already been done. *"Flatness"* — in a globalized world that feels fully mapped and full of smart people everywhere, it's easy to assume anything findable would already have been found by someone else.
- **There are two kinds of secrets.** *Natural secrets* — undiscovered facts about the physical world, uncovered by study (the domain most people over-credit, because physics PhDs "sound" authoritative even outside physics). *Secrets about people* — things people don't know about themselves, or hide because they don't want them known; found by asking "what are people not allowed to talk about?" These are underrated because you don't need advanced credentials to ask the right question — you need to notice what's taboo.
- **The best place to look is an important field that hasn't been institutionalized.** Physics is real, standardized, and picked-over; astrology is unstandardized but doesn't matter; something like nutrition matters enormously, isn't a real university major, and had most of its foundational studies done decades ago and often flawed — exactly the profile of a field that could still yield secrets.
- **A company stops finding secrets when its culture stops looking for them.** HP's decline (from $9B in 1990 to $135B by 2000 on real invention — the DeskJet 500C, OmniBook, OfficeJet — down to $23B by 2012 after pivoting to services, a Compaq merger, and boardroom infighting) is presented as a cautionary case: once the technologist faction lost to the "night watchman" governance faction, the company stopped inventing and started managing decline.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
### What to do once you find one — the "company" answer

there's a golden mean between telling nobody (you build nothing with anyone) and telling everybody (you have no edge left). That mean is a company: the best entrepreneurs build every great business around a secret hidden from the outside, and sharing it deliberately, with only the people you need, turns each recipient into a fellow conspirator invested in protecting and executing on it.

**Diagnostic questions for finding a business secret**, run both angles: *Natural* — "what important truth about how the world works has nature not yet told anyone?" *Human* — "what are people in this space not allowed to say, or not admitting to themselves?" Airbnb, Uber, and Lyft found secrets by seeing spare capacity (empty rooms, idle cars, willing drivers) that regulators, incumbents, and even most entrepreneurs looked past — the secret wasn't a new technology, it was noticing untapped supply and demand hiding in plain sight.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Before building, explicitly classify your core belief as a secret (hard but achievable, worth pursuing) or a mystery (possibly impossible, deprioritize) — don't spend years on something that can't be resolved by effort.
- Look for underinstitutionalized-but-important fields rather than crowded, standardized ones; a field with few credentialed experts and old, flawed foundational research is a better hunting ground than a "real major."
- Ask the human-secrets question directly of people in an industry: "what do you know that you're not saying publicly?" — it requires no special credential, just willingness to ask what's taboo.
- Control disclosure deliberately: decide who specifically needs to know your secret to help build it, and stop there — broadcasting it dilutes the edge, hoarding it entirely prevents you from ever building a team around it.

## Notable examples
- Ted Kaczynski's manifesto divided goals into satisfiable-with-minimal-effort, satisfiable-with-serious-effort, and unsatisfiable — the "easy/hard/impossible" trichotomy, used here as a diagnostic even though his conclusion (technology should be destroyed) is rejected.
- Andrew Wiles kept his work on Fermat's Last Theorem secret for years before revealing it in 1993, finally proving the 358-year-old conjecture in 1995 — evidence that sustained failure by everyone else doesn't prove a problem is impossible.
- Financial bubbles (1999 internet, 2005 housing — Greenspan's "no bubble in home prices nationwide") are what happens when a whole market stops believing prices could hold secrets, i.e., stops believing markets can be systematically wrong.
