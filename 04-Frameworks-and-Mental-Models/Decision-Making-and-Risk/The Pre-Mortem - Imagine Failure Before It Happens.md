# The Pre-Mortem - Imagine Failure Before It Happens

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Frameworks and Mental Models Index|Frameworks and Mental Models]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Decision-Making-and-Risk/Decision Making and Risk Map|Decision Making and Risk]]

## One-line summary
Before committing, assume the decision has failed and independently explain why; prospective hindsight exposes threats hidden by optimism, sunk costs, confirmation, self-interest, and group conformity.

## Context
Teams naturally invest more effort in explaining why an idea will work than why it will fail. Asking “what could go wrong?” still permits vague reassurance. A pre-mortem makes failure concrete and asks participants to work backward from an imagined outcome.

## Key insights
- **Prospective hindsight makes risk easier to see.** Treating failure as an accomplished fact shifts the task from predicting whether failure is possible to diagnosing its causes.
- **Independent writing protects the first signal.** If discussion begins before each person records a view, authority and groupthink narrow the risk set.
- **The exercise attacks several biases at once.** Optimism hides downside, confirmation selects supportive evidence, self-serving bias exaggerates skill, sunk costs defend prior investment, and groupthink suppresses dissent.
- **The objective is prevention, not pessimism.** A discovered failure mode can lead to mitigation, a cheaper test, a changed design, or an informed decision to stop.
- **Red flags should change the plan.** A ritual that generates concerns without owners, triggers, and contingencies only creates the appearance of rigor.
- **The method applies beyond projects.** Careers, partnerships, investments, hires, launches, and major purchases all benefit from examining the future regret state before commitment.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
Run a five-step pre-mortem:

1. **Set the stage:** identify the decision and make clear that the project—not any person—is being challenged.
2. **Fast-forward:** state, “It is one year later and this failed badly.” Make the outcome specific.
3. **Write independently:** each participant lists internal and external causes without discussion.
4. **Share and cluster:** collect every cause, group related risks, and identify leading indicators.
5. **Respond:** assign prevention, contingency, owner, trigger, and review date for the material risks; stop or redesign if exposure remains unacceptable.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Include someone close enough to understand execution and someone independent enough to challenge the team’s story.
- Ask “What did we know but fail to act on?” and “Which assumption looked obvious only after failure?”
- Rank risks by probability, impact, detectability, and reversibility.
- Convert the top risks into an early test or kill criterion before full commitment.
- Repeat the pre-mortem when the project changes materially; old mitigations do not automatically cover a new plan.

## Notable examples
Bartlett’s team spent a year preparing a podcast network. Immediately before the irreversible hiring decision, imagining why it would fail surfaced talent dilution, host dependence, economic risk, and loss of focus. The team stopped the project and concentrated on the existing podcast, which then grew rapidly.
