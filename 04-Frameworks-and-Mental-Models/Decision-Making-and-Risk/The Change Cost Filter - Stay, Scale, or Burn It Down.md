# The Change Cost Filter - Stay, Scale, or Burn It Down

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Frameworks and Mental Models Index|Frameworks and Mental Models]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Decision-Making-and-Risk/Decision Making and Risk Map|Decision Making and Risk]]

## One-line summary
Every change carries a hidden performance tax, so most ideas should wait; but when a better opportunity has far higher upside, count opportunity cost instead of sunk cost and be willing to burn down the smaller game.

## Principle
Founders underprice the cost of change and overprotect sunk effort. The decision is not "is this idea good?" but "after the disruption, is this the highest-return use of attention, capital, and people?"

## Scenario
Use this when a founder is bored, tempted to pivot, split across multiple businesses, considering a new location, or holding a smaller asset because it took years to build. It also applies when a team wants to make constant process improvements that feel individually sensible but collectively slow the business.

## Diagnosis
- New ideas feel more rational than the current business because the hard parts are still unknown.
- The founder keeps changing sales, onboarding, team, or product processes before previous changes settle.
- Multiple small businesses or avatars compete for the same leadership attention.
- A unit has not reached capacity, yet the founder wants another location or model.
- The founder defends a business by the work already invested rather than future return.

## Why It Happens
New ideas start in uninformed optimism. Existing businesses expose their real friction, so they feel less attractive precisely because the founder knows more. Inside the current business, even a good change can create a short-term drop while people relearn. Outside it, sunk cost makes a smaller opportunity feel too precious to release.

The mechanism is quantifiable. In Hormozi's experience, any change he makes to a working process — a new sales script, onboarding flow, leadership structure — produces roughly a **guaranteed 20% short-term decrement** while people relearn, even when the change is genuinely superior. So a tweak he *hopes* will add 5% actually trades a certain 20% loss (recovered over four or five months) for a maybe-5% gain — and, on the alternate timeline, people left alone tend to get better at their jobs and deliver that 5% anyway. Founders systematically ignore the guaranteed downside and overestimate the upside.

Role clarity and time are additional parts of the change cost. A work redesign is more credible when it names which responsibilities move, who will own them, what friction the handoff removes, and how the new arrangement improves an outcome the organization already values. A limited prototype makes these costs visible before a permanent change is imposed.

## The 20% change-cost razor
Because change carries this hidden tax, Hormozi requires a **risk-adjusted 20% improvement before making any change at all** — in practice, a change he believes has a ~50% chance of producing a ~40% improvement. Everything below that bar goes on a list rather than into the business. This single razor, he says, has been one of the most useful of his career and has improved his businesses dramatically — mostly by getting him to leave working processes alone.

## Recommended Action
1. Put every new idea on a list instead of immediately assigning it to the team.
2. Let it season for weeks; if it still matters, compare it against the other best ideas.
3. Require a large risk-adjusted improvement before changing a working process.
4. Channel novelty into areas that need constant creative output — ads, content, and sales hooks — so your creative energy is *accretive* to the business ("paint the city, don't burn it down") rather than detonating a working process.
5. Keep passive investments passive; if they become active, compare them against active-income returns.
6. Before opening a new unit, prove the current one can run without the founder and still sustain or grow.
7. When a superior opportunity is clearly orders of magnitude better, sell, shut down, or delegate the old asset quickly enough to reclaim attention.
8. When you do wind something down, do it gracefully: stop taking new customers, give existing ones ample notice (e.g. six months) at maintained service levels, and hand them to two or three trusted providers in exchange for referral kickbacks — turning a shutdown into a passive referral annuity.
9. Accept that "some shit stays fucked": at any time there are a hundred things you could improve, but fixing one tends to break four others, so leave defects alone until the cost of fixing them is lower than the cost of living with them.
10. For a role redesign, specify the work to stop, delegate, or transfer; the new owner; the expected organizational benefit; and a trial period with an explicit review date.

## Example from the AMA
One founder had a roofing business that could run on two to three hours a week, plus real estate and other side ventures. Hormozi reframed the trade-off: the cost of a bigger roofing company is the exciting things he must stop pursuing. Passive assets are fine only if they stay passive; once they consume time, they must compete with the active business.

Another founder owned a commercial construction company and had started an elevator company with better margins and recurring revenue. Hormozi said the reasonable path was to hire leaders for the old business, but the "Alex" path was to fire-sale or exit the lower-upside company if the elevator opportunity was clearly better. His reasoning: the founder is the asset, and the skills learned in the first business transfer into the better vehicle.

To a serial-visionary founder juggling five businesses, Hormozi laid out the 20% razor in full: every change to a working process costs a guaranteed ~20% for a hoped-for ~5%, so he keeps an ever-growing "big list of ideas" on his phone instead of calling emergency meetings, lets each idea season for weeks, and only pulls one when it clears a risk-adjusted 40%-at-50%-odds bar and beats the other ideas already on the list. He tells the story against himself — "when leaders get bored they just [ __ ] it all up and burn down the city" — and prescribes channeling that creative itch into marketing and content (his own outlet) rather than into the operating business. And when a real-estate-law-firm owner asked how to shut down his smaller marketing agency, Hormozi gave the graceful wind-down: stop new intake, give clients six months' notice at full service, and refer them to three trusted providers for kickbacks — converting the shutdown into ~$100k/year of passive referral income.

## Caveats / Exceptions
- Do not burn down a working business for a fantasy. The better opportunity must be proven enough to justify the disruption.
- Do not use "focus" to ignore an existential future constraint. If the future constraint will stop the business, plan it even while solving today's constraint.
- Not every process defect deserves immediate repair. Some issues should stay broken until the cost of fixing them is lower than the cost of living with them.
- A new location is not scaling if the existing location still depends on the founder or has large unused demand capacity.
