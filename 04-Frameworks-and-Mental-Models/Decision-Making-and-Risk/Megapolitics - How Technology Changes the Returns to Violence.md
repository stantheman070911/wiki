# Megapolitics - How Technology Changes the Returns to Violence

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Frameworks and Mental Models Index|Frameworks and Mental Models]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Decision-Making-and-Risk/Decision Making and Risk Map|Decision Making and Risk]]

## One-line summary
Institutions change scale when technology changes whether coercion is more effective in large centralized systems or in small distributed ones.

## Context
Political explanations usually start with leaders, ideologies, or public preferences. The megapolitical lens starts one layer deeper: which technologies and physical conditions make assets easy to seize, territory cheap to defend, or power economical to project?

## Key insights
- **The organization of power follows its economics.** When the payoff from organizing violence rises with scale, larger jurisdictions and centralized institutions gain an advantage; when small units can defend themselves efficiently, sovereignty fragments.
- **Assets shape their own protection systems.** Stationary crops, factories, and infrastructure expose concentrated value to seizure or disruption. Portable knowledge and distributed digital assets reduce some of that leverage while introducing new vulnerabilities.
- **Weapons influence political equality.** Cheap, widely dispersed weapons usable by ordinary people tend to distribute coercive power; expensive or monopolizable systems tend to concentrate it.
- **Production scale and protection scale reinforce each other.** Mass production favored large markets, cities, firms, unions, and states. Smaller production units and distributed networks can weaken the economic case for equally large coordinating institutions.
- **Institutional lag creates transition risk.** Organizations built for the previous power equation persist after their economic foundation changes, producing rising cost, brittle control, and attempts to preserve obsolete privileges.
- **A mechanism is not a prophecy.** Technology changes the feasible range of institutions, but culture, law, geography, and path dependence still influence which form actually emerges.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
Assess a system through five megapolitical variables:

1. **Offense versus defense:** Is it becoming cheaper to project power or resist it?
2. **Scale of coercion:** Does a larger force gain increasing or diminishing returns?
3. **Scale of production:** What is the smallest unit that can create value competitively?
4. **Technology dispersion:** Can critical tools be monopolized, or are they widely available?
5. **Asset mobility:** Can valuable people, capital, and information exit the jurisdiction or organization?

The likely institutional direction is the joint result: concentration when scale and capture pay, devolution when mobility and distributed defense pay.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Before explaining a structural change through ideology, identify what became cheaper, more portable, easier to defend, or easier to disrupt.
- Test whether an institution's current scale still lowers total coordination and protection costs.
- Separate centralized efficiency from centralized vulnerability; map single points whose capture or failure disables the whole system.
- For strategy, place valuable capabilities in modular units that can continue operating when a central node fails.
- Treat forecasts cautiously when they infer a single political outcome from a technological trend; write at least two plausible adaptations.

## Notable examples
Farming created storable, stationary surplus and raised the rewards to organized plunder and protection. Gunpowder later increased the cost and scale of warfare, helping large fiscal states outcompete fragmented feudal authorities. The book argues that microprocessing reverses part of that equation by letting small groups create, move, protect, and sometimes disrupt value at much lower cost.
