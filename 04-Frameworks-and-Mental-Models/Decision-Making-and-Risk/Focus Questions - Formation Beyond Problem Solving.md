# Focus Questions - Formation Beyond Problem Solving

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Frameworks and Mental Models Index|Frameworks and Mental Models]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Decision-Making-and-Risk/Decision Making and Risk Map|Decision Making and Risk]]

## One-line summary
Alongside solving the urgent problem in front of you, live into a slower question about who the decision is helping you become.

## Context
Presenting problems are concrete, consequential, and noisy, so they naturally capture attention. A life organized only around solving them may become efficient without becoming wiser: years later, the person has a stockpile of resolved issues but has not deliberately matured.

## Key insights
- **A presenting problem asks what to do.** It usually concerns a choice, obstacle, or outcome in the transactional world.
- **A focus question asks who you are becoming.** It directs attention to identity, values, formation, and the longer arc of a life season.
- **The two questions can be related without being identical.** Solving the transaction may expose the deeper formation issue, but neither substitutes for the other.
- **Focus questions are lived, not answered immediately.** Their useful span may be months or years, allowing daily experience to generate evidence.
- **Urgency is not importance.** Problems keep reminding you of themselves; becoming rarely has a deadline and must be protected deliberately.
- **Community improves the question.** Trusted listeners can detect a missing relationship, value, or time horizon that the individual cannot see alone.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
Use the **double agenda**:

1. Name the **presenting problem** in a solvable form: “Which option should I choose?”
2. Step back and ask, “What question is this season of life posing about who I am becoming?”
3. Draft a **focus question** that is open, personally consequential, and usable for observation.
4. Continue ordinary due diligence on the presenting problem.
5. Over several weeks or months, notice how the focus question changes what you attend to, discuss, prototype, or value.
6. Refine the question when experience or community reveals a deeper formulation.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Avoid yes-or-no focus questions; prefer “How…?”, “What…?”, or “Who…?” questions that can mature with you.
- Give the question a season rather than a deadline, then review it during weekly reflection.
- Keep evidence: moments, tensions, conversations, and decisions that illuminate the question.
- In a group, discuss formative reflection before advice about the presenting problem; otherwise problem-solving will consume all available time.
- Retire or rewrite the focus question when it no longer changes attention or when a more consequential question emerges.

## Notable examples
Harmony’s presenting problem was whether to stay in a nonprofit role or launch a demanding start-up. Her first focus question asked how the coming decade of work should shape who she would be at sixty. A community member deepened it by asking what she and her husband wanted “us” to be at sixty, prompting a conversation that changed the decision context without mechanically choosing for her.
