# Choosing Well - Narrow, Decide, Let Go, and Move On

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Frameworks and Mental Models Index|Frameworks and Mental Models]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Decision-Making-and-Risk/Decision Making and Risk Map|Decision Making and Risk]]

## One-line summary
Good choosing requires enough options to find quality, few enough to decide, more than one way of knowing, and a deliberate transfer of attention from rejected paths to the chosen one.

## Context
Important decisions often remain stuck after sufficient research because the real obstacle is too many options and an unwillingness to lose any of them. The aim is not to prove which future is objectively best but to make a sound choice with current evidence and then build it into a satisfying reality.

## Key insights
- **Options have value only when realized.** A list so large that it prevents action provides theoretical variety but no practical optionality.
- **Effective choice usually needs three to five alternatives.** More research cannot repair a list that the mind cannot meaningfully compare.
- **Discernment integrates ways of knowing.** Analysis should be combined with emotional, intuitive, bodily, social, and reflective evidence—not replaced by impulse.
- **The best choice is unknowable in advance.** Outcomes depend on future events and on the energy invested after choosing.
- **Reversibility can reduce satisfaction.** Continually keeping rejected paths psychologically open invites comparison and drains commitment.
- **Letting go works by moving toward something.** Attention is easier to redirect into the chosen path than to suppress from the paths not taken.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
Use four steps:

1. **Gather:** create a rich set of credible options.
2. **Narrow:** group related options, then reduce the active list to no more than five.
3. **Choose discerningly:** use facts and analysis, then create quiet space for emotional, intuitive, bodily, and social knowing.
4. **Let go and move on:** document why the choice was reasonable, close the rejected options, and direct energy into the next action.

To “grok” a difficult choice, live mentally as if Alternative A were decided for one to three days, reset, repeat for B and C, then compare the experienced selves.

## Tactics / how to apply
- If cutting an option triggers immediate protest, treat that reaction as new preference data and restore it while removing another.
- When remaining options feel indistinguishable, recognize a can’t-lose situation and choose by a secondary consideration.
- Establish reflective practices before high-stakes decisions; subtle signals are hard to hear under deadline pressure.
- Write a decision note recording evidence, trade-offs, and why the choice was adequate given current knowledge.
- When regret loops begin, reread the note and take one concrete step deeper into the selected path.

## Notable examples
Andy compared two routes into health policy by inhabiting each choice rather than endlessly analyzing it. Becoming “Med School Andy” felt more coherent, so he chose medical training. He then moved on by designing ways to make that decade itself relevant to policy instead of continuing to mourn the faster route.
