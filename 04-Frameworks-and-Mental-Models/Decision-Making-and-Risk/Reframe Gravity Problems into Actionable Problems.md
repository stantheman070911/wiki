# Reframe Gravity Problems into Actionable Problems

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Frameworks and Mental Models Index|Frameworks and Mental Models]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Decision-Making-and-Risk/Decision Making and Risk Map|Decision Making and Risk]]

## One-line summary
A problem becomes designable only when it is both real and actionable; fixed realities must be accepted, while attachment to one failed solution must be reframed.

## Context
Many stuck situations consume thought without offering a possible intervention. Distinguishing an unchangeable “gravity problem” from an “anchor problem” tied to one assumed solution prevents wasted effort and exposes the nearest useful question.

## Key insights
- **Actionability is part of the problem definition.** If nothing you can do could change the stated condition, it is not a design problem in its current form.
- **Gravity problems are facts being argued with.** Age, a settled past, or a structural constraint may be painful, but treating reality itself as the assignment creates permanent gridlock.
- **Some apparent gravity problems are merely difficult.** A large cost or risk does not make a change impossible; evidence should distinguish genuinely fixed conditions from feared ones.
- **Anchor problems bind a goal to one solution.** When the chosen solution fails, the person concludes the goal is impossible instead of generating alternative routes.
- **Acceptance is not approval.** It releases attention from an unwinnable contest so it can be applied to the terrain that remains movable.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
Diagnose the stuck point with three questions:

1. **Is the condition true?** Remove assumptions, exaggerations, and secondhand rules.
2. **Can I take an action that could materially change it?** If no, accept it as gravity and restate the desired life within that reality.
3. **Am I treating one solution as the goal itself?** If yes, name the underlying function and generate multiple ways to achieve it.

The output should be a question that begins with “How might I…” and permits more than one answer.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Write the complaint in one sentence, then circle every term outside your control.
- Replace each fixed term with a constraint: “Given that X is true, how might I still achieve Y?”
- Ask what the blocked solution was supposed to provide—income, belonging, autonomy, status, or creative expression—and ideate from that function.
- Seek disconfirming evidence before declaring a constraint permanent.
- If no action remains after honest reframing, practice acceptance and redirect the project rather than repeatedly analyzing it.

## Notable examples
A person who cannot become taller has a gravity problem if the goal is literally height. If the actual goal is confidence or athletic participation, height becomes a constraint inside a new, actionable design question. By contrast, insisting that a specific unavailable job is the only route to meaningful work is an anchor problem: the purpose is still attainable, but the assumed route must be released.
