# Believability-Weight Your Decision Making

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Frameworks and Mental Models Index|Frameworks and Mental Models]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Decision-Making-and-Risk/Decision Making and Risk Map|Decision Making and Risk]]

## One-line summary
Neither pure autocracy (one person decides) nor pure democracy (one person, one vote) reliably produces the best organizational decisions — weighting each person's opinion by their demonstrated track record and the quality of their reasoning ("believability") does, and it's precise enough to define and systemize rather than leave as a vague call for "listening to experts."

## Context
Fifth chapter of Work Principles; supplies the third leg of Dalio's master formula (**Idea Meritocracy = Radical Truth + Radical Transparency + Believability-Weighted Decision Making**), turning the openness established in earlier chapters into an actual decision mechanism rather than just an information-sharing norm.

## Key insights
- **Believability, precisely defined**: someone is believable on a given question if they (1) have repeatedly and successfully accomplished the thing in question — Dalio's rule of thumb is three or more times — and (2) can logically explain the cause-effect reasoning behind their conclusions. Both parts are required; a good track record without a coherent explanation, or a coherent-sounding explanation without a track record, doesn't qualify.
- **An effective idea meritocracy requires understanding the merit of each person's ideas, not treating all opinions as equal.** If you can't do something yourself, don't presume to tell others exactly how to do it — and remember everyone has opinions, and most of them, on any given topic, are not well-informed.
- **Find the most believable people who disagree with you, on purpose.** Attend to their *reasoning*, not just their conclusion. Inexperienced people can still have great ideas — believability is domain-specific, not a global ranking of people — and be explicit about your own confidence level when you present a view.
- **Know whether you're the teacher, the student, or a peer in a given exchange**, and calibrate your assertiveness and open-mindedness accordingly — it's more important that the student understand the teacher's reasoning than the reverse.
- **Understand how people came by their opinions.** Ask directly: what data, what reasoning? Direct your questions to the people who are actually believable on the topic, and track believability systematically over time (via tools like Baseball Cards or the Dot Collector) rather than relying on impressions that don't update.
- **Disagreeing must be efficient.** Know explicitly when to stop debating and move to deciding. Believability-weighting *supplements* rather than replaces a Responsible Party's authority — an RP can overrule the weighted vote, but only at their own peril, since ignoring a strong believability signal repeatedly is itself informative. In practice, work through disputes with a small number (roughly three) of the most believable people, not a fixed committee size.
- **Care more about whether the decision-making system is fair than about whether you personally get your way in any given decision.**

## Framework / model (if applicable)
**Believability = track record (3+ successes) + explainable causal reasoning.** Use this two-part test explicitly when deciding how much weight to give someone's opinion on a specific question — and remember it's scoped to the specific domain, not a permanent label on the person.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Before a consequential group decision, informally rank the room by believability *on that specific question* — don't let seniority or confidence substitute for a real track record plus real reasoning.
- When someone disagrees with you, ask them to walk through their reasoning before you respond to their conclusion — the reasoning is where the actual signal is.
- If you're a Responsible Party overruling a strong believability-weighted consensus, do it deliberately and know you're taking on real risk by doing so, not casually.
- Build (even informally) a running record of who's been right about what, so "believability" is based on evidence rather than memory or personality.

## Notable examples
- **The 2012 Dot Collector vote on Mario Draghi.** Bridgewater's raw team was split roughly 50/50 on whether Draghi would defy German objections and print money to buy Eurozone bonds; the believability-weighted vote correctly predicted he would — a case where weighting genuinely outperformed a flat headcount.
- **The 1982 collapse** (see [[Be Radically Open-Minded - The Ego Barrier, the Blind-Spot Barrier, and Triangulating Truth]]) is the direct origin of this entire chapter: Dalio's own confident-but-wrong depression call taught him that unweighted confidence — his own included — is not a reliable decision input.
