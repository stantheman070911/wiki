# Risk of Ruin - Time Probability, Repeated Exposure, and Survival First

## One-line summary
A favorable average outcome is irrelevant if repeated exposure eventually reaches an irreversible loss that prevents you from playing again.

## Context
Many analyses confuse an ensemble of independent people taking one risk with one person taking the risk repeatedly through time. The average can look attractive while every continuing path eventually encounters bankruptcy, death, or another absorbing barrier.

## Key insights
- **Sequence matters.** A loss early in the path changes or ends every later opportunity; outcomes cannot always be averaged across people or reset between periods.
- **Ruin invalidates ordinary expected value.** No later upside compensates a participant who can no longer continue.
- **Small repeated risks accumulate.** A one-in-ten-thousand failure is not “one-off” when the same person or system takes comparable exposures thousands of times.
- **All exposures share the same survival budget.** Financial, operational, health, and reputational tail risks can combine even when each is analyzed separately.
- **Risk tolerance depends on the layer.** A replaceable component may fail so the larger system learns; the entire organization, ecosystem, or civilization cannot use ruin as feedback.
- **Courage and precaution are compatible.** Risking a lower, renewable layer to protect a higher, irreplaceable one can be both brave and prudent.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
Use the **survival-first test**:

1. Name the absorbing barrier: bankruptcy, permanent injury, loss of license, catastrophic breach, or systemic collapse.
2. Combine correlated exposures across the full portfolio and time horizon.
3. Estimate repetition frequency, not just single-event probability.
4. Identify which layer bears the loss and whether it is replaceable.
5. Cap position size so a bad sequence cannot end participation.
6. Seek convex exposure: limited downside with retained upside.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Set hard limits for leverage, concentration, burn rate, and dependencies before evaluating upside.
- Stress-test sequences, including several losses in a row and failures that arrive before recovery.
- Keep reserves and redundancy outside the same failure domain as the primary system.
- Prefer many bounded experiments to one irreversible bet.
- Recalculate risk after every loss; a position safe at the original capital level may be unsafe after drawdown.
- For systemic risks, require stronger evidence and precaution than for personal, recoverable experiments.

## Notable examples
One hundred people each visiting a casino once may show a stable percentage of failures; one person visiting repeatedly stops forever on the day of ruin. Russian roulette can have a positive one-shot monetary expectation yet guarantees eventual death if repeated. The same logic applies less dramatically to leverage, chronic exposure, and operations with a small but persistent catastrophe rate.
