# Trust Your Second Voice - Energy Is the Language of Your Soul

## One-line summary
Beneath the voice of fear is a second voice — intuition — that speaks not in words but in energy, and most "hard decisions" aren't unclear at all; you already know the answer and are just negotiating with your fear.

## Context
The inner critic (see [[Fire the Coach - Your Inner Critic Is Just Fear]]) is not your only inner voice. The second voice goes by many names — intuition, higher self, soul, God, the universe, source — and unlike the coach it doesn't communicate through fear. **It communicates through energy:** the excitement that lifts you, or the dread in your gut when something is off. *Energy is the language of your soul.*

## The insight
- **The energy shift is observable.** Coaching a student torn between Job A (safe, known) and Job B (scary, or just an idea), you ask open questions — *"What excites you about A? About B? If you knew you wouldn't fail, tell me about each."* Within minutes the room's energy changes: heaviness and lag when they describe the thing they feel they *should* do, lift and aliveness when they describe what they actually want. Both people feel it. Almost every time, the student knew the answer before walking in.
- **The real question is not "which?" but "am I afraid?"** For most big decisions: *are you unclear, or are you afraid?* Usually the latter — "your soul already told you and everything else is just a negotiation with your fear." Fear is a poor way to run a life.
- **This is old wisdom.** Prayer (Christianity), meditation (Buddhism), Steve Jobs' "have the courage to follow your heart and intuition," Rumi's "your heart knows the answer — run in that direction." Hearing the second voice is one thing; *following* it is the harder discipline.

## Application — write your fears down
Hearing intuition doesn't help if fear keeps you frozen. The tool is simple (not easy): **put your fears on paper.**

1. **Externalize them.** Fears are most dangerous lurking in the subconscious, where you can't even see them paralyzing you. On paper they stop being a nebulous cloud.
2. **Convert each fear into a solvable problem.** Rewrite it as a question you can attack. *"I can't do this because I have loans"* → *"How do I reduce my burn, start this, and still cover my loans?"* This doesn't make the answer easy; it changes what you focus on — a problem you can solve instead of a fog that stops you.
3. **Use the energy test on real choices.** For each option, notice whether describing it gives energy or drains it. Weight the answer that lights you up; treat the heavy "should" answer as a candidate fear to write down and solve.

> "Fear in your head is paralyzing; fear on paper can just be a problem to be solved."

The one decision underneath all decisions: **which voice will you listen to** — fear, or your second voice?
