# Solve the Problem You Have, Not the One You're Afraid Of - Metrics Over Narrative

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Frameworks and Mental Models Index|Frameworks and Mental Models]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Decision-Making-and-Risk/Decision Making and Risk Map|Decision Making and Risk]]

## One-line summary
A scary industry narrative (AI will kill my business, a competitor will eat my market) only deserves strategic attention once it shows up in your actual numbers — until then it's a story, and redirecting resources to fight it is solving a problem you don't have instead of the one you do.

## Principle
Founders often carry a background fear — a macro trend, a disruptive technology, a well-funded competitor — that colors every decision even when the business's own metrics show no sign of it. The discipline is to separate the narrative from the data: if churn, conversion, or growth aren't moving, the threat isn't binding yet, and effort belongs on the metric that is actually broken, not the story that is loudest.

## Scenario
Use this when a founder wants to pivot product, launch a new initiative, or fundamentally change strategy because of a trend they've heard about (AI, a new platform, a competitor's funding round) rather than because a specific business metric has moved.

## Diagnosis
- The stated reason for a proposed strategic shift is a trend or narrative ("AI is disrupting our industry"), not a number.
- When asked directly, the founder admits the relevant metric (churn, conversion, retention) hasn't actually changed.
- The people supposedly being taken by the disruptive trend were never paying customers to begin with (e.g., DIY-inclined users who wouldn't have bought regardless).
- Attention and resources are being split between the real, working part of the business and a hedge against the feared trend.
- The founder has "a story" about the future but no current evidence from their own funnel.

## Why It Happens
Narratives are stickier than data because they're vivid, socially reinforced (everyone is talking about the same trend), and don't require pulling up a dashboard to feel true. Metrics require the discomfort of actually checking; a narrative can be adopted for free. Founders also gain a kind of cover from narrative-driven pivots — "the market changed" feels less personally exposing than "our conversion process has a hole in it" — even though the latter is usually the real, fixable constraint.

## Recommended Action
1. Before reacting to any industry narrative, ask: has this shown up in a specific number yet (churn rate, conversion rate, CAC, lead volume)? If not, it is not yet a strategic problem.
2. Separate the population the narrative describes from your actual buyers — a trend that affects people who were never going to pay you is irrelevant to your business.
3. Redirect the energy the narrative is consuming toward the channel or metric with proven, measurable unit economics.
4. Revisit the narrative on a schedule (e.g., quarterly) by checking the metric, not by re-reading headlines — let the data promote the story to "real problem" status, not vice versa.
5. If a genuine future threat is real but not yet urgent, sequence it deliberately (see [[Demand or Supply - The One-Constraint Diagnosis, Three Functions, and the Ticking Time Bomb]]) rather than reacting to it today.

## Example from the AMA
A website-as-a-service company doing $20M/year on 100% outbound cold calling worried aloud that AI was "constantly degrading and decaying" its product, since customers could increasingly build their own sites with ChatGPT. Hormozi pushed back: churn was only "slightly ticking up," and the people now vibe-coding their own websites were unsophisticated buyers who "never bought your stuff to begin with." Rather than splitting focus between defending against AI and building new product, he recommended doubling down on the channel with proven unit economics (paid/inbound, given a strong 29-month average customer lifetime) and revisiting product strategy only if a real business metric — not the AI narrative — moved. He also advised getting prospects to prepay a quarter upfront to offset the rising CAC of the new channel, without changing terms for existing customers.

## Caveats / Exceptions
- This is not an argument for ignoring real future constraints — a genuinely existential, dated threat (an expiring lease, a regulatory deadline) still needs to be planned for even before it shows up in today's metrics.
- The absence of a metric moving *yet* doesn't prove a trend is false, only that it isn't binding today; keep monitoring rather than dismissing permanently.
- Founders closest to an industry sometimes see real signal before it's statistically visible — weigh domain expertise, but require it to cash out in a falsifiable prediction about a specific metric.
