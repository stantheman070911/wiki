# Absurdity as a Brand Signal

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Frameworks and Mental Models Index|Frameworks and Mental Models]]

## One-line summary
A conspicuous, apparently impractical choice can communicate a brand’s identity more memorably than a list of useful features because costly deviation from convention gives people a story worth retelling.

## Context
Functional competence is expected and therefore difficult to notice. A distinctive act, feature, ritual, or environment can serve as compressed proof of what the brand values—provided the absurdity reinforces the positioning rather than distracting from it.

## Key insights
- **Useful sameness becomes invisible.** Customers rarely retell the expected collection of competent features.
- **The strange detail becomes the summary.** People use the most unusual observable choice as evidence about the whole organization.
- **Costly signals are credible.** An inefficient, playful, or non-optimized choice can communicate conviction precisely because a purely short-term actor would not make it.
- **Absurdity creates earned distribution.** Journalists, customers, and employees need a concrete object or story to photograph, describe, and repeat.
- **Polarization is often healthier than indifference.** A clear brand can repel people outside the intended audience while creating stronger attachment among those it serves.
- **The tactic decays through imitation.** Once every competitor copies the same unusual device, habituation turns it into another convention.
- **Random weirdness is not strategy.** The device must reveal a true value, audience, or promise; otherwise it produces attention without useful association.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
Evaluate a proposed **brand signal** with four questions:

1. **Identity:** What does this choice say about who we are and are not?
2. **Retellability:** Can a customer describe it in one vivid sentence or image?
3. **Cost:** Does it require enough commitment to be credible rather than cosmetic?
4. **Coherence:** Does the resulting attention reinforce the position we want to own?

## Tactics / how to apply
- Inventory the feature, ritual, policy, packaging element, or environment customers mention without prompting.
- Replace generic claims such as “innovative” with one observable act that makes the claim unnecessary.
- Test the signal as a story: if customers share it, what conclusion will listeners draw about the brand?
- Give frontline staff permission to preserve the distinctive ritual when efficiency pressure would erase it.
- Monitor when the device becomes category wallpaper and evolve the expression without abandoning the underlying value.

## Notable examples
Bartlett’s early agency installed an oversized blue slide in an office with few employees. It was rarely used for transport, but it became the image journalists repeatedly used to convey youth, disruption, and playfulness. Tesla’s unconventional mode names and hidden playful features similarly generated conversation that conventional specifications did not.
