# Three Market Positioning Styles - Head to Head, Big Fish, or New Game

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Frameworks and Mental Models Index|Frameworks and Mental Models]]

## One-line summary
Choose a market strategy that matches reality: win an understood category, dominate an underserved subsegment, or create a new category only when existing frames cannot express the value.

## Context
Selecting a market category also commits the company to a way of winning. The right style depends on whether the category already exists in buyers' minds, whether it has a leader, whether a distinct subsegment has unmet needs, and whether the company can afford to educate a market.

## Key insights
- **Head to Head accepts the existing game.** It uses the category's established criteria and claims the product is best for most buyers.
- **A challenger needs a structural advantage.** Fighting a strong leader on its own criteria is rarely viable for a small company.
- **Big Fish, Small Pond changes the target, not the category.** It uses familiar category assumptions while serving a narrowly identifiable group better than the general-purpose leader.
- **Narrow focus can accelerate growth.** Homogeneous buyers are easier to reach, references transfer more strongly, and word of mouth travels within the community.
- **Create a New Game requires teaching the problem first.** Buyers must learn why the category exists, how to evaluate it, and why the creator should lead it.
- **Category creation is expensive and slow.** A larger or better-funded follower can capture the market after the pioneer has paid to educate it.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
Choose among three styles:

1. **Head to Head:** the category is understood, no unbeatable leader blocks you, and objective evidence shows superiority on accepted buying criteria.
2. **Big Fish, Small Pond:** the category and leader are clear, but an identifiable and sufficiently large subsegment has important unmet needs you can defendably serve.
3. **Create a New Game:** no existing category highlights the product's value, a major shift explains “Why now?”, and you have the resources to define both the problem and purchase criteria over time.

The default for a small challenger is usually Big Fish, Small Pond; category creation is the last resort, not a branding flourish.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Validate that the market exists in customer minds rather than assuming industry insiders' awareness is widespread.
- For Head to Head, identify the accepted buying criteria and collect hard proof that you outperform on them.
- For Big Fish, define a listable segment, quantify its unmet need, ensure enough prospects exist to meet near-term targets, and show competence on the category's table stakes.
- Choose a niche whose needs are hard for the leader to copy without harming its broader position.
- For a new category, articulate the enabling change, the cost of the newly visible problem, why existing categories fail, and the evaluation criteria the new market requires.
- As a niche succeeds, expand through adjacent segments where references, expertise, and proof remain transferable.

## Notable examples
Eloqua initially focused on the unusual “demand generation” practitioners who valued behavioral tracking and process automation. As those once-fringe behaviors became mainstream, the company broadened the frame to “marketing automation,” helped define the category, and grew to a public company later acquired by Oracle.
