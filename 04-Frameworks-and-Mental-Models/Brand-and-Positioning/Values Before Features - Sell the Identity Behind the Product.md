# Values Before Features - Sell the Identity Behind the Product

## One-line summary
A strong sales message makes the brand's beliefs legible before listing features, allowing buyers to choose both an outcome and an identity they want to reinforce.

## Context
Feature-heavy pitches answer what a product does but often fail to answer the prospect's prior question: “Why should I care?” In crowded categories, clearly expressed values create relevance and differentiation when functional claims are easy to copy.

## Key insights
- **Values create a reason to care.** A product claim explains utility; a value explains why the work exists and why this company approaches it differently.
- **Identity turns agreement into attraction.** Buyers are more likely to remember and advocate for a brand when its beliefs reflect the kind of person they are—or want to become.
- **Values must constrain behavior.** Words such as quality, simplicity, or service matter only when they visibly change product decisions, customer experience, and what the company refuses to ship.
- **Conviction is evidence only when paired with delivery.** Founder care can make a message credible, but repeated customer outcomes are what convert that conviction into trust.
- **Values complement rather than replace outcomes.** The buyer still needs a concrete result; values explain why this brand is the believable and emotionally resonant vehicle for it.
- **The customer buys an emotional transition, not merely a function.** A drill may produce a hole, but the desired result can continue through completion, safety, pride, belonging, or status; the useful message follows the change to its human endpoint.
- **Worldview determines which value is legible.** A claim matters only when it connects with how the chosen audience already interprets the category, risk, and people like them.
- **Price, symbols, and experience also communicate values.** A brand cannot isolate identity in copy while its pricing, design vernacular, service behavior, or product tradeoffs tell a contradictory story.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
Build a **worldview-to-proof chain**:

1. **Worldview:** Define how the chosen customer already sees the category, risk, and desired future.
2. **Change:** Follow the functional job to the emotional or status outcome the customer actually seeks.
3. **Belief:** State what the company holds to be true about that customer, category, or future.
4. **Behavior:** Name the operating choice or refusal that belief requires.
5. **Proof:** Show a product detail, policy, price, or customer experience where the behavior is visible.
6. **Identity:** Describe the customer for whom that belief feels like self-expression and the group whose recognition matters.
7. **Outcome:** Connect the identity and proof to the practical and emotional result being purchased.

A value that cannot produce a behavior or proof point is decoration, not positioning.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Choose three to five values and define one observable behavior and one refusal for each.
- Rewrite the opening of the pitch to move from shared belief to customer outcome, then support it with only the most relevant features.
- Remove generic values that any competitor could claim without changing how it operates.
- Audit the full customer journey for moments that contradict the stated beliefs.
- Ask customers what the choice says about them; use recurring language to sharpen the identity layer without inventing an aspiration they do not hold.
- Map two customer-valued positioning axes and choose an edge the brand can truthfully occupy; the tradeoff makes the belief more credible than a claim to be good at everything.
- Audit price, visual symbols, onboarding, and service recovery as parts of the message, not merely as execution after the message.

## Notable examples
Apple's “Think Different” positioning associated the company with people who challenge conventions. The products still needed to perform, but the campaign gave buyers a compact belief and identity through which to interpret the functional evidence. Tesla similarly made an electric car a status-bearing story for affluent technology adopters: speed, safety, efficiency, and audacity made their existing luxury cars feel culturally obsolete before they were functionally unusable.
