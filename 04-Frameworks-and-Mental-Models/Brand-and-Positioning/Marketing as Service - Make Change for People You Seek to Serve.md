# Marketing as Service - Make Change for People You Seek to Serve

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Frameworks and Mental Models Index|Frameworks and Mental Models]]

## One-line summary
Marketing is the patient work of creating a worthwhile change for a chosen group, then earning their trust by showing up with empathy and consistency.

## Context
Mass marketing treats attention as something to seize and people as targets to pressure. A service orientation starts elsewhere: decide whom the work is for, understand the change they seek, and build a product, story, and experience that help them move from their present state to a better one.

## Key insights
- **Marketing is change, not promotion.** The relevant output is a change in behavior, belief, status, or possibility—not the volume of messages sent.
- **Service begins with two questions.** “Who is it for?” creates responsibility to a specific audience; “What is it for?” defines the change the work promises to make.
- **Empathy replaces universalism.** People act from their own worldviews, fears, desires, and status narratives. The marketer's task is to understand those perspectives, not insist that everyone interpret the offer the same way.
- **The product and the marketing are one system.** The story, price, design, customer experience, and delivery must all make the same truthful promise.
- **Generosity requires sustainability.** Work that cannot support continued delivery eventually abandons the people it claims to serve.
- **Professionalism means separating the work from the self.** A rejected message is feedback about the choice of audience, promise, or execution—not a verdict on the maker's worth.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
Use a five-part service-marketing sequence:

1. **Create:** Make a contribution worth offering, built around a meaningful and defensible change.
2. **Choose:** Define the smallest group whose needs and worldview make the work relevant.
3. **Match:** Express a true story in the symbols, language, and channels that this group understands.
4. **Connect:** Help early users experience the change and give them a reason to spread it.
5. **Commit:** Keep the promise, learn from response, and show up long enough for trust and culture to compound.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Complete the statements: “This is for people who ___,” and “After engaging with it, they will be able to ___.”
- Interview intended users for the words they use about their desired change, fears, alternatives, and sense of status.
- Audit the offer from product through service recovery; remove any touchpoint that contradicts the stated promise.
- Write a clear exclusion: name whom the work is not for so that the chosen audience can recognize itself.
- Ship work that is good enough to create real engagement, then improve it from observed behavior rather than private perfectionism.
- Judge a marketing choice by whether both the organization and the customer would be satisfied if the mechanism were made public.

## Notable examples
VisionSpring initially presented affordable eyeglasses as a shopping choice and struggled to convert villagers who had never bought glasses. When representatives let people try the glasses and made returning them the required action, loss aversion and direct experience changed the decision context; purchases roughly doubled without changing the product.
