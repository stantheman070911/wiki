# Pattern Disruption - Win Attention With a 10 Percent Difference

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Frameworks and Mental Models Index|Frameworks and Mental Models]]

## One-line summary
The brain relaxes on patterns and perks up when one breaks — so in any saturated market, win attention by disrupting the pattern (in packaging, marketing, or the product itself) by about 10%: different enough to stop the scroll or the shopper, familiar enough that they still know how to use it.

## Context
Charlotte Jarden built Charms (water-bottle accessories, 0→$10M with no paid ads or investors) and Wall Candy (patterned stick-on wall hooks), both from spotting "white space" — an aisle or feed where everything looks the same. Pattern disruption is her core mechanism for standing out in oversaturated categories, and she argues it applies to any business: products, websites, ads, thumbnails, even a personal brand.

## Key insights
- **Find the white space first.** Look at every competitor and ask what they all do the *same* — that sameness is where the white space hides. Then ask what's missing / who nobody talks to. She saw a Target aisle of all-white command hooks and a water bottle with no accessories market; the gap was the idea. You don't have to invent something new — the gap already exists in every industry.
- **Disrupt on one of three axes:**
  - **Packaging** — people are ~20% more likely to buy a product with more interesting packaging. Build a mood board of every competitor and use the colors/fonts/shapes/formats they *don't* (e.g. sunscreen packaged like whipped cream).
  - **Marketing** — reframe the category to a new audience or a polarizing angle (Dude Wipes making butt-wipes manly; Liquid Death making water polarizing).
  - **Product** — change the product form itself in response to the customer (Kitsch's solid shampoo *bars* answering plastic-waste complaints).
- **The 10% rule.** You only need ~10% difference. Go too far and people can't functionally use it; the target reaction is "this is cool and different, but I can still use it." One question in the buyer's head: the plain option or the interesting one?
- **Build a viral-worthy moment into the product.** In 2026 the product itself should have an on-camera "what is that?!" moment (spraying whipped-cream sunscreen). Does it go viral just by being used?
- **Surprise is the hook.** "Not knowing what comes next" stops people — unexpected formats, street interviews, a chicken nugget dipped in Cane's sauce inside a Stanley. Attention is engineered pattern-breaks, not big budgets.
- **Community turns a trend into a brand.** Gimmick vs. lasting brand is decided by whether a community forms around it (a founder-with-a-problem story, e.g. Bloom); trends without community spike and fizzle (Stanley).

## Application
- Map the category: list every competitor and find the sameness; that's the white space.
- Pick one axis (packaging, marketing, or product) and disrupt it ~10% — distinct enough to stop attention, familiar enough to stay usable; prototype it cheaply yourself first.
- Engineer a "what is that?" moment into the product so it markets itself on camera.
- Validate the disruption with strangers (not friends/family) before investing; then build community so the disruption becomes a brand, not a fad.
- Apply the same lens beyond products — to thumbnails, ads, landing pages, and your personal brand.
