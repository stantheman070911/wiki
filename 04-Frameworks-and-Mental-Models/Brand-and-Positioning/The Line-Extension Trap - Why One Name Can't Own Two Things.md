# The Line-Extension Trap - Why One Name Can't Own Two Things

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Frameworks and Mental Models Index|Frameworks and Mental Models]]

## One-line summary
Hanging a successful name on new products feels logical from the inside but weakens the position in the mind — because a name can only stand for one thing, and share of mind, not share of market, is what you actually own.

## Context
Ries and Trout call line extension "the marketing sickness" of its era. It is the most counterintuitive positioning lesson: the very leverage that seems free (a known name) is what quietly erodes the asset. The principle generalizes directly to personal brands and product lines today.

## Key insights
- **Line extension is inside-out thinking.** The internal logic — "customers know us, they'll accept our new product if our name is on it" — is overwhelming and hard to refute from inside the company. It ignores what the name means *in the mind*.
- **A name is a hook to one position.** When a name stands for one thing, the prospect knows exactly what it is; stretch it across categories and it stops meaning anything specific. The name is the connector to a rung on a mental ladder — one name, one rung.
- **Share of mind beats share of market.** Scott had the lion's share of the paper market but owned no *position*: ScotTowels, ScotTissue, Scotties, Scotkins, BabyScott all diluted the name. "Scott" on a shopping list means nothing, while "Charmin, Kleenex, Bounty, Pampers" each name a specific product.
- **Abandoning a position is nearly irreversible.** A position is like valuable real estate; line-extending weakens your basic position and leaves you "adrift without an anchor" (Levi's stretched into casual clothes and saw its jeans position undermined by designer labels).

## Framework / model (if applicable)
**The shopping-list test.** Write the name on a shopping list — does the reader know exactly which product will come back? A clean position passes (Kleenex, Pampers); a line-extended name fails (Scott). If the name is ambiguous, the position is diluted.

**When line extension can work.** It has real *short-term* advantages (instant recognition, easier trade acceptance, cheaper launch) and can work when:
- The category is new or the extension is minor and clearly the "house" endorsing a distinct product.
- **Reverse line extension ("broadening the base")** — taking a narrow product wider to the same promise (Johnson's Baby Shampoo extended to adults who wanted mildness) rather than stretching a name across unrelated categories.
The rule of thumb: use a **new name** for a genuinely new position; use the **house name** only when it endorses without diluting.

**The seesaw of short vs long term.** Line extension buys short-term sales while spending long-term position. Protein 21 rode its name into hairspray, conditioner, concentrate, and "Protein 29 for men" — and its shampoo share fell from 13% to 2%.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Before extending a name, run the shopping-list test; if the extension muddies what the name returns, use a new name.
- Launch genuinely new positions as separate brands (the leader's multibrand play), not as extensions of the flagship.
- Weigh the short-term recognition gain against the long-term dilution of the position — usually the position is worth more.
- For personal brands: resist stretching your name across unrelated topics; each new "position" you claim weakens the one you own.

## Notable examples
- Scott paper (share of market without share of mind); Protein 21 → 2% after extension; Levi's jeans position undermined by its own casual-wear extension.
- Johnson's Baby Shampoo — successful *reverse* extension (broadening the base to adults).
