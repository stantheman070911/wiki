# Positioning Sequence - Alternatives, Attributes, Value, Customers, and Market

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Frameworks and Mental Models Index|Frameworks and Mental Models]]

## One-line summary
Build positioning outside-in: start with what best customers would do without you, derive unique attributes and value, identify who cares most, then choose the market and optional trend.

## Context
Positioning components are interdependent, so starting with a feature list, a desired segment, or a category label can quietly bake in the wrong competitive frame. The sequence matters because “different” and “valuable” exist only relative to the alternatives customers actually consider.

## Key insights
- **Competitive alternatives are broader than competitors.** The real substitute may be a spreadsheet, an intern, a manual process, or doing nothing.
- **Attributes must be objective.** “Easy to use” and “great service” are claims until a capability or independent proof explains why they are true.
- **Features become value only through a customer goal.** Move from attribute to enabled benefit to a measurable outcome the buyer wants.
- **Value points should cluster into themes.** Positioning emphasizes the few differentiated outcomes that drive consideration, not every feature customers appreciate.
- **Best-fit customers are defined by affinity, not demographics alone.** Their situation makes the value urgent; they buy faster, discount less, retain, and refer.
- **The category is selected late.** Its purpose is to make the previously derived value obvious, not to preserve the company's original self-description.
- **Trends are optional overlays.** They can answer “Why now?” but cannot substitute for a clear market category.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
Use the **A–A–V–C–M–T sequence**:

1. **Alternatives:** what would best-fit customers use or do if this product did not exist?
2. **Attributes:** what capabilities, delivery model, expertise, or proof do you have that those alternatives lack?
3. **Value:** what benefits do those attributes enable, and which customer goals do those benefits advance?
4. **Customers:** what observable characteristics make some buyers care much more about those value themes?
5. **Market:** which existing frame triggers comparisons and purchase criteria that favor your differentiated value?
6. **Trend:** what current change reinforces the value and urgency without obscuring what the product is?

## Tactics / how to apply
- Begin with a short list of customers who understood the product quickly, bought quickly, stayed, referred others, and would strongly resist losing it.
- Group two to five common alternatives from those customers' point of view before listing differentiators.
- Build a table from each unique attribute to benefit, value, objective proof, and the customer characteristic that makes the value important.
- Narrow the target only as far as needed to support near-term revenue goals; expand after gaining a defensible beachhead.
- Use a cross-functional team led by the business owner, and explicitly suspend the team's inherited product labels during the exercise.
- Capture the result on a one-page canvas that keeps alternatives, attributes, value themes, best-fit characteristics, and category visibly connected.

## Notable examples
A CRM company had a relationship-modeling capability that most enterprises ignored but investment banks valued intensely because relationships drove revenue. Repositioning from generic enterprise CRM to CRM for investment banks made the unique capability central, enabled higher pricing, and helped revenue grow from about $2 million to more than $70 million in eighteen months.
