# The StoryBrand SB7 Framework - Customer as Hero, Brand as Guide

## One-line summary
Organize a brand message as the customer’s story: a hero wants something, faces a problem, meets a competent and empathetic guide, receives a plan and call to action, avoids failure, and reaches a visible success.

## Context
Customers ignore messages that require too much interpretation or focus on the company’s history and ambitions. Story structure reduces that cognitive load by giving people a familiar map for understanding what the brand helps them achieve.

## Key insights
- **The customer is the hero.** The person paying is already living at the center of their own story; a brand that competes for the hero role makes its offer feel self-serving.
- **The brand is the guide.** A guide understands the hero’s problem, has the competence to help, and remains focused on the hero’s transformation rather than its own importance.
- **One desire starts the story.** A specific, survival-relevant ambition opens a story gap; listing many unrelated benefits weakens the plot and the offer.
- **Conflict earns attention.** Naming the problem and its stakes creates urgency, while a plan and call to action convert that attention into forward motion.
- **Success must be pictured, not assumed.** Customers need a concrete before-and-after view of the life, feeling, status, or identity the product enables.
- **Transformation creates attachment.** Strong brands help customers become someone they want to be and explicitly affirm that new identity.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
The SB7 sequence:

1. **A character** wants one clear result.
2. **Has a problem** with external, internal, and possibly philosophical dimensions.
3. **Meets a guide** who expresses empathy and demonstrates competence.
4. **Who gives them a plan** that removes confusion or fear.
5. **And calls them to action** with a direct purchase step and, when needed, a lower-commitment next step.
6. **That helps them avoid failure** by naming credible negative stakes without fearmongering.
7. **And ends in success** by showing the achieved result and the customer’s identity transformation.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Answer three “grunt test” questions in five seconds: What do you offer? How does it improve my life? What do I do to buy it?
- Choose one controlling desire for the overall brand; create separate BrandScripts for divisions, products, or customer segments when their plots differ.
- Remove every message that does not help the customer understand their desire, problem, path, stakes, or successful outcome.
- Finish the identity statement: “We help customers go from X to Y,” then use that transformation in copy, imagery, and customer recognition.
- Test clarity by asking an uninformed person to repeat the offer and the next step without additional explanation.

## Notable examples
Apple shifted from feature-heavy technical messaging to positioning its products as tools for creative, independent customers. Tidal did the opposite at launch: its message centered wealthy artists’ compensation rather than the listener’s win; framing higher-fidelity audio as “music the way it was meant to be heard” would have made the customer the hero.
