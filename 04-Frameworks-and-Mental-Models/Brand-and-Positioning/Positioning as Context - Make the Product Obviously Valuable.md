# Positioning as Context - Make the Product Obviously Valuable

## One-line summary
Positioning deliberately selects the market context that makes a product's distinctive strengths easy for best-fit customers to understand and value.

## Context
Customers interpret an unfamiliar product through contextual clues such as its category, competitors, features, pricing, users, and messaging. If the company does not set that context, buyers will invent one—and the resulting comparison may hide the product's strongest qualities.

## Key insights
- **Context changes perceived value without changing the product.** The same performance can look ordinary in a subway and exceptional in a concert hall.
- **A market category supplies assumptions.** It tells buyers which alternatives, expected features, price range, and purchase criteria to use.
- **Default positioning is often historical baggage.** Products evolve away from the founder's original idea while the old label remains.
- **Markets move even when products do not.** A formerly useful category can become stale as technology, regulations, competitors, and customer preferences shift.
- **Positioning is business strategy, not messaging polish.** It affects the target customer, sales motion, pricing, product roadmap, customer success, and brand.
- **Weak positioning wastes downstream effort.** More promotion cannot compensate for a frame that makes the product confusing or unremarkable.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
Evaluate a position through **Frame–Comparison–Value–Fit**:

1. **Frame:** what familiar market does the product appear to belong to?
2. **Comparison:** what alternatives and buying criteria does that frame activate?
3. **Value:** do those comparisons put the product's strongest differentiated capabilities at the center?
4. **Fit:** do the customers who care most about that value recognize themselves in the position?

If any link fails, test a different context rather than merely rewriting the claim.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Ask prospects to describe the product after a short explanation; a gap between their description and that of happy customers indicates a positioning problem.
- List the category assumptions your current label triggers—competitors, table-stakes features, pricing, and expected use cases.
- Separate the product's origin story from what it has become; customers do not share the creator's history.
- Examine adjacent markets whose normal purchase criteria better highlight the product's strengths.
- Revisit positioning every six months and after major competitive, regulatory, technological, or economic changes.
- Translate a new position into the sales story, messaging baseline, pricing logic, and product priorities so the context remains consistent.

## Notable examples
Joshua Bell earned only $32.17 while playing world-class violin music beside a Washington subway station: the product was unchanged, but the context signaled “street performer,” not “concert virtuoso.” Dunford also describes a fast analytical database that struggled against general databases until it was reframed as a data warehouse, where its speed became the central reason to buy.
