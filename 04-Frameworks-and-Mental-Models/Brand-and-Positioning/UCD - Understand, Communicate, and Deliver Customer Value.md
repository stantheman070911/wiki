# UCD - Understand, Communicate, and Deliver Customer Value

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Frameworks and Mental Models Index|Frameworks and Mental Models]]

## One-line summary
Sustainable product-led growth requires one continuous promise: understand the outcome customers seek, communicate it accurately, and remove the gap between that perceived value and the experience the product delivers.

## Context
Acquisition fails when marketing attracts the wrong job, pricing obscures the exchange, or onboarding tours features without producing a result. The UCD framework joins customer research, messaging, pricing, and product experience around a single customer outcome.

## Key insights
- **Customers buy outcomes, not software categories.** The same product can serve a functional job, an emotional change, and a social signal; all three shape willingness to adopt and pay.
- **Perceived and experienced value must converge.** Marketing defines the expectation; product usage confirms or breaks it. The distance between them is the value gap.
- **Ability debt accumulates at every failed outcome.** Each confusing step, missing prerequisite, or unnecessary decision charges a cost in abandonment, support, and churn.
- **A product tour is not an outcome.** Showing every feature can increase knowledge while leaving the user no closer to the reason they signed up.
- **The strongest promise is specific and honest.** Overstating speed or capability may increase signups while worsening activation, trust, and retention.
- **Product data and customer research need each other.** Interviews reveal intent and emotion; behavior shows which actions actually correlate with success.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
Apply UCD in sequence:

1. **Understand:** Identify the core functional job, the feeling customers want or want to avoid, and how they want others to perceive them. Validate these with interviews and segmented usage patterns.
2. **Communicate:** State the outcome clearly, price around a comprehensible value exchange, set realistic timing, and help each buyer identify the right plan.
3. **Deliver:** Route the user toward the relevant result, remove ability debt from the critical path, and make the first product experience prove the promise quickly.

Revisit the sequence whenever signups, upgrades, retention, or expansion weaken; each symptom points to a possible breakdown in understanding, communication, or delivery.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Ask customers what they expected to accomplish, how success should feel, and what using the product helps them demonstrate to others.
- Segment the behavior of retained power users and churned users to locate the actions and prerequisites associated with value.
- Compare every marketing promise with observed time-to-value and remove claims the product cannot consistently fulfill.
- Direct intent-specific acquisition pages into the matching product workflow instead of a generic dashboard.
- Treat friction as ability debt: inventory every field, choice, integration, and prerequisite between sign-up and the first meaningful result.
- Make success states explicit so users recognize that they achieved a quick win.

## Notable examples
- A live-chat product is not fundamentally selling a chat window; it may be selling a better way to acquire customers, along with confidence for the operator and credibility with a manager.
- Canva routes a searcher asking how to make a poster directly into poster templates, shortening the path from stated intent to experienced value.
- Snappa removed mandatory email activation after discovering that 27 percent of signups never activated; the change increased monthly recurring revenue by 20 percent.
- The "hot spaghetti" analogy captures ability debt: promising a finished meal but handing users ingredients, tools, and a kitchen transfers the hard work back to the customer.
