# Understand That People Are Wired Very Differently - Baseball Cards and the Team Dimensions Profile

## One-line summary
Because everyone's brain is physiologically different, everyone genuinely experiences reality differently and no single person's perspective is complete — so success at anything involving other people requires knowing your own wiring, learning others' wiring explicitly (not by gut feel), and matching people to roles accordingly.

## Context
Grew out of a stuck bond-systemization project at Bridgewater, where "big picture" (conceptual) thinkers and "detail" (literal) thinkers on the same team each assumed the other was either unimaginative or head-in-the-clouds — and neither side had a shared vocabulary for what they actually were. That gap led directly to building formal, shared assessment tools instead of relying on intuitive judgments of "what someone is like."

## Key insights
- **The great brain battles are the substrate under most people problems.** Conscious vs. subconscious (creative insight often surfaces from the subconscious during unrelated activity, e.g. in the shower); feeling (amygdala) vs. thinking (prefrontal cortex) — "amygdala hijackings" are fast, intense, and self-resolving; and habit (governed by the basal ganglia) — the single most powerful lever for behavior change, since habits solidify through a cue → routine → reward loop and take roughly 18 months to become automatic.
- **Train your "lower-level," emotional self with kindness, like coaching a child — not through internal warfare.** Fighting your own impulses head-on tends to lose; redirecting them gently, repeatedly, works better.
- **Brain plasticity is real but limited.** You can train around a weakness, but you rarely eliminate your underlying cognitive preference (e.g., a natural detail-thinker can learn to see big pictures, but won't become a natural big-picture thinker).
- **A small number of dichotomies explain most of the variation that matters at work**: introversion/extroversion; intuiting (big-picture) vs. sensing (detail); thinking vs. feeling; planning vs. perceiving. Layered on top, the Team Dimensions Profile defines five collaboration roles: **Creators** (generate new ideas), **Advancers** (carry ideas forward, relationship-focused), **Refiners** (challenge and analyze ideas), **Executors** (implement, detail-focused), and **Flexors** (adaptable across roles).
- **Getting the right people in the right roles is the single highest-leverage move for succeeding at anything with a team.** Manage the way an orchestra conductor does — you don't personally play every instrument, you match musicians to parts.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
**Baseball Cards.** Bridgewater's tool for making "what someone is like" objective and cumulative rather than intuitive and forgotten: every employee gets a profile (like a baseball player's stats) built from psychometric assessments (Myers-Briggs and others) plus ongoing behavioral data, so people can be matched to roles by evidence instead of gut feel or likability. Initially resisted internally — people feared inaccuracy and being permanently pigeonholed — but became foundational to hiring, team design, and conflict diagnosis across the rest of Work Principles.

## Tactics / how to apply
- When a team is stuck and two people keep talking past each other, check whether it's a wiring mismatch (conceptual vs. literal, big-picture vs. detail) before assuming it's a values or effort problem.
- Build an explicit, shared vocabulary for describing how people on your team think (even informally) — a team that can name "you're a detail-thinker, I'm a big-picture-thinker" resolves friction faster than one relying on unstated assumptions.
- When restructuring a stuck team, don't just add headcount — check whether the *mix* of roles (Creator/Advancer/Refiner/Executor/Flexor) is missing something specific.
- Coach your own bad habits the way you'd coach a child through a mistake — with patience and repetition, not self-directed criticism.

## Notable examples
- The bond-systemization team was fixed not by working harder but by swapping Bob Prince's deputy — a "Flexor" who was excellent at executing Bob's ideas but provided no independent check — for a more independent, systematic thinker who could challenge the design.
- Dalio's son Paul's three-year experience with bipolar disorder taught him that thought itself is physiological: Paul's manic reasoning always felt completely airtight to him in the moment, which showed Dalio, in an extreme form, how universal this kind of self-deception is in everyone's ordinary thinking.
