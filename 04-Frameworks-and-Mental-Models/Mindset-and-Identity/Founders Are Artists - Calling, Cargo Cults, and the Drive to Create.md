# Founders Are Artists - Calling, Cargo Cults, and the Drive to Create

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Frameworks and Mental Models Index|Frameworks and Mental Models]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Mindset-and-Identity/Mindset and Identity Map|Mindset and Identity]]

## One-line summary
True founders are closer to artists than to any other profession — driven by an irrational need to create rather than by a job or a payoff — which is why entrepreneurship can't be assigned, copying a founder's surface behaviors ("cargo cult") produces nothing, and honest failure in the right environment is a setback, not a verdict.

## Context
Steve Blank spent decades as a founder and now teaches it. His central metaphor: the difference between a job and a calling is the difference between an accountant and an artist. Most songs, paintings, and plays fail — but artists don't make one and stop; they go to the next. That irrational drive to create is what real founders share, and it reframes how to think about who should start companies, why imitation fails, and how to survive failure.

## Founders as artists
- **A calling, not a job.** You can't divide a room into "half artists, half accountants" — a personality, risk profile, and passion divide them. The tell Blank uses with students: an offer from McKinsey is "the world's best job," but a startup is "something you're thinking about when you wake up before you hit the shower" — something you can't get out of your head. If a startup is just another job option to you, it isn't your calling.
- **Entrepreneurship can't be assigned.** The worst thing you can do is make it mandatory rather than something a person comes to. You can teach people *how to operate* (the search tools — see [[01-Business-Strategy/Business-Models-and-Customers/Startups Search, Companies Execute - The Lean Startup Stack|the Lean Startup stack]]); you can't install the drive.
- **Create first, but create to share.** The purest artists who create only for themselves are "starving artists." The interesting ones understand art is meant to be shared — creation begins in passion, then must be taken to an audience/market. Same for entrepreneurship: vision and passion first, then customer development and distribution.

## The cargo cult trap
Copying the *appearance* of success without the substance is a **cargo cult** — the WWII Pacific-islanders who built straw replicas of cargo planes and prayed for goods to appear. Young founders who replicate a successful entrepreneur's visible strategies, aesthetics, and status markers — without the underlying work, vision, and passion — are building straw planes. Magic doesn't come out of them.

## Failure, hubris, and the innovation cluster
- **"Failure is good" is a lie told by people who haven't failed.** Real failure — especially as the CEO responsible, not an employee of a failed company — is miserable. Lean Startup is not about celebrating failure; it's about *reducing* it. Resilience isn't a poster; it's getting up after a company melts down.
- **Innovation clusters grant multiple shots at the goal.** Where risk capital, culture, and entrepreneurs intersect, an honest, non-stupid failure isn't a badge of shame — investors bet on your vision and ability to assemble a team, so they'll back the next one. (Blank returned ~\$1B each to the two VCs who funded the startup *after* his \$35M failure.) Three or four failures in a row might end it; one won't.
- **Hubris is the specific killer: believing your own bullshit.** Blank's biggest failure (a video-game company) came from raising lots of money and generating great press while shipping bad products — he forgot to get out of the building because the press said he was a genius. A "reality distortion field" with no real product is fatal. Lean Startup itself came out of the *lessons-learned* pass after that failure — not just owning it, but asking "what did I used to do that I stopped doing?"
- **The right risk question:** "Can you die in this profession if you fail? Go broke? Sleep on your parents' couch? Be embarrassed?" If only the last one is true and it would stop you — don't start. (Cross-reference [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Decision-Making-and-Risk/Fear, Regret, and Asymmetric Bets|Fear, Regret, and Asymmetric Bets]].)
- **When people laugh at you, it's a test.** If ridicule stops you, you probably don't have it; if it makes you go faster, you're in.

## Application
- Test your own motive honestly: is this a calling you can't stop thinking about, or a job you're choosing? Blank's advice to the ambivalent — go take the "best job," and in a couple of years you'll know whether you must build.
- Stop cargo-culting: copy substance (the work, the customer contact, the vision), never just the aesthetics and status of someone successful.
- Expect failure to hurt; build in an environment (an innovation cluster / supportive network) that gives you another shot, and run the lessons-learned pass afterward.
- Watch for hubris: the more press and money you get, the more deliberately you must keep getting out of the building.
