# Think Big vs. Think Small - The Law of Income

## One-line summary
You are paid in direct proportion to the value you deliver to the marketplace, and the factor most people underuse is quantity — how many people you actually reach — so thinking big is really a decision about how many people's problems you're willing to solve.

## Context
Wealth File #4. Eker defines an entrepreneur simply as "a person who solves problems for people at a profit," which reframes "playing big" from an ego question into a service-scale question.

## Key insights
- **The Law of Income:** you're paid in direct proportion to the value you deliver, and value in the marketplace is set by four factors — supply, demand, quality, and *quantity* (how many people you actually serve). Quantity is the lever most people leave unused.
- **Two competitors, two intentions, two outcomes.** Eker opened a fitness chain intending 100 stores affecting tens of thousands; a competitor who started six months later intended one store. She earned a decent living; he got rich. Same market, different scale of intention.
- **Playing small usually traces to fear or feeling unworthy** ("I'm not important enough to make a real difference"), not to realistic self-assessment — it's a blueprint issue, not a capability ceiling.
- Thinking big isn't purely self-interested: "your life is not just about you... it's about adding your piece of the puzzle to the world," and the more people you genuinely help, the richer you become across every dimension, financial included.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Identify your natural talents (things you've always been good at) and write down where you could apply more of them in your work.
- Take whatever number of people you currently affect through your job or business and brainstorm at least three concrete strategies to reach 10x that number — think in terms of leverage, not just more hours.
- When evaluating a new venture or role, explicitly set the intended scale (how many people/customers) at the outset rather than letting it emerge passively — the intention set at the start shapes the ceiling.
