# I Create My Life vs. Life Happens to Me - Escaping the Victim Triangle

## One-line summary
Wealth requires believing you are at the steering wheel of your financial life; the alternative — believing life happens *to* you — pays off in attention and sympathy, but by the law of intention it also guarantees you stay "poor me."

## Context
This is Wealth File #1 in Eker's seventeen contrasts between rich and poor mindsets: rich people believe "I create my life," poor people believe "life happens to me." It's placed first because every other wealth file depends on taking ownership before it can be applied.

## Key insights
- **Victims leave three tells: blame, justification, complaining.** Blame points at the economy, employer, partner, or parents. Justification rationalizes with lines like "money's not really important" (untested — check the person's bank account). Complaining works via the law of attraction: what you focus on expands, so complaining is "focusing on what's wrong," which produces more of it.
- **"There is no such thing as a really rich victim."** Being a victim has a real payoff — attention — but attention gets confused with love. Chasing it makes you a people-pleaser at the mercy of others' approval, and it structurally requires staying broke (a rich victim has no audience).
- **You can be a victim or you can be rich, but not both.** Every act of blaming, justifying, or complaining is framed as "slitting your financial throat" — not because it's impolite, but because it reinforces the belief that outcomes are external.
- Choosing responsibility isn't about self-blame for bad luck; it's about which belief produces agency. "Consciously or unconsciously, it's still you" who is creating the mediocrity or the struggle, so it's also you who can create something else.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Physical circuit-breaker: the moment you catch yourself blaming, justifying, or complaining, slide a finger across your throat as a trigger to interrupt the pattern.
- Run a nightly debrief: write one thing that went well and one that didn't, then answer "How did I create each of these situations?" — including your part in situations involving other people.
- Try a strict 7-day no-complaining challenge (out loud *and* in your head) — the first few days often still carry "residual" negativity before the shift is felt.

## Notable examples
- A seminar attendee tells Eker "money's not really important"; he replies "you're broke, right?" — the tell is that people minimize what they don't have, not what they do.
- Eker's own real-time catch: watching Oprah interview Halle Berry about a $20M film deal, he noticed resentment rising in himself mid-broadcast and consciously flipped it to "Way to go, girl!" — evidence that even someone who teaches this has to keep practicing it (see the related admiration-vs-resentment file).
