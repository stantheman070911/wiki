# Finite vs Infinite Games - Winning by Emptying the Tank

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Frameworks and Mental Models Index|Frameworks and Mental Models]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Mindset-and-Identity/Mindset and Identity Map|Mindset and Identity]]

## One-line summary
Business, health, and marriage are infinite games — you don't win by winning, you win by staying in long enough to outlast everyone — so define winning as inputs you control and "nothing left in the tank."

## Context
Hormozi says it took him too long to learn the difference between finite and infinite frames, and that most burnout and quitting come from playing an infinite game by finite rules (chasing a scoreboard someone else set).

## Framework / model (if applicable)
- **Finite game:** known players, agreed rules, a defined end and a winner.
- **Infinite game:** known and unknown players, no fixed rules, and the point is to keep the game going. Marriage → stay married; health → stay in shape; business → stay in business. **By default, if you don't quit, you win.**

## Key insights
- **Last man standing, not best man wins.** Entrepreneurship rewards endurance over talent. "You win by staying alive long enough that you outlast everyone else."
- **Redefine winning as controllable inputs.** Shift the definition of "won" from outcomes you can't control (weight lost, sale closed) to actions you can (calories logged, calls made). "The more ways you measure, the more ways you can win."
- **Leading vs. lagging metrics.** Lagging = outputs (sales, weight). Leading = the activities that create them (calls, calories). Track and celebrate the leading ones.
- **Play to empty the tank.** Hormozi's personal win condition is the delta between how hard he tried and how hard he could try being zero. Chasing the external outcome takes too long and makes people quit; the input definition makes daily victory possible.
- **Wrong-game trap.** Gold medalists who then collapse were playing a finite game with an external scoreboard; Kobe reframed it as "I just love playing to the best of my ability" — game master, not player.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Write your goal as a daily input you fully control ("I made 100 calls" / "I published one piece"), and score the day on that.
- Remove "quit" from your menu of options; when stuck, try the next idea rather than exiting the game.
- When you hit the low point ("why do I even bother?"), label it: *this is where most people stop, which is why they don't win.*

## Notable examples
- Kobe Bryant, asked whether he feared losing or loved winning, rejected both frames in favor of playing to the best of his ability.
- Naval Ravikant: "What looks like work to other people should feel like play for you" — the artist paints to paint, not to finish.
