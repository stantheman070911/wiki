# Manage Your Money Well - The Habit Matters More Than the Amount

## One-line summary
"I'll manage my money once I have enough to manage" is backwards — the universe (and your own habits) treats management as the precondition for more money, not the reward for it, so the habit has to start with whatever amount you currently have, even if that's a single dollar.

## Context
Wealth File #14. This is the mindset half of the file; the concrete money-management system Eker teaches alongside it (dividing income across six named accounts) is significant and actionable enough to stand as its own tactical entry — see [[The 6 Jars Money Management System]].

## Key insights
- **"Until you show you can handle what you've got, you won't get any more."** Eker's ice-cream analogy: you wouldn't hand a triple-scoop cone to a child who just dropped a single scoop — the universe (and your own subconscious) works the same way with money.
- **The habit matters more than the amount.** One participant facing bankruptcy started allocating just $1/month across accounts, then committed to doubling it monthly (10¢, then 20¢, then 40¢...) — by month twelve she was allocating $2,048/month, and within two years put a $10,000 bonus straight into savings without needing it for anything else.
- **"Either you control money, or it will control you."** High income does not substitute for management skill — doctors, lawyers, and athletes are disproportionately represented among high earners who are functionally broke, because the habit, not the inflow, determines the outcome.
- Rich and poor differ in mismanagement in mirrored ways: rich people manage money well; poor people "mismanage their money well" — mismanagement itself is a practiced, consistent habit, not an accident.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Start managing money today regardless of amount — even borrowed or minimal funds — by splitting whatever you have across a small number of purpose-labeled accounts (see [[The 6 Jars Money Management System]] for Eker's specific allocation).
- If the amount feels too small to bother with, treat that feeling itself as the signal to start immediately — the habit is what compounds, not the starting balance.
- Track the felt difference between "managing money" and "avoiding money" over a few months — Eker's seminar participants consistently report the habit becomes enjoyable once established, not just tolerable.
