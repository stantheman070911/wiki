# Admire vs. Resent the Rich - Bless That Which You Want

## One-line summary
You can't become something you despise, so resenting rich people while wanting to be one is a contradiction the subconscious will resolve by keeping you poor — the fix is to practice admiring, blessing, and loving wealth wherever you see it.

## Context
Wealth File #6. Eker notes people who view the rich as "bad" but still want to be good cannot reconcile the two — "how can you be something you despise?" — making resentment of wealth a direct blueprint blocker, not just an attitude problem.

## Key insights
- **Resentment of the rich is disguised victim-talk** ("rich people took all the money so there's none left for me"), and it's contagious — even Eker caught himself resenting Halle Berry's $20M contract on live TV before consciously reversing it.
- **The trustworthiness argument (via Russell Conwell's *Acres of Diamonds*):** getting rich generally requires many people to trust you, which selects for honesty rather than against it — "ninety-eight out of one hundred of the rich men are honest... that is why they are rich."
- **Wealth amplifies existing character, it doesn't corrupt it.** Eker's own experience — moving into a wealthy neighborhood and finding the residents unusually generous, several personally leading major charity efforts — directly contradicted his prior "rich people are greedy" conditioning.
- **The Huna principle: "bless that which you want."** Seeing a beautiful home, car, or family and blessing it (rather than envying it) trains the same mental muscle needed to eventually hold that thing yourself.

## Tactics / how to apply
- When you notice resentment toward someone's success ("they're so lucky," "must be nice"), catch it in real time and consciously replace it with an explicit blessing statement.
- Practice the Huna exercise: browse homes, cars, or businesses you admire (in person or in media) and deliberately bless the owner and the object rather than critiquing it.
- Send a short note of genuine admiration to someone highly successful (not necessarily someone you know personally) — the act of articulating admiration, not just feeling it, does the reprogramming.
