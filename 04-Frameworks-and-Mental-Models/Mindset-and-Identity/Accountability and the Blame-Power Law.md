# Accountability and the Blame-Power Law

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Frameworks and Mental Models Index|Frameworks and Mental Models]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Mindset-and-Identity/Mindset and Identity Map|Mindset and Identity]]

## One-line summary
The first move out of poverty is total ownership — "it's my fault" — because wherever you point blame you also hand over power.

## Context
Hormozi frames accountability as the concrete foundation beneath every other skill: sales, marketing, saving, relationships. Build on blame and you build on sand; nothing stands on it.

## Key insights
- **The Blame-Power Law.** Whatever you blame for your situation is what you grant control over your life. Blame your mother, a race, a party, the economy — and that thing now owns your outcomes. The only frame that returns power to you is "it's my fault."
- **"Be right or be rich" (Zig Ziglar).** You can keep collecting evidence that your life is hard and be 100% correct, or you can let the story go and get better. You cannot both desire to improve and maintain blame — pick one.
- **The victim frame is "99% right, 100% wrong" (Dr. Cashy).** Taken to its natural extreme, the powerless frame always "wins" in the short term (each prediction of failure is confirmed) and guarantees the loss over a long enough horizon. Same structure as never investing: you avoid small short-term risk and guarantee the macro loss.
- **Every self-made person is self-made — including the person in poverty.** You may genuinely have had more disadvantages and be right about it. "And so what?" Being right about why you're stuck changes nothing; only ownership does.
- **"It's not your fault, but it is still your problem"** (Leila Hormozi). Excuses can be valid and still useless — an excuse is a permission slip for mediocrity.

## Tactics / how to apply
- When you catch yourself explaining an outcome by something external, restate it as "it's my fault / my responsibility / my problem to solve," then ask what *you* can now do.
- Run the challenge: name one time blaming something external actually made you better. You won't find it — that's the tell.
- Separate "whose fault" (irrelevant, only matters in a courtroom) from "what's the next action" (the only thing under your control).

## Notable examples
- Hormozi, 19, wrote a college essay from his mother's perspective; reframing her hardship dissolved years of blame and, by extension, the excuses he'd attached to his own relationships.
- A woman at a conference listed "six reasons this won't work for my business"; told "you're right, it never will," she immediately fought back with why it *would* — the extreme exposes the frame.
