# Failure Reframing and Immunity

## One-line summary
Failure immunity comes from treating life as an ongoing learning process and directing analysis only toward failures that contain a usable growth opportunity.

## Context
Prototyping increases the number of small experiments that do not work, so a life designer needs a disciplined relationship with setbacks. The aim is not to eliminate pain or poor outcomes, but to prevent them from becoming an identity judgment and to extract their useful information.

## Key insights
- **Failing a test is not becoming a failure.** A prototype that answers its question has produced value even when its hoped-for outcome does not occur.
- **Small failure accelerates large learning.** Low-exposure experiments make correction possible before identity, money, and time become deeply committed.
- **Life is an infinite game.** Finite projects have winners and losers; becoming more fully yourself is a process whose purpose is continued participation and growth.
- **Being, doing, and becoming form a cycle.** Identity informs action, action changes identity, and the new self can take a different next action.
- **Not every failure deserves analysis.** Simple mistakes and stable weaknesses often offer little return; identifiable growth opportunities merit focused learning.
- **Reality is the next starting point.** A setback changes the available terrain but does not end the design process.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
Run a **Failure Reframe** once or twice a month:

1. **Log failures:** list events that did not go as intended.
2. **Categorize:** mark each as a screwup, weakness, or growth opportunity.
3. **Extract insight:** for growth opportunities, name the critical failure factor and the critical success factor for next time.
4. **Change the system:** schedule the new behavior, constraint, check, or prototype.

For a screwup, acknowledge and repair it. For a persistent weakness, avoid or design around the triggering condition. Reserve deep analysis for a preventable pattern with an available fix.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Use neutral event language in the log; describe what happened without turning it into a trait.
- Ask whether the same failure is likely enough and costly enough to justify a system change.
- For an enduring weakness, add external support, remove the exposure, or partner with someone whose strengths compensate for it.
- Convert the growth insight into an observable next behavior within twenty-four hours.
- Keep prototypes small enough that their failure teaches rather than disables the larger project.

## Notable examples
After an angry client call, Dave Evans recognized that he had skipped the brief news-and-agenda check he routinely used in person. Unlike a rare screwup or a familiar weakness, this failure exposed a repeatable fix: begin phone calls by checking for changed context before launching the planned agenda.
