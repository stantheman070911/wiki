# Focus on Opportunities vs. Focus on Obstacles - Ready, Fire, Aim

## One-line summary
Whatever you habitually scan for — reward or risk, opportunity or obstacle — is what you find, because your field of focus determines what shows up; rich people take educated but time-boxed risks, poor people "prepare" indefinitely and let opportunities pass.

## Context
Wealth File #5. Eker distinguishes this from generic "positive thinking": it's about habitual perspective — what your mind automatically scans for in any situation — not about pretending problems don't exist.

## Key insights
- **Three default mindsets:** poor ("it won't work"), middle-class ("I sure hope this works"), rich ("it will work because I'll make it work"). The rich framing is responsibility-based, not optimism-based.
- **"What you focus on expands."** Because rich people scan for opportunity, opportunities abound for them — their problem becomes handling too many good options. Poor people scan for obstacles, so obstacles abound, and handling them becomes the full-time job.
- **Educated risk isn't reckless risk.** Rich people research and do due diligence, but in bounded time, then decide — they don't confuse endless "preparation" with actual readiness. Poor people's stalling often masquerades as prudence while the opportunity quietly expires.
- **"Ready, fire, aim."** You can't know everything in advance ("there are no straight lines in the universe" — you can only see to the next bend), so the move is to get minimally ready, take action, then correct along the way. Action always beats inaction.
- **"Enter the corridor."** To learn a business, get inside it in any capacity (even the lowest-status job) rather than researching from outside — you learn faster, make real contacts, and discover niches or dealbreakers you couldn't see from the sidelines.

## Tactics / how to apply
- For any project you've been "waiting to be ready" for, start now with whatever access you currently have — ideally inside someone else's operation first, to learn the mechanics before going it alone.
- Practice reframing: whenever something is labeled a "problem," explicitly restate it as an opportunity before responding to it.
- Run a 30-day gratitude list (ten things, read aloud daily) — the mechanism is training your default scan toward what you have rather than what's missing, which is the same muscle as scanning for opportunity over obstacle.

## Notable examples
- Eker researched a dessert café by eating the competition's product and gaining weight; switching to "enter the corridor" — taking a busboy job at a pie shop — led to real supplier contacts and, eventually, his first million (via exercise equipment, not pies).
