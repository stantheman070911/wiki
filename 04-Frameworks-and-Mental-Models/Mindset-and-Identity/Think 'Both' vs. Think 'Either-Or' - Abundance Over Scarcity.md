# Think 'Both' vs. Think 'Either/Or' - Abundance Over Scarcity

## One-line summary
When faced with an apparent trade-off — money or meaning, business or family, keep the agreement or keep the supplier happy — poor and middle-class thinking picks one side; rich thinking asks "how can I have both?" and treats that question as the actual creative work.

## Context
Wealth File #12. Eker frames this as a scarcity-vs-abundance split: poor and middle-class people live by "there's only so much to go around," while rich people assume, until proven otherwise, that a both/and solution exists and is worth the extra effort to find.

## Key insights
- **"How can I have both?"** is presented as a specific, repeatable question to ask at the exact moment an either/or choice is presented — reframing the situation from selection to invention.
- **The cake analogy:** rich people believe "you can have your cake and eat it too"; middle-class people believe "cake is too rich, so I'll only have a little piece"; poor people don't believe they deserve cake, so they settle for a doughnut and then focus on the hole.
- **Money circulates rather than depletes.** The same $5 passed through five people in sequence created $25 of total value without being spent up — countering the belief that one person's wealth necessarily subtracts from another's.
- **Money and character are independent variables.** Being kind, generous, spiritual, or a good person "has nothing to do with what is or isn't in your wallet" — those attributes come from character, and money simply amplifies whatever is already there, so "money vs. goodness" is a false either/or.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Whenever an alternative is framed as either/or (income vs. fulfillment, saving vs. enjoying, this client's terms vs. keeping the relationship), explicitly pause and ask "how can I have both?" before defaulting to a trade-off.
- When spending money, consciously note that the transaction creates value for both sides rather than depleting a shared pool — this is a deliberate counter to scarcity framing, not literal accounting advice.
- Use yourself as a visible counterexample to the "money corrupts" belief: be deliberately generous and kind in ways that are visible to people who hold the scarcity belief.
