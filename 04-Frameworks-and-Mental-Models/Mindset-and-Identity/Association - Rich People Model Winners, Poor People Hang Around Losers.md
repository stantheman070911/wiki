# Association - Rich People Model Winners, Poor People Hang Around Losers

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Frameworks and Mental Models Index|Frameworks and Mental Models]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Mindset-and-Identity/Mindset and Identity Map|Mindset and Identity]]

## One-line summary
Most people earn within 20% of the average income of their closest friends, so who you associate with functions as a financial ceiling or floor — rich people deliberately seek out and model successful people, poor people judge and pull them down to a shared comfort level.

## Context
Wealth File #7. Follows directly from admiring vs. resenting the rich (Wealth File #6): once you can admire success instead of resenting it, the next step is deliberately positioning yourself near it.

## Key insights
- **Energy is contagious — either you affect people or you infect them, and the reverse is also true.** Eker compares chronic negativity to "measles of the mind": proximity to complainers, criticism, and gossip spreads regardless of your own discipline.
- **Modeling successful people is a shortcut, not flattery.** "If they can do it, I can do it" treats existing rich people as a tested blueprint — there's no need to reinvent proven methods for success.
- **The response to a negative close relationship isn't necessarily to leave, but to refuse to try to convert them.** Be the model — succeed and be happy — and let them opt in if they choose to; if the relationship becomes genuinely limiting to your growth, that's when a harder decision is warranted.
- **Feeling unworthy to associate with the wealthy is itself the blueprint issue to fix.** Eker's blunt reframe for people awed by touching a millionaire: "if you really want to touch a millionaire, become one."

## Tactics / how to apply
- Read a biography of someone extremely successful in a field you care about, specifically mining it for their mindset and specific strategies, not just their story.
- Deliberately spend time in higher-end environments (a good club, a nice hotel lobby) to normalize proximity to wealth rather than treating it as foreign territory.
- Identify one specific person or recurring situation that reliably drags your energy down, and reduce exposure to it — even if that means less time with family in that specific context.
- Cut low-value negative inputs (trash TV, doom-scrolling bad news) that function as background "infection" even when no single instance seems significant.
