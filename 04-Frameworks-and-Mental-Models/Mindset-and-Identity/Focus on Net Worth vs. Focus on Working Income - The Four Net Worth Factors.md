# Focus on Net Worth vs. Focus on Working Income - The Four Net Worth Factors

## One-line summary
"How much do you make" is the wrong question — the true measure of wealth is net worth (everything you own minus everything you owe), and net worth is a four-factor equation (income, savings, investments, simplification), not just a bigger paycheck.

## Context
Wealth File #13. Eker points out that income alone reliably fails to create wealth because of Parkinson's Law — expenses rise in direct proportion to income — so a "millionaire" title refers to net worth, not salary, and building it requires working all four factors, not just the income one.

## Key insights
- **The four net worth factors, as a four-wheeled bus:** income (both working and passive), savings, investments, and simplification (deliberately lowering cost of living). Driving on one wheel — income alone — produces "a slow, bumpy ride full of struggle" even at high pay.
- **Working income vs. passive income.** Working income fills the funnel but requires ongoing active time; passive income (investment or business income earned without active involvement) is what actually builds durable net worth over time.
- **Simplification is the most overlooked factor.** Lowering your cost of living has the same net-worth effect as raising income, but compounds differently — one seminar participant reached financial freedom at 30 not by earning more but by selling a home, investing the proceeds, and deliberately living more simply.
- **Parkinson's Law explains why high earners often stay broke:** "as income goes up, expenses almost invariably go up too" (bigger car, bigger house, nicer clothes) — so income alone, without the other three factors, doesn't create wealth regardless of how large it gets.
- **Tracking the metric changes the metric.** "Where attention goes, energy flows and results show" — people who track net worth quarterly report it compounding, purely from the discipline of measuring it.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Create a net worth statement (assets minus liabilities) and update it every 90 days on a fixed schedule.
- Deliberately work on all four factors rather than only chasing higher income: increase savings rate, improve investment returns, and look for one specific lifestyle simplification that meaningfully lowers fixed cost of living.
- When evaluating a raise, sale, or windfall, ask which of the four factors it actually improves — a bigger paycheck that increases spending proportionally hasn't moved net worth at all.
