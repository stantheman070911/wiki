# Hardship as Origin Story - Heroes Use Pain

## One-line summary
Pain is a constant of growth; heroes and villains share the same painful backstory and differ only in what they do with it — so choose the more epic story and treat your hard chapter as the origin of who you're becoming.

## Context
Hormozi reframes suffering as neither avoidable nor meaningless but as the raw material of character and the story you'll one day tell. This is his answer to "what keeps you going."

## Key insights
- **Heroes use pain; villains are used by it (Donald Miller).** Both have the same orphan/wound backstory. Villains say "the world hurt me, I'll hurt it back"; heroes say "the world hurt me, I won't let others hurt this way." The pain is fixed; the response defines the path.
- **The epic-story razor.** Faced with two choices, pick the one that makes the more epic story — either an epic failure or an epic success. "No 85-year-olds regret their epic stories; they regret the ones they never told." More starting disadvantages = better story when you win.
- **Pain is a constant.** Growing, plateaued, or declining — you're in pain either way ("growing pains, not growing joys"). So being in pain isn't a signal something's wrong; the largest stressor is deciding that having problems is itself a problem.
- **Humans experience pain on the same scale.** A needle is a 10/10 to someone who's felt nothing worse; everyone has had their version of 10/10 pain, which normalizes it and leaves only "what do I do now?"
- **Document your life when it sucks.** The low-point photos and stories are the ones you'll use forever as proof of who you are; success-era footage is nearly useless.
- **Fulfillment over comfort.** The blacksmith parable: you are both the steel and the blacksmith. You cannot want strong character *and* an easy life — "the price of each is the other." Hormozi's thesis after a year off: hard work is the goal; emptying the tank is the point.

## Tactics / how to apply
- When resenting your current chapter, ask: to become the "savage hero" version of you, would you design an easy life? The hard chapter is the requirement.
- Capture the hard moments (notes, photos) deliberately — they become future fuel and evidence.
- Trade short pain for long gain, never short gain for long pain (comfort now = regret later).

## Notable examples
- Hormozi lost everything twice; his father's story (failing medical school years in a foreign language, rebuilding from $1,000 after a revolution seized the family's assets) taught that only education/skills can't be taken from you.
- Epictetus, paraphrased: what a shame to never meet hardship and so never see who you are.
