# Willing to Self-Promote vs. Averse to Selling

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Frameworks and Mental Models Index|Frameworks and Mental Models]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Mindset-and-Identity/Mindset and Identity Map|Mindset and Identity]]

## One-line summary
A great product nobody knows about earns nothing, so if you genuinely believe in your value, withholding it from people who need it is the actual disservice — rich people promote themselves and their offer with enthusiasm; poor people treat promotion as distasteful.

## Context
Wealth File #8. Eker locates aversion to selling as one of the more disguised blueprint blockers, because it can masquerade as humility or good manners while quietly capping income.

## Key insights
- **Four common roots of promotion-aversion:** a past bad experience being sold to aggressively; a past personal rejection when trying to sell something (fear of failure projected as distaste for selling); parental conditioning that it's impolite to "toot your own horn"; or a "high-and-mighty" belief that people should find you without you having to announce yourself.
- **"Build a better mousetrap" only works with five more words: 'if they know about it.'** The market is crowded regardless of quality; visibility is not optional.
- **Selling vs. writing well, as a diagnostic:** Robert Kiyosaki notes he's a *best-selling* author, not a *best-writing* one — one pays far more than the other, and conflating the two is a category error.
- **Leadership requires promotion by definition.** A leader needs followers, and getting followers requires selling a vision — "any leader who can't or won't promote will not be a leader for long," in politics, business, sports, or parenting.
- **The real test isn't whether you like promoting — it's whether you believe in what you're promoting.** If belief in the product is genuinely high, hiding it from people who need it is the actual ethical failure.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Rate your current product or service 1–10 on how much you believe in its value. If it scores 7–9, improve it until it's a 9–10; if it scores 6 or below, stop offering it and represent something you actually believe in.
- Invest specifically in marketing and sales skill (books, courses) until you can promote with full integrity and competence, rather than avoiding the skill because early attempts felt uncomfortable.
- Separate the three historical roots (past bad experience, past rejection, parental conditioning) and address each explicitly rather than treating "I don't like selling" as one undifferentiated block.
