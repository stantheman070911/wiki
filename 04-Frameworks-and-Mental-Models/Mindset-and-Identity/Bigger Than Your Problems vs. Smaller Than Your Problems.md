# Bigger Than Your Problems vs. Smaller Than Your Problems

## One-line summary
Problems don't go away as you get richer — you get bigger relative to them; the size of a problem is never the real issue, only the size of the person facing it, and growing yourself is the only permanent fix.

## Context
Wealth File #9. Eker generalizes this into a container metaphor: your wealth can never exceed the size of the "container" (your capacity to handle responsibility and problems) that holds it, which is why keeping money is a separate skill from making it.

## Key insights
- **The relativity of problem size:** a level-2 person facing a level-5 problem experiences it as huge; a level-8 person facing the identical level-5 problem experiences it as minor; a level-10 person doesn't even register it as a problem. The problem never changed — only the person's capacity did.
- **"If you have a big problem in your life, all that means is that you are being a small person."** Not a moral judgment — a direct statement that outer problems mirror inner capacity, consistent with the book's root-cause framing.
- **The container principle:** your wealth can only grow to the extent you do. A small "container" with a sudden inflow of money will overflow and lose it — this is why Eker lost his first million almost as fast as he made it, before rebuilding his internal capacity.
- **Rich people are solution-oriented; poor people are problem-oriented.** The mind generally focuses on one predominant thing — either you're working the solution or you're narrating the problem, rarely both at once.
- Handling capacity scales together: the bigger the problems you can handle, the bigger the business, the more employees, the more customers, and ultimately the more wealth you can handle and *keep*.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Use a physical interrupt when a problem feels overwhelming: say "mini me, mini me" out loud to snap attention back to your own capacity rather than the problem's apparent size, then take a breath and restate "I can handle this."
- When facing a specific problem, write ten concrete actions that could resolve or improve it — this forcibly shifts you from problem-narration into solution-generation.
- Treat "growing yourself" as the actual wealth strategy: instead of only chasing bigger opportunities, deliberately take on responsibilities slightly past your current comfort level so your capacity keeps pace with (or leads) your income.
