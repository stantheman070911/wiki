# Cringe Is a Status Signal - Shame, Guilt, and Documenting the Struggle

## One-line summary
"Cringe" is a defensive status play by the observer — so being called cringe is a sign your status is rising and you're on the right path; the only real mistake is being too scared of looking cringe to start and to document the climb.

## Context
Everyone's first content, first ad, first product is embarrassing. Hormozi shows his own excruciating early work to dismantle the fear of cringe that stops people from starting, using precise definitions of the emotions involved.

## Key insights
- **Three definitions.** *Shame* = breaking someone else's rules. *Guilt* = breaking your own rules. *Cringe* = supposed secondhand embarrassment — but it's really a **defensive status play**: when someone calls you cringe, it means your status is changing relative to theirs, so read it as evidence you're on the right path.
- **Interrogate the rules.** Whose rules are being broken? Did you agree to them? A rule is an if-then statement — is it even true, and how do you know? Most "that's cringe" verdicts come from people who have never cared deeply about anything, "and it shows."
- **Caring is what looks cringe.** You can make *anything* cringe just by caring about it (bodybuilding, competitive chess, making videos, writing a book). The cringiest thing of all is being scared of looking cringe (via Don Misetti). Hating on someone who tries reveals a lack of guts to commit to anything yourself.
- **Document the struggle.** Hormozi's one regret is not documenting the early struggle because he was ashamed of it. If you believe you'll win, the struggle becomes the story you tell (Kanye documented early because he believed he'd win). He started documenting the day he had $1,000 left after losing everything — "the beginning of my comeback story."
- **Don't judge chapter 1 against someone's chapter 25.** Comparing your beginning to another person's thousandth chapter is comparing to something "made up."

## Framework / model (if applicable)
**"You must deserve what you want."** Charlie Munger: "The world is too rational a place to reward the undeserving." To get what you want you have to be good — and the only way to get good is to be willing to be **bad for a very long time**. Cringe is the unavoidable toll of the be-bad-first period; you pay it by starting, taking feedback, and never stopping.

**Reading a "that's cringe" comment:** treat it as a status tell, not a verdict on the work. The observer is signaling their position is threatened — which correlates with you moving up.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Ship the embarrassing first version anyway; expect it to be bad and treat that as the entry fee to getting good.
- Document your struggle in real time (text, photos, video, posts) — it becomes proof and narrative once you win.
- When you feel the pull of "what would people think," name the specific person and ask whether their status anxiety should govern your life.
- Reframe any "cringe" reaction to your effort as a signal your status is rising.

## Notable examples
- Hormozi's archived first ad, first post ("please give me money"), first YouTube video and IG post — deliberately shown as painfully bad, then contrasted with his later work.
- 90 days after that first ad he lost everything a second time and made his first podcast — the documented comeback.
