# Constantly Learn and Grow vs. Think They Already Know - BE, DO, HAVE

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Frameworks and Mental Models Index|Frameworks and Mental Models]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Mindset-and-Identity/Mindset and Identity Map|Mindset and Identity]]

## One-line summary
Poor and middle-class people implicitly run success as HAVE→DO→BE ("once I have money, I could do what I want and be a success"); rich people run it as BE→DO→HAVE — become the kind of person who succeeds, and the doing and having follow — which is why wealth-building is ultimately about who you become, not what you accumulate.

## Context
Wealth File #17, closing the seventeen-file sequence. Eker calls "I know that" one of the three most dangerous phrases in English, because claiming to already know something forecloses the "learn-it-all" posture that every other wealth file depends on.

## Key insights
- **"You can be right or you can be rich, but you can't be both."** Being right requires defending your existing ways of thinking — the same ways that produced your current results — which structurally blocks adopting new, better ones.
- **BE, DO, HAVE (rich) vs. HAVE, DO, BE (poor/middle-class).** The rich sequence treats character and capability as the actual root; money and outcomes are downstream results, not the starting point. "The goal of creating wealth is not primarily to have a lot of money — it's to help you grow yourself into the best person you can possibly be."
- **"To get paid the best, you must be the best."** Compensation across nearly every field (sports, sales, investing, professional services) tracks skill level closely — continuous learning is the direct lever on earnings, not a side activity.
- **Learn from people who've actually achieved the specific result you want, not from peers at your own level.** "Rich people take advice from people who are richer than they are. Poor people take advice from their friends, who are just as broke as they are" — verify a would-be mentor or advisor's own track record before taking direction from them.
- **"Every master was once a disaster."** Skill, including financial skill, is learnable from any starting point — competence at anything (skiing, piano, money) follows the same acquisition process, so "I'm just not good with money" is treated as a training gap, not a fixed trait.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Commit to a fixed monthly learning cadence: at minimum one book, one educational recording, or one course on money, business, or personal development.
- Before taking financial or business advice from anyone, check whether they have actually produced the specific result you're seeking — not just credentials or confidence.
- Allocate a portion of income specifically to an education fund (courses, books, coaching) so continued learning has dedicated resourcing rather than competing against other spending.
- When you catch yourself thinking "I already know that" about a domain where your results don't match your stated knowledge, treat the phrase itself as a diagnostic flag.
