# Workplace Influence Mapping - Authority, Value, and Recognition

## One-line summary
Organizational politics becomes navigable when authority is separated from influence and influence is built through recognized, strategically aligned value.

## Context
Formal organization charts show who may decide, but they omit who shapes those decisions. Mapping both dimensions reveals the real power structure and suggests how to contribute without relying on title, volume, or manipulation.

## Key insights
- **Authority makes decisions; influence acts on authority.** Both are forms of power, but they travel through different relationships.
- **Healthy politics is the wielding of influence toward organizational value.** It is not synonymous with gossip or self-promotion.
- **Influence requires value plus recognition.** Invisible contribution may be noble but does not reliably alter decisions; recognition without value is fragile noise.
- **Power is issue-specific and dynamic.** Someone central to a technology decision may be peripheral to a hiring decision, and influence changes as contributions accumulate.
- **Strategic and cultural alignment make value legible.** A contribution matters politically when decision-makers recognize how it advances what the organization currently values.
- **Bad politics appears as a power play or values crisis.** The first advances personal interests over the enterprise; the second makes “value” unstable because no strategy guides choices.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
Map participants in a two-by-two:

- **IA:** influential authoritarian—can decide and shapes others' decisions.
- **NIA:** non-influential authoritarian—has formal decision rights but limited broader sway.
- **INA:** influential non-authoritarian—has no formal authority but is routinely heard.
- **NINA:** non-influential non-authoritarian—contributes without currently shaping the decision.

Then identify the issue's authority holders, the influencers they trust, and the strategically aligned value you can add and make visible.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Build a separate map for each consequential issue; do not treat general seniority as universal influence.
- Ask what outcome the authority holder is accountable for and what evidence would reduce decision risk.
- Identify an influential non-authoritarian whose priorities align with yours and offer a contribution that improves their case.
- Document repeated value through outcomes, useful analysis, or successful delivery rather than louder advocacy.
- Stay close enough to a values crisis to understand it but avoid becoming attached to a self-serving faction.

## Notable examples
Pete, a part-time clinic physician with software expertise, had no authority over a new medical-records system. He learned that nursing chief Esther was influential and concerned about patient-care workflows. By helping her translate those needs into usable software requirements, he joined the implementation team and improved the decision.
