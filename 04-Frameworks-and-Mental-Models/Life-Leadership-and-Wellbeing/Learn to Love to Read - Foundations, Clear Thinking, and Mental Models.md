# Learn to Love to Read - Foundations, Clear Thinking, and Mental Models

## One-line summary
The genuine love of reading is a superpower — but the leverage comes from building a high-quality foundation in the basics (math, science, the originals) and rereading the few great books, not from finishing many.

## Context
Naval attributes most of his material success and intelligence to reading one to two hours a day. In the age of Alexandria, the means of learning are abundant; the desire to learn is scarce. This entry is the practice of turning reading into clear, independent thinking.

## Key insights
- **Read what you love until you love to read.** Start with whatever genuinely interests you — there's no mission to accomplish. The habit is the point; "it almost doesn't matter what you read" at first, because interest compounds into better reading over time. The best book (like the best workout) is the one you're excited to return to daily.
- **The love of reading itself is the superpower.** Making it an actual habit (real people read a minute a day or less) is the highest-leverage move; one to two hours a day puts you in a tiny minority.
- **Books completed is a vanity metric.** The more you know, the more books you leave unfinished. Most nonfiction has one point and then example after example — get the gist and put it down. "If they wrote it to make money, don't read it." Don't finish out of obligation.
- **Reread the greats.** "I don't want to read everything, I just want to read the 100 great books over and over" — identify *your* great books (different books speak to different people) and absorb them slowly; the better the book, the more slowly it should be read. Rereading (and realizing you're ready to reread) is a feature.
- **It's "likes to read" vs "doesn't like to read," not "educated" vs "uneducated."** Notetaking is optional (Naval's is Twitter — distilling insights into aphorisms). To internalize, teach it to someone else; teaching forces learning.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
**Build a high-quality foundation.** Well-read but not smart usually means reading "the wrong things in the wrong order" — weakly-true axioms become the foundation, and every new idea is judged against a bad base. To build a solid foundation:
- **Stick to the basics and the hard sciences.** Very few dispute 2+2=4; mathematics, the hard sciences, and Microeconomics 101 are solid ground. "It's better to be really great at arithmetic and geometry than deep in advanced mathematics." Master logic and math and no book in the library will scare you.
- **Read the originals and the classics.** For evolution read Darwin before Dawkins; for economics read Smith, Mises, Hayek (or Marx) before any modern interpreter. Originals give you a worldview strong enough that you can then learn anything.
- **To think clearly, understand the basics.** "If you're memorizing advanced concepts without being able to re-derive them as needed, you're lost." Confusion while reading is the mental equivalent of gym soreness — you're building mental muscle.

**A 60-day clearer-thinking regimen:** read the greats in math, science, and philosophy; ignore your contemporaries and the news; avoid tribal identification; put truth above social approval. A perpetual learning machine is never out of options for making money — you can always see what's coming and come up to speed.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Make reading a daily habit built around genuine excitement; abandon books that don't grab you and skim to the gist of the rest.
- Deliberately reread the handful of books that are "great for you" rather than chasing new titles or completion counts.
- Prioritize foundations — arithmetic, geometry, microeconomics, the hard sciences — and read primary sources before interpreters.
- Treat reading-confusion as productive strain; push through and reread the hard books.
- Distill key insights in your own words (a tweet, an aphorism, a teach-back) to lock them in.

## Notable examples
- Naval reads ~1–2 hours/day and credits it for his success; he "picks up a lot of books and only gets through a few which form the foundation."
- Charlie Munger: "As long as I have a book in my hand, I don't feel like I'm wasting time."
