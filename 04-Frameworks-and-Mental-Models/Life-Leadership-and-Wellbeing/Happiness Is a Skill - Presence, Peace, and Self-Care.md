# Happiness Is a Skill - Presence, Peace, and Self-Care

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Frameworks and Mental Models Index|Frameworks and Mental Models]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Life-Leadership-and-Wellbeing/Life Leadership and Wellbeing Map|Life, Leadership, and Wellbeing]]

## One-line summary
Happiness is a learnable skill, not a lucky trait — the absence of the feeling that something is missing — and it is built through present-moment attention, chosen habits, meditation, and caring for the body, in that ascending order of real importance.

## Context
The existing entry [[決策、幸福與心智管理]] covers Naval's happiness *philosophy* (desire as a contract, the hedonic treadmill, peace of mind). This entry focuses on the *practice*: happiness as a trainable skill and the concrete self-care and habit work that develops it.

## Key insights
- **Happiness is a skill and a choice.** It's mainly the absence of desire, especially for external things — reality is neutral and "the world just reflects your own feelings back at you." If you believe happiness is a choice, you can start working on it like any other skill (just as nutrition or dieting is a skill).
- **Presence is the mechanism.** The mind exists in motion toward the future or past; the more present you are, the happier and more content. Even latching onto "I'm happy now" and trying to keep it drops you out of it, because you're making a permanent situation out of a temporary one.
- **Wealth, health, happiness — pursued in that order, but their importance is reverse.** Most people chase wealth first; happiness (then health) actually matters most. Don't sacrifice the top of the list for the bottom.
- **Peace is happiness at rest; happiness is peace in motion.** Aim for the equanimity that lets you interpret events without losing your innate peace.
- **Hedonic adaptation is weaker for natural things.** You adapt fast to man-made rewards (cars, houses, money) but far less to natural ones (food, sex, exercise, sunlight) — weight your life toward the latter. All screen activities correlate with less happiness; all non-screen activities with more.
- **"The more secrets you have, the less happy you're going to be."** Reducing the gap between your inner and outer self buys peace.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
**Develop the skill through habits and self-care ("Saving Yourself"):**
- **Meditation** — insight meditation lets you "run your brain in debug mode" and resolve the unresolved. A concrete protocol: sit for one hour a day for 60 days doing nothing, not resisting the mind. Everyday forms count too — hiking is walking meditation, journaling is writing meditation, showering is accidental meditation.
- **Body basics** — a diet closer to what we evolved to eat (mostly vegetables, less sugar — "not eating sugar keeps your mood more stable"), daily exercise ("working out every day made me happier"), and sleep. Raise serotonin without drugs via sunlight, exercise, positive thinking, and tryptophan.
- **Reset ritual** — caught in a funk, use meditation, music, and exercise to reset your mood, then choose a new path for the rest of the day.
- **Positive-sum over zero-sum** — politics, academia, and social status are zero-sum; "positive-sum games create positive people." Choose the games (and company) accordingly.

**"Easy choices, hard life. Hard choices, easy life."** Making the harder choice now (the workout, the honest conversation, the saved dollar) compounds into an easier life; the easy choice now compounds into a hard one.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Treat happiness as trainable: name it a skill and practice presence daily rather than waiting for circumstances to deliver it.
- Run a 60-day, one-hour-a-day meditation experiment (or substitute walking/journaling meditation) to build the observer's gap.
- Anchor the body basics — cut sugar, move daily, get sunlight and sleep — as the physical foundation of mood.
- Audit your day by one metric: how much was done out of obligation vs interest? Shift toward interest and away from screens.
- When stuck, run the meditation-music-exercise reset, then re-choose your path.

## Notable examples
- Naval's own arc: daily exercise and a 60-day meditation practice as direct happiness levers.
- "Don't take yourself so seriously. You're just a monkey with a plan" — deflating the self as a path to peace.
