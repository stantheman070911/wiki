# Life Design Compass - Workview, Lifeview, and Coherency

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Frameworks and Mental Models Index|Frameworks and Mental Models]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Life-Leadership-and-Wellbeing/Life Leadership and Wellbeing Map|Life, Leadership, and Wellbeing]]

## One-line summary
A useful life compass comes from making explicit what work means, what life means, and whether your actions align those beliefs with who you are.

## Context
Career choices feel directionless when they are judged only by salary, prestige, or a borrowed definition of success. A Workview and Lifeview create a personal reference point for deciding whether a path is coherent, even when the destination remains uncertain.

## Key insights
- **A compass supplies direction, not a map.** It cannot reveal a single correct future, but it can show whether a next step moves toward or away from a life that makes sense to you.
- **A Workview explains why work matters.** It addresses what work is for, what makes it worthwhile, how money and growth relate to it, and what distinguishes good work from merely acceptable work.
- **A Lifeview names the larger frame.** It covers meaning, human relationships, the good life, responsibility, joy, sorrow, and any spiritual or philosophical commitments that organize experience.
- **Coherency is active alignment.** A coherent life brings together who you are, what you believe, and what you do; it does not require every day or role to be perfectly balanced.
- **Views evolve.** The compass should be recalibrated as experience changes the language, priorities, and implications of your beliefs.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
Build the compass in three passes:

1. **Workview:** write roughly 250 words about why you work and what work should contribute to a life.
2. **Lifeview:** write roughly 250 words about what gives life meaning and how people ought to relate to one another and the world.
3. **Coherency check:** identify where the two views complement one another, where they conflict, and where current behavior contradicts either view.

Treat the result as True North: a direction used to interpret choices, not a rigid plan that dictates every move.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Write both views without using a job title, employer, or current constraint as the answer.
- Underline the three most consequential claims in each view and turn them into decision criteria.
- For a major option, score how well it expresses your identity, beliefs, and desired actions; investigate any sharp mismatch before committing.
- Revisit the compass annually and during major transitions. Preserve what still feels true, revise what experience has changed, and note which behaviors need redesign.
- When a decision is stuck, ask which option creates greater coherency rather than which one looks universally best.

## Notable examples
Janine's legal career looked successful from the outside but conflicted with the voice she recovered through journaling and poetry. Reconnecting her daily work with her Lifeview led her toward an MFA and a more coherent, if less conventional, path.
