# Wayfinding with Energy and Engagement

## One-line summary
When the destination is unknowable, use repeated evidence about engagement, energy, and flow to find the next promising direction.

## Context
People often search for an occupational label before collecting evidence about the work itself. Wayfinding reverses that sequence: observe lived experience closely, identify the conditions under which you come alive, and take the next navigable step.

## Key insights
- **Engagement shows where attention naturally holds.** Absorption is evidence that an activity fits some combination of your interests, skills, and working style.
- **Energy is separate from competence.** You can be good at work that depletes you and inexperienced at work that gives you energy; both signals matter.
- **Flow is high-resolution evidence.** Losing track of time, merging action with awareness, and feeling challenged without being overwhelmed reveal a productive fit between person and task.
- **Specific conditions matter more than labels.** The useful unit is not “marketing” or “management” but the activity, environment, interaction, objects, and users present in a good or bad moment.
- **Patterns beat isolated moods.** A journal converts vague recollection into observations that can be compared across weeks.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
Use a **Good Time Journal** for at least three weeks:

1. Record significant activities and rate both engagement and energy.
2. Once a week, review the log for recurring peaks, drains, and surprises.
3. Examine important moments with **AEIOU**: Activities, Environments, Interactions, Objects, and Users.
4. Identify flow experiences and ask which challenge level, feedback loop, and skill use made them possible.
5. Convert the strongest pattern into a question for a small experiment.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Log activities soon after they occur; memory overweights dramatic events and recent frustration.
- Separate the task from its wrapper: a draining meeting may reflect its participants or format, not the whole field.
- Mine prior peak experiences when the current week provides too little variation.
- Turn patterns into design constraints such as “frequent direct feedback,” “long blocks of solitary making,” or “real-time customer interaction.”
- Test one constraint at a time through a conversation, shadowing session, volunteer project, or temporary change in responsibilities.

## Notable examples
Donald initially framed his problem as being in the wrong company. Interviews and his Good Time Journal showed that people—not a new employer—were the missing source of energy. Reframing his role around building a culture where employees loved their work renewed the job without changing the workplace.
