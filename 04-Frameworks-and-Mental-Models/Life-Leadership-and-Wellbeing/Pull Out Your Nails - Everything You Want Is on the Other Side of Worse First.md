# Pull Out Your Nails - Everything You Want Is on the Other Side of Worse First

## One-line summary
The thing you're avoiding — the "nail in your head" — defines your life for as long as you leave it in, and the only reason you don't pull it out is that doing so makes things worse before they get better.

## Context
A viral video shows a woman with a literal nail in her head, suffering, insisting "it's not about the nail" and talking about everything except the obvious cause of her pain. We all accumulate nails — some we create, some we don't — and we leave them in for one reason: pulling them out hurts, and something has to get worse first. As Carl Jung put it, *"where your fear is, there is your task"* — and that task is your nail.

## What a nail is
The thing you're avoiding, afraid of, won't talk about, or ruminate on at 2 a.m. Common forms:
- A relationship that has run its course and needs to end
- A job that is slowly draining your energy
- A habit taking a toll that you know you need to quit
- Something deeper from your past you've stuffed down and never dealt with

**The cost of leaving it in — "Olivia":** engaged fast, felt dread in her gut before the wedding, married anyway because the invitations were sent and the deposit paid. The marriage was the wrong one. At 62 she's still in it — 37 years leaving that nail in, and it defined most of her life. *Pulling out your nails hurts, but leaving them in is even more devastating.*

## The "worse first" curve
Life isn't linear. You reach a **local peak or plateau** and get stuck — because the path to the next, higher peak first runs **downhill through a valley**, and you're unwilling to descend. Leave the relationship → a hard breakup and a stretch of being alone. Quit drinking → sobriety and even your friends feel boring for a while. Quit the job → money stress. The valley is the price of the next peak.

- **Everything you want is on the other side of worse first.** Internalizing this single graph changes how you act.
- The anticipated valley is typically *~100× worse in your imagination than in reality*, and the peak beyond is far higher than expected — Weaver describes the release as "a beach ball held underwater" shooting 30 feet into the air.

## Application — how to pull a nail
1. **Admit you have one.** Bring it out of the shameful shadows and start naming it out loud to people. This is the hard first step.
2. **Assemble a team** so you don't face it alone — friends, a therapist, an advisor.
3. **Resolve to pull it, knowing life gets worse first.** Pre-decide to accept the valley; the expectation of "worse first" is the exact thing that stops most people, so plan for it rather than being ambushed by it.
4. **Expect the overcorrection of imagination.** Remember the valley is smaller than it looks from the plateau — that memory makes each successive nail easier to pull.
