# Moment-Making - Crafted, Discovered, and Savored Experiences

## One-line summary
Meaningful moments can be deliberately arranged, attentively discovered, and later deepened through savoring.

## Context
If meaning only comes from rare milestones, most ordinary days become empty waiting rooms. Moment-making treats the raw material of an existing day as designable and makes meaning available without requiring a new career, location, or schedule.

## Key insights
- **Crafted moments are orchestrated in advance.** They use setting, timing, people, and intention to make a desired experience more likely.
- **Discovered moments are already arriving.** They require availability—the readiness to notice and receive what was not planned.
- **Savoring extends value without extending the event.** Re-entering a good experience in memory gives it more attention than was possible in real time.
- **The practice is the product.** A moment disappears as soon as it passes, so moment design is a never-ending prototype rather than a project that reaches “done.”
- **Depth often beats addition.** Getting more out of what is already happening can add meaning without cramming another activity into life.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
The **three modes of moment-making** are:

1. **Craft:** arrange a likely meaningful experience in advance.
2. **Discover:** interrupt autopilot long enough to notice an unplanned invitation in the present.
3. **Savor:** revisit a resonant moment soon afterward or during a weekly reflection and attend to its sensory and emotional detail.

Each mode converts attention into felt meaning at a different point: before, during, or after the event.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Craft one small weekly moment by controlling only a few variables: time, place, attention, and who is present.
- When a pleasant detail catches your attention, tell yourself “Don’t miss it,” pause for two or three seconds, and take in the sensory experience.
- Once a week, review the prior seven days, choose two to four bright moments, and re-enter each one before writing a short reflection.
- Do not grade the practice by whether it produced a better mood or more productivity; outcome-scoring turns moment-making back into a transaction.

## Notable examples
One crafted moment is waking early to meditate in a quiet favorite chair. A discovered moment is noticing two cars at a light playing the same song and sharing a spontaneous smile. A sudden savoring can be as small as pausing in a difficult conversation to actually taste the coffee already in your hand.
