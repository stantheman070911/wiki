# The ARC of Career Motivation - Autonomy, Relatedness, and Competence

## One-line summary
Sustainable work motivation grows when people can exercise meaningful choice, belong to a working community, and become more capable at work that matters.

## Context
External rewards can secure compliance, but they do not fully explain engagement. Self-determination theory identifies three innate needs—autonomy, relatedness, and competence—that workers and managers can deliberately strengthen inside many different roles.

## Key insights
- **Autonomy is volition, not isolation.** It means having meaningful control over how, when, or with whom work is done within real constraints.
- **Relatedness makes effort social.** Connection to colleagues, users, or a shared purpose turns a task into participation in something larger.
- **Competence makes growth rewarding.** Mastering a useful craft is intrinsically satisfying and expands future agency.
- **Money can crowd out intrinsic motivation.** Rewards attached to an already interesting activity can redirect attention from the activity to the payment.
- **Strengths still need development.** Natural ability is raw material; mastery requires deliberate practice, feedback, and increasing challenge.
- **Required weaknesses also deserve investment.** A role may require learning a nonpreferred skill well enough to unlock the rest of the work.
- **Passion is usually downstream.** Curiosity, practice, purpose, and hope often create passion after years of engagement.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
Audit a role across ARC:

1. **Autonomy:** Where can I initiate, choose a method, or own an outcome?
2. **Relatedness:** Whose success does this work serve, and which relationships make it meaningful?
3. **Competence:** Which strength can I deepen and which required weakness must I make serviceable?

For each dimension, choose one small change and observe whether engagement increases over four weeks.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Overdeliver reliably before asking for greater autonomy; trust grows from predictable outcomes.
- Initiate a low-risk improvement that does not require approval, then make its value visible.
- Add brief team check-ins, direct user contact, or peer collaboration to work that feels isolated.
- Practice one skill at the edge of current ability and obtain specific feedback on each repetition.
- Managers should define outcomes clearly, delegate methods where possible, and create visible paths for learning and contribution.

## Notable examples
Ann, a fast-food shift supervisor, could not rewrite the operating system, but she initiated a cross-shift handoff role that improved cleanliness and order accuracy. The change increased autonomy, strengthened relatedness between shifts, and demonstrated operational competence.
