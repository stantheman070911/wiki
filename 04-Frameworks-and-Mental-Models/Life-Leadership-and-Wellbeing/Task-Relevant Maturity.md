# Task-Relevant Maturity

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Frameworks and Mental Models Index|Frameworks and Mental Models]] → [[04-Frameworks-and-Mental-Models/Life-Leadership-and-Wellbeing/Life Leadership and Wellbeing Map|Life, Leadership, and Wellbeing]]

## One-line summary
The effective level of managerial direction depends on a person's maturity for the specific task—not their age, title, or general competence—and must change when the task or environment changes.

## Context
Leaders often choose a fixed identity such as hands-on or hands-off. That ignores the main variable: the subordinate may be expert in one responsibility and a novice in another, or may lose effective maturity when pace, ambiguity, or stakes suddenly rise.

## Key insights
- **Maturity is task-specific.** It combines achievement orientation, readiness for responsibility, education, training, and experience relevant to the exact work.
- **General success does not transfer automatically.** A strong sales manager can have low maturity when first running a factory; treating the promotion as proof of immediate competence sets both parties up to fail.
- **Low maturity needs structure.** The manager specifies what, when, and how, providing close teaching and monitoring.
- **Medium maturity needs two-way support.** The manager and subordinate reason together, with more communication, encouragement, and attention to individual judgment.
- **High maturity supports delegation.** The manager aligns on objectives, grants room in execution, and monitors sufficiently to avoid surprise.
- **Structure becomes internalized, not eliminated.** As capability and shared values grow, instructions move from external supervision into the person's own operating habits.
- **A sudden environmental shift can reset the style.** Crisis, a new technology, or unfamiliar scope can require temporary return to explicit command even with senior people.

## Framework / model (if applicable)
**TRM-style matrix:**

1. **Low TRM — direct:** define outcome, sequence, standards, checkpoints, and escalation conditions; demonstrate and inspect frequently.
2. **Medium TRM — coach:** ask for the person's plan, reason through tradeoffs, give support and corrective feedback, and share decision logic.
3. **High TRM — delegate:** agree on objectives and constraints, let the person choose execution, and monitor leading indicators at a lower frequency.

Reassess TRM for every material task change. The same person may occupy all three levels across different responsibilities at the same time.

## Tactics / how to apply
- Rate maturity by evidence from the specific task: prior repetitions, quality, judgment under exceptions, ownership, and ability to teach the work.
- Ask what the cost of an unsupervised mistake would be; customers should not pay the tuition for a subordinate's unguarded learning.
- State explicitly whether a comment is an order, a constraint, advice, or a question; subordinates often hear a manager's suggestion as a command.
- Reduce checkpoints only after repeated performance, and restore them when scope or conditions change.
- Raise TRM deliberately with training, rehearsal, staged responsibility, feedback, and access to the reasoning behind standards.
- Compare your intended style with the subordinate's experience of it; managers routinely see themselves as more delegating than their teams do.
- Test close friendships at work against the ability to deliver a hard review or direct instruction when the task requires it.

## Notable examples
Intel moved an outstanding field sales manager into factory leadership. His performance initially collapsed because his task-relevant maturity—not his overall maturity—was low in the new environment, then recovered as he learned. Grove also uses an army unit whose routine peacetime maturity becomes inadequate during an attack; the sergeant appropriately shifts from light monitoring to explicit commands until combat itself becomes familiar.
