The 3-Part Brand Story Framework
01. The Catalyst (Why Your Brand Exists & Why It Matters)

Every brand starts because something needed to change. This isn’t always a struggle—sometimes it’s an opportunity no one
else saw.


Example: Nike didn’t start because Phil Knight wanted to be rich—it started because he saw a gap in performance shoes.


Ask Yourself:

    •   What needs to change?
    •   What do you see that others didn’t?
    •   Why do you feel the need to act?


Your Answer:




02. The Core Truth (What Makes You Different & Why People Should Care)

A strong brand stands out—it doesn’t blend in. Blending in leads to being forgotten. This doesn’t mean being controversial for
controversy’s sake—it means having a core conviction that is different from the market and sharing that.


Example: The artist Russ believes that artists should own their music and stay independent to maintain creative control and be
fairly paid. He reinforces this belief through his music and press interviews.

   Waterfall Distribution Method Playbook                                                                      Section Title




            The Waterfall Distribution Method is the most effective way to maximize content output and increase
Objective   brand reach without reinventing the wheel. This strategy ensures that one piece of content fuels multiple
            platforms, making your content work harder for you. Originally popularized by GaryVee, this method has
            been refined over time into a repeatable system that turns a single high-value content piece into weeks of
            strategic distribution.


            This playbook will guide you through:

                1.   Identifying your pillar content (the foundational piece that starts the process)
                2.   Extracting micro-content to repurpose for multiple platforms
                3.   Breaking down formats to match audience expectations
                4.   Distributing strategically to maximize engagement and reach




Step 01     Identify Your Pillar Content
            Your pillar content is the foundation of your content strategy. It should be high-value,
            long-form, and packed with insights.

            Examples of Pillar Content:

                •    A YouTube long-form video (12+ minutes with deep insights)
                •    A high-value Twitter thread that breaks down a concept
                •    A newsletter issue that teaches a framework
                •    A long-form LinkedIn post sharing detailed strategy
                •    A podcast episode featuring expert discussion


            Key Question to Ask:

                If my audience only consumed one piece of my content per week, what would I want it to be?


            Your pillar content should be evergreen or highly relevant to your niche so that repurposing it over time still
            provides value.




Step 02     Extract Micro-Content (Mining for Gold)
            Once you’ve created your pillar content, the next step is to break it down into smaller,
            digestible pieces for distribution across multiple platforms.

            How to Find Micro-Content:

                •    Identify the most valuable 30-60 second clips from a long-form video or podcast
                •    Pull out standout quotes that could be shared as tweets or LinkedIn posts
                •    Summarize the key takeaways into a text-based format
                •    Find controversial or contrarian takes that could spark engagement

     Step 03

                       Each platform has its own content style, audience behavior, and best practices. Your
                       content should feel native to each platform while maintaining the core message.

                       Example Platform Breakdown:

                           •    YouTube Shorts, TikTok, Reels: 30-120 second clips pulled from your long-form video
                           •    Twitter/X: Break down insights into a thread (5-10 tweets max)
                           •    LinkedIn: Turn the core idea into a written post or a carousel
                           •    Instagram Stories: Share quick takeaways with engaging visuals
                           •    Email Newsletter: Package insights from the original content into an email sequence



     Step 04           Strategic Distribution Plan
                       To make this process efficient, systematize your workflow so that content is
                       consistently distributed.

                       The Waterfall Process:

                           1.   Post your pillar content first (YouTube, podcast, long-form post)
                           2.   Within 24-48 hours, schedule micro-content across secondary platforms
                           3.   Engage & iterate—monitor audience responses and double down on what’s working
                           4.   Repurpose winners—take high-performing content and find ways to expand on it


                       Pro Tip: Create a Content Calendar

                       Use a simple structure like:




         Monday                      Tuesday                Wednesday                    Thursday                Friday

         Post pillar              Twitter thread +             Instagram                TikTok/Reels        Email newsletter
          content                  LinkedIn post               carousel                video + second         + engagement
                                                           + YouTube short              LinkedIn post         on platforms




Final Thoughts: Why This Works
The Waterfall Distribution Method ensures:

     •    You don’t waste content—every piece has multiple uses
     •    You increase visibility—your audience sees your message multiple times in different formats
     •    You stay top of mind—repetition reinforces brand associations


If you execute this properly, one piece of content fuels an entire week (or more).

            A Content Hackathon is a dedicated day each quarter (or month) for the team to break out of routine content
Objective   production and create entirely new formats and styles that wouldn’t normally be produced. This practice
            fosters creativity, experimentation, and innovation, allowing the team to push creative limits and develop
            content that could become a new brand standard.




Step 01     Purpose & Notification
            Advance Planning & Communication

                •   Announce the Content Hackathon at least one month in advance so the team can plan accordingly
                •   Managers should work closely with their direct reports to ensure all regular tasks and deadlines
                    are completed beforehand, allowing the entire team to dedicate their full energy to the hackathon
                •   Make it clear that this day is fully blocked off for experimentation—no meetings, no client
                    calls, no distractions




Step 02     Hackathon Schedule & Structure
            Kickoff Meeting (9:00 - 9:15 AM)

                •   Set the tone: Emphasize that the focus is on creating content the team has never done before—this

                •   Go over the rules and expectations:

             A solid job description isn’t just a list of tasks—it’s a magnet for the right talent. If
 Objective
             you’re not crystal clear on what you need, you’ll attract people who aren’t the right fit.
             This worksheet will help you write a clear, compelling, and effective job description by
             breaking down what to include, why it matters, and how to make it stand out. You’ll get a
             a section-by-section breakdown, fill-in-the-blank template, and a real example so you’re
             not guessing. The goal? To make sure the right person reads this and immediately knows,
             “This is for me.”


Job Description Sections & What They Mean

              Role

                A couple sentences that sum up what this position is and why it exists; think of it
                as the job’s purpose in a sentence


              Responsibilities

                A clear, no-fluff list of what this person will actually do day-to-day; this helps them
                visualize the role and understand what’s expected


              Requirements

                The non-negotiables—skills, experience, or traits someone must have to be
                successful in this role; keep it focused on what truly matters


              Results

                What success looks like in this role. Instead of just listing tasks, this section
                defines what the outcome should be


              Availability

                The days/times you expect them to work; be upfront about schedule expectations
                to avoid misalignment later


              Compensation Range

                A transparent salary or pay range so candidates know what to expect
                before applying


              Company Core Values

                What your company stands for and how you operate; this isn’t just fluff—values
                determine culture fit, so it’s crucial to include

            To optimize efficiency and creativity in media teams by implementing a structured schedule that protects

 Part 01    Understanding the Maker vs. Manager Dynamic


                                    Makers                                                Managers

Part 02     Structuring the Workday
            To balance both needs, implement a split schedule:


            Morning: Maker Time (7 AM - 12 PM)

                •      Blocked for deep work, no meetings unless absolutely critical.
                •      Writers script, editors cut, designers design—without interruptions.
                •      Managers should avoid pulling makers into meetings during this time.


            Afternoon: Manager Time (12 PM - 5 PM)

                •      Reserved for meetings, strategy sessions, team check-ins, and collaborative work.
                •      Creatives who need to meet with others can schedule calls during this window.

            Monday = Manager Day

                       •   Primarily a maker day, but with flexibility for urgent meetings if needed.

                    Use Calendar Blocks
