# Zero to One: Notes on Startups, or How to Build the Future (Peter Thiel with Blake Masters) — Extraction Notes & Entry Map

*Source: book, "Zero to One: Notes on Startups, or How to Build the Future," by Peter Thiel with Blake Masters (2014, ~55,000 words, 14 chapters + conclusion). The raw OCR'd text is archived alongside at `2026-07-13_Book_PeterThiel_ZeroToOne.txt`.*

---

## Nature of the source
Thiel's Stanford-course-turned-book on how to build a company that creates new value ("0 to 1") rather than copying what already works ("1 to n"). Distilled from PayPal, Palantir, and Founders Fund experience. Every chapter carries a distinct, self-contained framework — this is one of the most-cited startup texts and the vault had no coverage of it before this pass.

5. **Ch. 7** — the power law of venture returns, why VCs miss it, and why individuals can't diversify their own careers. → *The Power Law of Venture Capital and Careers*.

8. **Ch. 10** — culture as mission not perks, the "why join as employee #20" question, hiring for sameness + role clarity ("do one thing"), the cult-consultant spectrum. → *The Mechanics of Mafia - Cult-Like Culture and the Recruiting Pitch*.
9. **Ch. 11** — sales is hidden, CLV vs. CAC, the distribution continuum (complex/personal/marketing-advertising/viral), the power law of distribution channels, selling to non-customers. → *Distribution Is Destiny - The Sales Channel Power Law*.
10. **Ch. 12** — substitution (globalization) vs. complementarity (technology), PayPal's Igor fraud system, Palantir, LinkedIn, the ideology of computer science, strong AI. → *Man and Machine - Why Computers Complement Humans Instead of Replacing Them*.

## Key specifics (for accuracy)
- **The contrarian question**: "What important truth do very few people agree with you on?" A good answer takes the form "most people believe x, but the truth is the opposite of x."
- **Monopoly's four characteristics**: proprietary technology (rule of thumb: 10x better), network effects (must be valuable at small scale first — Facebook started at Harvard), economies of scale, branding (Apple's brand rests on substance, not the reverse).
- **The power law**: at Founders Fund, Facebook alone returned more than the rest of their 2005 fund combined; Palantir returned more than everything except Facebook. VCs' rule: only invest in companies that could return the whole fund.
- **CLV vs. CAC** and the distribution continuum: complex sales (7-figure deals, e.g. SpaceX/NASA, Palantir) → personal sales (Box, ZocDoc) → "distribution doldrums" dead zone → marketing/advertising (Warby Parker) → viral (PayPal, Facebook).
- **The seven questions**: engineering, timing, monopoly, people, distribution, durability, secret. Tesla answered all seven; most cleantech companies (Solyndra, Better Place, MiaSolé) answered none.
- **PayPal's "Igor"**: human-plus-computer fraud detection hybrid that turned PayPal profitable in Q1 2002 after losing $29.3M the year before — the seed of the "complementarity, not substitution" thesis and later Palantir.
- **Board size**: three is ideal, never exceed five (private companies). CEO salary ceiling: no more than $150,000/year at an early-stage venture-backed startup.
- **Founder fat-tailed distribution**: founders are simultaneously insider/outsider, rich/poor-on-paper, charismatic/disagreeable — examples: Branson, Sean Parker, Howard Hughes, Bill Gates, Steve Jobs.
