## Nature of the source
The definitive research-backed account of **why things spread by word of mouth**. Berger's thesis: virality is *made, not born*; it's driven by the **message**, not special "influential" messengers; and ~93% of word of mouth happens **offline**, not on social media. The book distills a decade of studies (New York Times Most E-Mailed analysis, BzzAgent campaign data, 1.5M car sales, baby names) into a six-part framework — **STEPPS**: Social Currency, Triggers, Emotion, Public, Practical Value, Stories. One principle per chapter, each independently applicable.

## Key specifics (for accuracy)
- **STEPPS** = **S**ocial Currency, **T**riggers, **E**motion, **P**ublic, **P**ractical Value, **S**tories. Not a recipe — independent "salad toppings"; use any subset.
- **Message > messenger** — like a great joke, contagious content spreads regardless of who tells it; rebuts Gladwell's "special few."
- **7% not 50%** — only ~7% of word of mouth happens online (Keller Fay); we overestimate because online is visible/recorded.
- **Social Currency** — Please Don't Tell (secret bar), $100 cheesesteak, Snapple "Real Facts," frequent-flier tiers, Foursquare badges, Rue La La (insider access), McRib (manufactured scarcity). *Social incentives beat monetary ones* — paying crowds out intrinsic sharing.
- **Triggers** — Mars bar sales rose during NASA's Mars Pathfinder; "Friday" searched on Fridays; Kit Kat + coffee (+8% then +33% sales); dining-tray slogan lifted veg intake 25%; voting in a school raised school-funding votes. *Grow the habitat*; frequency must balance link strength ("red" is too diluted); trigger near the point of action (Man Drinks Fat vs. the bath-mat ad).
- **Emotion** — awe, anger, anxiety, amusement, excitement (high arousal) → more sharing; sadness, contentment (low arousal) → less. Running-in-place experiment: physical arousal alone lifted sharing to 75%. "United Breaks Guitars"; Google "Parisian Love"; the Heaths' "Three Whys."
- **Public** — Apple flipped the laptop logo for observability; social proof (kidney refusals cascade, tip jars, MBA career herding); make the private public (Movember moustaches, "I Voted" stickers); self-advertising products (Hotmail signature, white iPod earbuds); **behavioral residue** (Livestrong yellow bands, Lululemon bags); *reverse it to prevent behavior* — anti-drug ads and "don't steal wood" backfired by signaling that many do it.
- **Practical Value** — prospect theory (Kahneman): reference points, diminishing sensitivity; same $10 drive taken for a clock radio but not a TV; "sale" label lifted demand 50% at identical price; quantity limits +50%. **Rule of 100**: below $100 show %-off, above $100 show $-off. *Narrow* audiences can share more than broad ones. Beware sharing false-but-useful info (vaccine-autism fraud).
- **Stories** — stories are Trojan horses / proof by analogy; harder to argue with than claims. **Valuable virality**: the brand must be integral to the narrative (Panda cheese, Blendtec, Dove "Evolution", Subway/Jared) — else it fails (the "fool in the pool"/GoldenPalace, Evian "Roller Babies"). Allport & Postman: retelling sharpens toward critical details; irrelevant ones drop out.

## Caveats
- The examples are illustrative and dated (2013); the *principles* are the durable, portable signal — entries condense those, not the anecdotes.
- Berger notes the Livestrong/Armstrong case predates the doping revelations; the mechanism (behavioral residue) stands independent of the messenger.
