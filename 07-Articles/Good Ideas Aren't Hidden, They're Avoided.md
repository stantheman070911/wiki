# Good Ideas Aren't Hidden, They're Avoided

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[07-Articles/Articles Index|Articles]]

**Article of the day · ~2 min read**

There is a strange fact about good ideas: there are still some left. In a world of billions of educated, connected, ambitious people all searching the same internet, it should be obvious that anything worth finding has already been found. Peter Thiel calls this feeling *flatness*—the sense that the globe is small, fully mapped, and staffed everywhere by clever people, so any real opportunity would already be gone. Flatness is a good description of how the world feels and a poor description of how it works.

The reason opportunities remain is not that they are hidden far away. It is that reaching them means going somewhere uncomfortable, and discomfort is an extremely effective filter. The good ideas are less undiscovered than avoided.

What repels people is consistent. One repellent is tedium. Paul Graham calls it *schlep blindness*: the mind quietly refuses to see problems that would require dull, messy work—regulation, payments, logistics—so those problems stay unsolved by anyone willing to do them. Stripe was built on the schlep of programmer payments, and the very unpleasantness that kept others away was also its protection from casual competitors. A second repellent is taboo. Thiel argues that the most underrated secrets are about people: the things a group is not allowed to say, or will not admit to itself. Those require no credential to find, only a willingness to ask what is forbidden. A third is unfashionableness. Nutrition matters enormously, is not a prestigious academic field, and had much of its foundational research done decades ago and often flawed—exactly the profile of a place where truths could still be lying around, precisely because status-seeking talent has clustered elsewhere.

That clustering is the engine. Fashion compresses exploration: capable people crowd onto the same prestigious problems and the same crowded channels, which empties the surrounding territory of competitors and lowers the price of attention there. Sabri Suby, unable to afford search clicks that cost thirty-six dollars each while larger agencies spent a hundred and fifty thousand dollars a month, wired a radio station seven thousand dollars to run its unsold drive-time slots, recorded as a blind read on a cheap microphone. The spots produced roughly seven hundred leads in the first month, for one reason: he was the only digital-marketing agency on radio. The channel was cheap because it was empty, and empty because everyone else had copied one another into the expensive one.

The deepest repellent is social. A genuine secret has not been vetted by the mainstream, so pursuing one risks being not merely lonely but *lonely and wrong*—a prospect most people find unbearable even when they could tolerate being lonely and right. This is why Thiel's contrarian question—what important truth do very few people agree with you on?—is hard in two ways at once. The answer has to be true, and holding it has to cost you something.

None of this argues for reflexive contrarianism. Reversing the crowd mechanically still lets the crowd choose the question and the answer; you have only agreed to be defined by them in the negative. Two disciplines separate a real opportunity from a mere provocation. First, sort the secret from the mystery: a secret is unknown but reachable with effort, while a mystery may be permanently unsolvable, and years spent on the second are wasted. Second, earn enough expertise to tell an anomaly from your own ignorance. From a distance a mature field looks smooth; up close its edge is fractal, full of gaps—but only someone who has learned the map can tell a real gap from a place they simply have not read.

So the useful question is not *where is the idea nobody has found?* That keeps you hunting for buried treasure on the far side of the map. It is *what am I refusing to look at because it bores me, embarrasses me, or would make me the only one there?* The opportunities are rarely on the frontier of the unknown. They sit just behind the ordinary human reflex to look away.
