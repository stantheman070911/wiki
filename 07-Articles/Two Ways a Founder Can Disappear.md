# Two Ways a Founder Can Disappear

**Article of the day · ~2 min read**

There are two ways for a founder to disappear.

In the first, the founder is visible everywhere. Every unusual customer request, product decision, and quality problem returns to the same desk. Work moves quickly when that person is available and stops when they are not. The founder has not left the company; they have disappeared *into* its workflow.

In the second, the founder rises above the organization chart. Capable executives own their departments, reports arrive polished, and contact with frontline work becomes rare. The company can move without waiting, but the founder may learn about a decaying product or changing customer only after the signal has passed through several filters. This founder has disappeared from reality.

These failures look like opposites, but both confuse **authority flow with information flow**. Michael Gerber’s technician keeps absorbing the work personally, so the organization never learns to produce the result. Paul Graham’s warning about “manager mode” points the other way: delegation can turn branches of a company into black boxes. Graham also cautions that founder mode is not a license to avoid delegation. The boundaries will vary; some delegation is unavoidable.

Picture a weekly product review built around that distinction. A named operator owns the release and makes the decision. The founder does not approve every screen or assign the follow-up tasks. But they sample the actual work, hear the customer’s language, and inspect two or three company-defining quality bars. Direct access does not silently cancel the operator’s authority.

Now a recurring defect appears. The operator fixes this release. Then the room takes a second pass, because Ray Dalio gives every problem two jobs: resolve the case and train or test the organizational machine. Why did the process produce this result? Does the team need a clearer example, a checkpoint, better training, or a different metric? Gerber’s loop keeps the standard from hardening into dead routine: test a change, measure it, install the better method, and reopen it when reality changes. The chain-store 3S model captures the sequence neatly—develop the know-how, standardize it, then simplify it for transfer.

Not every judgment can or should be reduced to a checklist. A founder may retain a few explicit decision rights where unusual context matters. But recurring judgment should become teachable, and exceptions need a visible route back to the process owner. If the founder overrides, the override is named, its reasoning is captured, and authority returns afterward.

At the end of the review, the founder has not taken over the operator’s follow-up work. The operator still owns the release and now carries a sharper standard. Work can continue when the founder is absent; uncomfortable information can still reach them when present. The company is learning to survive the succession test Jim Collins sets for durable leadership—without making its founder either the workflow or a ghost above it.
