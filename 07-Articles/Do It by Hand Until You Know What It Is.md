# Do It by Hand Until You Know What It Is

**Article of the day · ~2 min read**

A founder of a real-estate AI SaaS reported adding about 150 subscribers a week and losing roughly 100. The company had an onboarding checklist, instructional videos, and product tours—but no human involved in onboarding.

The prescribed response was deliberately inefficient: start with two live group onboardings a day. Watch customers create a first video and edit a first photo. Record where they hesitate and which steps need explanation. The sessions were not merely support. They were a measurement instrument for questions the company’s current dashboard could not yet answer.

An automated path embeds decisions made in advance: which steps exist, their default order, what counts as completion, and which variations the system is prepared to handle. That is leverage when the process is understood. When it is not, automation can harden assumptions and make their failure harder to see. **Manual work is not primitive automation. It is the research phase that tells automation what to become.**

Stripe’s founders performed “Collison installations,” setting up the product on willing users’ laptops. Airbnb’s founders went door to door in New York to recruit hosts and photograph their listings. At Viaweb, Paul Graham built stores for reluctant merchants; when he hit a missing feature mid-build, he implemented it and resumed the job. Customer friction could become product change within hours.

But manual service can create a flattering illusion. If a skilled human quietly carries every customer across the finish line, the company may learn only that skilled humans are helpful. When the eventual product requires customer action, let the customer perform the critical step while the operator records who they are, what they want, where they stop, and what intervention works. Those observations are hypotheses, not proof. Before encoding a pattern, compare later outcomes across representative users and test the remedy against a baseline. A step becomes a candidate for automation when similar users repeatedly encounter the same friction and the same remedy produces the same useful outcome.

Michael Gerber’s operating loop places orchestration after innovation and quantification: test a change, measure its impact, then make the better method the operating default. That default is not a guess about how work ought to happen; it is the best-known method produced by tested experience. A mature company can keep the loop alive without destabilizing everyone by forming a small, representative “test kitchen,” applying extra attention to a shared problem, preserving the failed results, and rolling out only what survives. Scale no longer has to mean losing contact with discovery.

If the software company links those sessions to later behavior, it will leave with more than support notes. It can see which customer types and acquisition channels are associated with retention, which early actions deserve testing as activation signals, and where confusion recurs. Only then should it rebuild the automated path. For a while, the person beside the customer is not standing in for missing software. They are finding out what the software is.
