# Design for the Second Audience

**Article of the day · ~2 min read**

On Apple's PowerBook, the lid logo originally doubled as a compass. With the computer closed, the apple faced the owner, making it easier to tell which edge to open. But once the laptop was open, everyone else saw the logo upside down. Steve Jobs asked Ken Segall's team which mattered more: having the logo look right to the owner before opening the PowerBook, or to everyone else while it was in use. Apple flipped the logo.

The laptop did not become faster; its owner actually lost a small orientation cue. Apple traded a moment of private convenience for public legibility. What changed was the experience of a second audience: the people in the room who might become the next customers.

It is easy to judge a product only through the first audience—the person who buys, uses, or benefits. But useful work often has witnesses: a colleague forwards the report, a friend notices the habit, an email recipient sees which service sent the message. **The first audience decides whether value exists. The second decides whether it spreads.**

This matters because social proof depends on visibility. Research summarized by Robert Cialdini shows that people use others' behavior as a shortcut for what is appropriate, especially when they are uncertain and the people observed seem similar to them. But private satisfaction produces no signal. A consultant may prevent a costly mistake, software may quietly save five hours, or a coach may improve someone's judgment. If nothing observable remains, a good result can still look like nothing happened.

Physical and digital products can make normal use visible. Hotmail made each message carry evidence of the service that sent it; Apple's white iPod cords made adoption visible in public. The harder case is service work, where the most valuable change may happen inside a decision or disappear into a prevented problem. Here the residue might be a decision map used in the next meeting, a progress record the customer wants to share, or a useful artifact that travels to another team. The “capture, don't create” principle applies: with the customer's permission, documentation can become part of doing the work rather than a campaign performed afterward.

But visibility is not automatically good. A large logo proves only that a logo is large. A visible trace should be useful to the customer first and recognizable to the observer second. A strange detail earns useful distribution only if it helps the observer infer the right value or identity. Publicizing an unwanted behavior can even normalize it by making the behavior seem common. The design question is therefore not merely, “Will people notice?” It is, **“What will they conclude—and what will they be tempted to do next?”**

Return to the PowerBook. Apple did not place an advertisement beside the product; it changed which way a real part of the product faced once work began. The first audience could tell Apple whether the laptop succeeded. The harder question was what someone watching from across the room could truthfully learn from that success.
