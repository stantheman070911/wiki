# Trust Should Have a Credit Limit

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[07-Articles/Articles Index|Articles]]

**Article of the day · ~2 min read**

At the medieval Champagne fairs, strangers did not trade by simply deciding to trust one another. The “Guards of the Fair” notarized contracts, enforced performance, could seize a defaulting debtor’s goods, and could bar that trader from future fairs. The last penalty worked because access to tomorrow’s market was worth more than profit from one betrayal today.

That arrangement exposes a mistake we still make: treating trust as a binary judgment about character. Someone is trustworthy or they are not; a partner is “like family” or kept at arm’s length. But trust works better as a **credit limit**—a level of exposure earned through evidence and raised through performance.

Before extending it, ask five questions. Can this person’s identity be linked to a history? What verifies the capability they claim? What do they lose if they defect? How much damage can occur before you notice? What remedy remains afterward? A persuasive answer to one question cannot compensate for silence on the others. Reputation without persistent identity can be discarded. Expertise without retained downside permits confident mistakes. Good intentions without containment can still produce ruin.

This is why skin in the game matters. When the person receiving the upside also bears meaningful downside, incentives, information, and learning move closer together. Yet exposure alone does not prove competence. A person can sincerely risk their own money and still be wrong. Track record matters too: repeated results under relevant conditions, supported by reasoning that can be examined. Transparency completes the picture by making problems visible—but transparency without judgment produces noise, just as judgment without transparency produces confident decisions from filtered information.

The practical alternative to blind trust is not permanent suspicion. It is **staged trust**. Give a new hire a real but bounded decision. Begin a vendor relationship with milestones, audit rights, or a paid trial. Land a client with a tightly scoped project, document what happened, then expand. Trusted referrers accelerate the first step because they lend reputation, but they should not erase the steps that follow. In every case, the sequence is the same: limit exposure, observe behavior, record the result, then widen authority.

This also changes what a “high-trust culture” means. It is not a place with fewer controls because everyone is assumed to be good. It is a place where truth travels quickly, promises are observable, consequences survive the decision date, and honest performance earns greater freedom. Controls become lighter as evidence accumulates, while catastrophic exposure remains bounded because even an excellent history cannot eliminate tail risk.

So the next time you are unsure whether to trust someone, replace the character verdict with a design question: **What is the smallest consequential commitment that would produce useful evidence without exposing either side to ruin?** Make that commitment clear, let reality answer, and adjust the limit.
