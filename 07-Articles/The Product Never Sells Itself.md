# The Product Never Sells Itself

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[07-Articles/Articles Index|Articles]]

**Article of the day · ~2 min read**

Better Place had the technology. The Israeli startup raised more than $800 million to build electric cars with swappable batteries—a genuinely clever answer to range anxiety. It sold roughly a thousand cars before going bankrupt in 2013. The engineering was not the problem. Almost no one could figure out how to actually buy the thing.

This is the failure founders least expect, because it contradicts the story they quietly tell themselves: build something good enough and the market will find it. Peter Thiel states the correction flatly—invent something new but no effective way to sell it, and you have a bad business no matter how good the product. Poor sales, not poor engineering, is the most common reason a company ends up with zero distribution channels working at all.

The lesson is easy to miss because good distribution is built to be invisible. Every selling job wears a euphemism—"account executive," "business development," "growth"—precisely so that no one feels sold to. When a product appears to sell itself, someone is usually selling it in the background, or fooling themselves. Thiel's line is the tell: if you don't see any salespeople, you're the salesperson.

Distribution also has a structure worth learning, not merely an effort worth admiring. It follows its own power law, where one channel that works beats three that half-work. Which channel is not a matter of taste; it is set by arithmetic. A customer's lifetime value has to clear what it costs to acquire them, and the price point decides the regime. Seven-figure deals need a founder in the room—Palantir's chief executive reportedly spent twenty-five days a month meeting clients himself. Hundred-dollar products need the biggest megaphone you can afford, which is why Warby Parker ran television ads. Between those sits a dead zone around a thousand dollars, where advertising is too broad and a salesperson too expensive per sale. That "doldrums" is why so many genuinely useful tools never reach small businesses. The obstacle is distribution economics, not product quality and not customer backwardness.

Choosing well also means choosing narrow. PayPal ignored the large, slow market of people wiring money abroad and went after eBay's roughly twenty thousand "PowerSellers"—a small segment that transacted constantly and had no decent way to pay each other. Dominating that first made PayPal almost impossible to dislodge. The highest-velocity segment beats the biggest one.

Here is the part that should rescue the product-obsessed from despair: distribution is not the opposite of building. It is how you find out what to build. The authors of *Traction* argue for spending something like half your attention on getting customers from the very start—not because promotion outranks the product, but because cold traffic is an honest test. Friends and early enthusiasts tolerate friction that strangers never will; putting the product in front of people who owe you nothing exposes where it leaks, and it exposes it sooner. Sell early, and the market tells you what to make.

That same work rewards the contrarian. When every competitor bids the same keywords, attention there is expensive and your message dissolves into the crowd. Sabri Suby, late to a market where agency clicks cost thirty-six dollars, instead wired a radio station seven thousand dollars to run the leftover spots nobody else wanted—recorded on a cheap desk microphone—and pulled seven hundred leads in a month, because he was the only digital agency on radio. The fishing is best where the fewest go.

None of this diminishes the product. It relocates it. The best-*known* option wins the default, not the best one, and being best-known is something you design on purpose—before you build, not as a reward a good product collects on its own behalf. So the first question about anything you make is not only "is this good?" It is "who, exactly, will carry it to the people who need it—and if I can't picture that person, have I noticed that it's me?"
