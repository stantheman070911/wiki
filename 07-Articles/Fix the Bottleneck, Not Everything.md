# Fix the Bottleneck, Not Everything

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[07-Articles/Articles Index|Articles]]

**Article of the day · ~2 min read**

When a business stalls, the instinct is to add: a new channel, a new product, a new hire, a new tool—sometimes a new company. Addition feels ambitious. Often, it is avoidance. The existing business has visible friction; the new idea has none only because you haven't operated it yet. So you trade one known, solvable problem for a fresh collection of hidden ones.

The harder truth is that a system does not improve merely because many things improve. Throughput rises only when the one step limiting the whole system improves. A production line with 9,999 of 10,000 steps working still moves at the speed of the one that is stalled. In a service business, that constraint usually sits on one of two sides: **demand** or **supply**. If you cannot fulfill what you sell, more leads make the problem worse. If you have idle capacity, polishing fulfillment does not create customers. Growth is an alternating rhythm: fix the side that is binding until the constraint moves, then follow it.

Diagnosis begins with a counterfactual: assume the thing you want has already happened. If your leads doubled tomorrow, would the rest of the business hold? If not, traffic is not the problem. Then check the numbers. A frightening industry story is not the binding constraint today unless it appears in churn, conversion, acquisition cost, capacity, or another operating metric. Real future threats still deserve an owner and a date—but they should not steal today's resources merely because they are vivid.

Once you find the constraint, sequence matters. Question the requirement. Delete what should not exist. Simplify what survives. Only then accelerate and automate. Reversing that order creates the perfect machine for doing unnecessary work. The same bias appears in growth: when inefficiency is the constraint, train the current people before hiring more, improve the current product before adding another, and use the current tools before buying new ones. **Better beats more** because every addition creates another surface to manage—and another place for the next bottleneck to hide.

So try this today. Write down the outcome you want, the number that is not moving, and whether the blockage is demand or supply. Choose one intervention, one owner, one metric, and one review date. Put every other clever idea on a parking-lot list. When the number moves, diagnose again to see whether the constraint moved with it.

Progress is not how many things are moving. It is whether the bottleneck moved.
