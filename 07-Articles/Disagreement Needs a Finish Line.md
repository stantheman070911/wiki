# Disagreement Needs a Finish Line

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[07-Articles/Articles Index|Articles]]

**Article of the day · ~2 min read**

Peter Drucker tells of a meeting of one of General Motors' top committees. Alfred Sloan asked whether everyone was in complete agreement. They were. So he postponed further discussion until the next meeting, giving the group time to develop disagreement and discover what the decision was really about.

The anecdote is a defense of dissent. Its more useful detail is the deadline: **the next meeting**. Sloan did not turn a decision into a permanent seminar. He made room for conflict, then gave it a finish line.

Consequential decisions fail in both directions. Consensus reached too quickly can hide assumptions, special pleading, and missing alternatives. But disagreement allowed to run indefinitely stops serving the decision. It can become a contest of status, stamina, or eloquence. The first group closes before it has learned enough; the second keeps learning—or merely talking—after it needs to act. Disagreement is a phase of decision-making, not a permanent operating condition.

Before Sloan's committee reconvenes, each position should become harder to caricature. Treat it as a hypothesis: What is its central claim? What evidence would support it? What observation would weaken it? Paul Graham's hierarchy of disagreement is useful here because it moves attention away from the speaker's identity and tone toward the argument's central point. Ray Dalio adds a practical test: describe the other person's view back to them until they agree you understand it. Two uninterrupted minutes can reveal that apparent opponents are answering different questions.

Then friction can improve the work. Rick Rubin distinguishes cooperation from the struggle to have one's own idea win. In creative work, specific feedback about the piece—not a verdict on its maker—can expose the need beneath two competing proposals. Sometimes A and B produce a genuinely better C. The point is not compromise. It is to use difference to create an option that nobody could see alone.

But not every conflict yields a Choice C, and uncertainty does not disappear because everyone has spoken. The group therefore needs a closure rule chosen before the argument: a named responsible person, an agreed vote, or a clear escalation path. Everyone may have the right to question a decision; that does not give everyone equal authority to make it. Without this distinction, openness can decay into veto by persistence.

Closure does not require people to pretend they agree. It requires the decision to become work: state what was chosen, name the owner, assign the next action and date, and specify what evidence would justify reopening the question. The dissenting case is preserved as a thought-through alternative and a warning signal—not as permission to quietly undermine execution.

When a committee reconvenes after such a pause, the room may still contain disagreement. That is not failure. If the process worked, the decision-maker now has competing explanations, relevant tests, and a fallback if reality rejects the chosen path. Someone can decide. Someone can act. The dissenting minority knows how the decision can be revisited. Disagreement has done its job, and execution can begin.
