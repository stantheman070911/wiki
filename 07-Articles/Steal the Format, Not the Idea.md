# Steal the Format, Not the Idea

**Article of the day · ~2 min read**

Everyone starting out believes the same flattering thing: that the way to win attention is to think of something no one has thought of before. It's the most expensive belief in content. When you build from a blank page, the odds that a stranger resonates with your brand-new idea are near zero — and you've spent your scarcest resource, effort, finding that out.

The creators who win almost every time do the opposite. They don't invent; they **reverse-engineer**. The insight underneath their success is that a piece of content has three layers — *topic*, *format*, and *hook* — and they rank in the exact opposite order of how beginners rank them. Topic (what it's about) matters least. Format (the structure — the reaction, the before/after, the walk-up, the silent-film story) matters most, and it's the part you're *supposed* to copy: nobody owns a trending format, and the algorithm already knows how to push it. The hook decides whether anyone sees any of it. Steal the format, wrap it in a hook about the viewer, insert your brand, and you've skipped the lottery entirely.

This isn't a short-form trick. It's how craft has always been learned. Ask a copywriter how to get good and the answer is **copy work**: by hand, transcribe the greatest ads and essays word for word, an hour a day for months, until the rules and texture live in your fingers. It's how you learn an instrument — you play "Jingle Bells" before you write a symphony. You copy until you can feel *why* the great ones work, and only then do you recombine. Originality, it turns out, is just good theft, metabolized.

The pattern even scales into a mental model. The way to build any framework is to **distill by commonality** — study the things that already work, find what they share, and name it. That's what a "format" is: a viral outlier, templatized. When something of yours hits, don't treat it as a one-off miracle. Break it into its checkable features, then **double down** — repurpose and repost the same skeleton. One proven format, run again and again, beats a hundred original ideas that never land.

So the move for today is small and slightly humbling. Open the app, find three videos with a million views from *small* accounts (proof the format — not the following — is carrying them), and write down the skeleton. Then build yours inside it. Save your creativity for the 10% that's actually yours: your story, your proof, your voice. Rent the other 90% from what already works.

Originality is overrated. Fluency is the game — and fluency is learned by copying until it's yours.
