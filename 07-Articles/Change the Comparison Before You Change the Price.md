# Change the Comparison Before You Change the Price

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[07-Articles/Articles Index|Articles]]

**Article of the day · ~2 min read**

When a prospect says, “That's too expensive,” the reflex is to defend or lower the number. Both accept the buyer's frame: your offer belongs in the same box as the cheaper alternative. Often, the objection is not a verdict on price but evidence that the buyer cannot see a meaningful difference. When two offers look equivalent, the cheapest one wins. A discount does not escape that comparison; it competes harder inside it.

Buyers evaluate relative to context. The contrast principle shows that the same thing feels different depending on what appeared before it. Positioning makes a related market-level point: people organize choices on mental ladders—leader, alternative, also-ran—and filter claims through them. “Better quality” rarely creates a new rung because every seller says it. A challenger can win by owning an unclaimed difference: a different audience, occasion, method, lower-risk promise, or experience the leader cannot credibly own. **Do not ask how to look better inside the existing category. Ask what you want the buyer to compare you with.**

But differentiation cannot be copywriting pasted onto identical delivery. Start with the category's recurring pain: what is unreliable, confusing, slow, risky, impersonal, or difficult? Remove one of those failures in the operation, then make the removal obvious in the offer. Technical features should be translated into the scene the customer actually wants—and the feeling attached to it. Aesop, the skincare retailer, illustrates the shift: diagnosis replaces a generic pitch, sampling comes before selling, and packaging and ritual become part of the product. The function may be familiar; the experience is no longer interchangeable.

Once the difference is real, make it easy to believe. Plain language beats insider vocabulary, and **proof must be louder than the claim**: use a demonstration, before-and-after, specific result, or relevant customer story. Reduce the remaining risk with a guarantee. A strong offer makes the result more desirable and believable while reducing delay and effort. Now the buyer is not comparing feature lists alone. Raising the price by itself is not positioning. A premium needs a visible reason, and delivery must keep the promise.

So try this today. Complete the sentence a price-shopping customer would use: “You are another ___.” List five things customers hate about that category, then choose one you can genuinely remove. Change the operation, state the difference as a one-sentence outcome, and attach one piece of proof. Then ask three past customers, “How would you describe us to a friend?” If the phrase repeats, it may be the position you already own. Build around it before you touch the price.

When offers look equivalent, arguing about the number will not end the price war. Change the comparison before you change the price.
