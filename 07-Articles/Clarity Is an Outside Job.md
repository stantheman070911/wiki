# Clarity Is an Outside Job

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[07-Articles/Articles Index|Articles]]

**Article of the day · ~2 min read**

At a TED conference, a brilliant molecular biologist stood up to present his latest findings and lost the room in roughly four seconds. He opened exactly as he would with peers—no problem stated, no stakes, straight into the experiments, in the vocabulary of his subfield. Steven Pinker, who was watching, recalls that every one of the several hundred people in the room could see that nobody understood a word. Everyone, that is, except the biologist.

The popular theory of bad communication says it is deliberate: experts dress up banal ideas in gobbledygook to sound sophisticated, or hide behind jargon to keep outsiders out. Pinker rejects that. The people writing impenetrably are often brilliant, with plenty to say and no wish to obscure it. They suffer instead from the curse of knowledge—a term borrowed from economics for the near-impossibility of subtracting what you know from your own mind and imagining a reader without it. Jargon, unexplained acronyms, and stacked abstractions are not choices. They are symptoms.

What makes the curse genuinely cruel is that it cannot be detected from the inside. What is obvious to you does not feel like it needs explaining—that is what obvious means—so introspection keeps returning a false all-clear. The biologist was not clueless about biology. He was clueless about where his audience was standing, and no amount of staring at his own slides could have told him.

The cost is not confined to lecture halls. Alex Hormozi describes a Harvard-trained clinic owner whose ads for a technical depression treatment kept explaining the mechanism—and kept failing. The prescription was to drop the copy far below the expert's comfort zone, to something like a third-grade reading level, and to sell only the outcome and the removed risk: you're sad, we make you happy, insurance pays. Every word a reader must pause to decode is a moment they stop listening. Hormozi's summary of the stance is three words: "clear over clever." And because credentialed rivals won't lower themselves to plain language, the expert who does gets the market's attention almost uncontested.

It would be easy to hear all this as a concession—smart people talking down. The opposite is closer to the truth. Paul Graham treats plain rewriting as a reasoning instrument: when a sentence resists being said simply, the defect is usually in the idea, not the phrasing, and some of his essays changed their thesis mid-revision because the writing exposed a better one. Steven Bartlett ran the same test in public, committing to explain one idea a day in plain language; whatever the audience couldn't restate became the syllabus for what he hadn't actually understood. Simplification is not a courtesy extended to the reader. It is an audit of the writer.

Notice what every working remedy has in common. Show the draft to an intelligent reader outside your field and watch where they stumble. Run the copy through a readability score—a mechanical stand-in for that reader. Read it aloud, letting your ear catch what your eye forgives. Publish on a cadence and track the questions that come back. Not one of these happens inside your own head, because inside your own head is precisely where the curse lives.

So when your next explanation doesn't land, the tempting diagnosis is that the audience wasn't sharp enough. The likelier one is that you couldn't feel what you know. The fix is not to think harder in private. It is to get the draft in front of someone who owes you nothing—before the version that matters goes out.
