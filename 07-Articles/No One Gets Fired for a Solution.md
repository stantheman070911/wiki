# No One Gets Fired for a Solution

> **Up:** [[Home|Home]] → [[07-Articles/Articles Index|Articles]]

**Article of the day · ~2 min read**

In July 1979, International Harvester's planners delivered a thick sheaf of paper titled "Corporate Strategic Plan." Five operating divisions, each with market-share targets and cost reductions, and a forecast profit curve rising smoothly off the page. Nothing in it was false. What the document never mentioned was that Harvester's work rules let senior employees transfer jobs at will, each transfer setting off a cascade of others; that its profit margin had run at roughly half its competitors' for years as a result; and that it had the worst labor relations in American industry. Richard Rumelt calls this the elephant in the elevator, and notes that you cannot find it by studying the plan, because the plan does not mention it. Over the following six years the company lost more than three billion dollars, closed thirty-five of its forty-two plants, and shed eighty-five thousand workers, leaving fifteen thousand.

The planners were not lazy—they produced hundreds of pages. What they never produced was a sentence naming the obstacle. Rumelt's verdict is blunt: if you fail to identify and analyze the obstacles, you don't have a strategy—you have a stretch goal, a budget, or a list of things you wish would happen.

That sentence goes unwritten not because it is hard to research, but because a diagnosis is not a finding. It is a judgment about the meanings of facts, which means it can be wrong, and wrong in public. Lou Gerstner made that bet against consensus at IBM in 1993. Everyone, inside the company and on Wall Street, had diagnosed IBM as too integrated for a fragmenting industry; separate stock offerings for the pieces were already being prepared. Gerstner decided the opposite—that IBM's problem was failing to use the integration it had. Same company, same facts, two readings, each ruling out everything the other recommended. He could not prove his was right beforehand. It was.

Shrink the scale and the same failure passes for ordinary competence. Neil Rackham's research found that the biggest mistake weaker sellers make is to hear a problem and answer it with their solution—and that talking about solutions too soon produces objections and lowers the odds of a sale. The resistance that feels like a difficult customer was largely manufactured by the seller who skipped ahead. Keenan's version: a stated need is only a hypothesis. "No dashboard" is a condition, not a problem; the missed decisions and lost revenue it causes are the problem, and until someone names which, any proposal is a guess wearing a price tag.

Where the field genuinely splits is over who is entitled to make the diagnosis. Rackham's answer is the buyer—keep asking until they say it out loud, because a conclusion they reach themselves is one they will defend. Matthew Dixon and Brent Adamson point the other way: in complex categories customers do not want to be walked through problems they already know they have, they want to be told what they should be worrying about. Grainger's reps taught customers to sort spending into planned and unplanned, then showed that forty percent of a typical maintenance budget sat in a category they had never thought to track. The reaction being engineered is not "you understand me" but "I'd never thought about it that way." Opposite prescriptions, one shared premise: what the customer says is wrong is not what is wrong.

Inside a company the failure has no counterparty at all, which makes it easier to get away with. Ray Dalio calls going straight from noticing a problem to proposing a fix the most common failure in management, and offers a test for whether you have actually arrived: root causes are adjectives, not verbs. "He missed the deadline" describes an event. "He is disorganized under time pressure" describes something that will recur, and can therefore be designed around. His list of why managers diagnose badly ends on the two honest items: ego, and fear of the consequences of saying it plainly.

Those two are the whole story. A solution is safe: borrowed from a competitor, lifted from a book, defensible as best practice, and when it fails the failure belongs to circumstances. A diagnosis is attributable. It says this is what is wrong here, these other things are therefore not the point, and I could not prove any of it before we committed. Nobody is blamed for proposing a reasonable remedy to an unnamed disease—which is precisely why the remedy is what gets proposed, in the plan, on the call, and in the meeting where everyone already knows the real problem and waits for someone else to name it.

Harvester's planners wrote hundreds of pages. The sentence they left out was the only one that mattered, and it went unwritten because someone would have had to put their name on it.
